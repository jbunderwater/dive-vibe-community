## Carrington Reefs

Carrington Reefs is a multi-section reef system in the Balabac Strait of southern Palawan, near the maritime border between the Philippines and Malaysia's Sabah state.

## Overview

The Carrington Reefs occupy a position near Balabac Island, among the southernmost reefs in Philippine waters. The reef system comprises several interconnected coral structures rising from the strait's floor, creating a varied diving environment with different sections offering distinct marine communities. The Balabac Strait is known for its strong tidal currents and exceptional marine biodiversity, placing it firmly within the Coral Triangle's most productive zones. Coral coverage is healthy, with mix of hard coral gardens on the shallower sections and gorgonian sea fans on the deeper slopes.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Balabac Strait, southern Palawan, Philippines
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Coral reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 10-25 meters
- **Current**: Tidal, often moderate to strong
- **Best Time**: November to May

## Marine Life

The Balabac Strait's currents support diverse marine life at Carrington Reefs. Napoleon wrasse, bumphead parrotfish, and large grouper are representative of the reef's undisturbed character. Barracuda schools patrol in the mid-water. Sea turtles nest and forage in the broader Balabac area and are regularly encountered at the reef. The area also supports populations of reef sharks and occasional dugong sightings in nearby seagrass areas.

## Dive Profile

Explore individual reef sections in sequence, moving with the current between structures. Each section offers coral gardens at varying depths from around 5 meters down to 20 meters on the outer slopes. The most productive diving is typically along the current-exposed faces of each reef, where fish aggregations are densest.

## Entry and Exit

The Balabac area is remote and requires either a chartered boat from Balabac town or inclusion on a southern Palawan liveaboard route. Entry by giant stride; use an SMB for ascent. Drift diving between reef sections is common when current is running.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Plan for a drift dive if current is running — fighting it is exhausting and unproductive
- The multi-reef structure rewards multiple dives; try different sections on successive dives
- Balabac's marine environment is among the least impacted in the Philippines — treat it with care
- Night diving here, if your boat supports it, reveals different critters not seen in the day
- Confirm the dive plan with your guide as currents in the strait can change direction quickly

## Safety Considerations

Tidal currents in the Balabac Strait can be strong and difficult to predict without local knowledge. Always dive with an experienced local guide or divemaster familiar with the site. Carry drift diving equipment including an SMB, reel, and whistle. The nearest medical facilities are in Balabac town, with more comprehensive care requiring transport to Puerto Princesa.

## Photography

The undisturbed reef ecosystem offers photography of species and reef scenes rarely captured at more visited sites. Wide-angle coral garden shots and portraits of large resident fish are the primary subjects. The current-washed sections are excellent for dynamic schooling fish images.
