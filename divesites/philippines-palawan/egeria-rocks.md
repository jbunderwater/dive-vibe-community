## Egeria Rocks

Egeria Rocks is a submerged rock formation in the southernmost Balabac area, one of several named hazards in the Balabac Strait that double as productive dive sites in the undiscovered waters near the Philippine-Malaysia maritime boundary.

## Overview

Named as a navigational reference on historical charts, Egeria Rocks sits near the southern tip of the Balabac Island group. The underwater structure consists of a series of rock formations with coral growth colonising the surfaces over centuries of undisturbed accumulation. With a maximum diving depth of 18 meters, the site is relatively shallow and suitable for extended exploration. The rocks create sheltered zones on their lee side where fish aggregate, and the exposed faces attract current-feeding species. This is one of the southernmost dive sites in the Philippines and sees extremely limited recreational dive traffic.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Southern Balabac area, Philippines
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Submerged rocks / reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 18 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 10-25 meters
- **Current**: Tidal, can be brisk
- **Best Time**: November to May

## Marine Life

The rock formation's crevices and overhangs shelter resting whitetip reef sharks, lobster, and moray eels. The exposed current-facing sides host sea fans and soft corals with attending anthias and basslets. Sea turtles are regularly seen in this part of the Balabac Strait. Large grouper occupy rocky overhangs, and schools of snapper hover in loose aggregations just above the structure.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the base of the rocks and work upward, exploring the fissures and overhangs for sheltering species. The shallowest sections of the rocks at 3–5 meters form a convenient area for finishing the dive before your safety stop. A torch is useful for illuminating the darker crevices.

## Entry and Exit

Access requires a chartered vessel from Balabac or liveaboard inclusion, given the extreme southerly location. The crossing can be rough in bad weather. Entry by giant stride; the boat should maintain position nearby as return surface swims can be challenging in current.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Bring a torch — the rock crevices harbour species not visible without a light
- Check for resting whitetip reef sharks under ledges and in cave-like recesses
- The shallow maximum depth allows long bottom times; use them for thorough exploration
- This site is so infrequently dived that fish may approach out of curiosity — remain still
- Be aware of your position relative to the rocks if current shifts during the dive

## Safety Considerations

Remote location with limited emergency access. The rocks themselves present an entanglement and abrasion hazard in current — maintain neutral buoyancy and avoid contact with the structure. Always carry an SMB and ensure your buddy system is maintained throughout the dive.

## Photography

Exceptional for macro work in the rock crevices — shy species that retreat at more visited sites remain approachable here. Wide-angle shots of the rock structure with backlit divers or sea fans make striking images. Shark portraits in the crevices are possible with patience.
