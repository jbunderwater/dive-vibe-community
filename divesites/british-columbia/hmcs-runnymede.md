## HMCS Runnymede

HMCS Runnymede is a historic wreck dive in British Columbia, North America.

## Overview

HMCS Runnymede was a River-class frigate built in 1944 that served on North Atlantic convoy escort duties during World War Two before being decommissioned and grounded as a breakwater at Kelsey Bay on northern Vancouver Island in the late 1940s. It rests near its sister ships HMCS Lasalle and HMCS Longueuil in Discovery Passage, where the tidal flows through the Inside Passage support exceptional marine life density typical of this stretch of BC coast. The steel hull, now heavily colonized by encrusting organisms, provides habitat for lingcod, cabezon, and large rockfish populations.

## Wreck History

HMCS Runnymede (K678) was a River-class frigate built by Canadian Vickers Ltd. at Montreal, Quebec. She was laid down 11 September 1943, launched 27 November 1943, and commissioned on 14 June 1944. After working up at Bermuda, Runnymede was assigned to Mid-Ocean Escort Force escort group C-5, known as the "Barber Pole Squadron" for the distinctive striped markings on their funnels. She served as a trans-Atlantic convoy escort until May 1945, at times as Senior Officer's Ship of her group. Following the end of the European war, Runnymede was sent to Esquimalt to undergo tropicalization refit in preparation for Pacific operations, but the work was suspended after Japan's surrender. She was placed in reserve at Esquimalt and paid off on 19 January 1946. Sold to Capital Iron & Metals Ltd. in 1947, Runnymede's hull was expended as a breakwater at Kelsey Bay, BC in 1948. She lies near HMCS LaSalle and HMCS Longueuil along the Kelsey Bay foreshore.

## Marine Life

As with the adjacent HMCS LaSalle and Longueuil hulks, HMCS Runnymede's steel in the tidal flow of Discovery Passage supports dense plumose anemone colonization and rich invertebrate communities. Lingcod and cabezon occupy exposed hull sections. Rockfish — copper and quillback species among others — shelter in recesses. Giant Pacific octopus use the corroded hull. The three frigate hulks together form an extended substrate that creates a larger effective artificial reef habitat than any single vessel alone.

## Dive Profile

Runnymede's hull lies along the Kelsey Bay foreshore in proximity to the LaSalle and Longueuil hulks (coordinates within a few hundred meters of each other). The deteriorated steel structure sits partially buried on the silty bottom of Discovery Passage. Dive depth reaches approximately 28 meters on the seaward face. Discovery Passage currents require careful tidal planning.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access from Campbell River or Kelsey Bay Marina at Sayward. The three Kelsey Bay frigates and the broader Discovery Passage dive environment (including HMCS Columbia at Campbell River) are served by Campbell River-based charter operations.

## Tips and Recommendations

A sign at the Kelsey Bay site reportedly identifies the hulks left to right as Lasalle, Longueuil, Cardena, and Runnymede — useful for orientation. A multi-day Campbell River liveaboard or charter itinerary covering all three frigates plus the Campbell River artificial reefs provides the most efficient access. The "Barber Pole Squadron" history gives Runnymede a distinctive wartime story worth knowing before the dive.

## Safety Considerations

Discovery Passage tidal currents; dive within slack-water window only. Drysuit required (7–10°C). Partially collapsed structure; no penetration without proper assessment. Remote location with limited emergency services.
