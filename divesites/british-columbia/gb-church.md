## G.B. Church

G.B. Church is a historic wreck dive in British Columbia, North America.

## Overview

The G.B. Church was a 175-foot freighter deliberately scuttled on August 11, 1991 off the northeast shore of Portland Island near Sidney — it was the Artificial Reef Society of BC's very first artificial reef project. The ship sits upright with the main deck at around 15 metres and the bottom at roughly 26 metres, making it one of the most accessible wreck dives in the Gulf Islands and a manageable circumnavigation in a single dive. Rockfish, lingcod, wolf eels, and giant Pacific octopus have all taken up residence on the hull, and in spring it's not unusual to find lingcod or cabezon guarding egg masses on the deck plating.

## Wreck History

The G.B. Church was a steel-hulled coastal freighter 53 meters (175 feet) in length, originally built in 1943 under the name Cerium and designed as a munitions carrier during the Second World War. The Artificial Reef Society of BC (ARSBC) acquired the vessel for its inaugural artificial reef project — a process that began in 1989 and required extensive decontamination, including purging of all fuel and oil systems, removal of hazardous materials, and cutting of strategically placed diver access points throughout the hull. The ship was deliberately scuttled on 11 August 1991 within Princess Margaret Marine Park off the northeast shore of Portland Island, near Sidney, on a flat sandy bottom chosen for its proximity to local dive operators and its economic benefit to the region. The G.B. Church was the first of eight vessels that ARSBC would sink over the following decades; it served as a proving ground for the more complex naval ship projects that followed. National Geographic featured the wreck in a documentary titled "Search for the Giant Octopus."

## Marine Life

Giant Pacific octopus (the subject of the National Geographic documentary) are regularly found under the hull on the sandy bottom near the bow. Wolf eels occupy the interior and crevices of the hull. Rockfish shelter throughout the wreck structure. In spring, lingcod and cabezon are often observed guarding egg masses on the deck plating. Nudibranchs and sculpins inhabit the encrusting growth on the hull exterior. The hull is now heavily colonized by encrusting tubeworms, plumose anemones, and other invertebrates, with the wreck functioning as a self-contained artificial reef habitat.

## Dive Profile

The G.B. Church sits upright on a flat sandy bottom with the main deck at approximately 15 meters and the maximum depth to the bottom at approximately 26–27 meters. The ship is 53 meters long, making a complete circumnavigation achievable in a single dive with efficient air management. Diver access holes cut throughout the hull allow penetration — the ARSBC designed the access points so that natural light is always visible, making this a friendly wreck for divers new to penetration diving. Visibility in the Gulf Islands is typically 9–20 meters, best in fall through winter.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access only. Sidney is the primary departure port, approximately 5 km from Portland Island. Charter operators in Sidney (including Esquimalt Ecotours and Emerald Ocean Charters) run regular trips to the site. The wreck lies within Princess Margaret Marine Park, a protected area within Gulf Islands National Park Reserve.

## Tips and Recommendations

Spring is the best season to find lingcod or cabezon with egg masses on the decks — a remarkable behavioural observation in relatively shallow, clear water. Carry a torch for wreck interior exploration. The upright position and always-visible-light access points make this a good introduction to wreck penetration with appropriate training. Plan two dives: one circumnavigation and one interior exploration.

## Safety Considerations

Water temperature 8–12°C; drysuit required. Wreck penetration carries entanglement and disorientation risk — use a penetration line and carry redundant lights. The sandy bottom can be disturbed by fin kicks inside the hull, reducing visibility rapidly. The site sits in Princess Margaret Marine Park; collecting is prohibited.

## Photography

This wreck is consistently ranked among BC's best for underwater photography. Wide-angle shots along the full hull length show the scale of the structure. The giant Pacific octopus under the bow is the marquee subject. Spring lingcod with egg masses provide behavioral wide-angle opportunities. The encrusted hull exterior and anemone-covered superstructure suit ambient-light shooting in the Gulf Islands' clear water.
