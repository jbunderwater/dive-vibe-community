## Fraser Rock

Fraser Rock is a submerged reef dive site in Howe Sound, British Columbia, Canada, accessed by boat from the Sunshine Coast or Squamish area.

## Overview

Fraser Rock rises from the cold, nutrient-rich waters of Howe Sound, offering an intermediate reef dive through kelp forests and coralline rock formations. Water temperatures range from 7-12°C year-round, making a drysuit strongly recommended. Visibility typically runs 6-15 metres and improves significantly in winter and early spring when plankton blooms subside. Tidal currents can be moderate to strong — time dives around slack water for the most comfortable conditions.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Howe Sound, British Columbia, Canada
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Coral reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 18 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 6-15 metres
- **Water Temperature**: 7-12°C year-round (drysuit recommended)
- **Current**: Moderate to strong — dive at slack water
- **Best Time**: Year-round; visibility best October to April

## Marine Life

Fraser Rock's rocky reef structure supports a dense Pacific Northwest assemblage. Giant Pacific octopus (GPO) are common residents in crevices and under ledges. Wolf eels are regularly spotted in their rocky dens. Lingcod patrol the reef edges while schools of black rockfish and copper rockfish hover in the water column. Puget Sound king crabs, sea cucumbers, and colourful plumose anemones carpet the substrate. Harbour seals frequently visit the site, and Steller sea lions may pass through in season.

## Dive Profile

Descend from the mooring or anchor point to the reef top at around 6-8 metres, then follow the contours down to the maximum depth of 18 metres along the rock faces. The most productive marine life zone lies between 8 and 15 metres where kelp transitions to open rock. Allow adequate air for a 3-minute safety stop at 5 metres and plan your ascent well before reaching reserve pressure given the cold water and potential current.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access only. Anchor or moor clear of the reef structure to avoid damaging marine life. Descend directly from the vessel and note any current direction before descending. Re-board the boat promptly on surfacing — Howe Sound sees regular vessel traffic and surface conditions can change quickly with afternoon winds.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Dive at or near slack tide to minimise current exposure
- A drysuit is strongly recommended; water remains cold year-round
- Carry a surface marker buoy (SMB) and deploy it before ascending
- A dive torch reveals GPO and wolf eels in crevices even during the day
- Winter months offer the best visibility when jellyfish and plankton are minimal

## Safety Considerations

Cold water, boat traffic, and tidal currents are the primary hazards at Fraser Rock. Always monitor your air consumption carefully in cold water — gas use increases significantly. Deploy your SMB at depth before ascending. Inform someone ashore of your dive plan and expected return time. Howe Sound weather can shift rapidly; check the marine forecast before departing.

## Photography

Fraser Rock offers strong macro and wide-angle opportunities. GPO encounters are a highlight — approach slowly and avoid touching the animal. Wolf eel portraits reward patient divers with lights. Plumose anemone gardens and encrusting sponges photograph well in the 8-15 metre zone. Early morning dives in calm conditions give the cleanest natural light.
