## Forest Friend

Forest Friend is a historic wreck dive in British Columbia, North America.

## Overview

The Forest Friend was a five-masted barquentine built in Washington State in 1919 for the Pacific lumber trade, scuttled in the early 1950s as part of the Royston Wrecks breakwater near Comox on Vancouver Island. The Royston collection — roughly 14 historic vessels sunk between 1936 and 1962 to protect the Comox Logging Company's booming grounds — is now a registered provincial archaeological site. At a shallow maximum depth of 12 metres, the Forest Friend's wooden hull frames and kelp-draped structure make it one of the more accessible wrecks at Royston, inhabited by rockfish, lingcod, and the occasional giant Pacific octopus.

## Wreck History

The Forest Friend was a five-masted barquentine built in Washington State in 1919 as a lumber carrier. The vessel later hauled sawdust and hog fuel (wood waste) in Canadian waters before being scuttled at Royston in the early 1950s. It is one of approximately 14 vessels that the Comox Logging & Railway Company deliberately sank between 1936 and 1959 to form a breakwater protecting their log-dump wharf and booming grounds in Comox Bay. The Royston Wrecks collection was surveyed by the UASBC and the Institute of Nautical Archaeology in 2011 and subsequently protected under the BC Heritage Conservation Act as a registered archaeological site, making removal of any artifacts illegal.

## Marine Life

The complex wooden hull framework and surrounding eel grass beds support a diverse community of fish and invertebrates. Rockfish shelter among the deteriorating timbers. The wrecks provide protected eelgrass habitat that attracts various sea life typical of sheltered northern Vancouver Island coastal waters. Plumose anemones attach to exposed metal fittings. Most of the Forest Friend is buried or obscured, limiting fish visibility compared to more open-structure wrecks, but the site still draws nudibranchs and invertebrates to the wood and metal surfaces.

## Dive Profile

Maximum depth is approximately 12 meters — a relatively shallow, accessible dive. The wooden hull has significantly deteriorated and much of the structure is buried in sediment; the most visible features are the hull ribs and scattered fittings. Bull kelp grows over and around the wreck, creating a kelp-forest feel. Visibility in Comox Bay is variable and can be reduced by plankton blooms and runoff. The Royston Wrecks lie close to shore in an exposed bay: sea conditions can be rough in southerly winds.

## Entry and Exit

Boat or kayak access. The Royston Wrecks lie in Comox Bay near the community of Royston, a short drive south of Courtenay. Shore viewing is possible from the adjacent public greenway trail at low tide. Some divers access the site by small boat or kayak from the shoreline. The site sits on private property; diving is subject to the conditions of the Heritage Conservation Act.

## Tips and Recommendations

Dive on calm days — the site is exposed to southerly winds and swells that make conditions dangerous in any weather. Visibility is best on an incoming tide before turbidity increases. The shallow depth and kelp environment suit recreational open-water divers, but the exposed location and limited underwater structure make this less compelling than other Royston wrecks such as the steel naval vessels at the eastern end of the breakwater.

## Safety Considerations

Water temperature 8–12°C; drysuit recommended. The site is exposed to southerly swells; avoid in strong south or southeast winds. The Provincial heritage site designation means zero artifacts may be removed or disturbed. Shallow depth (12 m) reduces decompression concerns but surface conditions are the main hazard.
