## Shag Reef

Shag Reef is a submerged rocky reef in the southern Gulf Islands of British Columbia, Canada, positioned in active tidal channels between the Gulf Islands and the San Juan Islands of Washington State.

## Overview

Shag Reef sits in the channel systems south and east of the Gulf Islands, close to the international border. The area is characterised by strong tidal flows between the Gulf Islands and San Juan Islands, creating excellent conditions for filter-feeding invertebrates and a productive fish community. Water temperatures are 7–12°C throughout the year. Visibility ranges 6–15 metres depending on the season and tidal phase, clearing nicely in late summer. The site takes its name from the pelagic cormorants (shags) that historically roosted on nearby rocks.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Southern Gulf Islands, British Columbia, Canada
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 18 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 6–15 metres
- **Current**: Moderate to strong; time carefully
- **Best Time**: July to October

## Marine Life

The tidal flows around Shag Reef support dense orange and white plumose anemone colonies on current-swept surfaces. Lingcod patrol the reef edges and occupy ledge positions throughout the site. Copper rockfish, quillback rockfish, and painted greenling are resident. Giant Pacific octopus have den areas in the deeper boulder zones. Harbour seals are highly visible, particularly during and after tidal runs when feeding activity increases. Nudibranchs are present on encrusted surfaces across the reef.

## Dive Profile

Descend on the reef structure during slack water and work the terrain at 10–15 metres, exploring the more sheltered lee faces first. If any current is running, position along the current-fed face where anemone concentrations are densest. Work back to shallower depths as gas depletes and complete a safety stop in the 5-metre zone before surfacing.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access from Sidney or other southern Gulf Islands marinas. The site is close to the US border — ensure valid vessel documentation. Conditions here can deteriorate if weather moves through the channel system, so monitor the forecast carefully.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Timing is crucial — the best diving is within an hour of slack water
- The current-swept face has the densest anemone life — worth the extra current management
- GPO searches in the boulder areas are productive; bring a strong torch
- Fuel planning is important if launching from Sidney — account for potential delays on the water

## Safety Considerations

Tidal currents in the Southern Gulf Islands and US border channel areas can be aggressive. Always have a float plan filed. Deploy SMB before surfacing in the channel area — boat traffic is constant, including commercial and recreational vessels. Cold water protection (drysuit recommended) is essential for multi-dive days.

## Photography

Anemone wall photography is outstanding when current brings out the feeding postures. Wide-angle images of the reef structure with a diver in frame capture the Gulf Islands character. Lingcod and rockfish portrait photography rewards patience and slow movement.
