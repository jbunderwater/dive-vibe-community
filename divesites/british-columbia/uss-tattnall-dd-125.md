## USS Tattnall (DD-125)

USS Tattnall (DD-125) is a historic wreck dive in British Columbia, North America.

## Overview

The USS Tattnall (DD-125) was a 314-foot (95.8 m) Wickes-class destroyer that served with the US Navy through both World Wars, earning three battle stars in World War Two for operations in the Elba-Pianosa Islands area (Italy), the Hyeres Islands operation (southern France), and Okinawa picket duty, before being scuttled as part of the Royston breakwater near Comox on 21 August 1947. The Royston Wrecks collection — one of the most historically diverse wreck sites in Canada — spans vessels from a 19th-century sailing ship to WWII-era warships, all within the same protected cove on eastern Vancouver Island. The deteriorating steel hull of Tattnall now lies among the other Royston vessels, encrusted with anemones and sheltering rockfish, lingcod, and giant Pacific octopus in the cold waters of Comox Bay.

## Wreck History

USS Tattnall (DD-125) was a Wickes-class destroyer laid down on 1 December 1917 at Camden, New Jersey, by the New York Shipbuilding Corporation and launched on 5 September 1918. She was reclassified as high-speed transport APD-19 in July 1943 and served through World War Two. Decommissioned on 17 December 1945 at Puget Sound and stricken from the Navy list on 8 January 1946, the vessel was sold on 13 November 1946 to Pacific Metal Salvage Company of Seattle. She was towed to Royston, British Columbia, and scuttled on 21 August 1947 as part of the breakwater protecting the Comox Logging and Railway Company's log-booming operations. Parts of her hull were still visible above the waterline as of 2009. She is one of approximately 14 historic vessels forming the Royston Wrecks, a breakwater collection that dates from 1876 to 1943 and is protected under the BC Heritage Conservation Act.

**Note on sources:** The vessel's sale date per NavSource (13 November 1946) and Wikipedia (17 October 1946) differ slightly; both confirm scuttling at Royston on 21 August 1947. Three battle stars are confirmed by Wikipedia. WWII operations confirmed: Elba/Pianosa area, southern France Hyeres Islands operation, and Okinawa picket duty.

## Marine Life

The hull sits in cold Comox Bay waters and is encrusted with plumose anemones, encrusting sponges, and hydroids typical of the northern Strait of Georgia. Lingcod, various rockfish species, and giant Pacific octopus use the steel structure for shelter. Nudibranchs and feather stars have been documented on the surrounding bottom.

## Dive Profile

The wreck rests at approximately 6–10 metres on the tidal flats at Royston, substantially shallower than the frontmatter maximum of 28 metres — the Royston Wrecks are primarily an intertidal and shallow-subtidal site accessible on calm days. Visibility is variable and affected by tidal movement. Low tide reveals more structure; high tide allows access to deeper hull sections.

## Entry and Exit

Accessible from the Royston Seaside Trail via a public park. Shore or small boat access on calm days. The Heritage Conservation Act prohibits removing artefacts. Diving is informal; there are no dedicated dive services on site.

## Safety Considerations

- Water temperatures in Comox Bay are cold year-round (6–10°C); a drysuit is strongly recommended.
- The wrecks are on tidal flats — check tides carefully before entering the water.
- The protected heritage status means no wreck penetration is sanctioned; interiors are unstable.
- Strong southerly winds can make conditions hazardous rapidly; plan for swift exits.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Visit at low tide to see the most hull structure before diving.
- The Melanope (launched 1876) is the oldest and most historically distinctive vessel in the collection.
- Combine with a surface tour along the Royston Seaside Trail for context on all 14 vessels.
- Local dive charters from Courtenay or Comox can provide guided access.
