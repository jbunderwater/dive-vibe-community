# Danger Reefs

A reef complex near Gabriola Island with excellent rockfish populations and cold-water invertebrates.

## Overview

Danger Reefs is located in the Strait of Georgia near Gabriola Island, one of the Gulf Islands that lies just east of Nanaimo. The reef complex — named for its navigational hazard status for surface vessels — creates an excellent underwater terrain with varied depth profiles and diverse microhabitats. The site is accessible from Nanaimo or Gabriola Island and offers typical intermediate-level Strait of Georgia diving. Water temperatures are 8–11°C and visibility averages 8–15 metres.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Strait of Georgia, near Gabriola Island, Nanaimo area, British Columbia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef complex
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 18 metres
- **Typical Visibility**: 8–15 metres
- **Current**: Moderate tidal currents in the Nanaimo area
- **Best Time**: Late spring through autumn

## Marine Life

The varied reef structure of Danger Reefs creates habitat for a broad range of species. Giant Pacific octopus are found throughout the reef complex in rocky crevices. Wolf eels inhabit permanent dens in the deeper rocky sections. Lingcod are numerous and some larger individuals occupy favoured territories year after year. Copper and quillback rockfish are abundant. Plumose anemones form extensive colonies on exposed surfaces. Nudibranchs are well-documented at Gabriola Island reef sites. Harbour seals follow dive groups regularly.

## Dive Profile

The reef complex spans multiple rock structures from shallow to 18 metres depth. A systematic approach to the dive is recommended — navigate from one reef structure to the next using a compass. Spend the deeper portion of the dive exploring the base sections of the larger rocks, then work upward toward the reef crests for safety stops. The complexity of the site rewards multiple visits.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access from Nanaimo or Gabriola Island. The reef complex is identifiable on navigational charts as a hazard area. Use GPS to mark individual dive entry and exit points within the complex. Anchor on sand between the reef structures. BC Ferries to Gabriola Island passes nearby — deploy SMB before any ascent.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Multiple reef structures mean this site benefits from planning your dive route in advance
- Gabriola Island dive sites are part of the broader Nanaimo-area dive scene with good charter availability
- The Nanaimo area's artificial reefs can be combined with Danger Reefs for a full dive day
- Wolf eels at this site have been visited by divers for many years — approach slowly

## Safety Considerations

Tidal currents in the Nanaimo/Gabriola area require careful planning. Heavy ferry traffic to Gabriola Island makes this a high-traffic surface area — SMB deployment is critical. Complex reef navigation requires compass skills. Cold water (8–11°C) demands drysuit protection.

## Photography

The reef complex offers diverse photographic compositions at multiple depths. Wolf eels and octopus in den entrances are classic Pacific Northwest subjects. Wide-angle reef panoramas capture the rock structure and invertebrate growth. For macro work, carefully inspect sponge surfaces for nudibranchs and other invertebrates.
