## British Columbia

Cold-water diving along Canada's Pacific coast, where emerald kelp forests shelter giant Pacific octopus, wolf eels, and sixgill sharks across more than 25,000 kilometers of coastline.

## Description

British Columbia offers 172 documented dive sites spanning depths from 12 to 46 meters, with 132 reef sites, 16 wrecks, 15 wall sites, 5 kelp sites, and 4 muck sites. The nutrient-rich waters between mainland BC and Vancouver Island create some of the most productive cold-water ecosystems on the planet, with winter visibility reaching its best when plankton blooms subside and river runoff stops. Jacques Cousteau ranked BC's waters second only to the Red Sea for marine life diversity, and the region's artificial reef program has sunk several decommissioned warships to create world-class wreck dives.

### Diving Opportunities

- **Shore Diving**: 13 accessible shore dive sites including Porteau Cove Provincial Park (20 minutes north of Vancouver with artificial reefs and easy entry) and Saxe Point near Victoria
- **Boat Diving**: 159 boat-accessible sites spread across Howe Sound, the Gulf Islands, Nanaimo, Campbell River, Discovery Passage, and the remote waters around Port Hardy and God's Pocket
- **Wreck Diving**: 16 wreck sites including the HMCS Saskatchewan and HMCS Cape Breton artificial reefs off Nanaimo, and the Barnard Castle near Race Rocks
- **Wall Diving**: 15 wall sites with dramatic drop-offs, including sites along Vancouver Island's eastern shoreline

### Accessibility

- **Getting There**: Vancouver International Airport (YVR) is the primary gateway with extensive international connections. Victoria International Airport (YYJ) serves southern Vancouver Island. BC Ferries operates routes from Tsawwassen and Horseshoe Bay to Vancouver Island (Swartz Bay, Nanaimo) and the Gulf Islands
- **Dive Operators**: Vancouver Diving Locker (operating 50+ years), International Diving Centre, Rowand's Reef Scuba Shop (since 1971), and Diving Sports offer training, rentals, and guided trips from the mainland. On Vancouver Island, Nanaimo Dive Outfitters runs wreck charters, and Rendezvous Dive Adventures (since 1973) operates custom dive charters from the northern island
- **Accommodation**: Full range of options from Vancouver and Victoria hotels to remote dive lodges at God's Pocket near Port Hardy and waterfront lodges on Quadra Island
- **Transportation**: A vehicle is essential for reaching most shore dive sites. BC Ferries connects the mainland to Vancouver Island and the Gulf Islands. Water taxis serve more remote dive areas around Port Hardy and the Sunshine Coast
- **Facilities**: Full-service dive shops in Vancouver, Victoria, Nanaimo, and Campbell River offer drysuit rentals, air and nitrox fills, and guided shore and boat dives

### Marine Life & Environment

- **Water Conditions**: Water temperatures range from 6-12C (44-54F) year-round. Winter (October-March) offers the best visibility, often exceeding 15 meters, while summer visibility can drop to 3-9 meters due to plankton blooms and river runoff. Strong tidal currents are common at sites like Race Rocks and Dodd Narrows and require careful slack-tide planning
- **Marine Biodiversity**: Giant Pacific octopus (growing to 6-meter arm spans), wolf eels, lingcod, rockfish, harbor seals, Steller sea lions, bluntnose sixgill sharks (summer months), massive fields of plumose anemones, nudibranchs, Dungeness crab, lion's mane jellyfish, and dense kelp forests
- **Conservation**: Race Rocks Ecological Reserve protects one of the richest marine environments in southern BC. God's Pocket Marine Provincial Park and several Rockfish Conservation Areas along the coast restrict fishing to protect marine habitats

## Additional Information

- **Best Time to Visit**: October through March for best visibility and clearest water; June through August for warmer water temperatures and longer daylight hours, plus summer sixgill shark encounters
- **Currency**: Canadian Dollar (CAD)
- **Language**: English
- **Safety**: Drysuit required year-round due to cold water temperatures averaging 6-12C. Strong tidal currents at many sites demand dive planning around slack tide windows. Cold-water rated regulators are recommended. Below 18 meters, dive lights are essential to see the full color spectrum. Current tables and local knowledge are critical for sites like Dodd Narrows and Race Rocks
