## Laurel Whalen

Laurel Whalen is a historic wreck dive in British Columbia, North America.

## Overview

The Laurel Whalen was a wood-hulled auxiliary schooner built in 1917 that became the very first vessel deliberately scuttled at Royston when it was sunk on August 20, 1936, starting what would eventually become a 14-ship breakwater protecting the Comox Logging Company's booming grounds near Comox on Vancouver Island. The Royston Wrecks collection grew over the following 25 years and is now a registered provincial archaeological site protected under the BC Heritage Conservation Act. The wooden hull structure of Laurel Whalen has deteriorated significantly over the decades but still provides a framework for marine life, including anemones, nudibranchs, and rockfish drawn to the complex substrate.

## Wreck History

The Laurel Whalen was a wood-hulled combination steam-and-sail vessel built in Victoria, BC in 1917 as a lumber carrier. The schooner later served as a cement barge and finally as a floating fish cannery before the Comox Logging & Railway Company scuttled her in place on 20 August 1936 — making her the first vessel deliberately sunk at Royston to begin the breakwater that would eventually grow to 14 ships. The breakwater protected the company's log-dump wharf and booming grounds in Comox Bay. The Royston Wrecks collection was surveyed by the UASBC and the Institute of Nautical Archaeology in March 2011, GPS-mapped, and subsequently declared a registered provincial archaeological site under the BC Heritage Conservation Act.

## Marine Life

The Royston Wrecks collectively support eelgrass beds in the adjacent shallows that provide nursery habitat for juvenile fish. The wooden hull of the Laurel Whalen has significantly deteriorated over nearly 90 years, but the remaining timber framework and adjacent rocky substrate attract nudibranchs, encrusting invertebrates, and the occasional rockfish. The protected breakwater environment also offers habitat for various sea life typical of sheltered northern Vancouver Island coastal waters.

## Dive Profile

The Laurel Whalen lies in shallow water in Comox Bay — the site's frontmatter lists a maximum depth of 28 m, but the Royston breakwater wrecks are generally shallow (the hulks are partially visible at low tide). The wooden hull has deteriorated substantially and is largely buried in sediment, limiting the underwater structure available for exploration. Visibility in Comox Bay is variable, often reduced by plankton blooms and freshwater runoff.

## Entry and Exit

Shore or small-boat access from the Royston foreshore, south of Courtenay on Vancouver Island. The Royston Greenway trail (built on an old rail grade) provides public access to the shoreline adjacent to the wrecks. The site is on private property; the heritage site designation governs access. Some divers and snorkelers access the site by kayak or small inflatable from the shoreline on calm days.

## Tips and Recommendations

Combine a dive visit with low-tide shore viewing — the upper portions of multiple wrecks are partially exposed and provide historical context before going underwater. Dive only on calm days free of southerly wind and swell. The shallowness (under 10–12 m along the breakwater line) and heavily deteriorated wooden hull make Laurel Whalen less compelling as a dive objective than the steel naval vessels at the eastern end of the Royston breakwater.

## Safety Considerations

The site is fully exposed to southerly swells — do not dive in any southerly wind or weather. Water temperature 8–12°C; drysuit recommended. BC Heritage Conservation Act prohibits disturbing or removing any part of the site. Very shallow water reduces decompression risk but surface conditions in bad weather are the primary hazard.
