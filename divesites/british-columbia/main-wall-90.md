## Main wall 90'

Main wall 90' is a wall dive site in British Columbia, North America.

## Overview

Main Wall at Madrona Point in Nanoose Bay is considered one of the best shore dives on central Vancouver Island, dropping to around 27 metres (90 feet) along a substantial rocky wall packed with nooks and overhangs that almost guarantee encounters with wolf eels and giant Pacific octopus. The Strait of Georgia's clean, cold water delivers visibility that typically exceeds that of more southern BC dive sites, and large lingcod are frequently seen perched on ledges along the wall face. This is the deepest and most advanced of the four dive areas at Madrona Point, and most divers tackle it first when planning a multi-dive day at the site.

## Marine Life

Main Wall at Madrona Point is among the most reliably productive sites on central Vancouver Island for large marine life encounters. Giant Pacific octopus are found in crevices and under boulder overhangs throughout the wall — divers report finding six or more on a single dive. Wolf eels — both mated pairs and colorful juveniles — inhabit the deeper wall recesses. Lingcod of all sizes perch on ledges, hunting from elevated positions. Multiple rockfish species are present in the rocky zones, including quillback, copper, and black rockfish. Cabezon occupy rocky areas. Boot sponges and cloud sponges colonize the wall face alongside dense plumose anemones (white and orange) and bright crimson anemones. Nudibranchs in many species are distributed across sponge-covered surfaces. Pacific spiny dogfish patrol the site in summer. Steller sea lions and California sea lions from a nearby log dump frequently approach divers in fall and winter. Schools of herring and anchovy are present seasonally.

## Dive Profile

Main Wall is the most advanced of the four dive plans at Madrona Point, maxing out at approximately 29 meters (95 feet). A compass bearing from the right beach entrance leads divers offshore approximately 100 meters to a white marker buoy, passing through the Maze rock formations en route. The wall itself extends westerly from the buoy position, offering a long traverse with numerous cracks, crevices, and boulders at the base. Descend immediately to depth on arriving at the wall to maximize bottom time, then work back up the face as air decreases. Nitrox (32–36%) is commonly used. Visibility averages 9–15 meters (30–50 feet) and is best in winter (sometimes exceeding 25 meters); spring and early summer plankton blooms can reduce surface visibility to 3 meters, though it typically improves below 10 meters.

## Entry and Exit

Shore dive accessed from Madrona Drive in Nanoose Bay. Drive north from Nanaimo approximately 20 minutes on Island Highway 19, turn right on Northwest Bay Road, then right on Beaver Creek Wharf Road, which connects to Madrona Drive — follow to the dead end. Parking is 2–3 cars at the road end with additional street parking. Surface-swim to the white marker buoy before descending, or use compass navigation. No facilities on-site; nearest services in Parksville (5–10 minutes).

## Tips and Recommendations

Use steel tanks (100 cu. ft. or larger) and Nitrox to manage bottom time at 29 m. Plan this wall as the first dive of the day while air is full. The Maze dive (40–60 feet) works well as a second dive after Main Wall, giving a complete multi-depth day at a single site. Winter delivers the best visibility. Sea lions may approach divers assertively in fall — remain calm, avoid touching, and do not corner them. Bring a torch for exploring crevices where octopus den.

## Safety Considerations

Water temperature 8–12°C year-round; drysuit required. No significant tidal current at Madrona Point. Maximum depth 29 m requires careful no-decompression limit management. Poor visibility in spring may result in missing the wall entirely — surface-swim to the buoy if uncertain. Limited parking at a busy site; arrive early on weekends.

## Photography

Madrona Point Main Wall is one of BC's premier sites for invertebrate macro and wide-angle photography. Giant Pacific octopus in their dens are the marquee subject; multiple individuals per dive is common. Mated wolf eel pairs in crevices suit close-focus wide-angle. The plumose anemone-covered overhangs and boot sponge walls offer ambient-light reef wide-angle compositions. Nudibranchs in high diversity suit dedicated macro work. Sea lion encounters in fall and winter are exceptional behavioral wide-angle opportunities.
