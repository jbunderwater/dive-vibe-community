## McKenzie Bright

McKenzie Bright is a reef dive site in British Columbia, North America.

## Overview

McKenzie Bight is a highly regarded dive site on the southeast shore of Saanich Inlet, located within Gowlland Tod Provincial Park near Willis Point on Vancouver Island. An underwater wall drops from around 9 meters down to over 36 meters, draped in plumose anemones, sponges, and encrusting invertebrates, and the site is one of the few places in BC where sixgill sharks are reliably spotted in summer months at depths around 30 meters. Visibility can be reduced by plankton blooms in spring, but the site offers excellent diving through much of the year.

## Site Information

- **Location**: British Columbia, North America
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Coral reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 18 meters

## Marine Life

McKenzie Bight is well known for an exceptional diversity of species concentrated in a single accessible site. Wolf eels inhabit crevices in the boulder-strewn bottom. Giant Pacific octopus — with specimens of considerable size reported (one diver noted a spread of approximately 5 meters) — den in the gravel and rocky areas, which are noted as more productive for octopus than the wall itself. Lingcod of large size are frequently encountered. Cloud sponges are present, making this one of the few shore dives in BC where they can be found. Sea stars are distributed across the site (populations have been reduced by sea star wasting disease in recent years). Harbor seals are regularly seen. Plumose anemones in vibrant white and orange cover the wall face and overhangs. Nudibranchs in multiple species occupy sponge-covered surfaces throughout the shallows. Sixgill sharks (bluntnose sixgill, Hexanchus griseus) are occasionally sighted at depths around 25 meters (80 feet) and deeper in summer months — these encounters are seasonal and unpredictable.

## Dive Profile

The wall at McKenzie Bight begins at approximately 9 meters (30 feet) and descends past 37 meters (120+ feet), with the frontmatter listing a maximum depth of 18 m for the primary dive zone. The underwater terrain features giant boulders with cracks and crevices leading into an underwater gully. The site is located within Gowlland Tod Provincial Park on the southeast shore of Saanich Inlet near Willis Point — a sheltered inlet on the Saanich Peninsula. The area is described as generally protected from strong tidal influence, making conditions more predictable than exposed tidal-pass sites. Visibility is variable: excellent reports of 30+ meters are recorded in good conditions; spring plankton blooms can reduce it significantly.

## Entry and Exit

Shore dive. A well-marked pathway leads from the parking area to the water — the nearest entry point is over 200 meters from parking, and the path is rocky; a cart or wheeled gear carrier is strongly recommended. Multiple entry points are available at different distances. The site is within Gowlland Tod Provincial Park near Willis Point on the Saanich Peninsula, approximately 20 minutes north of Victoria.

## Tips and Recommendations

Bring a gear cart — the rocky path to the water is long enough to make carrying a tank and full drysuit kit tiring. Focus attention on the gravel areas adjacent to the wall face for octopus dens; encounters are frequent in this zone. For sixgill shark sightings, dive to 25 m or deeper in summer (June–August) and be patient — the encounters are not guaranteed and the sharks have been absent for multiple seasons at a stretch. Cloud sponges appear at depth; keep distance and avoid fin kicks near them (they are extremely fragile and long-lived). Parking can fill on summer weekends.

## Safety Considerations

Water temperature in Saanich Inlet is 7–12°C; drysuit required. The inlet environment creates spring plankton blooms that can reduce surface visibility significantly — navigate by compass or follow the wall. The rocky access path requires careful footing in full dive gear. The wall extends beyond recreational limits (40 m+); monitor depth carefully. No on-site facilities or emergency services.

## Photography

McKenzie Bight is one of BC's top macro photography sites, with exceptional nudibranch diversity in the shallow zone. Giant Pacific octopus encounters suit close-focus wide-angle. Cloud sponges at depth provide textured ambient-light backgrounds. Wolf eels in their crevices suit portrait-style wide-angle with a wide rectilinear or fisheye lens. Sixgill shark sightings, when they occur, offer rare wide-angle blue-water subjects not available at most BC dive sites.
