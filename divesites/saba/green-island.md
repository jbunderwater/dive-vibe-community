# Green Island

A small volcanic island pinnacle on Saba's north coast, circumnavigated at 9–24 metres — nurse sharks in barrel sponges, Bermuda chubs in schools, a gorgonian grove, and a historic anchor from Saba's sulphur-mining era lying on the sandy base.

## Overview

Green Island is a small volcanic islet on Saba's northern coast, classified as a pinnacle dive — divers circumnavigate the submerged flanks of the island rather than diving a flat reef. The mooring sits at 9 metres (30 feet); the sandy base is at 24 metres (80 feet). The site is weather-dependent — it is less frequently dived than the western sites and requires calm conditions or southerly winds to be safely accessible. Sea Saba notes that this is "less often dove" precisely because of the weather dependency. The flanks host a gorgonian grove, bushy black coral, and feather black coral colonies, alongside elkhorn coral formations. Of particular note on the sandy base: "Popeye's large anchor," embedded in the sand from Saba's historical sulphur-mining era, serves as a navigational waypoint and an unusual historical feature. Dark volcanic sand (rather than the white sand typical of carbonate reef environments) surrounds the base. Visibility is typically 20–30 metres.

## Marine Life

Sea Saba confirms nurse sharks resting among the barrel sponges at Green Island — a consistent sighting at this site. Bermuda chubs school in dense formations around the upper flanks. Queen angelfish are present. The dark volcanic sand around the base hosts flying gurnards, batfish, and sand tilefish — a distinctive assemblage associated with Saba's volcanic sand environments. Nurse sharks, fairy basslets, and orange-spotted filefish have also been documented (Endless Caribbean). The gorgonian grove and black coral provide structural habitat for invertebrates and small reef fish.

*Note: Previous descriptions listed hawksbill turtles, spotted moray eels, Caribbean spiny lobster, and southern stingrays as features of Green Island. None of these are confirmed by Sea Saba's Green Island-specific description. Hawksbill turtles and morays are common throughout the Saba Marine Park and may be encountered here but should not be presented as definitive Green Island species.*

## Dive Profile

Descent to the mooring at 9 metres, then circumnavigation of the islet flanks at chosen depth. The gorgonian grove and black coral are found at mid-depth (12–18 m). The sandy base at 24 metres is reached on the deeper sections of the circumnavigation, where Popeye's anchor can be found. NDL at 20 metres allows approximately 50–55 minutes for a full circumnavigation. Current is typically manageable but can increase on open-water sides.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive only. The site requires calm conditions and is accessed less frequently than the western pinnacles — the operator will confirm availability on the day. DSMB required.

## Tips and Recommendations

Ask the guide specifically about Popeye's anchor — it is the most unusual feature of this site and worth finding. The dark volcanic sand is distinctly different from carbonate-sand sites and is worth noting if you have not seen it before. Watch the barrel sponge clusters for the nurse sharks — they tend to rest motionless in or near the larger sponge formations. Green Island is best as a morning dive when visibility on the north coast is freshest before any afternoon swell builds.

## Safety Considerations

Weather dependency is the primary operational consideration — do not attempt Green Island in deteriorating conditions. The north coast can be more exposed to swells than the western sites. Maintain buoyancy around the black coral and gorgonian colonies. DSMB required.

## Photography

The gorgonian grove provides strong wide-angle foreground subjects at 12–15 metres with good ambient light. Nurse sharks resting in barrel sponges are classic soft-light portrait opportunities. The dark volcanic sand around the base creates unusual compositional contrast against the pale pinnacle flanks. Flying gurnards on the sandy bottom — walking on their pectoral fins — are always distinctive photographic subjects.
