# Man O War Shoals

Twin volcanic peaks rising to 15 and 20 feet below the surface, with a sandy base at 21 metres — the only place in the Saba Marine Park where Atlantic Spadefish are spotted, alongside banded jawfish, sand tilefish, and thick sponge and coral coverage.

## Overview

Man O War Shoals is a twin-peaked volcanic rock structure on the eastern side of Saba, located east of Diamond Rock, both sites forming part of the Man O War Shoals zone (Saba Park sites 6, 7, and 9). The structure features two summits that rise to approximately 15 and 20 feet below the surface, connected at a base that sits on a sandy bottom at 70 feet (21 metres). The mooring at 20 feet enables a distinctive figure-eight dive pattern: divers descend, circle the base of each shoal in a loose figure-eight, and spiral upward between the peaks. The structure is blanketed in thick sponge and cup coral coverage throughout, and black coral appears on the deeper sections. Sea Saba describes this as one of the Saba Marine Park's most "diverse and luxuriant" environments. Visibility is typically 20–30 metres.

## Marine Life

Man O War Shoals is the **only site in the Saba Marine Park where Atlantic Spadefish are reliably observed** — confirmed by Sea Saba as unique to this location. Banded jawfish are also documented here, described by Sea Saba as "very uncommon" sightings that attract attention from experienced divers. Sand tilefish are present on the sandy base — active, fast-moving fish that build sand mounds they retreat into when threatened. Schools of sergeant majors are abundant throughout the structure. Black coral is present on the deeper sections. African pompano have been documented at this site. Ocean pelagics are reported on the outer sandy edges.

*Note: Previous descriptions mentioned eagle rays and stingrays concentrated in sandy channels at this site. These have not been confirmed by Sea Saba's site-specific page or by independent sources focused on Man O War Shoals. Eagle rays are confirmed at Third Encounter. The sandy base may occasionally produce stingray encounters but these should not be presented as a reliable sighting.*

## Dive Profile

Divers descend to one shoal crest and navigate between the twin peaks using the sand channels as natural corridors, following a figure-eight circuit around the base structures before spiraling upward. The maximum depth at the sandy base is 21 metres; the shoal crests at 5–6 metres provide ample bottom time (NDL at 18 metres: approximately 50–55 minutes). The mooring at 6 metres makes a practical safety stop location.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access from Fort Bay, with permanent mooring maintained by the Saba Conservation Foundation. Entry by giant stride. The wide sandy base between the two shoals makes navigation straightforward. DSMB required for the ascent.

## Tips and Recommendations

The Atlantic Spadefish are the unique draw of this site — their distinctive oval shape and vertical striping make them immediately recognisable. Look for them in mid-water between the two peaks or on the outer sandy apron. The banded jawfish require a slow, low approach — kneel on the sand at a distance and wait for them to re-emerge from their burrows. Sand tilefish mounds are visible in the sandy areas; note how quickly they dive into their constructed burrows when approached. The figure-eight dive pattern can be varied — experienced divers can do multiple circuits at different depths.

## Safety Considerations

Current flows through the channels between the shoals and can be stronger than it appears on descent. Do not stray beyond the shoal complex into open water without a DSMB. The sandy channels provide reliable navigation corridors back to the mooring in any current direction.

## Photography

The Atlantic Spadefish are the photographic highlight — a wide-angle or medium zoom approach works best as the fish tend to maintain distance. Sand tilefish are faster-moving and require a patient, low approach for portraits. The black coral on the deeper sections needs supplemental lighting for full colour rendering. Wide-angle shots of the twin peaks from the sandy base with schooling fish in mid-water are the classic composition.
