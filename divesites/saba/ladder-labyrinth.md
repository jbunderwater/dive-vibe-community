# Ladder Labyrinth

A volcanic lava-flow reef near Saba's historic Ladder Bay, where ancient lava formed a complex of 10-foot ridges, sandy alleyways, and warm sulfurous sand — one of the island's best sites for jawfish, lancer dragonets, and Caribbean macro life.

## Overview

Ladder Labyrinth sits on the west coast of Saba, just southwest of the Babylon mooring, near the historic Ladder Bay — the original stone-stepped landing point cut into Saba's cliffs before the island had a road. Underwater, the site is a jumble of ancient lava flows that have solidified into a network of approximately 10-foot coral and rock ridges, creating a labyrinth of alleyways, sand chutes, and nooks. This is not a pinnacle or wall dive — it is a characteristically Saban volcanic reef of moderate depth that rewards slow, methodical exploration. The mooring is at 12 metres (40 feet); maximum depth is 24 metres (80 feet). As the ridges transition eastward, they give way to sand chutes, which in turn produce their own resident community of burrowing species. A distinctive feature: the volcanic sand here is warm and tinged sulfurous yellow in spots, a reminder that Saba's volcano is between eruption periods, not extinct. Visibility is 20–30 metres.

## Marine Life

Ladder Labyrinth is a macro site. The ridge crevices shelter shrimp, crabs, and Saba lobster in high density. In the sandy alleyways between ridges: yellowhead jawfish and the less-commonly-seen pikeblennies and lancer dragonets are the star residents — all three species probe or hover above their burrows and retreat quickly when approached, requiring patient observation. Tarpons congregate near the mooring line and are reliably present at safety stop depth. Filefish and schoolmasters occupy the outer ridge areas; chromis cloud the upper reef sections. Nudibranchs, flamingo tongue shells, and fingerprint cyphoma snails feed on soft corals throughout the ridges. Frogfish have been reported in the Ladder Bay area by multiple divers.

*Note: Glassy sweepers and hawksbill turtles are confirmed in the broader Ladder Bay zone but not specifically documented at Ladder Labyrinth's mooring by Sea Saba — treat as possible encounters rather than reliable sightings.*

## Dive Profile

The mooring is at 12 metres. The dive begins at the seaward side of the ridges and works inward toward the labyrinth. The 10-foot ridges form natural corridors that divers navigate at 12–20 metres; the sand chutes leading east are explored at the deeper end (20–24 m). A full circuit of the main labyrinth takes 35–45 minutes. Safety stops are at the mooring line in the company of tarpons. The warm sulfurous sand patches can be felt by hand and are worth noting as a dive highlight for interested divers.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access from Fort Bay, permanent mooring maintained by the Saba Conservation Foundation. Entry by giant stride. The site's moderate depth makes it a good afternoon dive after a deeper morning pinnacle. DSMB required for ascent.

## Tips and Recommendations

Slow down. Ladder Labyrinth rewards divers who move carefully between ridges rather than swimming through quickly. The yellowhead jawfish are skittish — approach slowly and at eye level. The pikeblennies and lancer dragonets require even more patience. Look at the underside of every ridge overhang for lobster and shrimp. The warm sandy patches are a distinctive Saban feature — fan the sand gently near a yellow-tinged spot to feel the warmth from below. A dive torch illuminates the crevice organisms effectively.

## Safety Considerations

The ridges are all passable without overhead environments — this is not a cave dive. Buoyancy should still be managed carefully to avoid contact with encrusted ridge walls. The sandy alleyways are navigable in any direction, but maintaining awareness of the mooring position is important in the more complex central sections. DSMB required.

## Photography

Ladder Labyrinth is one of Saba's best macro sites. Yellowhead jawfish hovering over burrows, lancer dragonets in the sand, and flamingo tongues on sea fans are all reliable close-up subjects. Wide-angle lenses suit the ridge corridor scenes where two divers can fit side by side. The tarpon safety stop provides an opportunity for silvery wide-angle portraits in the mooring column.
