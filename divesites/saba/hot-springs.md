# Hot Springs

A geothermally active dive site where warm volcanic vents seep through the seafloor alongside coral heads, seagrass, and a resident nurse shark — one of Saba's best night dive locations.

## Overview

Hot Springs is a unique dive site named because of hot water springs venting from the seafloor — direct evidence that Saba's volcano is between eruptions, not extinct. The site consists of coral-encrusted boulders, coral heads separated by sandy bottom, and seagrass patches. Sea Saba describes the vents as producing temperature differences detectable by dipping hands into the sand where it changes colour. Large anchors on the sandy bottom serve as navigation landmarks. The site descends from the mooring at 12 metres (40 feet) to a maximum of 24 metres (80 feet). Visibility is typically 20–30 metres. Hot Springs is a popular night dive destination where bioluminescent ostracod mating displays have been documented.

## Marine Life

Sea Saba describes Hot Springs as home to an "extremely friendly resident nurse shark," stingrays, garden eels in the sand, sea turtles grazing in the seagrass, and flying gurnards. Seahorses have been observed mating at this site. Crustaceans are abundant and particularly active on night dives. On night dives, bioluminescent ostracods put on mating displays in open water. *The previous description's claims about French angelfish, queen angelfish, green morays, and barracuda as primary species at this site are not confirmed by the primary operator and have been removed.*

## Dive Profile

Descent via mooring line to approximately 12 metres (40 feet). Divers navigate through the coral heads and sandy areas, following the large anchors as landmarks. The volcanic vent areas are in the sandy bottom where colour changes signal geothermal activity — placing hands near the sand colour-change zone reveals a distinct temperature increase. Maximum depth is 24 metres (80 feet). A typical dive is 40–50 minutes.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access only from Fort Bay. The site uses a permanent mooring maintained by the Saba Conservation Foundation. Entry is standard boat-style giant stride or backward roll. The shallow mooring crest at 10 meters provides an excellent safety stop location.

## Tips and Recommendations

The volcanic vent detection technique: look for sand that changes colour compared to the surrounding area, and gently place your hand near (not in) the discoloured sand to feel the temperature difference. Do not disturb or block the vent openings. Hot Springs is one of Saba's best night dive sites — bioluminescent ostracod displays and active crustacean behaviour make after-dark diving here particularly rewarding.

## Safety Considerations

The slightly elevated water temperature near vents is natural and presents no hazard. Do not insert hands or equipment into vent openings. Current can develop on the outer reef section at 20–24 metres. Standard Saba Marine Park rules apply: no touching, no collecting, DSMB required.

## Photography

The volcanic vent areas are unusual macro subjects — the visual distortion from warm water rising through sand creates compelling images. Flying gurnards and garden eels are photogenic in their natural habitat. On night dives, the bioluminescent ostracod displays are a striking wide-angle subject. The resident nurse shark is highly approachable and a standard subject for wide-angle photography.
