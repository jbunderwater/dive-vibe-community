# Torrens Point

Known as "Saba's Nursery" — the island's most protected dive site, with boulder caves, a 60-foot swim-through tunnel, seagrass beds, and reliable juvenile marine life. The entire dive can be done in under 8 metres, making it ideal for beginners and snorkellers.

## Overview

Torrens Point is one of Saba's most sheltered and accessible dive sites, located in the protected bay on the island's northern side. Sea Saba calls it "Saba's Nursery" — a reference to both the diverse juvenile marine life found here and the fact that it serves as an ideal introduction to Saba diving. The bay is protected from prevailing swells during normal weather, producing calm surface conditions and gentle underwater motion. The site features a mix of large volcanic boulders, small caves, and — a feature often missed in descriptions — a swim-through tunnel approximately 60 feet (18 metres) long that experienced divers can explore at the site's deeper end. The mooring sits at 7 metres (25 feet); maximum depth is 16 metres (50 feet), though much of the most interesting diving takes place in less than 8 metres. A Coral Nursery is located at Torrens Point where Saba's operators offer divers participation in active coral restoration — fragments of acropora and staghorn coral are grown on metal racks and later transplanted to damaged reef sections. Bait balls are frequently encountered at this site. The site also serves as an officially marked snorkel trail (the Edward S. Arnold Snorkelling Trail).

## Marine Life

Torrens Point consistently delivers encounters with juvenile and resident species. Juvenile spotted drums — one of the most sought-after reef sightings in the Caribbean — are reliably found near the boulders and overhangs. Green turtles and hawksbill turtles are both confirmed here. Spotted lobster and Caribbean spiny lobster shelter beneath boulder overhangs. Spotted snake eels occupy sand channels between boulders. Octopus are regularly encountered during exploratory diving among the rocks. Great barracuda patrol the site perimeter. Flatworms in vivid colours are found on the rocky surfaces. Bait balls form irregularly but are a dramatic encounter when present.

## Dive Profile

Descent is via mooring at 7 metres. The standard beginner route explores the boulder field and small caves in the shallow zone (3–8 m) before working toward the deeper boulder sections at 10–16 m. Experienced divers and guides lead the 60-foot tunnel at the deeper end — a genuine feature that elevates this site beyond a simple shallow reef. The Coral Nursery is accessible from the mooring area and can be included in the dive on request. Safety stop at 5 metres on the mooring; snorkelling is possible directly from the mooring in calm conditions.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access from Fort Bay. Permanent mooring maintained by the Saba Conservation Foundation. Entry by giant stride or backward roll. The calm, shallow nature of the site means exits are straightforward. Excellent site for a final easy dive at the end of a multi-day trip. DSMB should still be carried.

## Tips and Recommendations

Ask the operator specifically about the Coral Nursery visit and the swim-through tunnel — these are not always included in a standard Torrens Point dive but are available on request. Spend time in the boulder field looking carefully at the rock surfaces and underneath overhangs for the juvenile spotted drums and flatworms. The shallow profile means long bottom time — take advantage of it for slow, methodical observation. This is Saba's best site for a relaxed night dive given its calm conditions and easy navigation.

## Safety Considerations

Current can develop on incoming tides even in this protected bay. Fire coral is present on some boulder surfaces — maintain buoyancy and avoid contact. Sea urchins are common in sandy gaps between rocks. DSMB required.

## Photography

Torrens Point is excellent for both wide-angle and macro photography. The boulders in 3–8 metres provide beautiful ambient light for fish portraits. Juvenile spotted drums with their long trailing dorsal fins are classic Caribbean macro subjects. The wide-angle possibilities inside the 60-foot tunnel suit photographers comfortable navigating in tight spaces. Bait ball encounters, when they occur, are among the most dramatic wide-angle moments available at any Saba site.
