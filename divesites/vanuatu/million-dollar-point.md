## Million Dollar Point

Million Dollar Point is one of the world's most unique dive sites — a deliberately created dump of millions of dollars of WWII US military equipment pushed into the sea in 1945, forming a sprawling artificial reef of recognizable military hardware on a gently sloping shore-accessible site near Luganville, Espiritu Santo.

## Overview

When WWII ended in August 1945, the United States had accumulated an enormous supply base at Espiritu Santo — one of the largest in the Pacific theater. The French and British colonial governments offered to purchase the surplus equipment, but at prices the US military deemed insultingly low. Unwilling to give the equipment away, the US command made the decision to destroy it: everything was driven or pushed off the coral shelf at this point into the sea. Jeeps, bulldozers, trucks, cranes, forklifts, field guns, Caterpillar tractors, and crates of supplies by the thousands entered the water. The value of the dumped material has been estimated at anywhere from $250 million to $1 billion in contemporary terms — hence the name.

Eighty years later, every piece of equipment is now an artificial reef structure, encrusted with hard and soft corals, sponges, and the full range of tropical reef life. The slope begins at 3 meters and extends to 40 meters at the deepest accessible point, with the main equipment concentration in the 10–25 meter zone. The equipment is still largely recognizable — steering wheels project from coral-covered trucks, bulldozer treads and blades are clearly identifiable, engine blocks are visible inside coral-encrusted hoods.

## What to Expect

Shore entry is easy — a short walk down the beach to the water's edge and a wade into shallow water reveals the first pieces of equipment within meters. The shallow sections (3–10 meters) are suitable for snorkelers as well as divers. The concentration of equipment increases with depth, with the main assemblage of heavy vehicles in the 12–22 meter zone. The deepest items — large cranes and industrial machinery — rest at 30–40 meters.

The site produces excellent marine photography: the combination of recognizable man-made objects, dramatic coral encrustation, and natural light makes for compelling wide-angle compositions. Lionfish are ubiquitous throughout the equipment. Barracuda and trevally hunt in the water column above. Hawksbill turtles are regular sightings grazing on the coral growth covering the equipment.

## Practical Information

- **Depth Range**: 3–40 meters
- **Visibility**: 15–30 meters typical
- **Currents**: Generally mild; sheltered shore location near Luganville
- **Entry**: Shore entry — easy access suitable for all levels
- **Best For**: All divers from beginners to advanced; iconic historical site; excellent photography

## Marine Life Highlights

Lionfish, barracuda, trevally, hawksbill turtles, moray eels, snapper, fusiliers, groupers, black coral trees at depth, soft coral and hard coral encrusting all equipment, nudibranchs, scorpionfish.
