## USNS General Hoyt S. Vandenberg

USNS General Hoyt S. Vandenberg is a historic wreck dive in Florida Keys, North America.

## Overview

The Vandenberg is a 522-foot former Cold War missile-tracking ship scuttled approximately 6–7 miles south of Key West on 27 May 2009, now the second-largest artificial reef in the world. She lies with the main deck at approximately 100–108 feet and upper structures reaching 55–70 feet deep. Hurricane Irma in September 2017 pushed the bow roughly 10 feet deeper, rotated the wreck about 30 degrees, and destroyed most of the vessel's iconic parabolic tracking dishes — only skeletal remnants of the dishes remain on the main deck. Goliath groupers, moray eels, barracuda, bull sharks, and lionfish are regulars, and the vessel's hundreds of compartments reward multiple dives across different depths and certification levels. This is an Advanced dive: the main deck sits at 100+ feet, requiring Nitrox planning and solid deep-water skills.

## Marine Life

Goliath grouper are resident in the cargo holds and machinery spaces, and the upper structures host moray eels, tarpon, and large schools of barracuda. Bull sharks are frequently sighted around the hull, with the occasional sea turtle moving across the deck. Lionfish are documented on the wreck. The superstructure has become encrusted with sponges and soft corals. No site-specific source confirmed scorpionfish at the Vandenberg; that species has been removed from this description pending verification.

## Wreck History

The Vandenberg was originally laid down on 22 February 1943 and launched on 10 October 1943 at Kaiser shipyards in Richmond, California as the troop transport USS General Harry Taylor (AP-145). At 522 feet 10 inches in length with a displacement of 9,950 tons light and 17,250 tons loaded, she carried troops during WWII. She was renamed USAFS General Hoyt S. Vandenberg on 11 June 1963 in honor of the former Air Force Chief of Staff, then reacquired by the Navy in 1964 and redesignated T-AGM-10 as a missile range instrumentation ship, tracking ballistic missile tests and supporting US space program operations including Apollo and Gemini missions. Scenes for the science-fiction film Virus (released 1999) were filmed aboard her while she sat in the James River Reserve Fleet in Virginia. After years of preparation by the city of Key West and the Vandenberg artificial reef project, she was scuttled on 27 May 2009 approximately 6–7 miles south of Key West, becoming the second-largest artificial reef in the world. Hurricane Irma in September 2017 shifted the bow roughly 10 feet deeper (now reaching ~165 feet at the deepest point), rotated the wreck ~30 degrees, and destroyed most of the iconic parabolic tracking dishes — skeletal metal remnants of the dishes remain on the main deck but the recognizable dish structures are gone.

## Dive Profile

Post-Hurricane Irma depths: the shallowest accessible structures reach 40–55 feet, the main superstructure runs 55–75 feet, and the main deck sits at approximately 100–108 feet. The sand bottom alongside the deepest sections reaches ~165 feet at the bow end. The parabolic tracking dishes were destroyed by Irma — only metal remnants remain on the main deck at approximately 65–75 feet. The vessel's bridge, crow's nest, balloon hangar, and hundreds of interior compartments are still accessible via openings cut into the hull during pre-sinking preparation. The size of the ship means no single dive covers the whole wreck — multiple dives at different depths are the norm.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive only. The Vandenberg is served by mooring buoys; descending and ascending on the buoy line is standard practice given the depth and the open-water location. Multiple Key West operators (Lost Reef Adventures, Key West Dive Center, and others) run two-tank trips, with operators typically requiring open-water divers to be paired with a guide and to have logged recent dives.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Advanced Open Water plus Nitrox certification is the practical minimum for the main deck (100–108 feet).
- Wreck Diver and Deep Diver training are strongly recommended for any penetration of the lower decks.
- Plan multiple dives — the bridge, balloon hangar, and upper structures are a different experience from the deeper deck runs. Note the radar dishes were destroyed by Hurricane Irma in 2017.
- Bring a primary light and backup for any internal exploration.

## Safety Considerations

Depths of 100+ feet put divers well into the deep range with limited no-deco time on air; nitrox extends bottom time meaningfully. Open-water currents at the site can be strong and variable. Penetration of the Vandenberg is not safe by default — the elevator shafts, cargo holds, and lower-deck corridors require proper wreck training, redundant lights and gas, and a continuous guideline. Hurricane shifts have left some internal hazards; do not enter areas you cannot back out of cleanly.

## Photography

Note: the Vandenberg's iconic parabolic tracking dishes were destroyed by Hurricane Irma in 2017. Wide-angle photography of the bridge, crow's nest, balloon hangar, and the broad upper deck silhouette remains compelling. The typically clear blue water at this offshore site suits wide-angle lenses and strobes. Goliath groupers and schools of barracuda at the superstructure level are reliable wide-angle wildlife subjects.
