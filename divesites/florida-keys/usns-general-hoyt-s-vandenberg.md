## USNS General Hoyt S. Vandenberg

USNS General Hoyt S. Vandenberg is a historic wreck dive in Florida Keys, North America.

## Overview

The Vandenberg is a 522-foot-10-inch former Cold War missile-tracking ship scuttled 7 miles south of Key West on 27 May 2009, now the second-largest artificial reef in the world and voted the number one wreck dive on Earth by Scuba Diving magazine readers. She lies with the main deck at 100 feet and her tallest superstructure reaching 55–70 feet deep. Hurricane Irma in September 2017 moved the wreck, deepened the bow by roughly 10 feet, and rotated the hull about 30 degrees; the iconic parabolic tracking dishes, already damaged and reattached after the 2009 sinking, were largely reduced to metal skeleton remnants by the storm. Goliath groupers, moray eels, barracuda, and bull sharks are regulars, and the more than 1,000 compartments reward multiple dives across different depths and certification levels.

## Marine Life

Goliath grouper are resident in the cargo holds and machinery spaces, and the upper structures host moray eels, tarpon, and large schools of barracuda. Bull sharks and nurse sharks are regular sightings around the hull, with the occasional sea turtle moving across the deck. Lionfish and scorpionfish are also commonly noted on the wreck. The superstructure and upper decks have become heavily encrusted with sponges and soft corals over 15+ years on the bottom.

## Wreck History

The Vandenberg was originally laid down on 22 February 1943 and launched on 10 October 1943 at Kaiser Co., Inc. in Richmond, California (Yard 3) as the troop transport USS General Harry Taylor (AP-145). At 522 feet 10 inches in length with a displacement of 9,950 tons light and 17,250 tons loaded, she carried troops during the final year of WWII. She was acquired by the US Air Force in 1961 and renamed in honor of General Hoyt S. Vandenberg, then redesignated T-AGM-10 in 1964 as a missile range instrumentation ship, tracking ballistic missile tests and supporting US space program operations. During production of the science-fiction film Virus (released January 1999), she served as the filming location for the fictional Russian research vessel; some Cyrillic lettering applied for the film was still visible on the hull when she was scuttled. After years of preparation by the city of Key West and the Vandenberg artificial reef project, she was scuttled on 27 May 2009 approximately 7 miles south of Key West, becoming the second-largest artificial reef in the world. On 10 September 2017, Hurricane Irma moved the wreck 200–300 feet, deepened the bow by roughly 10 feet, and rotated the hull about 30 degrees; she now sits with her deepest point at roughly 165 feet. The parabolic tracking dishes, which had already broken off their pedestals during the 2009 sinking and were reattached in 2011, were heavily corroded and structurally degraded before Irma and are now reduced to scattered metal remnants at the site.

## Dive Profile

The tallest upper structures reach to within 55–70 feet of the surface, with some repositioned structural elements as shallow as 40 feet. The main deck runs at 100–105 feet, and the sand falls away to roughly 140 feet (with the deepest point near 165 feet post-Irma). Note: the iconic parabolic tracking dishes were heavily damaged during the 2009 sinking, re-secured in 2011, and were further degraded by Hurricane Irma in 2017; only scattered metal remnants remain where the dishes once stood. Wreck-trained divers can swim a continuous 475-foot run along the starboard section of Deck 1 and explore the cargo holds, stair towers, elevator shafts, and hallways through openings cut into the hull during preparation. The size of the ship means no single dive covers the whole wreck — multiple dives at different depths are the norm.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive only. The Vandenberg is served by mooring buoys; descending and ascending on the buoy line is standard practice given the depth and the open-water location. Multiple Key West operators (Lost Reef Adventures, Key West Dive Center, and others) run two-tank trips, with operators typically requiring open-water divers to be paired with a guide and to have logged recent dives.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Advanced Open Water plus Nitrox certification is the practical minimum for the main deck.
- Wreck Diver and Deep Diver training are strongly recommended for any penetration of the lower decks.
- Plan multiple dives — the upper superstructure and bridge area are a different experience from the deeper main deck and hull runs.
- Bring a primary light and backup for any internal exploration.

## Safety Considerations

Depths of 100+ feet put divers well into the deep range with limited no-deco time on air; nitrox extends bottom time meaningfully. Open-water currents at the site can be strong and variable. Penetration of the Vandenberg is not safe by default — the elevator shafts, cargo holds, and lower-deck corridors require proper wreck training, redundant lights and gas, and a continuous guideline. Hurricane shifts have left some internal hazards; do not enter areas you cannot back out of cleanly.

## Photography

Prior to Hurricane Irma, the Vandenberg's twin parabolic tracking dishes were the most recognizable wide-angle subjects on the wreck. As of 2017, those dishes exist only as scattered metal fragments at their former attachment points on the main deck. The broad upper-deck silhouette, crane structures, and the 522-foot hull remain compelling wide-angle subjects, and the typically blue water 7 miles offshore allows full-hull framing when conditions cooperate. Wide-angle lenses and strobes remain the standard rig.
