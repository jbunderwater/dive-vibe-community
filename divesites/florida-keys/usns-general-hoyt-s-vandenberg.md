## USNS General Hoyt S. Vandenberg

USNS General Hoyt S. Vandenberg is a historic wreck dive in Florida Keys, North America.

## Overview

The Vandenberg is a 522-foot, 10-inch former missile-tracking ship scuttled approximately 7 miles south of Key West on 27 May 2009, now the second-largest intentionally sunk artificial reef in the world and voted the number one wreck dive on Earth by Scuba Diving magazine readers. She lies with the main deck at 100–105 feet and her tallest superstructure reaching 55–70 feet deep, with the enormous parabolic tracking dishes and radar equipment from her Mercury, Gemini, and Apollo mission-tracking duties creating extraordinary swim-through terrain. Goliath groupers, moray eels, barracuda, and tarpon are regulars on the wreck, and the more than 1,000 compartments reward multiple dives across different depths and certification levels. Note: some sources describe the site as "6 miles" from Key West while others consistently state "7 miles" — the difference likely reflects measurement from different points on the island.

## Marine Life

Goliath grouper are resident in the cargo holds and machinery spaces, and the upper structures host moray eels, tarpon, and large schools of barracuda. The superstructure and radar dishes have become encrusted with sponges and soft corals, and parrotfish, wrasses, and damselfish use the hull as habitat. Nassau grouper, moray eels, and occasional nurse sharks have been documented in the deeper sections. Note: "bull shark" sightings are frequently mentioned in promotional descriptions of the wreck, but site-specific trip reports from dive operators primarily document goliath grouper, barracuda, tarpon, and moray eels rather than regularly encountered bull sharks — individual encounters occur but are not reliably documented as a resident species.

## Wreck History

The Vandenberg was originally laid down on 22 February 1943 and launched on 10 October 1943 at Kaiser Co., Inc., Richmond, California, as the troop transport USS General Harry Taylor (AP-145). At 522 feet 10 inches in length with a displacement of 9,950 tons light and 17,250 tons full load, she carried troops during the final years of WWII. She was reacquired by the US Air Force in July 1961 and renamed in honor of General Hoyt S. Vandenberg; the Navy reacquired her in July 1964 and redesignated her T-AGM-10 as a missile range instrumentation ship and Advanced Range Instrumentation Ship (ARIS 2), tracking Mercury, Gemini, and Apollo space missions and ballistic missile tests. In 1998, scenes for the science-fiction film Virus were shot aboard her; she stood in for a fictional Russian vessel called the Akademik Vladislav Volkov, with Cyrillic lettering visible on the hull afterward. After years of preparation by the city of Key West and the Vandenberg Artificial Reef Project, she was scuttled on 27 May 2009 approximately 7 miles south of Key West, becoming the second-largest artificial reef in the world. Hurricane Irma in September 2017 shifted the wreck; she now sits with her deepest point at roughly 165 feet, with the bow shifted approximately 30 degrees and moved 200–300 feet from its original position.

## Dive Profile

The tallest upper structures — including the two large parabolic tracking dishes that gave the ship her distinctive profile — reach to within 55–70 feet of the surface, with some repositioned structural elements as shallow as 40 feet. The main deck runs at 100–105 feet, and the sand falls away to roughly 140 feet (with the deepest point near 165 feet post-Irma). Wreck-trained divers can swim a continuous 475-foot run along the starboard section of Deck 1 and explore the cargo holds, stair towers, elevator shafts, and hallways through openings cut into the hull during preparation. The size of the ship means no single dive covers the whole wreck — multiple dives at different depths are the norm.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive only. The Vandenberg is served by mooring buoys; descending and ascending on the buoy line is standard practice given the depth and the open-water location. Multiple Key West operators (Lost Reef Adventures, Key West Dive Center, and others) run two-tank trips, with operators typically requiring open-water divers to be paired with a guide and to have logged recent dives.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Advanced Open Water plus Nitrox certification is the practical minimum for the main deck.
- Wreck Diver and Deep Diver training are strongly recommended for any penetration of the lower decks.
- Plan multiple dives — the radar dishes and bridge are a different experience from the deeper deck runs.
- Bring a primary light and backup for any internal exploration.

## Safety Considerations

Depths of 100+ feet put divers well into the deep range with limited no-deco time on air; nitrox extends bottom time meaningfully. Open-water currents at the site can be strong and variable. Penetration of the Vandenberg is not safe by default — the elevator shafts, cargo holds, and lower-deck corridors require proper wreck training, redundant lights and gas, and a continuous guideline. Hurricane shifts have left some internal hazards; do not enter areas you cannot back out of cleanly.

## Photography

The Vandenberg's twin parabolic tracking dishes and the broad upper deck silhouette are among the most recognizable subjects in the Keys, and the typically blue water 7 miles offshore lets wide-angle shooters capture the full structure of the dishes when conditions cooperate. Wide-angle lenses and strobes are the standard rig.
