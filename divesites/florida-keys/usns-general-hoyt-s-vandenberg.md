## USNS General Hoyt S. Vandenberg

USNS General Hoyt S. Vandenberg is a historic wreck dive in Florida Keys, North America.

## Overview

The Vandenberg is a 522-foot former missile-range instrumentation ship scuttled 7 miles south of Key West on 27 May 2009, now the second-largest artificial reef in the world and voted the number one wreck dive on Earth by Scuba Diving magazine readers. She lies with the main deck at 100–105 feet and her tallest superstructure reaching 47–70 feet deep, with the enormous parabolic tracking dishes — used to monitor Mercury, Gemini, and Apollo missions from 1962 to 1983 — creating extraordinary swim-through terrain. Goliath groupers, moray eels, barracuda, and sharks are regularly reported, and the scale of the ship rewards multiple dives across different depths and certification levels.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Florida Keys, North America
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 42.7 meters

## Marine Life

Goliath grouper are resident in the cargo holds and machinery spaces, and the upper structures host moray eels, tarpon, and large schools of barracuda. Bull sharks and nurse sharks are regular sightings around the hull, with the occasional sea turtle moving across the deck. Lionfish and scorpionfish are also commonly noted on the wreck. The superstructure and radar dishes have become heavily encrusted with sponges and soft corals.

## Wreck History

The Vandenberg was laid down on 22 February 1943 and launched on 10 October 1943 at Kaiser shipyards in Richmond, California as the troop transport USS General Harry Taylor (AP-145). At 522 feet 10 inches in length with a displacement of 9,950 tons light and 17,250 tons at full load, she carried troops to the Pacific during WWII and participated in the 1957 Hungarian Relief program, transporting thousands of refugees to Australia. Transferred to the US Air Force in 1961 and redesignated T-AGM-10 by the Navy in 1964, she served as an Advanced Range Instrumentation Ship off Cape Canaveral, using stabilized radar tracking domes to monitor Mercury, Gemini, and Apollo capsules and record the terminal phase of ballistic missile re-entries until retirement in 1983. In 1998, scenes for the science-fiction film Virus were filmed aboard her. She was scuttled on 27 May 2009 approximately 7 miles south of Key West. Hurricane Irma in September 2017 moved the wreck approximately 200–300 feet, rotated the bow about 30 degrees, and pushed the deepest sections down by roughly 10 feet; she now sits with her hull bottom at approximately 140–165 feet depending on location.

## Dive Profile

The tallest upper structures — including the parabolic tracking dishes — reach to within 47–70 feet of the surface, making them accessible to Open Water certified divers (accompanied by a guide). The main deck runs at 100–105 feet, and the hull bottom falls to roughly 140–165 feet post-Irma depending on location. Advanced Open Water certification is the practical minimum for the main deck; Deep Diver and Wreck Diver training are required for deeper penetration of the lower decks, cargo holds, stair towers, and elevator shafts. The size of the ship means no single dive covers the whole wreck — multiple dives at different depths are the norm.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive only. The Vandenberg is served by mooring buoys; descending and ascending on the buoy line is standard practice given the depth and the open-water location. Multiple Key West operators (Lost Reef Adventures, Key West Dive Center, and others) run two-tank trips, with operators typically requiring open-water divers to be paired with a guide and to have logged recent dives.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Advanced Open Water certification is required for the main deck; Open Water divers may access the upper superstructure and dishes with a mandatory guide (available from Key West operators at additional cost).
- Wreck Diver and Deep Diver training are required for any penetration of the lower decks, cargo holds, and elevator shafts.
- Plan multiple dives — the dishes and bridge at 47–70 feet are a different experience from the 100-foot main deck runs.
- Bring a primary light and backup for any internal exploration.

## Safety Considerations

Depths of 100+ feet put divers well into the deep range with limited no-deco time on air; nitrox extends bottom time meaningfully. Open-water currents at the site can be strong and variable. Penetration of the Vandenberg is not safe by default — the elevator shafts, cargo holds, and lower-deck corridors require proper wreck training, redundant lights and gas, and a continuous guideline. Hurricane shifts have left some internal hazards; do not enter areas you cannot back out of cleanly.

## Photography

The Vandenberg's twin parabolic tracking dishes and the broad upper deck silhouette are among the most recognizable subjects in the Keys, and the typically blue water 7 miles offshore lets wide-angle shooters capture the full structure of the dishes when conditions cooperate. Wide-angle lenses and strobes are the standard rig.
