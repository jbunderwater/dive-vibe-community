## Spiegel Grove

Spiegel Grove is a historic wreck dive in Florida Keys, North America.

## Overview

The USS Spiegel Grove is a 510-foot Navy landing ship lying in the Florida Keys National Marine Sanctuary off Key Largo, scuttled in 2002 and now ranked among the premier wreck dives in the United States. Hurricane Dennis in 2005 rolled the ship upright from its original listing position, and it now rests with the main deck at 70–90 feet and the bow in roughly 95 feet of water, with the mast reaching up to 60 feet. Every surface is encrusted in sponges and soft corals, Goliath groupers colonise the holds, and divers can reach into a series of purpose-cut swim-throughs without full penetration — though the sheer size means a hundred dives still won't reveal everything.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Florida Keys, North America
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 42.7 meters

## Wreck History

The ex-USS Spiegel Grove (LSD-32) was a Thomaston-class dock landing ship laid down at Ingalls Shipbuilding in Pascagoula, Mississippi in September 1954, launched on November 10, 1955, and commissioned on June 8, 1956. She was 510 feet long with an 84-foot beam, named for the Fremont, Ohio estate of President Rutherford B. Hayes, and served the U.S. Navy in amphibious operations until decommissioning on October 2, 1989. Acquired for use as an artificial reef off Key Largo, she sank prematurely on May 17, 2002 — settling several hours ahead of schedule, rolling to starboard, and ending up upside-down with the bow protruding from the surface. In June 2002, Resolve Marine Group rolled her onto her starboard side at an additional cost of $250,000, and in July 2005 Hurricane Dennis shifted the hull onto her keel upright — the orientation originally intended.

## Marine Life

Florida-Scubadiving.com reports over 144 fish species documented on the wreck, including large groupers, cubera snapper, barracuda, jacks, reef and nurse sharks, and sea turtles. Operators consistently observe schools of pelagic fish swirling around the superstructure, with the encrusted crane arms and railings draped in colorful sponges and soft corals.

## Dive Profile

The wreck sits upright on sand at Dixie Shoal roughly 6 miles off Key Largo. The top of the superstructure is approximately 60 feet (some sources note 60-65 feet depending on tide), the main deck sits around 80-90 feet, and the sandy bottom alongside the hull reaches 130-135 feet. The ship is 510 feet long and 84 feet wide — large enough that operators commonly say "you could dive it a hundred times and not see all of it." Many of the original openings have been widened and lined for daylight swim-throughs accessible to advanced divers without committing to true penetration.

## Entry and Exit

The site is accessible only by boat (about a 25-30 minute run from Key Largo) and is moored, never anchored. Operators tie off to one of several permanent mooring buoys positioned around the wreck. Divers descend the mooring line to the superstructure.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Operators typically require Advanced Open Water certification at minimum, with deep dive experience within the past year; some require proof of 25 logged dives.
- Nitrox extends bottom time meaningfully at the 80-100-foot decks and is widely supported by Key Largo operators.
- Plan dives around the superstructure and deck features (crane arms, conning tower, deck guns) on a first visit before descending to the lower hull.
- Multiple operators offer a PADI "Spiegel Grove Diver" distinctive specialty course oriented to the site.

## Safety Considerations

- True penetration requires proper overhead-environment training and equipment; silt inside the hull can drop visibility to near zero and cause disorientation. Daylight swim-throughs are not a substitute for wreck-penetration certification.
- Currents at the site can be strong; brief on drift contingency and surface-marker buoy use before splashing.
- The 60-foot deck is well below recreational no-decompression limits for repetitive dives — monitor computers carefully and plan conservative profiles.
- Sun exposure on surface intervals 6 miles offshore is intense; bring water, sun protection, and a wind layer.
- Visibility ranges widely; on cloudy days the superstructure can fade into blue-green haze and divers should stay close to the mooring line on descent.

## Photography

Florida-Scubadiving.com notes the ship's crane arms, encrusted with sponges and corals, are a signature wide-angle subject; the upright orientation since 2005 makes the superstructure photogenic and navigable, though the depth and ambient light loss favor strobes and fast wide lenses.
