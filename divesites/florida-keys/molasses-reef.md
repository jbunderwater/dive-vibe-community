## Molasses Reef

Molasses Reef is a reef dive site in Florida Keys, North America.

## Overview

Molasses Reef is the most frequently visited reef dive in the Upper Keys and arguably the most dived reef in the world, a classic outer reef system located 6 miles southeast of Key Largo within the Florida Keys National Marine Sanctuary. The well-defined spur and groove topography runs from 6 feet near the crest down to over 90 feet at the outer ledges, with elkhorn corals, towering brain corals, sea fans, and enormous barrel sponges providing habitat for over 600 species of marine life including sea turtles, nurse sharks, and large schools of snapper and grunt. Eagle Ray Alley is a named sand channel on the northern half of the reef that runs seaward from shallow Molasses to Logan's Run, with high-relief coral ledges on either side; eagle rays are occasionally reported passing through, but ScubaBoard regulars note that sightings are not guaranteed and no more likely here than elsewhere on the reef. A large mechanical winch from the 1887 wreck of the schooner Slobodana adds historical interest at the Winch Hole.

## Marine Life

NOAA describes the 247-acre Sanctuary Preservation Area as famous for "clear water, fish populations, and numerous boulder corals." Nurse sharks are commonly found resting beneath ledges, spiny lobster and banded coral shrimp share the deeper holes in the spur-and-groove walls, and spotted stingrays and yellowhead jawfish inhabit the sand channels of Eagle Ray Alley — with eagle rays passing through occasionally but not reliably (ScubaBoard regulars confirm sightings are not guaranteed). Reef sharks have been recorded along the alley walls, and large schools of permit gather at the southern end of the SPA near Permit Ledges where current pushes off the Gulf Stream.

## Dive Profile

Molasses is a classic outer-reef spur-and-groove system with depths ranging from roughly 6 feet at the crest down to more than 90 feet at the outer ledges, and high-relief coral heads that rise 12-15 feet from the bottom along the spurs. The 45-foot lighted steel reef marker tower stands in approximately 9 feet of water and is the navigational reference for the site. More than 30 numbered mooring buoys mark distinct sub-sites including North Star, Winch Hole, Hole in the Wall, Aquarium, Eagle Ray Alley, Spanish Anchor, Fire Coral Cave, and Permit Ledges. Spanish Anchor at buoy 21 (south end) features an 8-foot Spanish-era anchor embedded in coral in 25-30 feet, and the Winch Hole holds a large mechanical winch from the 1887 wreck of the schooner Slobodana.

## Entry and Exit

The reef sits roughly 6 miles southeast of Key Largo, a 30-minute run by boat from town. Anchoring on the reef is prohibited; all vessels must tie off to one of the 30+ numbered mooring buoys installed and maintained by the Sanctuary.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Choose buoys at the northern end of the reef for less current and easier conditions; the southern end runs stronger as it sits closer to the Gulf Stream and is better suited as a drift dive.
- Operators rotate buoys based on conditions, so requesting a specific sub-site (Spanish Anchor, Eagle Ray Alley, Winch Hole) ahead of time helps.
- The shallow crest is excellent for long second-tank dives and snorkelers, while the outer ledges below 60 feet reward advanced divers with better pelagic encounters.

## Safety Considerations

- Sun exposure on surface intervals is severe — bring water, reef-safe sunscreen, and sun protection.
- The site is named in part for Fire Coral Cave; fire coral is widespread on the spurs and easily contacted with a careless fin or knee.
- Mid-summer reef temperatures regularly hit 86 degrees F; NOAA documented widespread bleaching during the 2023 heat event, and divers should never touch corals.
- The southern end's current can be strong; brief with the boat on drift procedures and surface-marker buoy use before entering.
- The SPA is a strict no-take zone — no spearfishing, no lobstering, no collecting of corals or shells, no fish-feeding.

## Photography

Key Largo's summer visibility on outer reefs commonly runs 60-80 feet (occasionally to 100 feet in clear winter mornings), making Molasses well-suited to wide-angle reef and seascape photography across the spur-and-groove ridges and over the historic Spanish Anchor.
