## Turtle Rock

Turtle Rock is a boulder dive site in the southern Similan Islands National Park, Phang Nga Province, Thailand. As its name suggests, the site is one of the most reliable spots in the Similans to encounter resting green sea turtles (Chelonia mydas) at close range in calm, accessible conditions.

## Overview

The site centres on a cluster of granite boulders rising from a sandy bottom at 20–25 meters. The boulders are worn smooth by water action and colonised with encrusting coral, sponges, and small soft coral patches. Overhangs and gaps between the boulders create sheltered resting spots favoured by green turtles, which haul themselves into recesses to rest between feeding bouts. The site is beginner-appropriate: depth is modest, current is light on most days, visibility is generally good, and the topography is simple enough to navigate without a complex dive plan. It functions well as an introductory boulder dive or as a second, more relaxed dive following a deeper site.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Similan Islands National Park, south Similan area, Phang Nga Province, Thailand
- **Entry Type**: liveaboard
- **Site Type**: Boulder
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 25 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–30 meters
- **Current**: Minimal to light
- **Best Time**: November to May

## Marine Life

Green sea turtles are the defining encounter — they are found resting in the overhangs and crevices between boulders, often in pairs or small groups. Their relaxed demeanour at this site makes sustained observation possible, and they are frequently seen hauled into a crevice with their head resting on a ledge. Hawksbill turtles also appear, more actively foraging on the boulder surfaces. Whitetip reef sharks rest on the sandy bottom around the boulders. Moray eels occupy cracks in the formation. Various species of damselfish and wrasse populate the boulder surfaces. The sandy areas at the base hold garden eels and the occasional blue-spotted stingray.

## Dive Profile

Begin on the outer boulders at maximum depth (18–25 m) and work inward and upward through the formation, exploring the overhangs where turtles rest. The mid-depth boulders (10–18 m) offer the most turtle encounters. The shallower inner zone (5–10 m) above the formation is productive for reef fish and a good safety stop location. The dive is short on area — it is a site that rewards slow, careful exploration rather than covering distance.

## Entry and Exit

Liveaboard and day-trip access. The sheltered position makes this an easy zodiac operation in most conditions. Mooring available for visiting vessels.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Approach resting turtles slowly and from the side — entering an overhang from directly in front disturbs the animal and risks a collision
- Do not block the turtle's exit path with your body position
- Allow 10–15 minutes observing the boulder surface at a given depth before moving on — cryptic species like frogfish and ghost pipefish are present but require patient scanning
- Torch into the overhangs to spot resting soldierfish and juvenile sweetlips sheltering in the darker recesses
- This site is well-suited for controlled buoyancy practice at depth — maintaining hover above the sand without touching the bottom

## Safety Considerations

Turtle Rock is among the most forgiving sites in the Similan archipelago. Current is rarely a factor, depth is well within beginner limits, and the topography is clear. The main risk is minor and relates to beginner diver control around the boulders: inadvertent contact with sharp encrusted surfaces. Maintain a comfortable clearance from all structures and fin level to avoid stirring sand. Always carry an SMB.

## Photography

Green turtle portraits from this site are among the most accessible in the Similans. The overhangs allow a stable approach with the turtle stationary; shoot at eye level with a wide-angle to 60mm range. For natural colour rendition in ambient light at 12–18 m, a white balance correction (or shooting RAW) will recover the warm tones. Turtle-and-reef compositions with ambient blue-water background are an alternative to tight portraits. Frogfish, if located, are classic 100mm macro subjects in the sandy zones.
