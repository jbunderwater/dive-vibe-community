## East of Eden

East of Eden sits on the sheltered eastern face of Island 7 (Koh Similan) in the Similan Islands National Park, Phang Nga Province, Thailand. It represents the calmer, coral-garden character of the Similans' east side — a strong contrast to the surge-exposed granite boulder sites on the western faces of the same islands.

## Overview

The site is a classic Indo-Pacific sloping reef, beginning in a shallow sandy bay at around 5 meters and descending through tiered coral platforms to 40 meters at the outer slope. The east coast of the Similans faces into the Gulf of Thailand influence and is protected from the full Andaman swell, resulting in calmer conditions, lower current, and better visibility on most diving days. The hard coral coverage here — particularly table corals (Acropora hyacinthus), staghorn corals, and massive Porites heads — represents some of the healthiest reef structure in the national park. This is considered the "garden" of the Similans: accessible, species-rich, and reliably productive for wildlife encounters.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Similan Islands National Park, east side of Island 7, Phang Nga Province, Thailand
- **Entry Type**: liveaboard
- **Site Type**: Coral reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 40 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–35 meters
- **Current**: Light to moderate; generally gentler than west side
- **Best Time**: November to May

## Marine Life

Hawksbill and green sea turtles are the most reliable encounters — they feed on the hard corals and algae throughout the site and are often unconcerned by diver presence. Whitetip reef sharks patrol the deeper slope and rest on sand patches below 25 meters. Napoleon wrasse (humphead wrasse) are regularly spotted in the 15–25 m range, often alone or in pairs, and are approachable given the site's relatively calm conditions. Bumphead parrotfish occasionally move through the shallower table coral zones in small groups, scraping loudly at the coral. Blue-spotted stingrays rest on the sandy patches between coral heads. On the deeper slope, barracuda and trevally patrol in loose aggregations, and whitetip sharks are typically found resting in pairs or groups on the sand.

## Dive Profile

Begin in the shallow sand and rubble zone (5–8 m) before transitioning onto the reef crest. The middle slope (12–20 m) holds the densest hard coral coverage and is the most productive for turtle and Napoleon wrasse sightings. The outer slope (20–35 m) features more open terrain with scattered coral heads, larger fish, and deeper-dwelling reef sharks. Return via the reef crest for a final safety stop in the shallows. This is a good site for maintaining a comfortable mid-range depth and covering a large area of reef.

## Entry and Exit

Liveaboard and occasional day-trip access. The sheltered east coast makes zodiac entry and exit straightforward in all but the worst weather. The site is moored during peak season.

## Tips and Recommendations

- This is one of the best sites in the Similans for turtle encounters — move slowly and avoid approaching from above or directly in front
- The shallow hard coral zone (5–10 m) is excellent for a second dive or a safety stop: good light, clear water, and active reef life
- Napoleon wrasse are curious and will sometimes approach divers on their own — do not attempt to touch or feed them
- Check the sandy patches in the shallows for blue-spotted stingrays and garden eels retreating as you approach
- A standard dive light helps in the shadows under table corals where small crustaceans and nudibranchs shelter

## Safety Considerations

East of Eden is the gentler introduction to Similan diving. Current is rarely strong, surge is minimal on most days, and depth is easily managed on a long profile dive. The primary hazard is depth — the slope continues well beyond recreational limits and divers can gradually drift deeper while watching wildlife. Monitor your depth gauge and computer actively. Visibility is excellent and disorientation is unlikely. Always carry an SMB.

## Photography

The table coral formations photographed with a wide angle and a diver for scale are a signature image of this site. Turtle portraits are excellent here — the animals often rest on coral heads at 8–12 m, allowing a careful approach to within a metre or two for frame-filling images. Napoleon wrasse are large enough for wide-angle portraits but also detailed enough for medium macro on their facial scales. Ambient light is generous on the shallow reef crest from mid-morning; no strobe may be needed for coral wide-angles in good visibility.
