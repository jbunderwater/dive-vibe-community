## Tako Flats

Tako Flats is a shallow sand channel with scattered coral heads near the western tip of Molokini Crater, named for the Japanese word for octopus (tako) and known for the three species of octopus that shelter among its rubble and coral patches.

## Overview

Tako Flats occupies a sand channel bordered by coral heads inside Molokini Crater, near the western tip close to Reef's End. The site is characterised by relatively calm water conditions — sources consistently describe it as one of the most sheltered spots within the crater — and a sandy bottom interspersed with coral rubble and discrete coral heads. Three octopus species are confirmed here: the day octopus (he'e mauli), the sand octopus, and the white-striped octopus. The sand octopus is primarily nocturnal and buries itself during daylight hours; the day octopus is active during the day and hunts openly on the reef. Manta rays visit Tako Flats to feed on fish larvae. The site's calm conditions and relatively shallow depth make it approachable for intermediate divers and an excellent complement to deeper crater dives.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Molokini Crater, Maui, Hawaii (western inner crater)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 15 meters

## Marine Life

Confirmed site-specific species from multiple independent sources: day octopus (he'e mauli), sand octopus, white-striped octopus, manta rays (feeding on fish larvae during spawning season), moray eels, nudibranchs, and barracuda. The broader Molokini interior also hosts lionfish and damselfish, which are documented at Tako Flats by some sources. Octopus sightings are the primary draw and are reported consistently by dive operators.

## Tips

- Go slow and scan the sand and rubble carefully; day octopus are masters of camouflage and will be missed at normal swimming pace.
- The sand octopus is rarely seen during the day — look instead for the slight mound of sand or a telltale pile of shells (their middens) at burrow entrances.
- Manta ray visits are most frequent during fish spawning periods; ask your operator about current activity before the dive.
