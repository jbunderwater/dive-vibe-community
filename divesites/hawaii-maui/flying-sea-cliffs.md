## Flying Sea Cliffs

Flying Sea Cliffs is a dramatic back wall dive at Molokini Crater featuring a series of undercut hanging shelves beginning at around 15 metres, with the wall dropping vertically to approximately 40 metres below the largest shelf.

## Overview

Flying Sea Cliffs sits on the outer, ocean-facing back wall of Molokini Crater. The defining feature is a large undercut shelf that juts out at roughly 15 metres (50 feet) below the surface; beneath the lip, the wall is sheer and undercut, plunging another 40 metres (130 feet) to the seafloor. Divers typically explore the underside of this shelf, which is encrusted with red sponges and tube corals that burst into colour under torch light. Shadowy crevices along the undercut hide spiny lobsters and ghost shrimp. The exposed position and depth make this strictly an advanced dive, conducted as a live-boat operation with no permanent mooring.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Molokini Crater, Maui, Hawaii (outer back wall)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 40 meters

## Marine Life

Sources confirm at this site: red sponges and tube coral on the underside of the shelf, spiny lobsters, and ghost shrimp in crevices. The broader back wall of Molokini is documented to host whitetip reef sharks, grey reef sharks, moray eels, slipper lobsters, longnose hawkfish in black coral, eagle rays, and occasional manta rays — but source accounts for Flying Sea Cliffs specifically focus on the invertebrate community on the shelf underside rather than fish species. No site-specific fish species list has been independently confirmed for this exact section of the wall.

## Tips

- A high-powered primary torch is essential to illuminate the tube coral and sponges on the underside of the shelf; without it, the colour is lost in the shadow.
- The undercut geometry creates surge, which can make hovering under the shelf challenging — Advanced Open Water and buoyancy practice in open water is advisable before this dive.
- Conditions on the exposed south face deteriorate quickly in afternoon swells; morning departures are standard practice for all Molokini back wall sites.
