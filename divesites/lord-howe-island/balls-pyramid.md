## Ball's Pyramid

Ball's Pyramid is the world's tallest volcanic sea stack and Lord Howe Island's premier advanced dive destination, located approximately 20 kilometres southeast of the main island.

## Overview

Ball's Pyramid rises 572 metres from the ocean surface and sits at the top of many experienced divers' bucket lists. The surrounding underwater terrain features dramatic vertical walls, sea caves, and coral-encrusted rock stacks. Two primary dive locations operate here: Observatory Rock, where 5–8 metre high stacks shelter divers from southerly current and are covered in colourful corals and aquatic plants; and South East Rock (also called Sunken Rock), a series of 2–3 metre ledges descending from the island's north side to around 25–30 metres. Dives range from following a coral wall to drift diving amid pelagic schools. All Ball's Pyramid trips are weather-dependent and cannot be pre-booked; operators schedule trips on availability when conditions and diver numbers permit.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Approximately 20 km southeast of Lord Howe Island (50-minute boat ride)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 25 meters
- **Special Note**: Trips weather-dependent; pre-booked package holders receive priority

## Marine Life

Ball's Pyramid is believed to be the only place on Earth where the Ballina Angelfish (*Chaetodontoplus ballinae*) is reliably observed on recreational scuba. This species is endemic to eastern Australia and normally inhabits depths of 15 to 120 metres on offshore reefs and seamounts; it is exceptionally accessible here at around 25 metres, specifically confirmed at the South East Rock dive area. Other confirmed species at the site include Galapagos sharks (aggregations of 60 or more have been reported during blue-water safety stops), large schools of silver trevally, violet sweep, amberjack, kingfish, silver drummer, rainbow runners, painted rock lobsters, slipper lobsters, conspicuous angelfish, banded angelfish, black cod, white-spotted chromis, sweetlips, snappers, and occasional sea turtles. Dolphins, marlin, and wahoo have been reported during the boat transit.

## Dive Profile

Dives typically begin at Observatory Rock or South East Rock depending on conditions. At Observatory Rock, divers explore among the dramatic rock stacks in 10–20 metres, with shelter from prevailing current. At South East Rock, the wall descends from the ledges at 5 metres to around 25–30 metres. Dives conclude with a blue-water ascent, during which Galapagos sharks frequently congregate around the group. Careful current awareness and air management are essential. The site is recommended for Advanced-certified divers with current experience.
