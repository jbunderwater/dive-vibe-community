## Comets Hole

Comets Hole is Lord Howe Island's "house reef" — a shallow, freshwater-formed lagoon hole surrounded by a 3–4 metre high coral reef wall, consistently rated one of the best beginner dives on the island for its sheer abundance of marine life.

## Overview

Comets Hole formed from natural freshwater upwelling, which prevents the sandy base from silting up and maintains exceptional water clarity. The white sandy bottom reflects light upward, illuminating the surrounding coral wall and making it one of the best photography sites on the island. It sits within the sheltered lagoon just minutes from the boatshed, diveable in virtually all weather conditions. The site is frequently described as offering "a new species per minute" — a compact site with remarkable density of marine life. Maximum depth is 7 metres and the site is accessible by shore entry.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Lord Howe Island lagoon (shore entry; minutes from boatshed)
- **Entry Type**: Shore entry
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 7 meters
- **Coral Wall**: 3–4 metre high coral reef wall surrounding the hole

## Marine Life

Comets Hole supports an exceptionally well-documented species list. Confirmed sightings at this site include: endemic McCulloch's Anemonefish, large schools of lined catfish, decorator crabs, coral gobies, white-mouthed moray, slipper crayfish, trevally, painted morwong, silver drummer, three-striped butterflyfish, three species of lionfish, the Lord Howe Island moray eel, marble shrimp, zebra morwong, Double Header Wrasse, Lord Howe Butterflyfish, black stingrays, kingfish, sweetlips, stripeys, footballers, Peacock Flounder, and Galapagos Whaler sharks along the reef edge. Bull rays and long-tailed whip rays also cruise the reef perimeter. Diligent naturalists occasionally find pigmy seahorses and rare hawkfish.

## Dive Profile

The site features a natural hole of approximately 7 metres maximum depth, surrounded by a continuous coral wall 3–4 metres high. The freshwater upwelling keeps the sandy base clear year-round. Divers typically enter from shore, swim to the hole, and spend most of the dive circling the coral wall at various depths. The white sand base and shallow depth mean excellent ambient light even on overcast days. Extended bottom times are standard here. Suitable as a first dive and as a reliable foul-weather alternative.
