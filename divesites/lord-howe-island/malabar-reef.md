## Malabar Reef

Malabar Reef is a multi-depth fringing reef on the northern face of Lord Howe Island, known for macro photography, nudibranchs, pelagic fish schools, and swim-throughs at 10–21 metres.

## Overview

Malabar sits on the northern face of Lord Howe Island and forms part of the island's fringing reef system. The site presents a series of hard coral reef structures with large anemones at the top and deeper areas of bryozoa, swim-throughs, and archways. At 18 metres the reef is considered one of the best dives on the island for photography. The site is accessible in most conditions and typical visibility outside the lagoon reaches 20–40 metres in summer.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Northern face of Lord Howe Island (fringing reef)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 18 meters
- **Depth Range**: 10 metres at reef top to 21 metres on sand

## Marine Life

Confirmed species at Malabar include nudibranchs, Spanish dancers, large varieties of anemones with clown fish, schools of trevally, kingfish, silver sweetlips, dotted sweetlip, and small Galapagos sharks. Crayfish are found in and around the reef. The site is noted as a strong macro photography location due to the diversity of nudibranch species found here.

## Dive Profile

The reef top begins at around 10 metres with large anemone colonies. Working deeper, the reef transitions through hard coral structures with swim-throughs and archways. Maximum depth is approximately 18 metres before reaching sandy bottom at 21 metres. The 18-metre depth zone offers the best photography opportunities. Most marine life activity is concentrated between 10 and 18 metres.
