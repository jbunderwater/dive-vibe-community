## Malabar Reef

Malabar Reef is a northern-face reef system at Lord Howe Island, featuring overhangs, coral walls, reef fingers, and sandy zones from 10 to 21 metres — part of a broader reef network that also includes Malabar West and The Landslide.

## Overview

Malabar sits on the northern face of Lord Howe Island and is protected in southerly winds and swell. The site is part of a reef system that runs west toward Fishy Point, with multiple discrete dive sites within the area: Malabar, Malabar West, The Landslide, and Malabar Third Reef. The main Malabar site features overhangs, coral walls, and fingers of reef from 10 to 21 metres. The deeper sections at around 18–21 metres on sand are considered the most productive for photography and marine life encounters. Depth has been corrected to 21 metres based on multiple sources (previously listed as 18 metres in error).

## Site Information

- **Location**: Northern face of Lord Howe Island; 10–20 minute boat ride from the boatshed
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 21 meters
- **Protected in**: Southerly winds and swell

## Marine Life

Confirmed species at Malabar include reef sharks, Butterflyfish, Parrotfish, and Turtles (Howea Divers). The broader Malabar reef system hosts Crayfish, Spanish Dancers, Shrimp, Anemonefish, Nudibranchs, Cardinal Fish, and Eels. Malabar Third Reef (deeper section) features ledges and caves with Morays, Spiny Lobsters, Slipper Lobsters, Nudibranchs, and gatherings of Black Blotched Stingrays. Malabar West adds Cardinalfish, Bullseyes, Soldierfish, Lionfish, Octopus, and Spiny Lobsters.

## Dive Profile

Diving at Malabar ranges from 10 metres on the reef tops to 21 metres on sandy sections. Begin in the shallower reef areas and work along the overhangs and reef fingers toward depth. The deepest areas near 18–21 metres on sand offer the best photography and macro encounters. The site is sheltered enough for comfortable diving in most conditions when southerly swell is running.
