## Malabar Reef

Malabar Reef is a series of connected reef systems on the northern face of Lord Howe Island, ranging from 10 to 21 metres depth, known for exceptional macro photography and diverse pelagic fish encounters.

## Overview

Malabar is not a single site but a cluster of related reef systems, including Malabar, Malabar West, and Malabar Third Reef (also called Landslide), all running off the same reef structure. The reef consists of hard corals, bryozoa archways, and swim-throughs, with the top of the reef covered in large sea anemones hosting several clownfish species. Overhangs and coral walls characterise the deeper sections. Because of its northern exposure, Malabar offers reliable diving in a range of weather conditions. Depths of around 18–21 metres on the sand are considered the best sections for photography.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Northern face of Lord Howe Island
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef with archways, swim-throughs, and ledges
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 21 metres

## Marine Life

Malabar hosts a productive mix of macro and pelagic species. Confirmed sightings include schools of Trevally, Kingfish, Silver Sweetlips, Dotted Sweetlip, and small Galapagos Sharks in the water column. On the reef: multiple clownfish species in large anemones, nudibranchs, Spanish Dancers, Cardinalfish, Bullseyes, Soldierfish, Lionfish, Octopus, Spiny Lobsters, Crayfish, and Eels. The deeper sections of Malabar Third Reef have produced sightings of 25 or more Black Blotched Stingrays gathered together, as well as Morays and Slipper Lobsters in ledges and caves. Sea Turtles are occasional visitors.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the reef top at approximately 10 metres and follow the slope down to the sand at 21 metres. Swim-throughs and archways punctuate the middle section of the reef. Explore ledges and overhangs at depth for macro species. The deepest area (18–21 m) is consistently rated the best section for photography. Return up the reef slope via the anemone-covered top. No significant current in most conditions.
