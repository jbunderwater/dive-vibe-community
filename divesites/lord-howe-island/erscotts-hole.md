## Erscott's Hole

Erscott's Hole is a sheltered lagoon cavern site five minutes by boat from the boatshed, known for consistently excellent visibility and a luxuriant garden of staghorn coral.

## Overview

Erscott's Hole sits within a protected intertidal groyne inside Lord Howe Island's lagoon. The site is diveable in nearly all weather conditions due to the enclosed lagoon environment, making it one of the most reliable dives on the island. The hole features large stands of staghorn *Acropora* coral and dozens of other soft and hard coral species. Maximum depth is approximately 6–8 metres, and the shallow clear water makes it excellent for underwater photography. The tame fish population — conditioned to regular visits from glass-bottom boats — allows very close approaches for photographers. This is primarily a cavern environment with open overhead light, rather than a fully enclosed cave.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Inside Lord Howe Island lagoon (5-minute boat ride from boatshed)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Cave/cavern
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 8 meters

## Marine Life

Species confirmed at Erscott's Hole include Double Header Wrasse (a common sighting), Bluefish (noted for their ability to shift colour from dark green to sky blue), Spangled Emperors, and Neon Damsels. Rare finds reported at this site include Coleman's Pygmy Seahorse, Foster's Hawkfish, Marlinspike Auger shells, Spotted Snake Eel, and Beaked Leatherjacket. The site sits within an island ecosystem that supports 500 fish species and 90 coral species, but the species above are those confirmed at this specific location.

## Dive Profile

Maximum depth is 8 metres. Divers enter the cavern and explore the coral formations, which include dominant staghorn *Acropora* stands and a variety of smaller coral species. Extended bottom time is easily achieved at this shallow depth. Navigation is straightforward within the sheltered formation. The site is well suited to photographers who want unhurried time with the resident fish.
