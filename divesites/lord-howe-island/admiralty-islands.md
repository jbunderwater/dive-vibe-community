## Admiralty Islands

The Admiralty Islands are a cluster of volcanic formations approximately 15 minutes north of Lord Howe Island, offering around 25 individually named dive sites ranging from 5 to 35 metres depth across steep walls, boulder-strewn bommies, gently sloping ridges, and hidden caves.

## Overview

The Admiralty Islands group — which includes Sugarloaf, Noddy, Ruperts, Ryans, Roach, No-Name Island, and others — represents the most extensive dive area accessible from Lord Howe Island. Sites vary dramatically in character: Ruperts Reef is a rocky pinnacle dive from 24 m to 12 m; Noddy Reef features sheer walls; No-Name Island has boulder fields and macro critters; and Tenth of June (a sub-site covered separately) drops to 36 m. A surface interval at Ned's Beach for hand-feeding the resident fish is a popular option between double dives here. The Admiralty Islands are exposed to the Coral Sea, which drives soft coral growth and brings pelagic species to the walls.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Approximately 15 minutes north of Lord Howe Island by boat
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Pinnacle / varied terrain (walls, bommies, ridges, caves)
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate (some sites Advanced)
- **Maximum Depth**: 35 metres (varies by specific site)
- **Special Note**: Approximately 25 individually named dive sites across the island group

## Marine Life

The Admiralty Islands host a high diversity of endemic and tropical species. Confirmed site-specific sightings include Double-headed Wrasse (endemic), Lord Howe Island Butterflyfish (endemic), Conspicuous Angelfish, Half-banded Angelfish, Tasselled Scorpionfish (South Island), Splendid Hawkfish, McCulloch's Anemonefish, Cooks Scorpionfish, Painted Morwongs, Moray Eels, Sydney Octopus, Stingrays, Lionfish, and Peacock-tail Anemone Shrimp. Noddy Reef regularly produces Grey Morays, Kingfish, Galapagos Sharks, and Blue Drummers. Nudibranchs and Spanish Dancers are common across the group.

## Dive Profile

Plan for a morning double dive with a Ned's Beach surface interval. Depths vary by site: shallower bommie sites run 5–18 m; wall and pinnacle sites descend to 24–35 m. The terrain shift between sites is dramatic — a gentle reef slope at one site can give way to a sheer wall at the next. Follow the guide's briefing for the specific site you are diving. Mild currents along the exposed walls enhance fish aggregations but require awareness for newer divers.
