# Boulari Pass

A drift dive through the outer barrier reef near Amadee Island with year-round manta ray encounters at a documented cleaning station. Grey and blacktip reef sharks, coral trout, unicornfish, and occasional hammerheads patrol the pass, with visibility that can extend as far as the eye can see.

## Overview

Boulari Pass is a break in the outer barrier reef near Amadee Island, south of Noumea. The site is dived as a drift, riding the tidal current through the pass where nutrient-rich water from the open ocean funnels into the lagoon. Research has documented 851 manta ray sightings at two aggregation sites in Boulari Pass and Dumbea Pass, with an average of 2.3 individuals sighted per day -- these passes serve primarily as cleaning stations, where mantas return regularly rather than seasonally.

The coral plateau inside the pass extends to approximately 13 meters, dropping off steeply on the outer side. The pass is part of the Noumea-Abore Important Shark and Ray Area (ISRA), reflecting the significance of the site for these species. The depth range is broad: from around 6 meters over the reef plateau to 30 meters on the outer wall.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Outer barrier reef near Amadee Island, south of Noumea, New Caledonia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Drift dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters
- **Reef Plateau Depth**: 13 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20-40 meters (exceptional on the outer wall; "as far as the eye can see")
- **Water Temperature**: 22-27 degrees C (around 23 degrees C in October)
- **Current**: Moderate to strong (tidal drift)
- **Best Time**: Year-round for mantas (cleaning station); April-November for best visibility

## Marine Life

Manta rays are documented year-round at the cleaning station -- Boulari Pass is one of two primary manta aggregation sites in the Noumea-Abore marine area, with confirmed daily sightings. Grey reef sharks and blacktip reef sharks are resident. Coral trout, unicornfish, and schools of trevally and barracuda patrol the pass. Hammerhead sharks are occasionally encountered. The diverse coral reef in the pass supports high density of tropical reef fish.

## Dive Profile

Enter the water upstream of the pass and drift with the tidal current over the coral plateau at approximately 13 meters. Watch for mantas circling at the cleaning station, where they may hold position for extended periods. On the outer side, the reef drops steeply to 30 meters -- scan the blue water for pelagics. The drift ends as the pass opens into the lagoon, where the current dissipates and the boat picks up.

## Entry and Exit

Access by dive boat from Noumea -- approximately 40-45 minutes. Entry by giant stride upstream of the pass. Exit by SMB pickup in the lagoon. Alizee Diving at Port du Sud is one operator offering this site.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The mantas come to the cleaning station year-round, but activity is highest when plankton blooms are heaviest
- A wide-angle lens is essential for manta rays and the pass topography
- The cleaning station means mantas may hover nearby for extended periods -- outstanding photography opportunity
- Combine with the nearby La Dieppoise wreck for a full day's diving in the Amadee Island area

## Safety Considerations

The tidal current through the pass can be strong -- this drift dive requires comfort with current diving. Stay with the group and follow the guide's signals. Deploy an SMB well before surfacing for boat pickup.
