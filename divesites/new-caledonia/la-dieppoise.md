# La Dieppoise

A former Bay-class minesweeper (ex-HMCS Chaleur, MCB 144), 46 meters long, deliberately sunk on 19 January 1988 between Boulari Pass and Amadee Island. New Caledonia's best-known wreck, sitting upright at 26 meters with accessible interior spaces including the pilot house, dining room, and officer's quarters.

## Overview

La Dieppoise began life as HMCS Chaleur (MCB 144), a Bay-class minesweeper built by Port Arthur Shipbuilding Co. in Port Arthur, Ontario. Laid down 8 June 1951 and launched 21 June 1952, she was commissioned into the Royal Canadian Navy on 18 June 1954. After just three months of Canadian service, the vessel was transferred to the French Navy on 9 October 1954 and renamed La Dieppoise. She served as a minesweeper until 1973, then transitioned to coastal patrol duties in France's Pacific territories, basing in Noumea from 29 June 1976.

After paying off on 9 July 1987, the 46-meter vessel (152 feet) was deliberately sunk using two explosive charges on 19 January 1988 to create an artificial reef. She settled upright on the sandy bottom north of Tabou Reef in the Noumea lagoon, between Boulari Pass and Amadee Island.

The wooden hull has gradually deteriorated over the decades, opening the interior to exploration. The dining room, officer's quarters, pilot house, and captain's chair are accessible. The wreck is consistently cited as New Caledonia's most popular and accessible wreck dive, suitable for divers with appropriate wreck training.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Between Boulari Pass and Amadee Island, Noumea lagoon, New Caledonia (GPS: 22 deg 29' 10"S, 166 deg 26' 40"E)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 26 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20-30 meters (30-40 meters in cool season April-November)
- **Water Temperature**: 22-27 degrees C
- **Current**: Generally mild; site is sheltered from strong winds
- **Best Time**: Year-round

## Marine Life

The wreck hosts groupers, jacks, perch, trevally schools, coral trout, and colourful nudibranchs. Interior rooms shelter large fish populations, particularly during breeding periods. Eagle rays and turtles are occasional visitors. The aluminum structure and hull surfaces support encrusting coral and sponge growth after more than three decades of colonization.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the wreck and explore the upper structure before working deeper. The interior spaces -- dining room, officer's quarters, pilot house -- are accessible through deteriorated hull sections. The captain's chair and pilot house fittings remain in place. The sand at the base of the hull is at approximately 26 meters. The multi-level depth profile allows you to start deep and work shallower as the dive progresses.

## Entry and Exit

Access by dive boat from Noumea -- approximately 40-45 minutes to the site. Entry by giant stride. The wreck has a permanent mooring.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The pilot house and captain's chair are the signature photography subjects -- arrive early in the dive while air supply is full
- The multi-level depth profile makes this wreck excellent for managing bottom time
- Combine with a dive at nearby Boulari Pass (manta ray cleaning station) or Amadee Island Reef
- The Amédée Diving Club, the first dive operator to open in New Caledonia, operates from Amadee Island area

## Safety Considerations

The interior is accessible but remains an overhead environment. Do not penetrate beyond sight of natural light without wreck diving certification and appropriate equipment. The maximum depth of 26 meters requires proper gas management. Maintain good buoyancy to avoid disturbing silt inside the wreck.
