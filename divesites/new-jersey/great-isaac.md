## Great Isaac

Great Isaac is a historic US Navy tugboat wreck approximately 12 miles off Barnegat Light, New Jersey.

## Overview

The Great Isaac was a V4-M-A1 class ocean-going tugboat built in 1943 for the US Navy, measuring 185 feet in length. She served during the D-Day invasion at Normandy, towing concrete breakwater sections, and her captain received a Bronze Star for meritorious duty under fire. Less than three years later, on the foggy evening of April 16, 1947, the Great Isaac was struck by the Norwegian freighter Bandeirante and sank in 90 feet (27 meters) of water, 12 miles off Barnegat Light. No lives were lost. The wreck now lies on her port side, partially buried in sand, and is consistently ranked among the most popular intermediate-to-advanced wreck dives on the New Jersey coast.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Approximately 12 miles off Barnegat Light, New Jersey
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: ~27 meters (90 feet)

## Wreck History

The Great Isaac (hull #409) was built in 1943 by General Ship of East Boston, Massachusetts, for the United States Navy as a fleet ocean tug (V4-M-A1 class). She measured 185 by 37 feet and displaced 1,117 gross tons with a crew of 27. The Great Isaac participated in the June 1944 Normandy landings, towing floating concrete sections used to construct the artificial breakwaters (Mulberry harbors / gooseberries) that protected the Allied landing beaches. Her captain was awarded a Bronze Star for meritorious duty under fire.

On April 16, 1947, in foggy conditions off the New Jersey coast, the Great Isaac was struck by the Norwegian freighter Bandeirante. The collision gashed the engine room six feet deep. The tug sank in 90 feet of water 12 miles off Barnegat Light. There were no casualties.

## Marine Life

The Great Isaac's hull, encrusted with blue mussels, tubularian hydroids, and anemones, supports large populations of tautog (blackfish) and black sea bass throughout the warmer months. Cunner (bergall) pick through the encrusted surfaces at shallower depths. American lobsters — some notably large — shelter beneath both hull sections and in the gap between the wreck and the sand. The sandy flats around the wreck attract summer flounder (fluke). One longtime diver has reported recovering more than 1,000 artifacts from this site, most now in museums across New Jersey.

## Dive Profile

The Great Isaac lies on her port side in 90 feet (27 meters) of water, partially buried in sand. On good visibility days the bow outline is clearly visible, giving a dramatic ship-silhouette profile. The wreck rises several feet off the bottom at its highest points. A typical dive involves dropping to the wreck structure, working along the hull, and exploring the gap between the hull and the sand before ascending for a safety stop. Bottom time at 90 feet on recreational gas limits dives to approximately 25–30 minutes of productive bottom time.

## Entry and Exit

Charter boats depart from Barnegat Light and Point Pleasant Beach. Atlantic Divers is a noted local operator that regularly runs the Great Isaac. The 12-mile offshore transit takes approximately 30–45 minutes by charter boat depending on vessel and conditions.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Visibility can vary widely; on good-visibility days the overall wreck profile is one of the more photogenic in the area
- Check beneath the hull and in the sand gap for American lobster
- The wreck's D-Day history makes this a historically significant site; artifacts are protected
- Best diving conditions are June through October
- Carry a light for the deeper sections under the hull

## Safety Considerations

- Water temperature ranges from approximately 5°C (41°F) in winter to 21°C (70°F) in late summer; a drysuit or heavy wetsuit is required outside summer
- Depth of 90 feet (27 m) limits bottom time and requires careful gas management
- Partial sand burial and overhanging hull sections create some overhead conditions; do not penetrate without appropriate training
- Currents can be significant at this offshore site — check with the captain before descent
- Visibility is highly variable (3–15 meters typical); be prepared for limited-visibility navigation
