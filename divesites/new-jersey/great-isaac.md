# Great Isaac

A 194-foot intact WWII Navy fleet tug lying on its port side 12 miles off Barnegat Light in 90 feet — one of southern New Jersey's premier wreck dives with extensive penetration opportunities and prolific marine life.

## Overview
The Great Isaac was a V4-M-A1 class ocean-going fleet tug, 194 feet long, built in 1944 at General Ship in Boston, Massachusetts for the United States Navy. Considered one of the largest operating tugs in the world at the time, she served in the final year of WWII and continued in post-war service. On April 16, 1947, the Great Isaac sank off Barnegat Light following a collision with the Norwegian freighter Bandeirante; there were no casualties. She now lies intact on her port side in 90 feet of water, buried to the mid-line in sand, with her starboard side rising approximately 30 feet off the bottom. The intact hull and upright (on-side) orientation make the Great Isaac an excellent wreck for navigation and penetration diving. The exposed ribs of the deteriorating hull plating and the open deck beams provide numerous entry points. On clear days, the entire outline of the bow is visible from a distance, giving the site a classic "ghost ship" appearance.

## Site Information
- **Location**: 12 miles east of Barnegat Light, southern New Jersey (Barnegat chart area)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 27 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–40 feet; offshore conditions often good
- **Current**: Variable; can be present
- **Best Time**: Late spring through fall

## Marine Life
The Great Isaac is teeming with marine life. Large lobsters are found in abundance throughout the wreck — mussels cover the top of the hull providing excellent lobster habitat. Schools of sea bass and blackfish hover around the wreck structure. The boiler and engine room sections attract large eels. Locally the site is regarded as one of the best wreck dives in southern NJ for both marine life and structural integrity.

## Dive Profile
Descend to the deepest accessible part of the hull (buried side) and work up along the exposed starboard side. The bow section provides the most dramatic view — on a good visibility day, the full outline of the 194-foot tug is visible. The deck beam structure and exposed ribs create natural corridors into the interior. Penetration is possible through numerous openings, but the aging structure includes crumbling overhead sections and shifting engine mounts — exercise caution. Multiple dives are needed to fully explore the site.

## Entry and Exit
Charter dive boats depart from Barnegat Light Inlet for the approximately 12-mile run. Dina Dee Scuba is one of the local operators serving this site. The offshore location means conditions should be checked before departure. Ascend on the anchor line and complete a safety stop at 15 feet.

## Tips and Recommendations
- The bow is the signature feature — if visibility allows, position yourself to take in the full ship silhouette
- Penetration is tempting given the wide openings but exercise caution — the overhead is crumbling in sections and the engines are held by corroding mounts
- Mussels on the upper hull indicate prime lobster habitat — look carefully in crevices and under any overhang
- This is one of the best single-wreck dive sites in southern NJ; plan at least two dives to cover it properly
- Local charter operators from Barnegat Light Inlet (including Dina Dee Scuba) know this site well — take their advice on current conditions

## Safety Considerations
Advanced divers only. The 90-foot depth requires proper gas planning and a safety stop. Wreck penetration requires training and a wreck reel — the interior has documented hazards including crumbling overhead sections. Currents can develop offshore. Always carry a DSMB for surface signaling.

## Photography
The Great Isaac is one of the most photogenic wrecks in southern NJ. The exposed rib structure creates natural framing elements for wide-angle compositions. The bow silhouette in good visibility is a classic NJ wreck image. Marine life portraits — lobsters, sea bass, and eel — are excellent given the high population density.
