## Tolten

Tolten is a historic wreck dive in New Jersey Shore, North America.

## Overview

The Tolten was a Chilean cargo freighter torpedoed by the German submarine U-404 on March 13, 1942, while traveling unescorted from Baltimore to New York City in ballast — 32 nautical miles off Barnegat, New Jersey. The ship sank in six minutes. Of her 27 crew members, 26 perished; the sole survivor, fireman Julio Faust Rivera, was rescued 12 hours later by USS Larch. The Tolten holds the grim distinction of being the last named ship sunk during the early U-boat campaign that was publicly reported in newspapers; the War Department ceased announcing ship names after this sinking. The wreck lies at approximately 27–29 meters (90–95 feet) depth and has been wire-dragged since the war to reduce its hazard to navigation.

## Wreck History

The SS Tolten was built at the Aalborg Værft A/S shipyard in Aalborg, Denmark, launched and completed in June 1938. She measured 90.9 meters (298 feet) in length, with a beam of 13.3 meters (44 feet), and was assessed at 1,858 gross register tons. As a Chilean vessel, she was neutral at the time of her sinking. The attack was documented by both Allied records and confirmed by the U-boat commander's post-war admission that in the darkness he could not identify the ship's nationality. The sinking caused diplomatic tension between Chile and Germany, with anti-German demonstrations in Chile following the news.

After the war, the wreck was wire-dragged to reduce its menace to navigation. The bow section rises at least 6 meters (20 feet) off the bottom and remains recognizable; the stern is intact and penetrable. Both Wikipedia and Atlantic Divers independently confirm the ship's identity, sinking date, and U-boat responsibility.

## Marine Life

The Tolten's hydroids and anemone-covered ironwork host abundant lobster populations, particularly along the south edge in and around fallen masts and hull plates — Atlantic Divers notes it as a regular producer of large trophy lobsters, with a documented 26-pound specimen caught near the stern in the late 1980s. Tautog (blackfish) and black sea bass inhabit the wreck year-round. Scallops are sometimes found in the surrounding sand. The ScubaBoard trip report from May 2015 confirmed tautog, sea bass, lobster, and a large monkfish at the site.

## Dive Profile

The wreck lies at approximately 27–29 meters (90–95 feet) with the bow rising to roughly 20 feet off the bottom and the stern at full depth. Typical visibility runs 30–40 feet under good conditions, occasionally reaching 50 feet seasonally, though spring dives have been reported with as little as 10–15 feet on the bottom. Bottom temperatures can drop to 45°F (7°C) even in late spring. Charters from Point Pleasant Beach and Clark's Landing Marina (Point Pleasant) typically depart at 6–7 a.m. for a two-dive day.

## Entry and Exit

All access is by boat charter. Charter operators including Gypsy Blood Divers and DiveSeekers depart from Point Pleasant Beach and Clark's Landing Marina. The site is located approximately 32 nautical miles offshore from Barnegat, making this a half-day or full-day trip. Advanced open water certification is required by most charter operators.

## Safety Considerations

Cold water is the primary hazard — bottom temperatures hover near 45°F (7°C) well into spring, making a 7mm wetsuit or drysuit essential. The wreck has been wire-dragged and some sections are collapsed, creating entanglement hazards inside the hull. Penetration of the stern section requires a light, reel, and proper wreck training. Cold water sharply limits bottom time and can cause cold shock if exposure protection is inadequate.
