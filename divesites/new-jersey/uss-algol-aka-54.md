# USS Algol (AKA-54)

The largest vessel in New Jersey's Artificial Reef Program — a 400-foot intact WWII attack cargo ship sitting upright at the Shark River Reef, with its superstructure starting at 70 feet.

## Overview
The USS Algol (AKA-54) is a Arcturus-class attack cargo ship that served in both World War II and the Korean War. At 400 feet long and ranking among the largest vessels ever intentionally sunk as an artificial reef anywhere in the world, the Algol was purposely scuttled in 1991 at the Shark River Reef off Point Pleasant Beach as part of New Jersey's Artificial Reef Program. The ship sits completely upright on a sandy bottom with its main deck at 110 feet and the top of its three-story superstructure at 70 feet — making it accessible to a range of advanced divers. Multiple dives are needed to properly explore the vessel; the open cargo hatches, removed windows and doors, and accessible interior spaces reward divers with the experience to navigate safely. The Algol is often used for advanced open water training dives.

## Site Information
- **Location**: Shark River Reef, approximately 18 miles east-northeast of Point Pleasant Beach, NJ
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 45 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–30 feet; thermocline typically between 80–100 feet
- **Current**: Variable — can range from slight to very strong; check conditions
- **Best Time**: Summer and early fall for warmest water and best visibility

## Marine Life
The Algol is renowned for the massive mussel beds covering its superstructure, which attract large black sea bass (blackfish/tautog) and other reef fish. Large lobsters inhabit the lower decks and hull. Schools of scup, sea bass, and baitfish swarm around the wreck structure in season. Sand tiger sharks are occasionally spotted in summer.

## Dive Profile
The multi-level nature of the Algol makes it exceptional for decompression or no-decompression multi-level profiles. The top of the superstructure at 70 feet can be explored without going deep; the main deck at 110 feet is the next natural level; and the sandy bottom at 140–145 feet is accessible to advanced technical divers. Penetration of the vast interior is possible through numerous openings but requires proper wreck diving training and navigation skills.

## Entry and Exit
All access is via boat. Charter dive boats operate from Point Pleasant Beach and Manasquan Inlet. The site is approximately 18 miles offshore and takes 45–60 minutes by typical dive boat. Descend and ascend on the anchor or mooring line.

## Tips and Recommendations
- Multiple dives are needed to properly explore this enormous vessel — plan a full dive day
- The superstructure serves as a three-story artificial building — carry a compass and orient before penetrating
- A thermocline is almost always present between 80–100 feet; bring a dive light for the darker depths
- The removed smokestack left a distinctive teardrop-shaped scar useful for navigation on the wreck
- Check current conditions — this site can be challenging in even moderate currents given the vessel's size

## Safety Considerations
Advanced divers only. Currents can be very strong and unpredictable at this offshore location. Wreck penetration requires proper training and a wreck reel. The thermocline can dramatically reduce visibility at depth. Always plan a safety stop at 15 feet.

## Photography
The sheer scale of the Algol rewards wide-angle photography. The mussel-covered superstructure offers dramatic close-focus wide-angle compositions. Schools of blackfish and sea bass provide excellent fish portrait opportunities. Bring powerful strobes for the deeper sections.

## Nearby Sites
Other sites at the Shark River Reef complex include smaller vessels and structures near the Algol. Sea Girt Reef is another nearby artificial reef site.
