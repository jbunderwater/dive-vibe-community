## Texas Tower #4

Texas Tower #4 is a historic wreck dive in New Jersey Shore, North America.

## Overview

Texas Tower #4 was one of three offshore radar platforms built in the late 1950s as part of the Atlantic early-warning network, collapsing in a January 1961 storm that killed all 28 men aboard — the debris field now spreads across the sandy bottom at approximately 56 meters (185 feet), about 66 miles from Manasquan and Barnegat Inlets in New Jersey. The scattered wreckage of the massive steel platform, including collapsed legs, deck sections, and radar equipment, forms an enormous artificial reef colonized by encrusting sponges, and dense fish life. The depth and distance from shore make this exclusively a technical diving destination, requiring careful gas planning and decompression.

## Wreck History

Texas Tower #4 was a United States Air Force general surveillance radar station, one of three "Texas Tower" platforms placed off the Eastern Seaboard to extend contiguous radar coverage 300–500 miles seaward — providing an extra 30 minutes of warning against potential Soviet bomber attacks. It was positioned approximately 63 miles south-southeast of Long Island in 56 meters of water, twice the depth of the other two towers built off Cape Cod and Nantucket.

Construction and structural problems plagued the tower from the start. During transport in 1957, a storm tore off two support legs. Hurricane Daisy (1959) forced evacuation and required roughly $500,000 in repairs. Hurricane Donna (September 1960) further weakened the platform's legs, earning it the nickname "Old Shaky" from its crew. By January 1961, the station's commanding officer, Captain Gordon Phelan, repeatedly requested complete evacuation, but the Air Force refused — the Soviets were known to be monitoring the tower, and abandonment would risk capture of its classified radar equipment.

On January 15, 1961, a severe nor'easter with 80 mph winds battered the tower. At 10:30 a.m. an underwater support broke loose; by 2:00 p.m. cracks had appeared above the waterline; at 4:00 p.m. full evacuation was finally authorized, but helicopters could not fly in the storm. At 6:45 p.m. the station transmitted a distress call: "We're breaking up." Radar contact was lost at approximately 7:20 p.m. All 28 men perished. Sources conflict on the number of bodies recovered: the New England Historical Society reports only one body (Master Sgt. Troy Williams) was recovered after two days of searching, while Wikipedia states two bodies were recovered. A subsequent Senate investigation concluded that human error by engineers, contractors, the Air Force, and the Navy was responsible for the accident.

## Marine Life

The massive debris field acts as a deep-water artificial reef. Sea bass and codfish hold near the bottom among collapsed platform legs and steel wreckage; pollock aggregate in mid-water columns 20–50 feet above the bottom. In the water column above the structure, yellowfin and bluefin tuna are encountered seasonally, and mako and thresher sharks are reported around the site. X-Ray Magazine describes excellent visibility in the blue offshore water near the continental shelf edge.

## Dive Profile

The bottom lies at approximately 56 meters (185 feet), with the debris field rising 9–12 meters off the sand in places. This is a technical decompression dive requiring substantial bottom gas and decompression gas planning. The site sits approximately 66 miles from the nearest New Jersey inlets (Manasquan and Barnegat), resulting in long boat transits — charters typically depart before dawn. Four barges positioned 600 yards away in a U-formation on the east, south, and west sides provide additional structure to explore.

## Entry and Exit

All access is by boat only. The extreme offshore distance (roughly 66 miles from Manasquan Inlet and 12 miles northwest of the Hudson Canyon) means most charter trips are full-day or overnight expeditions. Charters staging from Point Pleasant Beach and Brielle, New Jersey service this site. Conditions at this depth and distance can deteriorate rapidly; charter operators monitor weather and sea state closely and cancel when necessary.

## Safety Considerations

This is one of the most demanding wreck dives in the northeastern United States. Depth alone (56 m) exceeds recreational limits and requires technical certification with trimix or equivalent experience. Long decompression obligations in cold water (bottom temperatures can be near 5°C / 40°F year-round at depth) create significant exposure risk — a drysuit is essential. The extreme offshore location means emergencies cannot be resolved quickly. Only divers with documented technical wreck diving experience should attempt this site.
