# Stolt Dagali

The stern of a 583-foot Norwegian tanker sliced off by a collision on Thanksgiving 1964, now lying on its port side 18 miles off Point Pleasant with a wall-like profile covered in colorful anemones and sponges.

## Overview
The Stolt Dagali was a 583-foot, 19,150-ton Norwegian steel-hulled chemical tanker. On the morning of November 26, 1964 — Thanksgiving Day — the vessel collided with the Israeli luxury liner SS Shalom in dense fog approximately 18 miles east of Point Pleasant Beach, New Jersey. The Shalom's bow sliced through the Dagali's port side amidships, shearing her stern completely free. The 145-foot stern section sank immediately, killing 19 of the crew members aboard that section. The remainder of the vessel was eventually towed to port. Today the stern section lies on its port side in 130 feet of water, with the highest point of the hull accessible at 55–65 feet — making this one of the only true multi-level wall dives in New Jersey. The smooth rust-red hull slopes away into the depths like a Caribbean wall dive, draped in hydroids, anemones, and encrusting sponges.

## Marine Life
Lobsters are prolific around the wreck and in the debris field. Large schools of cod and sea bass hover near the structure. Sea turtles, tuna, and occasional sharks have been observed at this site. The anemone and hydroid growth is exceptional — the smooth hull surface is covered in orange, white, and purple encrusting life reminiscent of a warm-water wall.

## Dive Profile
The wall-like profile of the port hull makes this an excellent multi-level dive. Begin at the top of the hull at 55–65 feet and work your way down to 100 feet or deeper as gas and decompression limits allow. The aft superstructure offers large, easily entered interior spaces. The propeller lies half-buried in the sand near the broken rudder — a highlight of any deep dive here. Careful gas management is essential given the depth range.

## Entry and Exit
Charter boats depart primarily from Manasquan Inlet and Point Pleasant Beach. The site is approximately 18 miles offshore — a 45–60 minute run in typical conditions. Anchor or mooring lines provide the descent reference in potentially low-visibility water.

## Tips and Recommendations
- The wreck's port hull acts like a wall — follow it from the shallowest point downward to plan your dive
- The aft superstructure has wide openings suitable for exploration by properly trained divers
- Look for the large propeller and broken rudder lying in the sand at maximum depth
- The site is described as reminiscent of a Caribbean wall dive — unusual and highly recommended for experienced NJ divers
- Large lobsters inhabit crevices throughout the wreck and debris field

## Safety Considerations
Advanced divers only. The maximum depth of 130 feet requires careful gas planning and a planned ascent. Currents can be present. Visibility can change dramatically with tidal cycle. Always carry a delayed surface marker buoy (DSMB) for ascent signaling.

## Photography
The Stolt Dagali offers some of the most visually appealing wreck photography in New Jersey. The anemone-covered hull in wide-angle creates stunning compositions. The propeller and rudder assembly at depth reward patient divers with excellent detail shots. Ambient light reaches the shallower sections of the hull well.
