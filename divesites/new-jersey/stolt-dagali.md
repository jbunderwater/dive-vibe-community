## Stolt Dagali

Stolt Dagali is a Norwegian tanker wreck approximately 18 miles east of Point Pleasant, New Jersey, selected as the NJCDC Memorial Wreck.

## Overview

The Stolt Dagali was a 582-foot (177 m) Norwegian motor tanker, 12,723 gross tons, that was cut in half by the Israeli cruise ship SS Shalom on Thanksgiving night, November 26, 1964, approximately 18 miles off Manasquan Inlet. Nineteen of the tanker's 44 crew members died when the Shalom's bow sliced through the Stolt Dagali's port side in dense fog at around 2:00 a.m., shearing off the stern section, which sank within seconds. The stern section — variously reported as 140 to 145 feet in length across sources — now rests in 130 feet (40 m) of water, with its highest point at approximately 65 feet — one of New Jersey's most dramatic and productive wreck dives. The New Jersey Council of Diving Clubs has designated the Stolt Dagali as its Memorial Wreck.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Approximately 18 miles east of Point Pleasant / Manasquan Inlet, New Jersey
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 40 meters

## Wreck History

The Stolt Dagali was a Norwegian-flagged motor tanker built in 1955 by Burmeister & Wain shipbuilders in Denmark. On Thanksgiving night 1964, she was transporting vegetable and coconut oil from Philadelphia to Newark, New Jersey, when she encountered the outbound SS Shalom — a 629-foot (192 m), 25,320-GRT Israeli passenger liner carrying 616 passengers bound for the Caribbean — in thick fog approximately 50 miles from New York Harbor.

The Shalom, traveling rapidly through the fog with a malfunctioning radar, struck the Stolt Dagali's port side at approximately 2:00 a.m., slicing the tanker cleanly in half. The 145-foot stern section sank immediately, killing 19 crew members who were asleep in the stern quarters. The bow section remained afloat due to intact watertight compartments and was towed to New York; it was later fitted with a new stern and continued in service. The maritime inquiry found both vessels at fault, with the majority of blame on the Shalom for failure to maintain a proper lookout and for operating with a malfunctioning radar.

## Marine Life

Decades of marine growth have converted the stern section into a high-profile reef that is heavily encrusted with sea anemones, hydroids, encrusting sponges, and blue mussels. Large schools of black sea bass, tautog (blackfish), pollock, and ling congregate around and above the structure. Monkfish (goosefish) are commonly reported resting on the seafloor around the wreck. American lobsters shelter beneath the stern overhang and in the cut end of the hull. Cunner are present throughout the mussel-covered surfaces at intermediate depths.

## Dive Profile

The site offers genuine multi-level diving. The highest point of the stern section rises to approximately 65 feet, allowing a bottom-phase exploration before dropping deeper. The main deck and hull structures run from roughly 75 feet down to the sand at 130 feet. The open cut end of the hull — where the Shalom's bow severed the vessel — creates a dramatic penetration opportunity and access into the engine room section; penetration requires proper overhead environment training and redundant lighting and navigation equipment. Visibility typically ranges 30–50 feet. The 65-foot top of the wreck can be explored by intermediate divers, but the sand at 130 feet and any penetration is Advanced territory.

## Entry and Exit

Charter boats depart from Manasquan Inlet and Point Pleasant Beach. Operators including Scubadelphia (running the Gypsy Blood from Point Pleasant Beach) offer regular charters to the Stolt. The 18-mile transit makes this a comfortable half-day run in good weather. Multiple dive-charter operators in the central NJ area service this site regularly.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The wreck is best dived May through September when water temperature and visibility are most favorable
- The open cut end of the hull is one of the most dramatic penetration opportunities on any NJ wreck, but requires proper technical overhead training
- Monkfish (goosefish) are a reliable find — look on the sand around the base of the wreck
- At 130 feet on air, bottom time is significantly limited; plan gas carefully and allow for a safety stop
- The NJCDC Memorial Wreck designation means the site holds special significance — artifacts should be left in place

## Safety Considerations

- Maximum depth of 130 feet (40 m) on air: significant narcosis risk, limited bottom time, mandatory safety stop required
- Water temperature is cold, typically 10–20°C at diving depths in season; a 7mm wetsuit or drysuit is recommended
- Currents can run at depth even when the surface is calm; confirm conditions before descent
- Penetration of the cut stern section is an overhead environment requiring proper training — this is not a recreational no-ceiling penetration
- Water temperature during the collision rescue operations was recorded at 3–5°C; winter/early spring diving requires drysuit
