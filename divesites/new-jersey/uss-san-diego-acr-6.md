## USS San Diego (ACR-6)

USS San Diego (ACR-6) is a historic wreck dive in New Jersey Shore, North America.

## Overview

The USS San Diego was a 504-foot armored cruiser that sank on July 19, 1918, after striking a mine laid by German submarine U-156, approximately 13.5 miles south of Patchogue off Fire Island, New York — the only major United States Navy warship lost in the Atlantic during World War I. She sank in approximately 28 minutes with the loss of six crew from a complement of 1,183. She lies inverted on a sandy seabed at 33 meters (110 feet), with the highest sections — her keel — at approximately 20 meters (66 feet) below the surface. Listed on the National Register of Historic Places since February 17, 1998, she is one of the most popular wreck dives in the northeastern United States and has earned the nickname "Lobster Hotel" for her abundant crustacean life.

## Wreck History

USS California (later renamed USS San Diego) was laid down on May 7, 1902, launched April 28, 1904, and commissioned August 1, 1907. She was renamed USS San Diego on September 1, 1914. The ship displaced 13,680 long tons standard (15,138 full load), measured 503 feet 11 inches in length with a beam of 69 feet 6 inches, and was classified as an armored cruiser (ACR-6).

On July 19, 1918, bound from Portsmouth, New Hampshire to New York City, San Diego struck a mine at 11:05 a.m. adjacent to the port engine room. German submarine U-156 had laid the minefield in the area. The explosion caused rapid flooding; the ship capsized and sank in approximately 28 minutes. Captain Harley H. Christy's order to abandon ship 15 minutes after the initial explosion saved all but six of the 1,183-man crew. Six men were lost: two killed by the initial explosion, one trapped in the crow's nest, one struck by a falling smokestack, and one crushed by a falling life raft.

A 2018 U.S. Navy investigation, conducted by Mobile Diving and Salvage Unit 2 through four site visits and dive operations since 2016, confirmed that San Diego was definitively sunk by a mine deployed by U-156 — ruling out earlier theories of torpedo attack or sabotage. The investigation was reported by USNI News in December 2018. The wreck is listed on the National Register of Historic Places (added February 17, 1998) at coordinates 40°33′N 73°00′W (approximately 40.550°N, 73.008°W), confirmed by Wikipedia and Shipwreck World GPS records. Note: the wreck lies in New York waters, off Fire Island; njscuba.net lists it under "New York Dive Sites," though it is commonly accessed from NJ charter boats.

## Marine Life

The inverted hull has hosted marine life for over a century. The wreck is widely nicknamed "Lobster Hotel" — PADI and War History Online both document the abundant lobster community inhabiting the structure. PADI's dive site listing also identifies sand tiger sharks among the marine life, along with anemones and fish sheltering in the wreck's crevices. X-Ray Magazine confirms large guns visible protruding from beneath the inverted hull structure, indicating the superstructure remains partially intact under the hull.

## Dive Profile

The keel sits at approximately 20 meters (66 feet) and the deepest points — where hull, deck, and superstructure meet the sand — reach approximately 33–34 meters (110 feet). The inverted orientation means divers navigate along the keel at the shallowest depth before descending to explore the wash-out areas at the bow and stern, where the maximum depth is reached. The wreck is enormous (504 feet) and takes multiple dives to cover fully. Visibility and conditions vary with season and tidal cycle.

## Entry and Exit

All access is by boat charter. The wreck lies approximately 13.5 miles south of Patchogue, New York, and is serviced by charter boats departing from Fire Island area marinas and from Long Island and New Jersey ports. The site is well-charted by the Navy and well-known to local operators. PADI recommends Advanced Open Water, Deep Diver, and Wreck Diver certifications; technical configurations (Tec 40/45/50) are appropriate for penetration diving under the hull structure.

## Safety Considerations

The inverted orientation creates overhead-environment conditions throughout — divers exploring beneath the hull edges are in a true overhead environment requiring appropriate training, lighting, and gas reserves. Cold North Atlantic water (typical temperatures 5–13°C / 40–55°F) demands a 7mm wetsuit or drysuit. The hull sections are beginning to collapse after more than a century underwater, creating risk of shifting structure. PADI's equipment recommendations include a knife, torch, and SMB as minimum equipment. More scuba divers have died at this wreck over the decades than crew members were lost in the original sinking — treat it with respect and plan conservatively.
