# USS San Diego (ACR-6)

The only major warship lost by the United States in World War I — a 503-foot armored cruiser sitting upside-down in 110 feet of water off Fire Island, regarded as one of the most popular wreck dives in the Northeast.

## Overview
The USS San Diego (ACR-6), previously named USS California, was an armored cruiser displacing 13,680 tons and measuring 503 x 70 feet. On July 19, 1918, while bound from Portsmouth, New Hampshire, to New York, the vessel struck what is officially recorded as a torpedo from German U-boat U-156 southeast of Fire Island, New York, though many historians believe a mine was responsible. She sank in just 28 minutes — the only major US warship lost during World War I. Though official records list six dead, revised estimates suggest 30–40 casualties. The San Diego lies upside-down on the seabed at 110 feet, with its highest point (the keel) at 65 feet. The inverted orientation creates dramatic swim-through passages along the hull and access to the superstructure-turned-cavern below. Six divers have died at this site, primarily from penetration accidents. Despite its location off Fire Island, New York, the USS San Diego is regularly served by NJ-based charter boats and falls within the broader Wreck Valley diving area.

## Site Information
- **Location**: Southeast of Fire Island, New York (accessible from NJ and NY inlets)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 33 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–30 feet
- **Current**: Can be strong; tidal currents at this offshore location
- **Best Time**: Late spring through early fall

## Marine Life
The inverted hull creates a vast overhead environment colonized by encrusting sponges, anemones, and hydroids. Large schools of sea bass and blackfish hover around the exposed bilge keels and hull plating. Lobsters inhabit the crevices where the superstructure meets the seabed. Sand tiger sharks are seasonally present in summer.

## Dive Profile
The keel at 65 feet is the natural starting point for exploration. Divers can follow the hull plating downward on either side to the sand at 110 feet. The underside of the original superstructure forms an inverted landscape that experienced wreck divers can explore. Penetration into the interior requires specific wreck training and experience — the silting corridors can reduce visibility to zero rapidly.

## Entry and Exit
Charter boats from both New Jersey (Point Pleasant, Manasquan) and New York (various Long Island inlets) service this wreck. The site is marked with mooring buoys during diving season. Descend on the line and ascend on the line or your own DSMB.

## Tips and Recommendations
- The inverted orientation is disorienting — take time to establish visual references before penetrating
- Wreck penetration here is particularly hazardous due to silt and corridor collapse risks
- The site is very popular — expect other divers and possible traffic on the line
- Carry a dive light even for exterior exploration; the overhanging hull creates significant shadow
- The bilge keels and external hull fittings are photogenic exterior features that don't require penetration

## Safety Considerations
Advanced divers only. Six fatalities at this site, primarily from penetration diving. Interior penetration requires specialized wreck training and redundant light systems. Currents can be strong and changeable. The depth of 110 feet limits bottom time significantly on air. The inverted orientation makes emergency exits from the interior extremely difficult without proper training.

## Photography
The San Diego is a premier photography wreck. The massive curved hull provides dramatic wide-angle compositions. External features including the prop shafts, hull plating, and bilge keels contrast beautifully against the sandy bottom. With good visibility, the keel rising from the murk is a compelling image. Interior photographers need serious lighting systems and wreck experience.
