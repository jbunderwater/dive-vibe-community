## Andrea Doria

Andrea Doria is a historic wreck dive listed under New Jersey Shore, though the wreck itself lies off Nantucket, Massachusetts — not in New Jersey waters.

## Overview

**GEOGRAPHY NOTE: This entry is mislabeled.** The SS Andrea Doria wreck lies at approximately 40°29'N 69°51'W — roughly 160 miles east-northeast of New Jersey and approximately 45 miles south of Nantucket Island, Massachusetts. The coordinates in this record (40.2958, -69.8508) correctly place the site off Nantucket, well outside New Jersey's territorial waters. This site should be recategorized under a Massachusetts or open-Atlantic destination, or removed from the New Jersey dataset.

The Andrea Doria was a 697-foot (212 m) Italian ocean liner operated by the Italia Line. On the night of July 25, 1956, in thick Atlantic fog approximately 45 miles south of Nantucket, she collided with the Swedish liner MS Stockholm. The Andrea Doria sank the following morning on July 26, 1956, with the loss of 46 lives. The Stockholm remained afloat and returned to New York under her own power. The wreck rests on her starboard side in approximately 73–76 meters (240–250 feet) of cold Atlantic water, with the top of the wreck at roughly 50 meters.

The site is widely known as the "Mount Everest of wreck diving" due to the extreme depth, cold thermoclines, strong and unpredictable currents, significant narcosis risk, and extensive overhead environment. More than a dozen divers have died on the wreck. Access is exclusively by long-range charter from ports such as New Bedford, Massachusetts, with multi-day expeditions required. The dive is realistically limited to technical divers using mixed gas.

## Wreck History

The SS Andrea Doria was built at the Ansaldo shipyard in Genoa, Italy, and launched in 1951. She entered service for the Italia Line in January 1953 on the Genoa–New York route. At 29,100 gross tons and 697 feet (212 m) in length, she was considered one of Italy's most technically advanced and artistically appointed ocean liners of her era.

On July 25, 1956, the Andrea Doria was westbound approaching New York in dense fog. The Stockholm, eastbound out of New York, was navigating in clear weather but at reduced radar awareness. At 11:10 p.m., the Stockholm's bow struck the Andrea Doria on her starboard side, tearing a 40-foot gash below the bridge. The Andrea Doria listed immediately to starboard, making half her lifeboats unusable. She sank at 10:09 a.m. on July 26, 1956. Of the 1,706 persons aboard, 46 died — all in the collision itself. The subsequent rescue operation, involving multiple vessels including the Île de France, saved over 1,660 people.

The collision inquiry apportioned blame to both vessels, and both shipping companies settled out of court.

## Safety Considerations

- This is a technical dive requiring advanced certification (trimix recommended) and significant wreck diving experience
- Depth of 73+ meters creates severe narcosis risk on air and significantly limits bottom time on any gas mix
- Strong and unpredictable currents, thermoclines, and long decompression obligations make every dive a serious undertaking
- The wreck structure is progressively deteriorating and collapsing; penetration requires proper overhead training and redundant equipment
- Water temperatures are cold (typically 5–10°C at depth) requiring drysuit
- Long surface transit (multiple hours each way) required; weather window reliability is poor
- This site has claimed the lives of more than a dozen divers — it should not be attempted without extensive technical diving experience
