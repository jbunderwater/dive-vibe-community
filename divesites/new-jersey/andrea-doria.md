# Andrea Doria

Nicknamed "the Mount Everest of wreck diving" — a 700-foot Italian luxury liner lying at 240 feet, 100 miles offshore, in cold, dark water with strong currents and a rapidly deteriorating hull.

## Overview
The SS Andrea Doria was a 29,083-ton Italian luxury liner, 700 feet long, launched in 1951 and considered one of the finest ships of the era. On July 25, 1956, she collided with the Swedish passenger ship Stockholm approximately 100 miles south of Nantucket in fog. The Doria sank the following morning with the loss of 46 lives. She now lies at 240 feet of water — technically the maximum depth accessible to advanced recreational divers using enriched air, and realistically a site almost exclusively dived by technical divers using trimix or heliox. The top of the wreck is at approximately 190 feet and is rapidly deteriorating. What was once the most recovered wreck in history has seen its passageways collapse, its famous "Gimbel's Hole" entry close off, and its china-room and promenade deck areas deteriorate beyond recognition. More than 22 divers have died at this site — more than any other recreational dive site. The site is organized into the NJ/NY offshore "Deep Sea" chart by the local diving community, with liveaboard charters departing from NJ and NY ports for the long overnight run.

## Site Information
- **Location**: Approximately 100 miles south-southeast of Nantucket, off the continental shelf
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 73 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 10–40 feet; highly variable — strong currents stir sediment
- **Current**: Strong to extreme; longshore and tidal currents are a constant hazard
- **Best Time**: July–August only (brief weather window; even then, conditions are often prohibitive)

## Marine Life
Despite the depth and conditions, the Andrea Doria hosts abundant marine life. Large schools of silver hake, pollock, and other pelagics circle the wreck. Massive sea bass and blackfish inhabit the deeper structural areas. The wreck has been observed with shark activity and large lobsters throughout. Blue sharks and occasional mako sharks are seen at the surface during the long offshore transits.

## Dive Profile
Only technical divers using trimix or heliox should attempt this site. A typical dive involves descent to the 190-foot top of the wreck, limited bottom time (10–20 minutes at depth before mandatory decompression), and a long ascent with multiple staged decompression stops. Bottom temperatures are 40–48°F requiring a dry suit. Gas management is critical — multiple stage bottles are standard practice. Penetration of the wreck is extremely hazardous given the ongoing structural collapse and silt conditions.

## Entry and Exit
Liveaboard charters depart from New Jersey and New York ports for the 8–10 hour transit to the site. Seasonal operations run July through August in most years. The long transit means any rescue or evacuation is many hours from shore — conservative diving is essential. Mooring or anchor descent lines are used; strong currents can make surfacing without a line dangerous.

## Tips and Recommendations
- Only attempt this dive with full technical training (trimix/heliox), multiple stage bottles, and extensive deep wreck experience
- The wreck's condition deteriorates each year — structures that were accessible five years ago may be collapsed now
- Connect with the NJ technical diving community (njscuba.net forums, ScubaBoard) for current condition reports before planning a trip
- The overnight liveaboard transit is itself an experience — blue sharks at the surface during the crossing are common
- Budget for proper gear: dry suit, wing/backplate, stage bottles, and redundant navigation equipment are all standard

## Safety Considerations
This site has the highest diver fatality count of any recreational or sport wreck dive site in the world. Extreme depth, mandatory decompression, strong currents, cold temperatures, poor visibility, and collapse hazards combine to create a genuinely life-threatening environment. Only the most experienced technical divers should consider this site — and even then, conditions frequently force dive cancellations.

## Photography
The Andrea Doria is an iconic photography subject, though the deteriorating condition limits opportunities that existed in the 1980s and 1990s. Wide-angle compositions of the porthole rows, collapsed superstructure, and massive hull plates are the primary subjects. Ambient light is minimal at 240 feet — powerful strobes are essential. The china and artifacts occasionally seen in the debris field are off-limits under US cultural heritage regulations.

## Nearby Sites
The Andrea Doria is isolated from other dive sites. The closest diving in the broader region is the Block Island/Rhode Island area (topside) or the various NJ offshore wrecks accessible at more modest depths.
