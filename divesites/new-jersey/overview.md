## New Jersey
**Country:** United States
**Region:** US East Coast

## Description
New Jersey offers some of the most historically rich and challenging wreck diving in the world. With an estimated 4,000–7,000 shipwrecks off its coast spanning centuries of maritime history, New Jersey's offshore waters — collectively known as "Wreck Valley" — attract experienced divers from across the globe. Sites range from WWI and WWII warships to torpedoed cargo vessels, collided ocean liners, and intentionally sunk artificial reefs. Cold water, strong currents, and limited visibility make this a destination for advanced and technical divers.

### Diving Opportunities
- **Wreck Diving:** The primary draw — hundreds of documented wrecks across the continental shelf, from inshore to 60+ miles offshore
- **Artificial Reefs:** New Jersey maintains 14 official artificial reef sites along the coast, many holding multiple vessels, subway cars, and army tanks
- **Deep Technical Diving:** Sites like U-869 (240 ft) and Andrea Doria (240 ft) attract technical divers using mixed gas rigs
- **Marine Life:** Wrecks serve as productive habitat for large lobsters, blackfish, cod, sea bass, and seasonal pelagics including sand tiger sharks and sea turtles

### Environmental Conditions
- **Water Temperature:** 35–45°F (2–7°C) in winter; 60–72°F (16–22°C) in summer at the surface; significantly colder at depth year-round
- **Visibility:** Typically 15–25 feet (5–8m); occasionally reaches 50 feet on excellent days at offshore sites
- **Currents:** Variable; can be strong at offshore wrecks — always check conditions before diving
- **Required Gear:** Dry suit or 7mm wetsuit strongly recommended year-round; dry suit required in winter
- **Minimum Certification:** Advanced Open Water for most offshore wrecks; technical certification for deep sites

### Accessibility
- **Getting There:** New Jersey dive boats depart from inlets including Point Pleasant, Manasquan, Barnegat Light, and Cape May
- **Season:** Year-round, though the most comfortable diving is May–October
- **Charter Boats:** Numerous dive charter operations serve the region, including Atlantic Divers and local operators

### Marine Life & Environment
- **Fish:** Large schools of blackfish (tautog), sea bass, porgies, cod, pollock, and ling populate most wrecks
- **Invertebrates:** Lobsters (some very large), mussels, anemones, hydroids, and encrusting sponges cover the wreck structures
- **Seasonal Visitors:** Sand tiger sharks in summer, sea turtles, occasional tuna and bluefish

## Additional Information
- **Best Time to Visit:** June–September for warmest water; May and October for good visibility with lighter boat traffic
- **Safety Considerations:** Cold water, strong currents, depth, and distance from shore make NJ wreck diving demanding. Advanced open water minimum; dry suit experience strongly recommended. Never exceed planned depth or bottom time on deep offshore wrecks.
- **Conservation:** Many wrecks are protected as historic sites; artifact removal is prohibited on designated sites including U-869 and USS San Diego
