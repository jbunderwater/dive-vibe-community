# Artificial Reef Site 15

An artificial reef in the Cape May area containing multiple vessel wrecks, subway cars, and army tanks in 50–75 feet of water — an accessible intermediate reef dive off southern New Jersey.

## Overview
New Jersey maintains a network of 14 official artificial reef sites stretching from Sandy Hook to Cape May, each containing multiple structures including vessel wrecks, decommissioned army tanks, tire units, and concrete castings deployed over decades. The coordinates for this entry correspond to the Cape May Artificial Reef area, located approximately 8.5 nautical miles off Cape May in depths of 50–75 feet. The Cape May reef is one of the southernmost and most diverse reef sites in the NJ system, incorporating numerous vessel wrecks including the clam dredge Wyoming, Coast Guard cutter Point Swift, and trawler Miss Beth, plus large concentrations of tire units, army tanks, and — notably — 50 Redbird subway cars submerged in 2003. The subway cars have become one of the most visually distinctive features of the NJ artificial reef system, providing complex habitat that rapidly colonized with mussels, sponges, and fish.

## Site Information
- **Location**: Cape May Artificial Reef area, approximately 8.5 nautical miles offshore of Cape May, NJ
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Coral reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate

- **Typical Visibility**: 15–25 feet; variable with tidal conditions
- **Current**: Moderate tidal currents typical
- **Best Time**: Late spring through fall; summer for warmest water

## Marine Life
Artificial reef sites in NJ are highly productive for marine life. Sea bass, tautog (blackfish), porgies, and flounder are abundant around the structural features. The subway car components, with their windows and door openings, host exceptionally large numbers of smaller reef fish. Large lobsters are found in and around the heavier structural materials. The Cape May area also receives warm-water species pushed north by the Gulf Stream in summer, including spadefish and occasional tropicals.

## Dive Profile
The reef consists of scattered materials rather than a single cohesive structure, so navigation between features is part of the dive. Most interesting features sit at 50–65 feet, with maximum depths around 70–75 feet. Divers can cover multiple structures in a single dive in favorable visibility. The subway cars are a standout feature and should be included in any dive plan.

## Entry and Exit
Charter dive boats depart from Cape May Inlet and other southern NJ ports. Private boaters with appropriate navigation equipment can access the site using published coordinates from the NJDEP Division of Fish and Wildlife. Mooring buoys may be available at some reef sites; otherwise, anchor carefully to avoid damaging reef structures.

## Tips and Recommendations
- The Redbird subway cars are a unique photographic subject — bring a wide-angle lens
- Sea bass populations on NJ artificial reefs are exceptional; this is a popular spearfishing and recreational fishing destination
- Check NJ DEP for current reef coordinates and any seasonal closures or regulations
- Tidal timing affects visibility significantly — dive at slack tide for best conditions
- The reef site covers a large area; communicate with your captain about which specific structures you want to target

## Safety Considerations
Intermediate difficulty. Tidal currents can create challenging conditions between structures. Use a DSMB for open-water ascents away from the boat. The offshore location (8.5 NM) means conditions can change — monitor weather forecasts.

## Photography
The subway cars are the most distinctive photographic subject on this reef type — the Redbird car bodies with their doors and windows create classic artificial reef scenes. Sea bass portraits are easy given the large population. Wide-angle reef overview shots are possible in good visibility. Bring a strobe or video light for the darker interior sections of the subway cars.
