## Escape Reefs

A cluster of rocky reef formations near the Alaska–BC border hosting abundant invertebrate communities in clean Pacific water.

## Overview

Escape Reefs are located in the outer waters near the southern end of the Alaska Inside Passage, close to the British Columbia border and the approaches to Prince Rupert Sound. This cluster of submerged reef structures benefits from strong tidal mixing that brings cold, oxygenated water and a steady supply of nutrients. The reefs are characterized by dense anemone colonies, sea star aggregations, and a fish community dominated by rockfish and lingcod. The name suggests a navigational context, likely indicating shoals that vessels once sought to avoid. For divers, the site represents one of the less-visited options in this productive corner of Southeast Alaska.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Southern Southeast Alaska, near Prince Rupert area
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 23 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 8–18 meters (26–60 feet)
- **Current**: Moderate to strong — slack tide required
- **Water Temperature**: 6–12°C (43–54°F)
- **Best Time**: June–September

## Marine Life

Escape Reefs support the full range of Inside Passage cold-water invertebrate species. Plumose anemones, sea urchins, Dungeness crabs, leather stars, and sunflower stars are all present. Black rockfish school in mid-water above the structure during summer months. Lingcod are common reef residents. Giant Pacific octopus occupy crevices in the deeper reef sections. Occasional harbor seal visits add to the experience.

## Dive Profile

The reef cluster allows divers to explore multiple small structures in one dive. Begin on the current-sheltered side during slack and work around to exposed faces as current subsides. The most productive depth range is 10–20 meters. All divers should remain in buddy contact, as navigating between multiple reef structures can be disorienting.

## Entry and Exit

Charter boat access from Prince Rupert or southern Southeast Alaska operators. No permanent moorings exist at this site. GPS positioning is required. SMB deployment on ascent is essential given the potential for boat traffic in this marine corridor.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Bring a compass and practice reef navigation — multiple structures can cause disorientation
- Winter diving (November–February) often provides dramatically clearer water in this region
- A 7mm hood or dry suit hood is necessary throughout the dive season
- Pre-plan turn pressures conservatively given the remote nature of the site
- Verify your dive operator has local knowledge of the current patterns at this specific cluster

## Safety Considerations

The proximity to the BC border means vessel traffic from both directions can be present. Strong currents can separate buddies quickly on exposed reef faces. Cold water and remoteness demand conservative dive planning and self-sufficient dive teams. Weather can deteriorate rapidly in this exposed outer coastal environment.

## Photography

Multiple reef structures within one dive offer variety for photographers. Anemone colonies in clusters provide textured wide-angle compositions. Macro subjects include nudibranchs, small crustaceans, and colorful sea stars. Video light or strobe is required to reveal the true palette of these cold-water communities.
