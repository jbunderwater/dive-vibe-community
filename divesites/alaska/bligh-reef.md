## Bligh Reef

Bligh Reef is a shallow rocky shoal off Bligh Island in Prince William Sound, best known as the site of the March 24, 1989 Exxon Valdez oil spill — the largest in US history at the time.

## Overview

Bligh Reef is located in northeastern Prince William Sound, off the southeast coast of Bligh Island, approximately 6 miles west of Tatitlek. The reef was named after William Bligh, who served as Master aboard HMS Resolution during James Cook's third voyage before gaining later fame through the Bounty mutiny. The reef has been the site of three known shipwrecks: the Alaska Steamship Company's SS Olympia grounded here in 1910; a tug named Pathfinder ran aground on December 24, 2009, rupturing its tanks and spilling diesel fuel; and most significantly, the Exxon Valdez on March 24, 1989.

At 12:04 AM on March 24, 1989, the oil tanker Exxon Valdez struck Bligh Reef while diverting from its shipping lane to avoid ice. The 987-foot vessel was carrying approximately 53 million gallons of North Slope crude oil. Eight of its eleven cargo holds were ruptured, spilling approximately 11 million gallons (257,000–264,000 barrels, depending on source) into Prince William Sound — at the time the largest oil spill in US history. Following the spill, US law (33 USC § 2733) mandated installation of an automated navigation light on the reef to prevent future collisions.

Bligh Reef's underwater terrain is rocky and irregular, descending to 30 meters in the dive zone. The reef serves as a halibut fishing ground and shrimp harvesting area. As a dive site the reef is rarely dived recreationally — it is remote, exposed to strong currents, and requires a boat from Valdez or Whittier. Its significance as a dive destination is primarily historical rather than biological, though Prince William Sound as a whole supports rich cold-water marine life.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Off Bligh Island, northeastern Prince William Sound (60°50'N, 146°53'W)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Rocky reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 10–40 feet (3–12 meters) in summer; better in winter/spring
- **Current**: Strong — slack tide required
- **Water Temperature**: 6–14°C (43–57°F) summer; colder at depth
- **Best Time**: May–September

## Marine Life

No site-specific dive reports confirming marine life at Bligh Reef itself were found. Prince William Sound waters generally support lingcod, rockfish, halibut, giant Pacific octopus, plumose anemones, and nudibranchs on rocky substrates. The reef is documented as a productive halibut and shrimp ground. Long-term ecological monitoring after the 1989 spill has documented the recovery of the Sound's broader marine ecosystem, but no recreational dive reports from Bligh Reef specifically were found.

## Dive Profile

Dive the rocky reef structure during slack tide. The upper reef zone at 8–18 meters features the most diverse bottom topography. Deeper zones approach 30 meters. Strong tidal currents make careful timing essential — obtain current tables and local guidance before diving.

## Entry and Exit

Access by charter boat from Valdez (the nearest port, approximately 20 miles from the reef) or from Whittier. No regular dive charter services specifically serving Bligh Reef are known — the site requires either a private boat or a custom charter arrangement. The remote location and open-water crossing demand a seaworthy vessel appropriate for Prince William Sound conditions.

## Tips and Recommendations

- This is a historically significant but rarely dived site — expect no infrastructure or established moorings
- Prince William Sound conditions can deteriorate rapidly — build in weather contingency days
- The EVOSTC (Exxon Valdez Oil Spill Trustee Council) has published extensive environmental recovery data for the Sound — useful background reading before visiting
- Dry suit and cold-water regulator are mandatory
- The site is more compelling as a historical experience than as a wildlife-focused dive

## Safety Considerations

Remote open-water reef in Prince William Sound with strong tidal currents and no established dive infrastructure. Emergency response is measured in travel time from Valdez or Tatitlek. Conduct a thorough pre-dive briefing, establish abort conditions, and carry redundant signaling equipment. Always dive with a buddy.

## Photography

The historically significant setting and cold-water rocky reef environment provide distinctive photographic context. Rocky bottom subjects and wide-angle reef topography are the primary underwater subjects. The reef's surface navigation light and surrounding Prince William Sound scenery provide surface-interval photography opportunities.
