# Ocho Rios Reef

A productive north-coast reef system near Jamaica's cruise port town, featuring spur-and-groove formations, small caverns, and an WWII-era minesweeper wreck nearby for a two-tank day.

## Overview

Ocho Rios is Jamaica's second most visited dive destination, offering a range of reef, wall, and wreck diving within easy reach. The reef system at Ocho Rios consists of spur-and-groove formations with sandy channels, small caverns, and overhangs. Named reef sites in the area include Great House Reef, Jewel Reef, Dickies Reef (approximately 60 feet/18m depth), Jacks Hall, and the Jamaica Wall (also called Ocho Rios Wall). The SS Kathryn — a former Canadian WWII-era minesweeper intentionally sunk in 1991 to create an artificial reef — lies nearby at approximately 40–60 feet (12–18 metres) and is Jamaica's most popular wreck dive. The water is warm (26–29°C) and visibility is moderate to good, typically 20–25 metres.

**Wreck note (SS Kathryn):** The vessel was sunk in 1991 as an artificial reef. It is described as a WWII-era Canadian minesweeper of approximately 120–140 feet (37–43 metres) in length — sources report different figures (120 ft per one Ocho Rios dive operator, 140 ft per multiple dive databases). No independent confirmation of its exact original name, build date, or wartime service history was found during research; only that it was a Canadian military minesweeper repurposed as an artificial reef in 1991.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Ocho Rios, Jamaica (north coast)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–25 meters (65–80 feet)
- **Current**: Light
- **Best Time**: December to April (dry season)

## Marine Life

The reef supports a typical Caribbean north-coast community. Parrotfish, blue tangs, barracuda, and schools of snapper and grunts are common on the reef structure. Hawksbill turtles are seen at multiple sites in the area. Nurse sharks rest beneath coral ledges on the deeper reef sections. Moray eels occupy crevices. Stingrays are found on sandy sections between reef ridges. The SS Kathryn wreck hosts stingrays, sergeant majors, nurse sharks, lobsters, eels, and snappers amid colonizing sea fans and barrel sponges. Lionfish have been reported at some sites in the area. No site-specific marine life reports were found for the generic reef area beyond these operator-reported observations.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the reef at 8–12 metres and follow the spur-and-groove formations. Sandy channels between coral ridges provide natural navigation. The deeper sections of the reef reach 18–20 metres at the outer edge. Many operators in Ocho Rios combine a reef dive with the SS Kathryn wreck for a two-tank trip. The wreck sits in 40–60 feet of water with the highest superstructure at approximately 20 feet from the surface, making it accessible to Open Water divers.

## Entry and Exit

Access by dive boat from Ocho Rios operators including ScubaCaribe Ocho Rios and operators associated with Couples Tower Isle resort. Entry via giant stride or back roll. Deploy SMB on ascent for boat pickup.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Combine this site with the SS Kathryn wreck for a diverse two-tank dive day
- The reef is close to Ocho Rios town — dive trips are easily organized from most resorts and cruise ship stops
- Morning dives provide the best light and least boat traffic
- Ask about the Dickies Reef site (60ft) which is popular for barracuda and stingrays

## Safety Considerations

Boat traffic near Ocho Rios can be significant due to cruise ship activity. Always deploy SMB before surfacing. Fire coral is present on some reef structures. Stay within your certification depth limits.

## Photography

The clear water and active fish life make Ocho Rios reef a versatile photography site. Schools of snapper and grunts provide motion subjects. The SS Kathryn wreck offers wide-angle wreck photography with marine growth on the hull superstructure.
