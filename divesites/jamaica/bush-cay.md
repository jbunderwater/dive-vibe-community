# Bush Cay

A small cay-fringing reef on Jamaica's north coast between Montego Bay and Falmouth, with calm, clear water and a productive surrounding reef system.

## Overview

Bush Cay is one of several small offshore cays along Jamaica's north coast that serve as anchor points for healthy reef systems. The cay is surrounded by a fringing reef that ranges from the shallows to 16 metres, with typical Caribbean spur-and-groove formations and sandy channels. The north coast here is sheltered and relatively calm, with visibility regularly exceeding 20 metres. The site is accessible from operators in both Montego Bay (to the west) and Falmouth (to the east). The reef is less frequently visited than the Montego Bay Marine Park sites, making for a quieter dive experience. Water temperature stays between 27–29°C year-round.

## Site Information

- **Location**: North Jamaica coast (between Montego Bay and Falmouth)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Coral reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 16 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–25 meters (65–80 feet)
- **Current**: Light
- **Best Time**: December to April (dry season)

## Marine Life

The cay reef supports a healthy mix of Caribbean reef fish: parrotfish, blue tangs, French angelfish, and French grunts are abundant. Hawksbill turtles are frequently sighted at cay fringing reefs along this coast. Nurse sharks rest on the sandy bottom at the base of the reef. Spotted moray eels and spiny lobsters inhabit crevices. Southern stingrays rest on the sandy channels between coral ridges. The shallower sections of the cay fringe are excellent for snorkeling, with sergeant majors and damselfish abundant in the surge zone.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the reef base at 10–12 metres and circumnavigate the cay reef. Keep the cay visible to one side for orientation. The deeper sections of the reef reach 14–16 metres on the seaward side. Ascend to the reef crest at 5–8 metres for the safety stop before surfacing. The circumnavigation gives a good overview of the diversity around the cay.

## Entry and Exit

Access by dive boat from Montego Bay or Falmouth operators. Entry via giant stride or back roll. Moor on the lee side of the cay to shelter from any prevailing wind. Deploy SMB before surfacing.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Cay reefs are often more productive than open reef because the cay itself provides additional habitat and current breaks
- Snorkeling the cay fringe is a good companion activity if conditions allow
- Combine with other cay sites in the area for a diverse day of diving

## Safety Considerations

The seaward side of the cay may experience more current than the sheltered lee side. Circumnavigate counterclockwise if current is from the east to have the current at your back on the return. Always deploy SMB before surfacing.

## Photography

Cay reefs offer the opportunity to photograph the cay fringe structure from below — wide-angle shots looking up toward the cay and surface create distinctive compositions. Turtle encounters at cay sites are common and make excellent subjects.
