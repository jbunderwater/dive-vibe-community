# Charles L. Brown Wreck

A 100-metre (320–327-foot) cable-laying vessel deliberately sunk on July 25, 2003 as an artificial reef, now lying on its starboard side at 30 metres depth and heavily colonised by corals and sponges home to large resident groupers and moray eels.

## Overview

The Charles L. Brown is a former AT&T cable-laying ship (built 1954 in Naples, Italy as CS Salernum; renamed Charles L. Brown in 1984) deliberately sunk on July 25, 2003 to create an artificial reef. The vessel measures approximately 100 meters (320–327 feet) in length. The ship was purchased by Sint Eustatius for a symbolic $1 USD, cleaned of contaminants, and scuttled by STENAPA. It lies on its starboard side on the sandy bottom at approximately 30 meters (100 feet), with the highest deck structures reaching to around 20 meters below the surface. DAN Alert Diver confirms the wreck lies on its starboard side with a debris field scattered in the sand below. In the more than two decades since sinking, the ship has been extensively colonised by hard and soft corals, tube sponges, and encrusting organisms, transforming it into a fully functioning artificial reef that rivals Statia's natural sites in biodiversity. The wreck is one of Sint Eustatius's headline dive attractions and the largest diveable structure in the island's marine park. Visibility is typically 20–30 meters. Multiple dives are required to fully explore the structure.

## Marine Life

Two decades of colonisation have made the Charles L. Brown exceptional for resident marine life. Large black groupers and tiger groupers have grown to impressive sizes in the absence of fishing pressure within STENAPA's marine park. Green and spotted moray eels occupy the engine room and interior sections. Lionfish (invasive, managed by STENAPA) are present in the deeper sections. Schools of snapper and grunt shelter in the superstructure. Southern stingrays patrol the sand around the hull. Hawksbill turtles rest on the deck structures regularly.

## Dive Profile

Divers descend the mooring line to the highest exposed deck structures at approximately 20 meters and work along the hull toward the bow. The wreck lies on its starboard side, so navigation differs from an upright wreck — the port side faces upward and the starboard side faces the sand. The engine room and interior sections are accessible with a torch. The keel and deepest sand level reach approximately 30 meters. Full wreck coverage requires 2–3 dives.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive with permanent mooring maintained by STENAPA. Access via Golden Rock Dive Center. Entry by giant stride. Maximum depth of 30 meters classifies this as an advanced dive requiring Advanced Open Water certification or equivalent.

## Tips and Recommendations

The Charles L. Brown rewards multiple visits — the 100-meter vessel is impossible to fully explore in a single dive. Because the wreck lies on its starboard side, plan the first dive to orientate yourself to the hull's new geometry before targeting specific areas. A torch is essential for the engine room and interior sections. The wreck photographs best in morning light. Carry a DSMB for the open-water ascent.

## Safety Considerations

Interior sections involve overhead environments — bring adequate lighting and do not penetrate beyond light range without wreck diving training. Maximum depth of approximately 30 meters limits bottom time; plan the dive to allow adequate reserve for a safety stop. Do not disturb the silt in interior sections, which can rapidly reduce visibility to zero.

## Photography

The scale of the vessel makes wide-angle the primary lens choice — fisheye or rectilinear 10–18mm captures hull structures, deck furniture, and winch mechanisms in context. The encrusted bow with a diver in the frame is a classic Statia image. Interior shots require strobe lighting to reveal the colour of encrusting organisms. Close-focus capability for the resident moray eels and groupers.
