# 新田洞窟・にたどうくつ (Niida Cave)

A shallow cave formation in the Kerama Islands offering an accessible overhead environment experience with dramatic interior formations at a surprisingly manageable depth.

## Overview

Niida Cave (新田洞窟, literally "new field cave") is an unusual cave dive in the Kerama Islands context: despite its advanced classification for the overhead environment, its maximum depth of just 15 meters makes it one of the shallowest advanced cave dives accessible from Okinawa. The cave formation is believed to be an uplifted reef cave — once part of a shallow submarine reef that was exposed and then submerged again through geological processes. The interior features a combination of biological colonisation (sponges, tunicates, soft corals) and possible geological formations. The shallow depth allows extended exploration time compared to the deep Miyako cave systems. The advanced rating reflects the overhead environment rather than depth.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Kerama Islands, Okinawa Prefecture, Japan
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Shallow cave
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced (overhead environment despite shallow depth)
- **Maximum Depth**: 15 meters (50 feet)
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–35 meters (65–115 feet)
- **Current**: Variable near the cave entrance; calmer inside
- **Best Time**: October to April; year-round access in calm conditions

## Marine Life

The cave entrance and interior harbour species adapted to the lower light regime. Soldierfish and cardinalfish aggregate in the cave in large numbers. The cave ceiling and walls are covered in encrusting sponges and cup corals in vivid colours. Lobsters occupy deep crevices within the cave system. The exterior reef around the cave entrance has full Kerama reef species diversity, including the reliable turtle encounters that characterise the Kerama Islands throughout. The shallow depth means natural light penetrates sufficiently for photosynthetic species near the entrance zone.

## Dive Profile

The shallow maximum depth is ideal for extended cave exploration with long bottom times — 45–60 minutes is achievable. Explore the cave entrance zone first before proceeding into the interior sections appropriate to your certification level. Natural light is present near the entrance; a torch is required for the interior. Air management follows cave protocol regardless of depth: one-third in, one-third out, one-third reserve. The surrounding reef can be explored before or after the cave portion of the dive.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access from Kerama Islands operators. The site is typically offered as a specialty dive by operators catering to cave-curious intermediate divers wanting a first overhead experience at manageable depth.

## Tips

- The shallow depth makes this an ideal site for developing overhead environment skills before attempting deep cave systems
- A cavern specialty course, if not full cave certification, is the minimum preparation for entering the deeper interior sections
- The colourful encrusting life on the cave walls is best appreciated with a dive torch
- Allow time to explore the exterior reef before and after the cave section — the surrounding Kerama reef is excellent
- The longer bottom time at 15 meters compensates for the cave specialisation: you can explore thoroughly

## Safety

Advanced rating for overhead environment applies regardless of the shallow depth. The same principle holds: you cannot ascend through rock. Maintain orientation to the cave exit throughout. Carry a primary and backup torch. Avoid silt disturbance on the cave floor. Follow cave protocol air management even though the shallow depth creates tempting extended times. Do not exceed your training level in the cave interior.

## Photography

The shallow, well-lit cave entrance is an excellent photography environment. Natural light from the entrance creates the classic cave-interior silhouette effect without requiring powerful artificial lighting. A wide-angle lens captures the entrance light with cave ceiling detail. Interior shots in deeper sections require strobes or video lights to illuminate the encrusting sponge and cup coral colours. The blend of colourful biological encrustation and cave geometry creates unique compositions.
