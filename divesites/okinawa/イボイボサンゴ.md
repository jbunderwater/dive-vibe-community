# イボイボサンゴ (Iboibo Coral / Bumpy Coral)

A distinctive Kerama Islands reef named for its characteristic textured coral formations — knobby, bumpy coral heads that create a unique topography and rich microhabitat.

## Overview

Iboibo Coral (イボイボサンゴ, literally "bumpy-bumpy coral") takes its evocative name from the distinctive textured surface of the dominant coral formations at this site. The reef is characterised by knobby, irregular coral heads — likely species such as Porites and brain corals — whose rough surfaces create abundant microhabitat for the small invertebrates and fish that shelter in the surface irregularities. The Kerama Islands context means this reef benefits from the famously clear water of the region and is protected within the Keramashoto National Park. The combination of textured coral topography and exceptional visibility makes this an outstanding macro diving site alongside its scenic wide-angle reef character.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Kerama Islands, Okinawa Prefecture, Japan
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Coral reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 25–40 meters (80–130 feet)
- **Current**: Moderate; variable by season
- **Best Time**: October to April for best visibility and conditions

## Marine Life

The textured coral surfaces create optimal habitat for cryptic and sessile species. Nudibranchs are abundant throughout the site, with the knobby surfaces providing ideal substrate for various species including blue dragon nudibranchs and Spanish dancer flatworms. Camouflaged species — scorpionfish resting on coral, octopus mimicking the bumpy texture — are regular finds for attentive observers. Longsnose hawkfish perch on sea fan tips and coral prominences. The larger coral heads host schools of juvenile reef fish sheltering in their irregular surfaces. Hawksbill turtles graze on the sponge-encrusted portions of the larger coral structures. Cuttlefish are a common Kerama sighting.

## Dive Profile

Slow, methodical exploration of individual coral heads rewards the attentive diver at this site. Descend to 25–30 meters for the deepest coral formations, then work upward through the mid-reef zone (15–25 meters) before finishing with extended time in the shallower coral tops (5–15 meters) where the densest fish activity occurs. Bottom time of 50–65 minutes is achievable.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access from Kerama Islands operators. The site's proximity to the island cluster means relatively short boat rides from any Kerama base.

## Tips

- Slow down and examine individual coral heads systematically — the macro life on the bumpy surfaces rewards patience
- Look on the sides and undersides of coral heads for hiding scorpionfish and moray eels
- The surface texture is perfect camouflage for cuttlefish resting on coral tops — check before finning over any coral head
- A 60mm or 100mm macro lens is the ideal primary setup at this site
- This site works well as a second dive following a more dramatic topography dive, shifting the focus to detail

## Safety

The Kerama Islands have strong but predictable currents in most conditions. Maintain awareness of the current direction throughout the dive. Contact with the textured coral formations should be strictly avoided — both to protect the coral and because many encrusting animals (fire corals, hydroids) cause skin irritation. Carry and deploy an SMB before ascending.

## Photography

This is primarily a macro site, though the overall reef scenery is beautiful in the "Kerama Blue" visibility. A 100mm macro lens reveals extraordinary detail on the textured coral surfaces — nudibranchs, shrimp, small reef fish in detail. The bumpy coral texture creates natural depth and texture in close-up images. Wide-angle with strobes captures the overall coral head as a landscape feature. Cuttlefish portraits in good Kerama visibility are achievable and highly photogenic.
