# シル東 (Shiru East)

The eastern face of the Shiru reef formation in the Kerama Islands — a current-influenced intermediate site with outstanding visibility and pelagic fish activity on the exposed ocean-facing slope.

## Overview

Shiru East (シル東) refers to the east-facing reef face of the Shiru formation, an underwater reef feature in the Kerama Islands. East-facing reef faces in the Kerama group tend to receive more current than sheltered western sides, creating nutrient-rich water that supports greater biomass. The eastern exposure also means the morning sun illuminates the reef face directly, providing excellent photographic light for the first half of the day. The maximum 30-meter depth allows thorough exploration of the full reef slope. The Kerama context means extraordinary visibility in a nationally protected environment.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Kerama Islands, Okinawa Prefecture, Japan
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Exposed reef slope (ocean-facing)
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters (100 feet)
- **Typical Visibility**: 25–40 meters (80–130 feet)
- **Current**: Moderate; strengthens in tidal phases
- **Best Time**: October to April for optimal visibility; morning dives preferred for light

## Marine Life

The current-influenced eastern face supports larger fish aggregations than sheltered sites. Schooling fish are prominent here — fusiler schools sweep through the water column above the reef. Trevally and jack species hunt in the current line. Sea turtles traverse the eastern reef face consistently. The reef itself supports healthy hard coral coverage with excellent sponge and sea fan communities. Anthias hover above the coral in colourful clusters at 15–25 meters. Reef sharks make occasional visits from the open ocean side.

## Dive Profile

Position upcurrent of the reef and descend to the deeper slope sections (20–30 meters) at the start of the dive when current and NDL are most favourable. Work progressively shallower along the reef face, using the current to carry you northward or southward. The shallower sections at 5–15 meters have the densest reef fish activity. Plan SMB deployment from the reef crest before the water-column ascent.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access from Kerama Islands operators. The eastern exposure means sea conditions should be assessed before committing to entry; if the swell has built from the east overnight, the site may be postponed in favour of sheltered alternatives.

## Tips

- Morning dives on an east-facing reef maximise the natural front-lighting advantage
- Position yourself at the current line edge to observe trevally and jacks hunting in the moving water
- The deeper sections warrant a careful descent plan — check NDL on your computer before dropping below 25 meters
- This site rewards return visits at different tidal states — the wildlife character changes substantially with current intensity
- Pair with Gahi Ura (sheltered side) on the same day for a contrast of exposed vs. protected Kerama diving

## Safety

Current management is the primary skill requirement. If current exceeds your comfort level at the entry position, signal the boat and be repositioned. Ascending in current requires SMB deployment in open water — practice this before the dive. The eastern exposure means sea state can change during a dive; maintain communication signals with your buddy and divemaster.

## Photography

East-facing morning light is excellent for wide-angle photography: the reef face receives direct frontal illumination, strobes are minimal needed, and natural colour saturation is high. Schooling fusiliers in good Kerama visibility make stunning wide-angle images. Trevally and jack hunting behaviour rewards a camera-ready, patient approach at mid-water. Anthias clouds above the coral slope are the most photogenic reef feature here.
