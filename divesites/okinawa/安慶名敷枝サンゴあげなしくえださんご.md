# 安慶名敷枝サンゴ・あげなしくえださんご (Agenashiku Branch Coral)

A Kerama Islands reef site named for the distinctive branching coral formations around Agenashiku Island — a shallow-to-deep coral garden in the heart of the protected national park.

## Overview

Agenashiku Branch Coral (安慶名敷枝サンゴ) takes its name from the branching coral formations associated with Agenashiku-jima (安慶名敷島), an uninhabited islet within the Kerama Islands group. The site name emphasises the branching coral character of the reef — likely referring to the acropora table and branching corals that are characteristic of high-quality Indo-Pacific reef in protected conditions. The Keramashoto National Park protection has allowed these acropora formations to develop in good health. Branching coral sites in the Keramas are particularly rich in the small reef fish species — chromis, anthias, damsels — that shelter in the coral branches, making this a site of exceptional fish diversity.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Agenashiku Island area, Kerama Islands, Okinawa Prefecture, Japan
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Coral reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 25–40 meters (80–130 feet)
- **Current**: Light to moderate; sheltered by island
- **Best Time**: Year-round; branching coral is most vivid in calm conditions

## Marine Life

Branching acropora coral formations are the ecological foundation of this site. These structures provide shelter for thousands of small damselfish and chromis that form dense clouds above each coral head. Longnose filefish and various butterflyfish species pick at the coral polyps. Hawksbill turtles bite sections of branching coral directly as part of their feeding strategy. Anthias (flower basslets) are numerous throughout the branching coral zone. The substrate between coral formations harbours nudibranchs, flatworms, and small crustaceans. The site is among the most fish-dense dives in the Kerama Islands.

## Dive Profile

Move slowly through the branching coral formations, pausing at each major cluster to observe the fish communities sheltering within. The deeper sections (20–30 meters) may have different branching coral species than the shallower zone — table corals are common in deeper current zones. Ascend through the progressively shallower coral gardens where fish density is highest. Avoid touching the branching coral — it breaks easily and recovers slowly.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access from Kerama Islands operators. Agenashiku Island is uninhabited; all access is by boat from Zamami or Aka island bases.

## Tips

- Move extremely slowly through the branching coral to avoid startling the small fish communities into dispersing
- The fish density here rewards extended hovering near a single large coral formation rather than covering distance
- Branching corals are among the most fragile reef structures — maintain strict neutral buoyancy and keep fins raised vertical when near them
- A 60mm macro is productive for the small chromis, damselfish, and various animals living within the branches
- This site and Iboibo Coral (イボイボサンゴ) represent complementary structure types — one smooth and bumpy, one branching — for a complete Kerama reef survey

## Safety

The primary consideration at a branching coral site is accidental coral damage from fins or body contact. Buoyancy skills are a genuine requirement. Current is generally light here, but any surge from swell can move divers unexpectedly — assess surface conditions before entering. Standard SMB deployment before ascending. Contact with fire coral and hydroids that may inhabit the coral framework can cause skin irritation.

## Photography

Dense fish communities above branching coral create spectacular wide-angle images — a fisheye or ultra-wide lens captures thousands of chromis above a single coral head in excellent Kerama visibility. The branching coral texture with associated fish provides natural depth and layering. Macro on the coral surface yields shrimp, gobies, and small nudibranchs that shelter within the branches. The exceptional Kerama Blue clarity makes this a site where natural-light photography often surpasses strobe-lit results for overall atmosphere.
