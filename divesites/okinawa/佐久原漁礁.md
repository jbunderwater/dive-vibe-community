# 佐久原漁礁 (Sakubaru Fishing Reef)

An artificial fish aggregation reef in the Kerama Islands, drawing large schools of fish and resident groupers to a structured underwater structure in clear Okinawan waters.

## Overview

Sakubaru Fishing Reef (佐久原漁礁) is an artificial reef structure placed in the waters of the Kerama Islands to attract and concentrate fish populations — a common fisheries management practice in Japan's subtropical island waters. Over time, these structures develop into thriving artificial reef ecosystems, colonised by corals, sponges, and the full complement of reef species. The Kerama Islands context is significant: this island group southwest of Okinawa main island is within the Kerama National Park and is noted for exceptionally clear water — "Kerama Blue" is a phrase used in Japanese diving circles to describe the extraordinary visibility of these waters, which regularly exceeds 30 meters. Diving in the Keramas provides a very different character from the cave-centric Miyako Islands or the wreck-oriented Okinawa main island.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Kerama Islands, Okinawa Prefecture, Japan
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Artificial reef (fishery structure)
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters (100 feet)
- **Typical Visibility**: 25–40 meters (80–130 feet; "Kerama Blue")
- **Current**: Moderate; can strengthen seasonally
- **Best Time**: October to April for optimal visibility; May humpback whale season adds seasonal interest

## Marine Life

Artificial reef structures in the Kerama Islands attract exceptional fish aggregations. Large schools of fusiliers and snapper circle the structure continuously. Resident groupers — particularly coral grouper and highfin grouper — have established territories within the structure's cavities. Sea turtles are abundant throughout the Kerama Islands and frequently visit artificial structures. Spotted eagle rays have been reported at this site. The structure surfaces are colonised by encrusting sponges, cup corals, and tunicates. Cuttlefish and octopus occupy the darker sections of the structure.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the structure from the mooring and systematically explore its exterior faces. The maximum depth of 30 meters allows thorough exploration with adequate NDL time. Work from the deepest sections upward, spending the most time at the more biologically active mid-structure zones (15–25 meters). The open water above the structure is worth monitoring for passing pelagics and turtles.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access from Zamami-jima or other Kerama Islands bases. The Kerama Islands are accessible from Naha by high-speed ferry (approximately 50 minutes) or by local inter-island boats. Local Kerama operators know the specific positions of the artificial reef structures in their area.

## Tips

- The "Kerama Blue" visibility is best appreciated with wide-angle photography — the exceptional clarity makes compositions clean and vivid
- Visit in late April to early May when humpback whales are occasionally sighted in Kerama waters during their northward migration
- Artificial reef structures require patient hovering to see the full range of inhabitants — don't rush through
- Combine with a natural reef dive in the Kerama Islands on the same day for a complete picture of both environments
- The Kerama Islands offer some of Okinawa's most consistent and reliable turtle sightings — encounters here are very common

## Safety

Standard Okinawan intermediate precautions apply. The Kerama Islands can experience stronger currents during tidal exchanges — check with local operators before diving. The exceptional visibility can be misleading about actual distances; maintain depth awareness. Decompression chamber access requires return to Naha by fast ferry — ensure dive insurance is active.

## Photography

The extraordinary "Kerama Blue" visibility is the defining photographic advantage of this site. Wide-angle images with the artificial reef structure in the foreground and vivid blue water extending into the distance capture the environment's unique character. Turtle portraits in clear, blue-lit water are achievable here — Kerama turtles are experienced with divers. The fish aggregation schools create compelling schooling images with minimal backscatter in the clear water.
