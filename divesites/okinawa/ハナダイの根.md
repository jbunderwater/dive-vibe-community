# ハナダイの根 (Hanada Pinnacle / Anthias Pinnacle)

A coral-capped pinnacle on Miyako Island's outer reef, famous for the dense clouds of anthias (花鯛, hanadai) that swirl around its summit in spectacular concentrations.

## Overview

Hanada Pinnacle (ハナダイの根, "anthias root/pinnacle") takes its name from the Japanese word for anthias — the vibrant, small orange-pink reef fish that form enormous hovering clouds above coral pinnacles throughout the Indo-Pacific. At this site, the pinnacle creates an upwelling effect that concentrates plankton and in turn draws anthias in staggering numbers. The pinnacle rises from deeper water to within 10 meters of the surface and is completely encrusted in hard and soft corals. The Miyako Islands' exceptional water clarity means the anthias clouds are visible as a shimmering orange mass from far above as you descend. This is one of Miyako-jima's most purely beautiful dive experiences.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Miyako-jima, Miyako Islands, Okinawa Prefecture, Japan
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Coral pinnacle
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters (100 feet) at the pinnacle base
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–35 meters (65–115 feet)
- **Current**: Moderate; pinnacle creates local current effects
- **Best Time**: October to April for clearest water and calmest approach

## Marine Life

Anthias (花鯛, hanadai — predominantly Pseudanthias squamipinnis and related species) are the defining species here, present in such concentration that they create a living orange cloud around the pinnacle's upper structure. Below the anthias zone, large-bodied inhabitants include potato grouper and coral grouper perched on the pinnacle surface. Sea turtles traverse the pinnacle regularly. Moorish idols and longnose hawkfish inhabit the upper section. The lower pinnacle walls support extensive soft coral and sea fan growth, with crinoids filtering the current. Pelagic species — including grey reef sharks and trevally — orbit the pinnacle at mid-water.

## Dive Profile

Descend directly to the pinnacle summit at 10 meters and spend the first minutes observing the anthias cloud in its entirety before descending the pinnacle walls. Work down to 20–25 meters on the walls, noting the transition from anthias territory to the larger resident fish zone. The 30-meter base can be visited briefly on an advanced dive plan. Ascend along the pinnacle face, ending with extended time at the summit in the 5–10 meter zone, which has the most intense anthias concentration.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access from Miyako-jima with operators who know the pinnacle's GPS position. The mooring is on the pinnacle itself. Current from multiple directions is possible — brief on conditions before descending.

## Tips

- Approach the pinnacle slowly and avoid sudden movements — the anthias cloud disperses briefly at disturbance but reassembles if you hold still
- The summit zone at 8–12 meters has the densest concentrations — spend as much time here as your air permits
- Grey reef sharks and trevally appear from the blue periodically — keep an eye on the water column at depth
- Bring a wide-angle lens as the primary shooting setup — the anthias cloud demands large field of view
- Morning visits before afternoon currents pick up offer the best experience

## Safety

Pinnacle dives require awareness of buoyancy around the coral structure — accidental contact with the encrusted surface destroys decades of coral growth. Current around the pinnacle can be multidirectional — stay aware of your position relative to the structure and the boat above. Plan air conservatively to ensure a proper ascent and safety stop at the pinnacle summit with its dense fish life.

## Photography

The anthias cloud is one of the great wide-angle underwater subjects in Japan. A 10–17mm fisheye or 16–35mm equivalent captures the full cloud against the blue water. Shooting upward from below the cloud creates striking backlit images with the surface light silhouetting thousands of fish. Macro opportunities exist throughout — the pinnacle walls host nudibranchs, flatworms, and various shrimps among the soft coral growth.
