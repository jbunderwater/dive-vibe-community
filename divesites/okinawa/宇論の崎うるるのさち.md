# 宇論の崎・うるるのさち (Ururuno Saki)

A cape-end reef in the Kerama Islands where a prominent headland creates current-influenced conditions and exceptional access to pelagic species in crystal-clear water.

## Overview

Ururuno Saki (宇論の崎) refers to a distinctive cape or point in the Kerama Islands where the underwater terrain extends as a reef promontory into open water. Cape-end dive sites in the Keramas are prized for the confluence of clear oceanic water and the current patterns that concentrate marine life. The Kerama Islands are famous throughout Japan for their "Kerama Blue" visibility — the region's waters, protected within the Keramashoto National Park, offer some of Japan's clearest ocean conditions with visibility regularly reaching 30–40 meters. The cape terrain here includes a pronounced wall or steep slope dropping to 30 meters, with an extensive shallow reef zone on top.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Kerama Islands, Okinawa Prefecture, Japan
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Coral reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 25–40 meters (80–130 feet)
- **Current**: Moderate to strong at the cape tip; sheltered inside
- **Best Time**: October to April for optimal visibility; currents most predictable in winter months

## Marine Life

Cape points in the Keramas reliably attract pelagic species drawn by the upwelling of nutrient-rich water around the headland. Spotted eagle rays are regularly encountered. Grey reef sharks patrol the outer edge. Large trevally and giant trevally ambush prey fish that gather at the cape tip. Hawksbill turtles are a near-constant feature throughout the Kerama Islands, and this site is no exception. The reef itself supports a rich array of Okinawan subtropical species — yellowfin surgeonfish, longnose hawkfish, and various parrotfish species. Sea fans and black coral trees characterise the deeper slope.

## Dive Profile

The standard approach follows the inside of the cape from the mooring, descending to 20–30 meters on the outer slope, then ascending along the reef crest as the current sweeps divers around the tip. Divers with strong current experience can position at the tip itself to observe the pelagic fish activity. The inside of the cape is the decompression zone — sheltered and shallow.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access from Kerama Islands operators. The exposed cape position means conditions must be assessed before entry. SMB deployment for open-water ascents when the current sweeps divers away from the reef structure.

## Tips

- Position yourself at the cape tip at depth facing into the current for the best pelagic encounters
- Eagle rays often appear suddenly from the blue at the cape edge — scan outward regularly throughout the dive
- The inside of the cape is an excellent safety stop zone — sheltered and with excellent shallow coral for observation
- Combine with a calm Kerama reef site for a contrast of exposed and sheltered diving on the same day
- Ask operators about current conditions that morning before committing to the cape tip section

## Safety

Current management is the primary safety consideration at a cape tip. Strong current requires the ability to abort the cape section and retreat to the sheltered inside if conditions exceed your capability. Deploy an SMB if swept into open water. The Kerama Islands' remoteness from Naha means emergency response planning is important — confirm insurance and decompression chamber location (Naha) before diving.

## Photography

Cape dives offer the best opportunity in the Kerama Islands for large-animal wide-angle photography. Eagle rays against the deep blue behind the cape, trevally in attack mode at the tip, and the sweeping reef contour all reward wide-angle lenses. The exceptional Kerama visibility ensures clean, saturated colours in the water column shots.
