# ウフタマ (Ufu Tama / Big Ball)

A distinctive Kerama Islands reef site named for a prominent rounded coral or rocky formation — a navigation landmark that also serves as an aggregation point for reef fish and turtles.

## Overview

Ufu Tama (ウフタマ) is an Okinawan dialect name meaning "big ball" or "large round thing" — a reference to a prominent rounded formation on this Kerama Islands reef that serves as a local landmark. In Okinawan dive site naming tradition, distinctive formations become anchoring references for the entire dive. The site is within the Keramashoto National Park and benefits from the park's protections and the exceptional "Kerama Blue" water clarity. The rounded formation creates a natural aggregation point where marine life concentrates. The maximum depth of 30 meters places the site in solid intermediate territory.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Kerama Islands, Okinawa Prefecture, Japan
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef with prominent formation
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters (100 feet)
- **Typical Visibility**: 25–40 meters (80–130 feet)
- **Current**: Moderate; varies by tidal phase
- **Best Time**: October to April for peak visibility; year-round diving possible

## Marine Life

The prominent rounded formation creates a focal point that larger reef fish use as territorial anchors. Large groupers — particularly coral grouper and potato grouper — are associated with the formation. Sea turtles rest on and around the prominent structure, visible from a distance in the Kerama's clear water. Anthias hover above the formation in dense orange clouds. The surrounding reef supports healthy hard coral, with brain corals and table corals extending across the reef slope. Cuttlefish are found in the adjacent reef zones. The formation's base crevices shelter moray eels and lobsters.

## Dive Profile

Navigate to the prominent rounded formation as the dive's anchor point and centre exploration around it in concentric circuits. Descend to its base at 25–30 meters, examine the crevices and shaded sides, then work progressively upward to the formation's top and the surrounding shallower reef. Ascending in a spiral pattern around the formation covers all its aspects. Bottom time of 50–60 minutes at these depths.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access from Kerama Islands operators. The formation's GPS position is known to local operators — they will guide you directly to it.

## Tips

- The formation itself is the primary focus — navigate using it as a constant reference point
- The groupers associated with large formations like this are territorial and often stay close; approach slowly for confident sightings
- Look at the formation from multiple angles before beginning exploration — this gives you a mental model for navigation
- The formation creates a tidal shadow on its downstream side — wildlife concentrates there in current
- This site works well for divers who prefer a single prominent feature to explore rather than covering large reef distances

## Safety

Moderate Kerama conditions. Current around the prominent formation may be amplified by the structure's flow-deflecting effect — maintain neutral buoyancy and don't let the current pin you against the formation's surfaces. Standard SMB deployment before ascending. The structure's base crevices may contain moray eels — look before reaching into any opening.

## Photography

The prominent rounded formation is a natural compositional anchor for wide-angle reef images. Position a diver model at the base with the formation rising above them for scale-showing images. Turtle portraits resting on the formation are iconic subjects. The anthias cloud hovering above the formation's summit creates striking images from below looking up. Close-focus wide-angle on the groupers associated with the formation can produce dramatic results.
