# 阿真牛瀬・あまうしじ (Ama Ushiji)

A rocky reef formation near Ama Beach on Zamami Island featuring dramatic underwater topography and a resident turtle population in classic "Kerama Blue" visibility.

## Overview

Ama Ushiji (阿真牛瀬) translates approximately as "Ama rocky/cattle reef" — the name refers to a prominent rocky reef outcrop or series of formations in the waters around Ama Beach, Zamami-jima. The site offers more varied topography than the flat sandy approach of Ama Beach Front, with ridges, valleys, and rock formations creating diverse microhabitats. The Kerama Islands context means exceptional water clarity and a nationally protected marine environment where fishing restrictions have allowed marine life to flourish undisturbed. This site functions as a complementary pair to Ama Beach Front, offering more topographic interest for divers seeking terrain variation.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Zamami-jima, Kerama Islands, Okinawa Prefecture, Japan
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Rocky reef with ridges and formations
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters (100 feet)
- **Typical Visibility**: 25–40 meters (80–130 feet)
- **Current**: Light to moderate; sheltered position near Ama Beach
- **Best Time**: Year-round; peak conditions October to April

## Marine Life

The rocky reef formations create a greater diversity of hiding spots and current-sheltered zones than flat reef systems. Large moray eels — giant moray and spotted moray — occupy the deeper crevices in the rock. Lionfish hover near rock overhangs, their venomous spines making them bold and unhurried. Octopus are regularly spotted in the rocky sections. Hawksbill and green turtles are, as throughout the Keramas, a near-guaranteed encounter. The rock surfaces support crustose coralline algae and dense encrusting organisms, attracting various nudibranch species. Schools of anthias hover above the rocky prominences.

## Dive Profile

Descend from the mooring to the main reef structure and navigate the ridges and valleys systematically. The more complex topography rewards methodical exploration rather than fast drift-style movement. Deeper sections at 25–30 meters offer crevice exploration; shallower ridge tops at 10–15 meters have the densest fish activity. Standard intermediate bottom time of 45–60 minutes.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access from Zamami-jima operators. This site is typically offered as part of the standard Zamami two-tank program.

## Tips

- The rocky formations demand more active navigation than the sandy reef approach — bring a compass bearing to the boat position
- Scan the rock surfaces and overhangs systematically for moray eels, which can be hidden by their perfect colour match with the rock
- Lionfish near overhangs — observe from a respectful distance and never touch
- The ridges and valleys create natural current breaks — explore on the sheltered sides in stronger current conditions
- This site pairs naturally with Ama Beach Front for a contrasting topography day

## Safety

Rocky reef terrain requires careful buoyancy — contact with rock is less forgiving than sand and may harbor spiny urchins or fire coral. Lionfish spines cause painful wounds — maintain awareness of their positions. Current through the rocky valleys can increase in certain tidal phases; assess before diving deep into crevices. Standard SMB deployment before ascending.

## Photography

Rocky reef topography creates more dramatic wide-angle compositions than flat sand-and-coral reefs — the ridges and valleys provide natural leading lines for the eye. Moray eel portraits in the crevices are a classic subject. Lionfish in their natural hovering posture reward a patient photographer who approaches slowly with a macro or close-focus wide-angle lens. Octopus in rocky habitat show excellent behaviour patterns.
