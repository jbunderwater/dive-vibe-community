# アントニオガウデイ (Antonio Gaudi)

A dramatic Miyako Island cavern named for the Catalan architect — its otherworldly interior formations suggest the organic, surrealist architecture of Barcelona's Sagrada Família.

## Overview

Antonio Gaudi (アントニオガウデイ) is a cavern and cave system on Miyako-jima named by local divers for the eccentric genius of the Spanish architect Antoni Gaudí, whose works are characterised by organic, fantastical forms. The underwater formations here — stalactites and stalagmites from when the cave system was above sea level during lower sea stands, combined with living encrusting sponges and coral growth — create an interior environment of genuinely surreal beauty. The site descends to 37 meters, placing the deepest sections at the advanced end of recreational diving. Its combination of depth, overhead environment, and exceptional visual drama makes Antonio Gaudi one of Miyako-jima's signature advanced dives.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Miyako-jima, Miyako Islands, Okinawa Prefecture, Japan
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Cavern and cave system with stalactite formations
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 37 meters (120 feet)
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–35 meters (65–115 feet)
- **Current**: Moderate; can be strong at the entrance
- **Best Time**: October to April; typhoon season (June to September) limits access

## Marine Life

The cave interior shelters extensive populations of soldierfish, squirrelfish, and cave-dwelling cardinalfish that aggregate in the darker sections. Crustaceans — lobsters, crabs, and shrimps — occupy the cavern walls. The stalactite formations are heavily encrusted with soft corals, sponges, and colonial tunicates that thrive in the low-flow cave environment. Sea turtles are occasionally found resting in the more spacious chambers. Nudibranchs are abundant on the encrusted cave surfaces, making this unexpectedly productive for macro enthusiasts.

## Dive Profile

Descend from the boat mooring to the cavern entrance. The main chamber is accessible to advanced open-water divers with good buoyancy; deeper interior passages require cave certification. Explore the stalactite formations in the upper sections of the cavern before descending to the deeper sections (30–37 meters) where the most dramatic formations are concentrated. Air management is critical at these depths in an overhead environment — turn back at one-third of your cylinder. Ascend through the chamber, observing the contrast between the dark cave ceiling and the lit cavern entrance.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access from Miyako-jima operators. The cavern entrance typically requires a brief descent along the outer reef before entering the system. Only operators with experience at this specific site should guide this dive.

## Tips

- Carry two torches minimum — a primary and a backup — for the deeper sections of the cave
- The stalactite formations are extremely fragile; position fins vertically to avoid contact during buoyancy adjustments
- Compare the upper and lower sections — the formations in the deepest section have a different character from the entrance area
- Experienced divers should look for the transition between ancient geological formations and modern biological encrustation
- Visit in the morning when some natural light enters the wider entrance sections

## Safety

Advanced rating reflects both the depth (37m) and overhead environment. Narcosis is a genuine concern at the maximum depth inside a cave — monitor your reasoning carefully and ascend promptly at any sign of impaired thinking. Gas management in cave environments follows the rule of thirds: one-third in, one-third out, one-third reserve. Do not exceed your certification level for cave penetration. Any silt disturbance in the deeper sections can become disorienting.

## Photography

The stalactite formations with overlaid living sponge and soft coral growth create extraordinary close-focus wide-angle compositions. Powerful strobes or video lights are essential — the interior at depth has no ambient light. Include divers in the frame to show the scale of the formations. The contrast between ancient grey limestone stalactites and the vivid orange, red, and purple of encrusting life rewards colour-accurate lighting techniques.
