# 嘉比前・がひまえ (Gahi Mae / Gahi Front)

The ocean-facing front reef of Gahi Island in the Kerama group — a productive exposed-face dive combining current-influenced topography with the Kerama Islands' signature water clarity.

## Overview

Gahi Mae (嘉比前, "Gahi front/facing side") is the seaward-facing complement to the sheltered Gahi Ura (Gahi Backside). The front reef faces the open Pacific, receiving cleaner oceanic water and more pronounced current than the island's lee side. This creates a richer nutrient supply and corresponding greater biological productivity. Gahi-jima (Gahi Island) is an uninhabited island in the Kerama group, and the lack of any human settlement means its surrounding reefs remain in near-pristine condition. The front reef's character is more dynamic than the sheltered back side, with coral formations shaped by exposure and current flowing across the reef top.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Gahi-jima, Kerama Islands, Okinawa Prefecture, Japan
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Exposed ocean-facing reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters (100 feet)
- **Typical Visibility**: 25–40 meters (80–130 feet)
- **Current**: Moderate to strong; varies with tide
- **Best Time**: October to April; exposed position benefits from winter calm; summer swell can limit access

## Marine Life

The oceanic exposure attracts species not commonly seen on sheltered sites. Large schools of bigeye trevally and golden trevally orbit the reef in hunting formations. Grey reef sharks are regular visitors from the open water. Sea turtles are abundant — the Kerama Islands generally have the highest turtle density in Japanese waters. Spotted eagle rays traverse the reef edge. Anthias and chromis hover above the coral in dense clouds on the shallower sections. The reef structure supports large table corals — among the most impressive in the Keramas — and dense sea fan gardens on the current-swept slope.

## Dive Profile

Enter carefully with current assessment from the boat. Descend to the deeper slope (20–30 meters) with the current assisting. Explore the table coral formations at 15–20 meters before moving to the shallower reef top. Current will carry divers along the front face; plan the dive to end in the calmer zone on one end of the reef where the boat can make a comfortable pickup. SMB mandatory for the ascent.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access from Zamami-jima or Aka-jima operators. Gahi-jima is uninhabited; boats from neighbouring islands reach it in 15–25 minutes. Sea conditions must be assessed — the exposed front face requires decent conditions for comfortable diving.

## Tips

- Contrast this with a Gahi Ura dive on the same trip for a complete picture of what sheltered versus exposed reef produces
- The table coral formations at 15–20 meters on the front face are some of the best in the Kerama Islands — allocate time to appreciate their scale
- Trevally hunting behaviour at the reef edge is best observed from mid-water facing the open ocean
- Current strength varies dramatically by tidal state — ask operators what conditions they expect before confirming the dive plan
- This is a site where wildlife encounters are strongly influenced by luck and timing — patience over multiple visits pays off

## Safety

The exposed front face requires intermediate-level current management capability. Strong current demands the ability to deploy an SMB while neutrally buoyant in moving water and to conduct a water-column ascent away from the reef. If current is too strong at the planned entry point, abort and dive the sheltered Gahi Ura instead. The Gahi Island context — uninhabited and relatively remote — means communication with the boat is critical throughout.

## Photography

Large table coral formations as dominant wide-angle subjects. Schools of trevally and bigeye trevally in current-swept open water require fast shutter speeds. Eagle ray encounters reward a readied wide-angle lens in hand throughout the reef edge portion. The clear Kerama water means excellent colour saturation in ambient-light reef photography — minimal strobe effort needed in the shallows.
