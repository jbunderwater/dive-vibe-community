## クロスホール (Cross Hole)

Located on Irabu Island, Cross Hole is considered the most beautiful light site in the Miyako area. A gentle slope leads to a sandy area, then into a horizontal cavern at roughly 25 metres. The defining feature is a cross-shaped opening in the ceiling that pours narrow shafts of sunlight into the cavern, creating dramatic light beams on the white sandy floor below.

## Overview

The site is more cavern than cave -- natural light is visible throughout, and the cross-shaped ceiling opening means a torch is unnecessary. Divers descend the slope to the cavern entrance, settle on the sandy floor, and look up through the cross as sunlight streams down in focused beams. The effect is most dramatic on clear days with high sun. The gentle topography and moderate depth make this accessible to AOW-certified divers despite the cave classification.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Irabu Island, Miyako-jima, Okinawa Prefecture, Japan
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Cave/cavern
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 27 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20-40 metres
- **Current**: Variable
- **Best Time**: Year-round; best light effects on sunny days with high sun (late morning to early afternoon)

## Marine Life

Near the cavern entrance and on the surrounding reef, Aquatic Adventure confirms sightings of damselfish, fusiliers, hawkfish, gobies, and razorfish. The cavern walls host nudibranchs and sea slugs. The reference in older descriptions to "redtooth triggerfish" could not be confirmed at this specific site in available sources.

## Dive Profile

Descend the slope to the cavern entrance at 20-25 metres. Enter and position on the sandy floor to observe the light beams from the cross-shaped opening. The cavern is spacious with no tight passages. Exit the way you entered and ascend along the reef slope for a safety stop in the shallows.

## Safety Considerations

AOW certification required due to the 20m+ entry depth. The cavern is open and well-lit but still qualifies as an overhead environment. Buoyancy control is important to avoid silting the sandy floor, which ruins the light effect for other divers.

## Photography

The cross-shaped light beams on white sand are the signature shot. Wide-angle from the cavern floor looking up. Timing matters -- midday sun produces the most intense beams. No strobe needed; natural light is the subject.
