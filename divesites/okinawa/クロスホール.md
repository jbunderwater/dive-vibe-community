# クロスホール (Cross Hole)

A distinctive cavern on Miyako Island featuring a cross-shaped opening that allows natural light to create a dramatic illuminated chamber accessible to entry-level divers.

## Overview

Cross Hole (クロスホール) is a cavern formation on Miyako-jima whose name describes its defining feature: a cross-shaped opening in the reef that floods the interior chamber with natural light in a recognisable cruciform pattern. Unlike the deeper Tori-ike passage system, Cross Hole is an open cavern — natural light is visible throughout, and no cave certification is required. The chamber is accessible from relatively shallow water, making it genuinely beginner-appropriate despite the cave classification. The cross-shaped opening creates a remarkable light display during morning dives when the sun is low and beams penetrate the opening from multiple angles. The site is one of Miyako-jima's most photogenic dive destinations.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Miyako-jima, Miyako Islands, Okinawa Prefecture, Japan
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Open cavern (natural light throughout)
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner (open cavern — natural light visible at all points)
- **Maximum Depth**: 27 meters (90 feet)
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–35 meters (65–115 feet)
- **Current**: Light to moderate; can intensify through the cavern opening
- **Best Time**: October to April for clearest water and calmest approach conditions

## Marine Life

The cavern interior shelters species that prefer the low-light, protected environment. Soldierfish and squirrelfish congregate in the darker recesses of the chamber, retreating from the light beams. Lobsters and crabs occupy the wall crevices. The outer reef surrounding the cavern entrance supports typical Okinawan subtropical reef species — yellowfin surgeonfish, various wrasse species, and sea turtles that regularly traverse the area. Nudibranchs and flatworms have been documented on the cavern walls, making this an unexpected macro diving opportunity.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the cavern entrance from the boat mooring. The maximum depth of 27 meters is at the deepest cavern floor section. Begin by exploring the entrance and the characteristic cross-shaped opening from below, observing the light patterns. Move into the chamber interior — natural light is visible throughout, providing orientation at all times. Explore the chamber systematically before exiting via the main entrance. The shallower sections above the main floor provide additional exploration time during the ascent.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access from Miyako-jima's dive operators. The cavern can also be approached from the surface by snorkellers peering down through the opening. Dive operators brief all divers on the cavern boundaries and the importance of not disturbing the floor silt.

## Tips

- Time the dive for 9–11am when sun angles most dramatically illuminate the cross-shaped opening from multiple directions simultaneously
- Buoyancy control is important here — silt on the cavern floor ruins visibility for everyone if disturbed
- Bring a torch to supplement natural light in the darker corners and to illuminate macro subjects on the walls
- This is an excellent first cavern experience for beginner divers before progressing to the more complex Tori-ike system
- The outer reef around the cavern entrance rewards exploration before or after the cavern portion

## Safety

While beginner-rated, the overhead environment demands responsible buoyancy and the understanding that surfacing through the rock is impossible — you must exit through the entrance. Natural light throughout eliminates navigation risk, but all divers should stay within the lit zone and not proceed into any dark passages. Always dive with a torch even in a "natural light" cavern. Follow the operator's specific instructions for this site.

## Photography

The cross-shaped light beams are the defining photographic subject — wide-angle images from the cavern floor looking up at the lit opening create iconic images. A fisheye lens captures the full cross pattern. Silhouettes of divers in the light beams create dramatic compositions. The soldierfish clusters in the darker corners reward macro lenses. Morning visits are essential for the most striking light beam effects.
