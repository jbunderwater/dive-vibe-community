# 通り池 (Tori-ike / Passage Pond)

One of Japan's most legendary cave dives — twin sinkholes on Miyako Island connected by an underwater passage that links an inland freshwater pond to the open ocean.

## Overview

Tori-ike (通り池, literally "passage pond") is among the most distinctive dive sites in all of Japan. Located on Miyako-jima in the Miyako Islands group of Okinawa Prefecture, the site consists of two adjacent sinkholes. The outer sinkhole connects directly to the ocean, while the inner pond is exposed to the sky but connected underwater through a complex cave passage system that reaches 40 meters depth. This through-connection creates a tidal halocline — a dramatic layering of saltwater and freshwater that produces an eerie optical distortion visible to divers. The cave walls are encrusted with tube worms and sponges, and the transition from ocean light to cave darkness to the greenish light of the inland pond is a genuinely otherworldly experience. The site is considered one of the top cave dives in Asia.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Miyako-jima, Miyako Islands, Okinawa Prefecture, Japan
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive (entry from outer ocean-connected sinkhole)
- **Site Type**: Connected sinkhole cave system
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced (cave diving certification strongly recommended for inner passage)
- **Maximum Depth**: 40 meters (130 feet)
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–30 meters (50–100 feet; varies by tidal state and halocline layering)
- **Current**: Tidal inflow and outflow through the passage; assess before entry
- **Best Time**: October to April for calmest seas; avoid during typhoon season (June to September)

## Marine Life

The unique connected system supports a blend of oceanic and cave-adapted species. Sea turtles enter the outer sinkhole regularly and have been observed swimming into the passage. Parrotfish and wrasse shelter in the sinkhole walls. The cave interior hosts shrimp, sponge-dwelling crabs, and various small fish adapted to low-light conditions. The halocline layer creates temporary confusion for marine species transitioning between salinity zones — fish are occasionally observed hovering at the halocline boundary. Nudibranch species have been documented on the cave walls alongside the encrusting fauna.

## Dive Profile

Entry is from the outer ocean-connected sinkhole, descending alongside the vertical walls. The halocline becomes visible at around 5–15 meters depending on tidal state — it appears as a shimmering, heat-mirage-like distortion. Advanced divers with cave orientation skills can follow the passage leading toward the inner pond; this requires careful buoyancy to avoid silting and responsible air management due to the overhead environment. The maximum passage depth reaches 40 meters. Non-cave-certified divers should explore the outer sinkhole only and observe the halocline from above.

## Entry and Exit

Access from Miyako-jima via dive boat. The outer sinkhole has a boat landing area; entry into the water involves descending the sinkhole walls or entering from a boat tied at the rim. Only operators with Tori-ike experience should be trusted for this dive. The site requires proper cave or cavern diving certification for the passage section.

## Tips

- Cave certification is strongly recommended before attempting the passage — at minimum, take a cavern diving speciality course
- Visit at slack tide when the tidal current through the passage is minimal
- The halocline is most visible and striking in the late morning when sun angles illuminate it from above
- Bring a primary dive torch and a backup — the passage interior requires artificial light
- The inland pond sinkhole can be viewed from the surface by non-divers — the full cave system context is impressive even without diving

## Safety

This is an overhead environment dive with significant risk for unqualified cave divers. Silt disturbance within the passage can reduce visibility to zero in seconds. Gas management for an overhead environment is different from open-water diving — turn back at one-third of your air supply at minimum. Never attempt the passage without a trained guide on your first visit and proper cave equipment. Tidal currents through the passage can be substantial during tidal changes.

## Photography

The halocline optical distortion is one of the most photogenic phenomena in diving — wide-angle images showing the distortion layer with the cave environment above and below create surreal, striking images. The vertical sinkhole walls lit from above by natural light create dramatic compositional frames. Cave interior shots require powerful strobes or video lights; natural light disappears quickly in the passage. The intersection of oceanic blue from below and greenish filtered surface light from the inland pond is uniquely beautiful.
