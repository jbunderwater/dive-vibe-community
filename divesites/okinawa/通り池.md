## 通り池 (Tori-ike / Twin Ponds)

One of Miyako-jima's "Big Three" dive sites and among the most famous cave dives in Japan. Two inland limestone ponds -- 75 metres and 55 metres in diameter -- are connected to the open ocean via a massive underwater tunnel, creating one of the most otherworldly dive experiences in the Pacific.

## Overview

Divers enter from the ocean side and swim through a large arch (ceiling at roughly 20 metres, sandy bottom at 40-45 metres) that connects the sea to the inland ponds. The signature experience is passing through the chemocline and thermocline where freshwater and seawater meet, creating a shimmering, distorted visual layer that shifts colours from deep blue to emerald green. Surfacing inside the pond, divers look up at limestone cliffs and sky -- while tourists on the walking path above peer down in surprise. The site is a designated national monument and natural treasure of Shimoji Island.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Shimoji Island, Miyako-jima, Okinawa Prefecture, Japan
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Cave/cavern
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 40 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20-40 metres
- **Current**: Occasionally strong at the ocean entrance
- **Best Time**: September to May; conditions depend on wind direction (south-facing site)

## Marine Life

The arch entrance and ocean approach attract whitetip reef sharks, giant trevally, dogtooth tuna, and sea turtles -- all confirmed by the operator who manages this site. Fusiliers and pyramid butterflyfish school on the ocean side. Amethyst anthias, anemonefish, and ringeye jawfish inhabit the arch walls. Deep-sea squat lobsters (Munida-type) occupy ceiling cracks inside the cave. The rock walls harbour nudibranchs and sea slugs. Kumejima otohime shrimp have been reported in ceiling crevices inside the cave. Napoleon wrasse are occasionally sighted but less frequently confirmed at this specific site than at other Miyako sites.

## Dive Profile

This dive requires two full descent-ascent cycles: descend from the boat to the ocean cave entrance at the arch (ceiling at ~20m, sandy floor at ~40m), transit through the cave while observing the chemocline colour shift from deep blue to emerald green, ascend to surface inside the southern pond (75m diameter), then descend and return the way you came. There is no alternative exit -- the route back is the only bailout. The arch transit at 20m exposure and the 40m seabed below demand careful gas management and excellent buoyancy. Most guided dives limit total bottom time to 20-30 minutes.

## Safety Considerations

This is a true cave dive with no alternative exit: divers must return the way they entered. Advanced OW certification is required and operators enforce this strictly; some require specific dive counts. Narcosis is a real risk at 40m. Current at the ocean entrance can be strong -- operators choose dive days based on sea conditions and abort if conditions are unsuitable. Buoyancy control must be excellent to avoid contact with the fragile National Monument limestone formations. The site should only be attempted with a guide who knows the site -- and only "on days when sea conditions and the team's skills are just right" (Aquatic Adventure). Do not penetrate solo.

## Photography

Wide-angle is essential. The classic shot is looking up through the tunnel entrance with the green pond light above, or capturing a diver silhouetted against the colour transition at the chemocline. The freshwater-saltwater mixing layer creates abstract, ethereal images. Morning light produces the best colour in the pond.
