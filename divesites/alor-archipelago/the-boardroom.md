## The Boardroom

The Boardroom is a muck dive site in the Alor Archipelago, East Nusa Tenggara, Indonesia — a shallow, sheltered bay with a flat volcanic sand floor that liveaboard guides liken to a meeting table for critters, where a remarkable variety of cryptic species coexist in close proximity.

## Overview

The Boardroom sits in a naturally sheltered inlet on Alor's northern coast, protected from the prevailing swell and almost entirely shielded from the strong tidal currents that define diving at the archipelago's exposed sites. The bottom is predominantly flat volcanic sand at 8–16 metres with scattered coral rubble, isolated sea grass patches, and the occasional sunken debris that creates habitat for critters in open sandy environments. The name alludes to the flat, businesslike expanse of the bottom and to the concentrated activity that divers find there — like attending a meeting where every seat is occupied. For divers who have spent previous dives managing strong currents and depth on the walls, The Boardroom is a welcome change of pace that often produces the trip's best macro finds.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Alor Archipelago, East Nusa Tenggara, Indonesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Muck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 16 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 8–15 metres
- **Current**: Negligible; sheltered bay conditions
- **Best Time**: April to November (dry season; cleaner water from reduced runoff)

## Marine Life

The Boardroom is known within Alor's liveaboard community for cephalopod diversity — wunderpus octopus, mimic octopus, and coconut octopus are all reliably encountered, along with smaller algae octopus and the occasional day octopus out hunting across the sand. Flamboyant cuttlefish are present year-round and are sometimes found in small groups. Juvenile form fish using the debris habitat are a guide speciality: juvenile batfish, juvenile sweetlips, and juveniles of several species known to mimic flotsam or weeds while they are small. Hairy frogfish and pygmy frogfish sit on sponges and coral rubble in multiple colour morphs. Seahorses — both thorny seahorses and the larger spotted seahorse — are found on the sea grass fringes, and robust ghost pipefish appear regularly with breeding pairs of dragonets visible in the shallower sections in season.

## Dive Profile

The site is dived very slowly across the full bottom area. Entry in the shallows at 5–8 metres allows an initial survey of the sea grass fringe before dropping to the main sand flats at 10–16 metres. Most productive habitat is at 10–14 metres where the sand-rubble interface concentrates the highest critter density. Total bottom time is driven by air consumption and interest rather than depth; dives of 70–80 minutes are typical. No current requires active fin management to stay position; focus is entirely on observation.

## Entry and Exit

Access by liveaboard or day-boat tender. Entry via giant stride or back roll into calm, shallow water. The tender anchors nearby. Deploy SMB before surfacing to signal the crew.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Start the dive by checking any debris — tyres, cans, bottles, and discarded fishing gear are colonised by frogfish and juvenile fish seeking shelter in open sand environments
- Wunderpus and mimic octopus tend to be active in early morning before the bottom heats in the afternoon; plan this dive as an early-morning slot if possible
- Bring a small torch even in daylight — rubble undersides and narrow crevices contain critters that are invisible without illumination
- Minimise bottom disturbance by staying neutrally buoyant and horizontal; silt clouds from fin sweeps reduce visibility and disrupt feeding behaviours you came to observe
- Point with a finger rather than touching — critter positions shared with your guide and buddy are easier to communicate with a non-contact gentle gesture

## Safety Considerations

The Boardroom is one of Alor's safest dive sites in terms of current and depth, but venomous species are present: blue-ringed octopus, stonefish, and scorpionfish are documented here. Stonefish are effectively invisible on the volcanic substrate; avoid standing on the bottom and be alert when reaching under rubble. Cone shells may be present — never pick up unidentified shells. The calm conditions can lead divers to be less attentive to no-decompression limits; even shallow dives should be managed with a dive computer.

## Photography

The Boardroom is a pure macro site. A 100 mm macro lens or equivalent handles most subjects, with a wet dioptre available for the smallest animals such as pygmy seahorses and juvenile dragonets. The dark volcanic sand background simplifies composition — isolating a colourful frogfish or wunderpus against a black substrate creates images with visual impact that requires no post-processing tricks. Cephalopods are ideal subjects because they often remain stationary for extended shooting sessions, allowing refined exposure and framing.
