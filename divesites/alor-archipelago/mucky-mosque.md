## Mucky Mosque

Mucky Mosque is a muck dive site in Kalabahi Bay, Alor, East Nusa Tenggara, Indonesia — a dark volcanic sand environment renowned among macro photographers for its exceptional density of Rhinopias and unusual invertebrate life, making it one of the most celebrated critter-hunting grounds in East Nusa Tenggara.

## Overview

Mucky Mosque sits in Kalabahi Bay on the western end of Alor Island, its name a reference to the mosque visible from the water and the characteristically dark, silty conditions on the bottom. The seabed consists of dark sand, rounded river stones, and scattered corals, with hydroids and rope structures providing perching spots for frogfish and pipefish. The site is a classic muck environment: calm, methodical, and productive for close-range work. It is popular for both day and night dives, with night dives yielding additional species. Depth reaches approximately 27 metres (90 feet) according to dive log accounts, deeper than some general descriptions suggest.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Kalabahi Bay, Alor Island, East Nusa Tenggara, Indonesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Muck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 27 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 6–15 metres
- **Current**: Minimal
- **Best Time**: April to November (dry season; best visibility)

## Marine Life

Mucky Mosque is one of the more reliable sites in Indonesia to find Rhinopias scorpionfish — both the paddleflap Rhinopias (*Rhinopias eschmeyeri*) and the weedy Rhinopias are documented here. Multiple species of frogfish and ghost pipefish (including halimeda, ornate, and robust ghost pipefish) are confirmed residents. Seahorses and dragonets are present. Wunderpus octopus and harlequin shrimp have been reported. A dive log published in the Underwater Journal documented specific animals including a paddle-flap Rhinopias (orange-coloured), bobtailed squids, pygmy squid (*Idiosepius pygmaeus*), Spanish dancer (*Hexabranchus sanguineus*), and a crocodile snake eel (*Brachysomophis crocodilinus*) at this site. Various crabs, nudibranchs, and shrimps are also present throughout.

## Dive Profile

Enter in the shallows and work systematically across the sand and rubble. The seabed transitions from shallow corals through the primary muck habitat to approximately 27 metres. Guides typically traverse the site slowly, pausing for extended observation of each subject. Night dives are productive for species that shelter during the day. Long bottom times of 70–80 minutes are practical given the calm conditions.

## Entry and Exit

Access by liveaboard or day-boat tender. Entry via giant stride into calm water. The tender anchors nearby. Deploy SMB before ascending.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Night dives here are highly regarded — additional species including bobtailed squid and Spanish dancers are active after dark
- Ropes and hydroids on the bottom are prime frogfish habitat; scan these carefully
- Move slowly — the site rewards patience and deliberate searching over ground coverage
- Carry a small torch even on day dives to illuminate beneath rubble and coral ledges

## Safety Considerations

Venomous species are present on the sand and rubble: stonefish and scorpionfish are effectively invisible against the substrate. Avoid touching the bottom and be alert when near rubble. Cone shells may be present — never pick up unidentified shells. Despite the shallow-seeming environment, monitor your no-decompression limits on deeper passes.

## Photography

Mucky Mosque is one of Alor's premier macro photography sites. A 100 mm macro lens or equivalent handles most subjects; a wet dioptre opens access to the smallest invertebrates. The dark volcanic substrate provides a naturally clean, out-of-focus background. The paddleflap Rhinopias in particular — with its elaborate fleshy appendages and vivid orange coloration — is among the most photographed subjects the site produces.
