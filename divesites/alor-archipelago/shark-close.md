## Shark Close

Shark Close is a wall dive site in the Alor Archipelago, East Nusa Tenggara, Indonesia — a current-exposed wall that lives up to its name with near-guaranteed reef shark encounters from the first descent, making it one of the most consistently exciting shark dives in Eastern Indonesia.

## Overview

Shark Close runs along a prominent headland where the convergence of two tidal flows creates an upwelling zone that concentrates fish life at the wall face. The wall drops from a shallow reef cap at 6–8 metres and descends vertically to 32 metres before transitioning to a steep rubble slope. The wall face is richly textured with soft coral thickets, wire coral spirals, and large barrel sponges. Sharks — predominantly grey reef sharks, whitetip reef sharks, and occasional silvertips — are a fixture rather than an occasional encounter, and groups of 5–15 animals around the prominent corner of the headwall are typical. The site's exposure to current means it is rated advanced, and conditions can vary significantly between tidal states.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Alor Archipelago, East Nusa Tenggara, Indonesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 32 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–30 metres
- **Current**: Strong; intensifies around the headwall corner
- **Best Time**: April to November

## Marine Life

Grey reef sharks are the defining species at Shark Close, typically found patrolling the wall face between 15 and 30 metres. Whitetip reef sharks rest on ledges throughout the dive profile. The headwall corner is a particular hotspot where the current forces nutrients into the water column and multiple shark species converge. Schools of big-eye trevally and yellowstripe scad orbit the wall in tight formations, and dogtooth tuna power through in single-minded passes. The reef cap supports dense populations of reef fish — surgeonfish, parrotfish, and wrasse schools — and the wall itself hosts large morays in the deeper crevices. Hawksbill turtles use the site's shallower sections and can often be observed feeding on the sponge-encrusted wall face.

## Dive Profile

Enter up-current of the headwall and descend along the wall face toward the corner at 25–30 metres, where shark activity is typically highest. Spend 10–15 minutes at depth observing the sharks from a stationary, relaxed position — the animals are habituated to divers who are calm but will move off if approached aggressively. Ascend along the wall, working the 15–20 metre range where soft corals are densest, and finish on the current-sheltered section of the wall behind the headland. The reef cap at 6–8 metres provides a comfortable safety stop zone with rich fish life.

## Entry and Exit

Access by liveaboard or day-boat tender. Entry up-current with a planned drift to the headwall and beyond to a downstream pickup point. SMBs are essential and should be deployed well before the safety stop ends. The tender skipper will time pickup to the drift, but conditions at the headwall corner can push divers off the planned track; a whistle or signal mirror helps the crew locate divers in choppy surface conditions.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Approach the headwall corner slowly and descend to depth before reaching it — sharks that are already settled on the wall are less likely to depart than those that see divers descending on top of them
- Calm body language at depth encourages sharks to remain at close range; avoid rapid arm movements, camera flashes pointed directly at them, or sudden ascent
- The current behind the headland on the sheltered side is dramatically lighter than on the exposed face — position yourself there for a comfortable rest and observation point
- Whitetip reef sharks resting on ledges will often allow very close approach by divers moving slowly from below their eye line

## Safety Considerations

The headwall corner concentrates current from two directions and can be challenging to maintain position. Divers who are swept off the wall into the blue water adjacent to the corner should maintain depth and drift with the current — the liveaboard crew tracks all divers and current drift paths are predictable. Do not panic and ascend rapidly if swept off the wall. Shark Close has multiple shark species present throughout the dive; standard protocols apply — remain calm, move slowly, and never touch or feed the animals.

## Photography

Shark Close is a defining site for shark photography in Alor. Wide-angle or fisheye lenses allow close-approach shots with the wall providing context behind the sharks. The key technique is patience: take up a position at the headwall, stay still, and let the sharks come to you for frame-filling compositions. Shooting toward the surface from depth silhouettes sharks against the lighter water column. The soft coral coverage on the wall also rewards medium-range wide-angle shots with the wall texture as a backdrop for the schooling fish.
