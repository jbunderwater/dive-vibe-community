## The Cathedral

The Cathedral is a wall dive site in south Alor, East Nusa Tenggara, Indonesia — also known as Motolang, it presents a dramatic coral wall of dense hard and soft corals with a signature swim-through at around 30 metres, and is distinguished by the cold upwellings that keep its corals extraordinarily healthy and vivid.

## Overview

The Cathedral (Motolang) earns its name from the impressive rock structure beneath the surface: massive jumbled boulders and a steep coral wall create a towering, cathedral-like impression underwater. A key feature is the swim-through cave at approximately 25–30 metres, which passes through the wall and emerges on the other side. The site is located in south Alor where strong thermoclines are common — surface temperatures around 27°C can drop to as low as 14°C below 30 metres, and these cold upwellings are directly responsible for the exceptional coral coverage: vibrantly coloured hard and soft corals cover the wall face in unusually high density. The pristine condition of the corals here consistently impresses divers accustomed to more visited reefs elsewhere in Indonesia.

## Site Information

- **Location**: South Alor, East Nusa Tenggara, Indonesia (also known as Motolang)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 35 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–35 metres
- **Current**: Strong; cold upwellings below 30 metres
- **Best Time**: May to October (best visibility and most consistent conditions)

## Marine Life

The Cathedral's wall and cave structures host a range of cryptic and macro subjects. Bargibant's pygmy seahorses (*Hippocampus bargibanti*) and pontohi seahorses (*Hippocampus pontohi*) are found among the coral formations. Leaffish, scorpionfish, and bluering octopus inhabit the nooks of the rock and coral structure. Bamboo sharks rest in the shallow overhangs on the upper wall. Large marbled rays have been observed passing through the deeper sections of the wall. A tawny nurseshark (*Nebrius ferrugineus*) has been documented resting inside the cave at approximately 25 metres.

## Dive Profile

Descend the wall from the reef crest and work toward the swim-through cave at 25–30 metres — passing through it is a highlight of the dive. Be prepared for a significant temperature drop below 30 metres; if cold upwellings are present, limit time at depth and work methodically back up through the 15–25 metre zone where the coral coverage is most impressive. Finish on the upper wall at 5–10 metres for the safety stop. Carry a torch for the cave passage to appreciate the coral and invertebrate life inside.

## Entry and Exit

Access by liveaboard or day-boat tender. Entry point is at the wall face. Exit by boat pickup using SMB deployment before surfacing. Surface conditions can be choppy in the strait if wind is up.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Bring a 5 mm wetsuit or hooded vest — temperatures can drop dramatically below 30 metres and the cold can shorten dives unexpectedly
- A torch is essential for the swim-through; the cave interior hosts encrusting life that is invisible without artificial light
- Look carefully in the coral structure for pygmy seahorses — they are small (less than 2 cm) and easily missed without a guide pointing them out
- The shallow upper wall at 5–10 metres warrants time at the end of the dive; coral density here is remarkable

## Safety Considerations

Cold upwellings can affect judgment and dexterity; monitor your depth closely and be aware of the no-decompression limits when exploring the 30 m+ zone. The swim-through is accessible for recreational divers, but do not proceed through any section unless you can clearly see the exit. Buoyancy control must be precise to avoid contact with the pristine coral walls inside the cave. Always carry an SMB.

## Photography

The Cathedral is an exceptional macro site. Pygmy seahorses require a 100 mm macro or equivalent with a dioptre; they are found in the coral structure and are very small. Wide-angle work captures the scale of the wall and the swim-through passage. The coral density at 15–25 metres makes for striking wide-angle compositions where the wall itself is the subject.
