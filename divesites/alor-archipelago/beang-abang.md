## Beang Abang

Beang Abang is a premier muck diving destination on the southern coast of Pantar Island, known for exceptional critter diversity and volcanic geography. The site features hot springs from underwater volcanoes and world-class critter hunting opportunities.

## Overview

Beang Abang is a sandy bay muck diving site with gently sloping seabeds, pristine coral reefs, and occasional overhangs teeming with macro life. The black volcanic sand substrate and unique geological features (geothermal hot springs from underwater volcanoes) create ideal habitat for critter species. Depths range from 5-20 meters. This is a specialty macro and muck diving destination that requires significant effort to reach—there is an additional USD 100 fuel surcharge for diving sites in the far south of Pantar. However, divers who make the journey are rewarded with some of Alor's finest critter discoveries.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Southern coast of Pantar Island, Alor Archipelago
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Muck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 10-25 meters
- **Current**: Light to mild; usually favorable for hunting
- **Water Temperature**: Warm (26-30°C); warmer near hot spring vents
- **Best Time**: Year-round diveable; May-September most consistent
- **Access**: Requires extra fuel surcharge ($100 USD) due to remote location
- **Specialty**: Critter, macro, and thermal feature diving

## Marine Life

Beang Abang is renowned for exceptional critter fauna: Rhinopias (both yellow and tasseled varieties—the signature species), frogfish in multiple color forms, ghost pipefish (robust and delicate varieties), ribbon eels, octopuses, harlequin shrimp, Coleman shrimp, seahorses, Ambon scorpionfish, mandarinfish, nudibranchs (numerous species), unusual crustaceans, cephalopods, and other Indo-Pacific muck dwellers. Rhinopias are the star of the show—reliable sightings make this a premier location for this prized critter.

## Dive Profile

Plan your dive with dedicated macro-hunting intention. Descend to the black sand slope and systematically search small areas thoroughly. The geothermal vents create localized habitat variation—explore around vent areas carefully. Work through depths between 6-20 meters, pausing frequently to examine substrate details and crevices. Spend significant bottom time (30-40 minutes) hunting for rare species. Return to shallow areas for final minutes and safety stop.

## Entry and Exit

Access by dive boat from liveaboard or resort. Entry via giant stride or back roll. Descent to black sand slope. Methodical navigation along the sand, investigating critter habitat. Monitor air carefully due to extended bottom time. Exit via boat crew pickup with SMB deployment at safety stop.

- Bring a torch/light for crevice and shadowed area illumination
- Move slowly and deliberately; patience is essential
- Research critter identification before diving—expect rare finds
- Budget significant bottom time (40+ minutes if air allows)
- The extra fuel surcharge is worth the exceptional critter encounters
- This site is ideal for dedicated macro photographers and critter specialists
- Consider booking a multi-dive package to maximize this remote site

## Safety Considerations

Beang Abang's shallow slopes and light currents make it relatively safe in terms of current hazard. However, the extended bottom time at depth increases nitrogen load—monitor decompression carefully and plan conservative ascent profiles. The black sand and reduced light mean visibility can be unpredictable—have a low-visibility contingency plan. Geothermal vents heat localized areas—watch for rapid temperature changes. Always use SMB and dive with a buddy who shares your macro focus. Exercise caution around critters and avoid forcing hands into hidden spaces. This site suits intermediate+ macro divers comfortable with longer, intensive dives.

## Photography

Exceptional macro subjects: Rhinopias in multiple colors and poses (primary draw), diverse frogfish species, ghostpipefish detail shots, ribbon eel portraits. Nudibranchs with exotic color combinations, crustacean close-ups, octopus behavior studies. Geothermal vent features (substrate detail near vents) provide unique environmental context. Dark substrate requires strong strobes and careful technique. Best during morning dives; macro composition should emphasize subject detail, behavior, and critter rarity.
