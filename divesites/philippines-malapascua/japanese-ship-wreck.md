## Japanese Ship Wreck

The Japanese Ship Wreck is the MV Dona Marilyn, a 98-meter passenger ferry that sank in 1988, now a thriving artificial reef and intermediate wreck dive.

## Overview

The "Japanese Ship Wreck" at Malapascua is actually the MV Dona Marilyn, a 98-meter passenger ferry that sank on October 24, 1988, during a typhoon while traveling from Manila to Cebu. The ship lies 33 meters deep on her starboard side, with the shallowest point of the wreck at around 17 meters. The wreck has become a thriving marine habitat with fish and coral creating an impressive ecosystem on the hull. This site offers 10-40 meters of visibility with water temperatures averaging 26-30°C.

## Ship History

The MV Dona Marilyn was a 98-meter Cebu-Manila passenger ferry that sank during a typhoon in 1988. The wreck rests on her starboard side at 33m depth, with the shallowest point at approximately 17m. The ship has become one of the most biodiverse artificial reefs in the region.

## Dive Profile

Begin your descent to the wreck top at approximately 17-20m. Explore the exterior hull features, which are heavily encrusted with coral and marine growth. Advanced divers with wreck training may explore limited interior penetrations. Most recreational diving stays on the exterior. Monitor air supply carefully due to depth. Ascend with adequate reserve for safety stops.

## Entry and Exit

Access is by dive boat from local operators. Entry is typically via giant stride or back roll. Follow the dive briefing for descent and ascent procedures. Deploy a surface marker buoy (SMB) during your safety stop for boat pickup. Coordinate with the boat crew for exit procedures.

## Marine Life

Batfish, lionfish, sweetlips, coral growth, soft corals, reef sharks, barracuda, trevally. The encrusted hull attracts macro life including nudibranchs and crustaceans. The wreck structure provides shelter for a diverse ecosystem.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Bring a dive torch for wreck exploration
- Maintain excellent buoyancy to avoid disturbing silt
- Do not attempt penetration without proper wreck training
- The heavily encrusted hull is a living reef ecosystem
- Bring a camera—excellent wreck and macro photography opportunities
- Check sea conditions before diving

## Safety Considerations

This intermediate wreck dive has variable currents and significant depth (17-33m). Maintain excellent buoyancy control to avoid stirring silt. Do not penetrate the wreck unless trained and equipped. Monitor air supply closely at depth. Be aware of jellyfish and sea urchins. Always dive with a buddy and carry a safety sausage (SMB).

## Wreck Penetration

Limited interior penetration is possible for trained wreck divers only. Most recreational divers enjoy exterior exploration of the heavily encrusted hull. The wreck's structure is stable and safe for external diving.

## Photography

Excellent for wide-angle wreck photography. The coral-encrusted hull creates dramatic subjects. Natural light filtering through openings creates compelling compositions. Macro photography of encrusted surfaces is outstanding.

## Historical Significance

The MV Dona Marilyn sank on October 24, 1988, during a typhoon. The wreck remains a popular historical dive site and has transformed into one of the region's most biodiverse artificial reefs.
