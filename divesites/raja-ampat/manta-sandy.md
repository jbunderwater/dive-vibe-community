## Manta Sandy

Manta Sandy is one of Raja Ampat's premier manta ray cleaning stations, located in the Dampier Strait. The site features a sandy bottom cleaning station — NOT a reef — confirmed by 5+ sources (lagaligoliveaboard.com, diveconcepts.com, divernet.com, padi.com, scubadiverlife.com). The site type has been corrected from "reef" to "muck" (sandy bottom). Difficulty is Beginner.

## Overview

Manta Sandy lives up to its name: the site features a shallow sandy bottom at 15–18 meters where reef manta rays (Mobula alfredi) hover motionlessly while cleaner wrasse work their gill plates, undersides, and mouths. Unlike Manta Point, which has a reef structure, Manta Sandy's cleaning station sits above an open sand flat, requiring divers to kneel on the sand (or better, rest horizontally above it) while mantas circle overhead and return again and again to the station. The site sees consistent manta activity throughout the dive season and often delivers encounters with three to eight individuals per dive. Water temperature averages 27–29°C with visibility of 15–25 meters.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Raja Ampat, West Papua, Indonesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Muck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 18 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–25 meters (50–80 feet)
- **Current**: Mild to moderate; manta activity is greatest during gentle incoming flow
- **Best Time**: October to April; mantas present year-round

## Marine Life

Sources confirmed at this specific site (lagaligoliveaboard.com, diveconcepts.com, divernet.com, padi.com, scubadiverlife.com): reef mantas (Mobula alfredi) and black reef mantas (approximately 40% of the local population are melanistic — all-black individuals), oceanic mantas; Pegasus sea moths (Eurypegasus draconis) on the sand; cleaner wrasse at 2 specific cleaning rocks at approximately 16 meters depth; cobias, golden trevallies, bumphead parrotfish, and blacktip reef sharks.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the sandy bottom at 15–18 meters near the cleaning station. Rather than "kneeling" on the sand (which disturbs sediment and damages the habitat), position horizontally with your body parallel to the sand surface, using the tiniest fin movements to maintain position. Breathe very slowly — bubbles directed at mantas during their cleaning pass may cause them to break away. Hold position and observe. Mantas make multiple passes at the station over a 30–60 minute window. After the manta session, explore the adjacent reef to complete the dive before ascending.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access from Dampier Strait liveaboards or day operators. The site's modest depth (18m max) makes it accessible in calm conditions from most boats. Entry via giant stride or back roll. SMB deployment on ascent.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Do NOT kneel on the sandy bottom — "kneeling" is a colloquial term, but sand disturbance ruins the encounter for everyone
- Hold a horizontal position above the sand with neutral buoyancy — this is the correct approach
- Exhale slowly and deflect your bubbles away from the mantas during cleaning passes
- Do not reach out to touch mantas under any circumstances — oils from hands disrupt their mucus coat
- This site pairs naturally with Manta Point for a full manta-focused day

## Safety Considerations

The site is shallow and typically low-current. The primary consideration is responsible manta interaction — approaching too close, directing bubbles at mantas, or breaking the cleaning station rhythm can end the encounter for all divers. Carry an SMB. Avoid group entry that creates excessive surface disturbance near the cleaning station.

## Photography

Manta Sandy is arguably the finest manta photography site in Raja Ampat. The sandy bottom provides a clean, uncluttered background for manta portraits. Shooting upward from below the manta against the surface light produces dramatic silhouette shots. Wide-angle glass (10–17mm) captures the full wingspan. Getting below the manta and shooting upward is the definitive composition. The shallow depth means ambient light is plentiful without strobe assistance.
