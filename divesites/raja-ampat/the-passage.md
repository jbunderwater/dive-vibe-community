## The Passage

The Passage is one of Raja Ampat's most iconic dive experiences — a narrow, mangrove-lined channel between Waigeo and Gam islands where tidal currents carry divers through an atmospheric underwater landscape of soft corals, sea fans, and mangrove-root ecosystems.

**Note on nearby site:** The OSM dataset contains two separate nodes along this waterway: "Selat Kabui" (at -0.4321515, 130.5515835) and "The Passage" (~500 m east at -0.4286672, 130.5610216). These appear to be two entry/reference points along the same continuous passage rather than genuinely distinct dive sites. Selat Kabui is the Indonesian geographic name for the waterway; "The Passage" is the name used by dive operators. See also: [Selat Kabui](selat-kabui.md) and [Kabui Pass](kabui-pass.md).

## Overview

The Passage is a narrow fjord-like channel cutting between the limestone karst walls of Waigeo and Gam, connecting Kabui Bay to the Halmahera Sea. The channel measures roughly 25 metres wide and its shallowest main section reaches approximately 5 metres depth, with walls dropping to around 35 metres. Overhanging mangroves and rainforest meet the water's edge above, while below the surface the walls are colonised with sponges, soft coral, and sea fans. The site is described as like "diving in a jungle river" — an environment unlike typical open-reef diving in Raja Ampat.

The nutrient-rich tidal flow feeds extraordinary soft coral growth. Multiple sources confirm orange sea fans reaching towards the surface and large purple sponges. Visibility is typically lower than open-water Raja Ampat sites (sources cite 15–30 ft / 5–10 m) due to tannins and particulates from the mangrove system and tidal mixing.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Between Waigeo and Gam Islands, Raja Ampat, West Papua, Indonesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Drift dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate (strong currents; confident drift diving required)
- **Maximum Depth**: 25 metres (most interest in 5–15 m range)
- **Typical Visibility**: 5–10 metres (lower than open-reef sites due to nutrients/tannins)
- **Current**: Tidal; can be strong through the narrows
- **Best Time**: October to April; tidal timing critical

## Marine Life

Multiple operator sources confirm the following species at The Passage:

- **Soft corals and sea fans**: Confirmed by multiple operators as the defining feature — giant orange sea fans and large purple sponges colonising the passage walls
- **Pygmy seahorses**: Yellow/pink Denise's pygmy seahorses and orange/pink Bargibanti pygmy seahorses confirmed by Original Diving
- **Nudibranchs and flatworms**: Confirmed across multiple sources
- **Wobbegong sharks**: Confirmed by Original Diving
- **Turtles**: Confirmed by multiple operator sources
- **Fusiliers, sweetlips, moray eels**: Confirmed at site
- **Archer fish**: Confirmed by Papua Diving (hunting insects from mangrove surface)
- **Epaulette (walking) sharks**: Reported at night dives in the area by Papua Diving; presence in the shallow passage margins reported but primarily nocturnal

Reef sharks, rays, barracuda, and occasional manta/eagle rays have been reported. Mantas are described as rare and opportunistic rather than a reliable sighting here.

## Dive Profile

The dive is a tidal drift through the passage, typically from the upstream end to a designated exit. Most of the interest is in the 5–15 metre range along the passage walls and mangrove root zones. The boat cannot follow divers through the narrowest sections and repositions to meet divers at the exit point. Tidal timing is essential — always confirm direction and timing with your guide beforehand.

## Safety Considerations

Strong tidal currents require confident drift diving ability. Ascent mid-passage is not always straightforward — plan to reach the exit before surfacing. Carry an SMB. Deploy at the passage exit to signal the pickup boat.

**Saltwater crocodile:** ScubaBoard documented a resident saltwater crocodile in the Kabui Passage as of October 2018, and a fatal attack on a tourist was reported in 2016. Confirm current safety status with your operator before diving. The crocodile situation may have changed since 2018, but this is a known risk factor specific to this site.

## Photography

Wide-angle photography through the mangrove root zone and along the passage walls are the signature compositions. Shooting upward through the water surface toward the limestone karst is a classic Raja Ampat image. Macro subjects (pygmy seahorses, nudibranchs) are productive on the passage walls. Visibility is lower than open-reef sites — factor this into lighting and lens choice.
