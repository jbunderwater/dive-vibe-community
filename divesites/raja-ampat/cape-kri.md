## Cape Kri

Cape Kri is one of the most celebrated dive sites on Earth. Located off the northeastern tip of Kri Island in Raja Ampat, West Papua, Indonesia, it holds the world record for the greatest number of fish species counted on a single dive: 374 species recorded in approximately 90 minutes by marine biologist Dr. Gerald R. Allen. That record has never been broken and stands as the most powerful single data point for Raja Ampat's claim to the highest marine biodiversity on the planet.

## Overview

The site is a sloping wall beginning in the shallows at the cape's tip and dropping steeply along Kri Island's flank to 30–40 metres. Current sweeps around the point, delivering nutrients that have built an extraordinarily productive reef. The wall face is blanketed in coral growth from top to bottom — sea fans, soft corals, encrusting sponges, and hard coral colonies compete for every centimetre of substrate.

What makes Cape Kri extraordinary is the simultaneous presence of species from across the reef food web. Schools of bumphead parrotfish are a signature encounter, moving through the site and biting chunks from the reef with an audible crunch. Napoleon wrasse, measuring over a metre, patrol the wall face. Schools of batfish, surgeonfish, snappers, and fusiliers fill the mid-water in overlapping layers alongside large schools of chevron barracuda, giant trevally, and dogtooth tuna. Reef sharks — whitetip, blacktip, and grey reef — circle beneath the schooling fish. Hawksbill and green turtles graze the wall. In the rubble and coral crevices live the small fish that account for much of Dr. Allen's species count: pygmy seahorses in the sea fans, nudibranchs, scorpionfish, gobies, and flatworms in numbers that defy categorisation.

Current runs around the cape and can be strong, sweeping divers along the wall. Timing the dive with the appropriate tidal phase is important. Visibility averages 15–25 metres, sometimes reaching 30 metres in optimal conditions. Water temperature holds between 27–30°C.

## Marine Life

- Bumphead parrotfish schools
- Napoleon wrasse patrolling the wall
- Whitetip, blacktip, and grey reef sharks
- Green and hawksbill turtles
- Schools of batfish, snappers, surgeonfish, and fusiliers
- Chevron barracuda, giant trevally, dogtooth tuna, and Spanish mackerel
- Pygmy seahorses on sea fans; nudibranchs, scorpionfish, and flatworms on the wall
- 374 fish species recorded on a single dive (world record, Dr. Gerald R. Allen)

## Dive Profile

The dive begins at the cape tip and follows the sloping wall, drifting with the current to explore the full extent of the reef. Depths of 15–30 metres are used to access sea fans and the shark-patrolled deeper sections, with the ascent to shallower terrain for the safety stop passing through the densest schooling fish activity. Entry and exit are by boat, with the boat following the group around the point.

## Practical Information

- **Depth range**: 3–30 m (wall continues to 40 m+)
- **Visibility**: 15–30 m
- **Current**: Moderate to strong (tidal)
- **Best time**: October–April
- **Water temperature**: 27–30°C
- **Entry**: Boat
