## Plane wreck

The C-130 Hercules is a four-engine military transport aircraft deliberately sunk in November 2017 as part of Aqaba's artificial reef programme, and is one of the most photogenic wreck dives in the Red Sea.

## Overview

The Lockheed C-130 Hercules was donated by the Royal Jordanian Air Force and scuttled on 16 November 2017, with politicians, press, and a brass band in attendance, as part of King Abdullah II's initiative to expand Aqaba's dive tourism. The aircraft was carefully prepared: hazardous materials removed, doors removed to allow safe interior access. The plane measures 30 metres in length with a wingspan of approximately 40 metres, and sits upright on a flat sandy bottom at a maximum depth of 17 metres, with the tail fin rising to around 5 metres below the surface. A plastic skeleton dressed in a flight suit and helmet is strapped into the pilot's seat — one of the site's most photographed features.

On 17 March 2020, a severe storm struck Aqaba with gale-force winds, and the aluminum fuselage could not withstand the forces: the plane was torn into several pieces, with wings, nose, and fuselage separated. The tail section remains broadly intact. Despite the storm damage, the wreck continues to attract fish and coral growth, and the scattered sections add to the site's exploration interest. The site is near the M42 Duster tank and both are frequently combined in a single dive.
