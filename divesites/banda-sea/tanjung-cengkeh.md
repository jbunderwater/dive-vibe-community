## Tanjung Cengkeh

Tanjung Cengkeh ("Clove Cape") is a wall dive site named after the clove trees that historically covered this cape on Banda Besar — the Banda Islands were, for centuries, the world's only source of cloves and nutmeg. The site features a steep slope draped in large gorgonians and sponges, with consistent mild currents and good visibility.

## Overview

The wall drops steeply from shallow water to beyond 35 m, covered in a dense display of colourful gorgonians, barrel sponges, and soft corals. The site is known for relatively calm conditions compared to more exposed Banda Sea sites, making it accessible for intermediate divers while still offering rich marine life. Royal angelfish, various butterflyfish species, and surgeonfish are resident throughout the wall.

Napoleon wrasse and bumphead parrotfish make regular appearances, and the gorgonians harbour hawkfish and smaller reef associates. The mild current keeps the water clear, and visibility is typically good — sources report clean water with 20+ m visibility on calm days.

## Dive Profile

Steep slope and wall from shallow to 35 m+. Mild to moderate currents. Water temperature approximately 28°C. Good visibility, typically 20–30 m. Intermediate-friendly conditions for most of the year.

## Marine Life

- Napoleon wrasse and bumphead parrotfish (regular)
- Royal angelfish, various butterflyfish
- Surgeonfish, wrasse
- Gorgonian fans with resident hawkfish
- Barrel sponges and soft corals throughout
- Reef fish aggregations along the wall

## Entry / Exit

Boat dive from liveaboard or Banda Neira day-trip vessel. Banda Besar is a short transit from Banda Neira harbour.

## Tips

- Best for divers who want a calmer wall dive after more challenging sites like Batu Kapal or Pohon Miring
- The gorgonians are most impressive in 10–20 m depth range
- A pleasant second or third dive of the day when conditions at exposed sites are challenging

## Photography

Well-suited to close-focus wide-angle work on the gorgonian fans. Napoleon wrasse and bumphead parrotfish encounters lend themselves to wide-angle fish portraits. Clear water improves ambient-light compositions.
