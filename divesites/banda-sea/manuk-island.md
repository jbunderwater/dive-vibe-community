## Manuk Island

Manuk Island is a remote, uninhabited volcanic island roughly 100 miles south of the Banda Islands, accessible only by liveaboard. Known as "Snake Island," it is one of the most unusual dive destinations in the Coral Triangle, combining active geothermal activity with extraordinary concentrations of sea snakes.

## Overview

Manuk's walls and sandy volcanic seabed host thriving populations of banded sea kraits (*Laticauda colubrina*) and olive sea snakes, drawn in by warm geothermal vents rising from the seafloor. Divers routinely encounter dozens of sea snakes on a single dive, coiling through corals, hunting in crevices, and swimming openly through the water column. Though venomous, sea kraits are docile and inquisitive around divers.

Beyond the snakes, the reef walls are covered in healthy cabbage corals, porites, and barrel sponges. Pelagic action is strong: chevron and yellowtail barracuda, jacks, rainbow runners, and unicornfish school in open water. Hammerhead sharks have been reported at Manuk Ridge and Palm Point, particularly when upwelling currents are active.

## Dive Profile

Walls drop steeply from the surface to beyond recreational limits. Most diving occurs in the 10–35 m range along the volcanic rock walls and over warm black sand at the base. Water temperatures can reach 31°C near geothermal vent areas. Visibility is typically 20–30 m.

## Marine Life

- Banded sea kraits and olive sea snakes (exceptionally dense population)
- Scalloped hammerhead sharks (Manuk Ridge, Palm Point)
- Chevron and yellowtail barracuda
- Jacks, rainbow runners, unicornfish
- Barrel sponges, cabbage corals, porites

## Entry / Exit

Boat dive from liveaboard. Manuk is a 100-mile transit from the Banda Islands — the high seas and distance make it an infrequent stop on Banda Sea itineraries. Only liveaboard vessels reach this site.

## Tips

- Best season: October to April
- October–November new and full moons bring strongest upwelling and peak hammerhead activity
- Respect sea snakes — maintain distance and avoid blocking their path to the surface to breathe

## Safety

Currents can be moderate to strong. Advanced diving skills required. Sea snakes are venomous; do not handle or corner them. Work with an experienced liveaboard guide.

## Photography

Manuk is exceptional for wide-angle sea snake photography. The black volcanic sand provides strong contrast for close-focus wide-angle shots. Bring both wide-angle and macro setups — geothermal vent areas also yield macro subjects.
