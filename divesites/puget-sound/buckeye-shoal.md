# Buckeye Shoal

A productive shallow shoal in the northern San Juan Islands with dense anemone fields and excellent invertebrate diversity.

## Overview

Buckeye Shoal is a submerged rocky rise in the northern San Juan Islands, sitting in an area of consistent tidal flow that delivers nutrients to a thriving cold-water invertebrate community. The shoal's elevated position in the water column means it catches current from multiple directions, keeping the marine life well-fed. White plumose anemones dominate the rock faces, interspersed with orange and red encrusting sponges, tube worms, and hydroids. Depths range to 18 metres across the shoal's varied terrain.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Northern San Juan Islands, Puget Sound, Washington
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Rocky shoal / reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 18 metres
- **Typical Visibility**: 8–15 metres
- **Water Temperature**: 7–11°C
- **Current**: Tidal, can be significant; plan for slack
- **Best Time**: June to October

## Marine Life

Buckeye Shoal produces consistent encounters with lingcod and multiple rockfish species. The shoal structure provides habitat for giant Pacific octopus in the boulder piles, and wolf eels are occasionally encountered in the deeper rock formations. Copper rockfish, quillback rockfish, and kelp greenlings are resident year-round. Invertebrate life is excellent throughout: Puget Sound king crabs, red sea urchins, sea cucumbers, nudibranchs (several aeolid and dorid species), and swimming scallops are all commonly encountered. Harbour seals may appear from the surface to investigate.

## Dive Profile

The shoal crest may be as shallow as 4–5 metres, allowing a useful safety stop at the end of the dive. Work from the crest down the sloping faces to the outer edges at 18 metres, spending time on the most richly encrusted vertical surfaces. Currents over the shoal crest can be noticeable even at slack, so maintain awareness of your position relative to the boat.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access only. Anchor or hold position on the down-current side during the dive and pick up divers who drift off the shoal on a controlled ascent. Giant stride or back-roll entry. Deploy SMB before final ascent; surface upwind of the vessel.

## Tips

- Check the tidal exchange amplitude before committing to this site — large exchanges make for difficult conditions
- The shoal crest is a good place to spend extra time at the end of the dive; the density of anemones and invertebrates here is exceptional
- A dive torch is worth carrying for close examination of plumose anemone bases, where nudibranchs and shrimp often hide
- Kelp greenlings are curious fish and will approach divers; keep still and they may come very close

## Safety

Strong tidal currents are the primary safety concern at shoal sites. An accurate slack tide calculation is essential. If you arrive at the water to find current running, abort until the next slack window. Dry suit is mandatory for water temperatures in this range. Carry an SMB on every dive — boat traffic throughout the San Juan Islands is year-round.

## Photography

The dense plumose anemone coverage on the shoal makes for striking wide-angle images. Macro shooters will find nudibranchs and small crustaceans in the anemone columns. Lingcod sitting still on the rock top provide good portrait opportunities. Strobes essential for colour accuracy.
