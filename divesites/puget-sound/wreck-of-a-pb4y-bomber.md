## Wreck of a PB4Y bomber

The PB4Y Privateer is a legendary deep-water wreck dive in Lake Washington, famous for being one of the most challenging and technically demanding dives in the Pacific Northwest. This four-engine military patrol bomber crashed in 1956 and remains one of the region's most distinctive wreck sites.

## Overview

The PB4Y Privateer wreck sits at approximately 155 feet (47 meters) off Magnuson Park in Lake Washington. The aircraft crashed during a training flight on August 26, 1956, when the pilot failed to properly configure the aircraft for takeoff. A Navy recovery attempt briefly brought the wreck to the surface, but portions of the structure collapsed during salvage operations and the aircraft sank a second time. This is an advanced technical dive site requiring specialized training and decompression equipment. Visibility is typically poor (poor to terrible) due to Lake Washington's silt conditions, and requires advanced buoyancy control and navigation skills.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Lake Washington near Magnuson Park (not Puget Sound proper)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive

## Marine Life

Lake Washington is colder and less biologically rich than Puget Sound proper. Limited marine life diversity at depth. Occasional fish species. The wreck structure itself attracts minimal encrusting organisms due to depth and silt conditions.

## Dive Profile

This is strictly an advanced technical dive. Descent to the wreck requires approximately 3-5 minutes from surface. The fuselage, engines, and tail structure are visible but navigating the large aircraft frame requires careful buoyancy control to avoid stirring silt. Typical bottom time on this depth requires mandatory decompression stops. Visibility is often extreme-limited (2-5 feet), requiring dive torches and careful surface-to-wreck line following. Total dive time including decompression typically exceeds 60 minutes.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access required. Divers must use a launch site on Lake Washington (Warren G. Magnuson Park or similar). This dive requires advanced gas blending, decompression planning, and redundant equipment. Not suitable for recreational diving or sport divers without tech training and experience.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Bring a dive torch to illuminate wreck interiors and dark overhangs
- Maintain proper buoyancy to avoid disturbing silt inside the wreck
- Do not attempt penetration without proper training and equipment
- Bring an underwater camera — this site offers excellent photography opportunities
- Check local weather and sea conditions before diving
- Respect marine life and maintain proper buoyancy to protect the reef

## Safety Considerations

Be aware of cold water, surge, limited visibility in this area. Dive within your certification limits and experience level. Strong currents are possible — carry appropriate signaling devices and be prepared to abort the dive if conditions deteriorate. Always dive with a buddy and carry a safety sausage (SMB).

## Photography

The wreck structure provides dramatic wide-angle subjects with natural light filtering through openings. A torch is essential for illuminating interior details and bringing out colors. Macro opportunities abound on the encrusted surfaces.
