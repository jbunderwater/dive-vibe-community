## Puget Sound

Cold-water diving in the Pacific Northwest's emerald waters, home to giant Pacific octopus, wolf eels, and the rare bluntnose sixgill shark at recreational depths.

## Description

Puget Sound offers 34 documented dive sites spanning depths from 12 to 47 meters, with 24 reef sites, 8 wrecks, 1 wall, and 1 artificial reef. The nutrient-rich waters of this inland sea stretching from Olympia to the San Juan Islands support an extraordinary density of marine life, including some of the largest invertebrates on the planet. With more than 75 shore-diving sites within an hour of Seattle and over 160 sites across the region, Puget Sound is one of the most accessible cold-water diving destinations in North America.

### Diving Opportunities

- **Shore Diving**: 2 accessible shore dive sites including Edmonds Underwater Park and Seacrest Park (Cove 2), both popular training and recreational sites with easy entries
- **Boat Diving**: 30 boat-accessible sites throughout the San Juan Islands, Hood Canal, and central Puget Sound, with operators running two- and three-tank charters
- **Wreck Diving**: 8 wreck sites including the SS Dauntless (1923 Mosquito Fleet steamship), USCGC Tiger (Coast Guard vessel), and the Wreck of a PB4Y bomber in Lake Washington at 47 meters (technical dive)
- **Wall Diving**: Yellow Bluff Reef in Hood Canal drops to 24+ meters with dramatic wall structure

### Accessibility

- **Getting There**: Seattle-Tacoma International Airport (SEA) is the primary gateway, with Bellingham International Airport (BLI) serving northern sites near the San Juan Islands
- **Dive Operators**: Underwater Sports operates six locations across the region. Bandito Charters runs two- and three-tank boat dives out of Tacoma ($75-100). Lu-Jac's Quest offers boat diving from Anacortes for San Juan Islands sites
- **Accommodation**: Abundant options throughout the Seattle metro area and the San Juan Islands, from budget hotels to waterfront lodges
- **Transportation**: A car is essential for reaching shore dive sites. Washington State Ferries serve the San Juan Islands; some boat operators depart from Anacortes, Tacoma, and Seattle
- **Facilities**: Full-service dive shops throughout the Seattle metro area offer rental gear including drysuits, fills, and guided dives

### Marine Life & Environment

- **Water Conditions**: Water temperatures range 7-13C (45-55F) year-round. Winter offers the best visibility at up to 30 meters (100 ft), while summer visibility typically drops to 3-15 meters (10-50 ft) due to algae blooms and glacier runoff. Strong tidal currents of 16-20 feet on full and half moons affect many sites
- **Marine Biodiversity**: Giant Pacific octopus (up to 150 lbs with 6-meter arm span), wolf eels, bluntnose sixgill sharks (best chances July-August at Redondo Beach), Steller sea lions, lingcod, rockfish, plumose anemones, nudibranchs, Dungeness crab, and colorful sea stars
- **Conservation**: Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife maintains Octopus Protection Areas throughout Puget Sound. Several marine reserves protect critical habitats in the San Juan Islands

## Additional Information

- **Best Time to Visit**: Winter (November-March) for best visibility up to 30 meters; summer (June-August) for warmer water and longer daylight, with sixgill shark encounters at Redondo Beach in July-August
- **Currency**: USD
- **Language**: English
- **Safety**: Drysuit required year-round due to cold water temperatures. Strong tidal currents are common, especially in the San Juan Islands — always plan dives around slack tide. A cold-water rated regulator is recommended. Many sites require current tables and careful dive planning
