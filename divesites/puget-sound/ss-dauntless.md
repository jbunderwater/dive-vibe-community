## SS Dauntless

The SS Dauntless is a historic Mosquito Fleet steamship resting in Puget Sound near Shilshole. This 93-foot wooden-hulled passenger ferry sank in 1923 and offers an excellent introduction to cold-water wreck diving.

## Overview

The SS Dauntless is a well-preserved wreck dive site featuring the remains of a historic steam-powered passenger ferry from the Mosquito Fleet era. Built in 1899 and sank in 1923, this vessel represents the maritime heritage of the Pacific Northwest. The wreck rests at approximately 70 feet (21 meters) and is boat-accessed from the Shilshole area. The wooden hull structure provides good exploration opportunities and the site attracts abundant cold-water marine life. Typical visibility ranges from 10-40 feet depending on season.

## Site Information

- **Location**: North of Shilshole, Puget Sound, North Seattle, Washington
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 21 meters
- **Water Temperature**: 8–12°C year-round

## Wreck History

The *Dauntless* was a 93-foot wooden-hulled passenger steamer operating as part of the Puget Sound Mosquito Fleet — the network of small private vessels that provided passenger and freight transport on Puget Sound before automobile ferries became dominant. She was owned by the Moe Brothers and was retired from service in 1923. On December 30, 1923, a storm caused the *Dauntless* to break loose from her moorings at Appletree Cove and drift across the Sound, ultimately grounding and sinking near Meadow Point, north of Shilshole. The wreck is accessible from the Shilshole launch by kayak or boat.

## Marine Life

The encrusted wooden hull hosts plumose anemones, nudibranchs, and colorful bryozoan mats. Lingcod and cabezon claim territories within the structure, and copper and quillback rockfish shelter among exposed timbers. Giant Pacific octopus may be found in sheltered hull sections. The Shilshole area's proximity to Seattle's central Sound brings reasonable populations of these cold-water species.

## Dive Profile

Depth approximately 21 metres. Single-tank dive. Drysuit essential. Wreck profile is relatively flat — wooden hull largely collapsed — so the site is explored as a debris field rather than an intact structure. Visibility in the Shilshole area typically 5–12 metres.

## Entry and Exit

Launch from Eddie Vine boat ramp at Shilshole Marina. The site is described as a manageable kayak or boat dive. GPS/depth sounder recommended as the wreck is not marked with a buoy.

## Tips and Recommendations

Bring a dive light to inspect the remaining hull cavities. Steel wrecks in the area affect compass accuracy; use GPS coordinates recorded before descent. The site is shallow enough for multiple passes but drysuit inflation and buoyancy management are important at this depth.

## Safety Considerations

Drysuit mandatory. Plan around slack water — tidal currents affect visibility and comfort. Derelict wooden structure poses entanglement risk. Carry SMB. Visibility can drop significantly in spring plankton season.
