## Edmonds Underwater Park

Edmonds Underwater Park is the flagship recreational dive site in Puget Sound — a 27-acre Marine Conservation Area and Bird Sanctuary with a signature underwater trail system, sunken wrecks, and some of the largest lingcod and cabezon documented anywhere on the West Coast.

## Overview

Located immediately north of the Edmonds–Kingston Washington State Ferry terminal, this no-take zone offers shore diving year-round on a shallow, well-marked site that reaches a maximum depth of 45 feet (14 metres). The park is maintained entirely by volunteers who lay and repair an anchored grid of ropes — marked with road-sign-style indicators — guiding divers through multiple submerged attractions. Water temperature holds at 45–55°F (7–13°C) year-round; a drysuit or thick wetsuit with hood and gloves is required. Visibility ranges from as little as 2 feet to 40 feet depending on season and tidal conditions, with autumn and winter typically the clearest.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Edmonds, Washington (north of Seattle), immediately north of Edmonds ferry terminal
- **Entry Type**: beach
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 14 meters
- **Water Temperature**: 7–13°C year-round
- **Conservation Status**: No-take zone, Washington Dept. of Fish & Wildlife

## Wrecks and Features

The park's primary artificial attractions include:

- **Tugboat Triumph**: An approximately 85-foot (26m) tug scuttled within the park boundaries — the main structural feature and a focal point for marine life.
- **Lopez Pontoon**: A concrete structure (approximately 100 by 20 feet) sunk November 4, 2009, featuring five chambers divers can easily enter and exit.
- The underwater rope grid system, rebuilt and maintained by weekend volunteer crews, prevents divers from getting disoriented in low-visibility conditions.

## Marine Life

Edmonds is documented to host very large lingcod and cabezon — some of the largest reported on the Pacific coast. Giant Pacific octopus are occasionally seen. Copper and quillback rockfish are common, along with surfperch, nudibranchs, and encrusting invertebrates (plumose anemones, sea stars, urchins, crabs). Gray whales have been spotted by divers at this site. Harbour seals pass through the park.

## Dive Profile

From the beach entry, follow the rope grid out to the descent point. The trail system runs east-west and north-south, with underwater signs at intersections. Shallow sections at 20–30 feet are ideal for orientation; the Triumph wreck and deeper sections extend to 45 feet. A single long dive of 40–60 minutes is typical, given the distance of the surface swim and the shallow maximum depth. Plan a safety stop at 15 feet before return.

## Entry and Exit

Shore entry from the dedicated diver beach. Parking, changing rooms, outdoor shower, and restrooms are available. Keep clear of the 300-foot exclusion zone near the active ferry terminal. The site is accessible at all tide stages. A local dive shop is nearby for air fills and equipment.

## Safety Considerations

Cold water is the primary hazard: drysuit or 7mm wetsuit with hood and gloves is mandatory for safe, comfortable diving. Visibility can drop to 2 feet or less during spring plankton blooms. The ferry exclusion zone must be respected — the Edmonds–Kingston route runs close to the park boundary. Carry a surface marker buoy (SMB) even on a shore dive.
