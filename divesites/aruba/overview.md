## Aruba

The SS Antilla — one of the largest diveable shipwrecks in the Caribbean at 127 meters — anchors a wreck-rich destination with calm, warm waters and year-round diving outside the hurricane belt.

## Description

Aruba, the westernmost of the ABC islands, sits just 29 km north of Venezuela and outside the main Caribbean hurricane belt, making it one of the most reliable year-round dive destinations in the region. Our database documents 14 sites ranging from 5 to 27 meters depth, including wrecks, reef sites, and wall dives. The island's sheltered west and south coasts provide calm, accessible conditions, while the wreck diving — led by the massive SS Antilla — ranks among the best in the Caribbean. Aruba's relatively shallow maximum depths and protected conditions make it particularly welcoming for newer divers while still offering enough wreck complexity to engage advanced divers.

### Diving Opportunities

- **Wreck Diving**: Wreck sites, making Aruba one of the most wreck-dense destinations in the Caribbean relative to its size. The SS Antilla (a 127-meter German cargo ship scuttled by her own captain on May 10, 1940) is the centerpiece, sitting upright at 18-20 meters with sections accessible for penetration by qualified divers. Other wrecks include the Pedernales (a WWII tanker torpedoed by a U-boat), the Jane Sea (deeper at ~27m), the California (a pre-WWII passenger steamship at 10-15m), and the Sonesta Plane Wreck.
- **Boat Diving**: Boat-accessible sites along the sheltered west coast. Two-tank boat dives typically cost $90-120 USD.
- **Shore Diving**: Shore-accessible sites, including reef dives along the south coast.
- **Reef Diving**: Reef sites with healthy Caribbean hard and soft corals, sponge gardens, and abundant reef fish along the leeward coast.
- **Wall Diving**: Wall dive site on the island's leeward side.
- **Night Diving**: Available through operators, with the Antilla being a popular night dive where the wreck comes alive with bioluminescence and nocturnal species.

### Accessibility

- **Getting There**: Queen Beatrix International Airport (AUA) receives direct flights from Miami, New York (JFK/EWR), Atlanta, Houston, Toronto, Amsterdam, and other major cities. The airport is centrally located near Oranjestad, close to most dive operations.
- **Dive Operators**: Aruba Addicts, Fly 'n Dive Aruba, Native Divers Aruba, Scuba Aruba, JADS Diving, and Red Sail Sports. Most are PADI-certified and operate from the hotel strip along Palm Beach and Eagle Beach.
- **Accommodation**: Wide range from budget apartments in Oranjestad to high-rise resorts on Palm Beach. Eagle Beach and Palm Beach areas offer the easiest access to dive operators.
- **Transportation**: Rental cars, taxis, and hotel shuttles. The island is compact (30 km long) and easy to navigate. Most dive sites are accessed from the west coast.
- **Facilities**: Full tourist infrastructure with hospitals, pharmacies, and equipment rental readily available. Aruba has a recompression chamber for dive emergencies.

### Marine Life & Environment

- **Water Conditions**: Water temperature 27-29°C (80-85°F) year-round — a 3mm wetsuit is sufficient. Visibility typically 20-30 meters (60-100 ft) on the leeward coast, with the best clarity May through August. The northeast coast is exposed to strong trade winds and rougher conditions, reserved for experienced divers.
- **Marine Biodiversity**: Four species of sea turtle (green, loggerhead, hawksbill, leatherback), spotted eagle rays, southern stingrays, seahorses in seagrass beds, moray eels, groupers, parrotfish, angelfish, and dense schools of reef fish. The Antilla wreck hosts large groupers, barracuda, and green moray eels within its structure. Sea turtle activity peaks May through November.
- **Conservation**: Aruba's Marine Park protects reef areas along the southern and western coasts. The island has enacted turtle conservation programs, and several dive operators participate in reef monitoring and cleanup efforts.

## Additional Information

- **Best Time to Visit**: Year-round diving. Aruba sits below the hurricane belt, making it one of the safest Caribbean destinations during hurricane season (June-November). May through August offers the calmest seas and best visibility. High tourist season runs December through mid-April.
- **Currency**: Aruban Florin (AWG); US dollars universally accepted.
- **Language**: Papiamento (local language), Dutch (official), English and Spanish widely spoken.
- **Safety**: Recompression chamber available on-island. DAN insurance recommended. The SS Antilla's shallowest sections are at ~12 meters (suitable for Open Water divers), but penetration dives require Advanced certification and experienced guidance. The Jane Sea wreck has strong currents and is recommended for advanced divers only.
