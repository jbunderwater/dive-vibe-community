## Pedernales Wreck

The Pedernales is the midsection of a 4,317-GRT British oil tanker torpedoed on February 16, 1942, resting in approximately 8 meters of water — one of Aruba's most accessible wreck dives and a direct piece of World War II history.

## Overview

The SS Pedernales was a lake tanker built in 1938 at Cantiere Riuniti dell'Adriatico, Monfalcone, Italy, operating for Lago Shipping Co. (Esso) between Lake Maracaibo, Venezuela, and the Aruba refinery. In the early morning of February 16, 1942, as part of Operation Neuland, U-156 (commanded by Kapitänleutnant Werner Hartenstein) surfaced and fired a single torpedo at the Pedernales while she lay at anchor. Hit amidships while carrying crude oil, the tanker immediately caught fire. Eight of the 26 crew members were killed; the master, Herbert McCall, was wounded.

The vessel was cut into three sections at the Lago dry dock. The fore and aft sections were welded together and transported under their own power to Baltimore for reconstruction — the rebuilt ship continued sailing until scrapped in Rotterdam in 1959. The midsection was left in Aruba and used for years as a bombing and gunnery target by the Dutch Navy after the war, which scattered the remnants into roughly eight sections of wreckage. What remains rests in approximately 8 meters of water, covered in encrusting corals and sponges.

The site is beginner-friendly: shallow, calm, with no penetration required and excellent visibility. Local operators describe it as one of Aruba's richest dive sites for sheer marine life variety.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Off Aruba's northwest coast, in front of Palm Beach
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 8 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–25 meters
- **Current**: Generally light
- **Best Time**: Year-round

## Marine Life

Documented species at this site include stingrays, sea turtles, pufferfish, scorpionfish, lobster, moray eels, and schools of grunts and goatfish. The flattened, coral-encrusted wreck sections create varied habitat that supports both reef fish and benthic species. Hawksbill turtles have been widely reported resting on the wreck.

*Note: Nurse sharks and eagle rays appear in some general Aruba wreck listings but are not confirmed by available sources as regular sightings at the Pedernales specifically. Removed per anti-hallucination policy.*

## Dive Profile

The wreck is broken into multiple sections across a wide area. At 8 meters maximum depth, the entire site can be explored in a single dive at a leisurely pace. Begin at the largest hull section and work outward through the scattered debris field, noting the coral and sponge growth on all surfaces. A safety stop at 5 meters is easily accomplished above the wreck.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive. Giant stride or back roll entry. Follow operator briefing for mooring and ascent procedures. Deploy SMB during safety stop.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Excellent choice for newly certified divers — conditions are consistently calm
- Snorkelable for strong swimmers, the wreck is that shallow
- The scattered nature of the wreckage means navigating the full debris field takes planning
- Pair with the Antilla for a contrasting double wreck day

## Safety Considerations

Standard precautions for boat diving. No overhead environments — wreck sections are open and flat. Always dive with a buddy and carry an SMB.

## Photography

The coral-encrusted wreck sections at very shallow depth allow for excellent natural-light photography without a torch. Wide-angle shots of the flat hull plates with turtle subjects above are a popular frame.
