## Sonesta Plane Wreck

Two intentionally sunk aircraft lie in front of Renaissance Island off Aruba's western coast: a scattered Convair 240 and the intact, upright NAMC YS-11 turboprop — together creating one of the Caribbean's most unusual wreck dives.

## Overview

The site holds two distinct aircraft at different depths. The **Convair 240** was sunk in the shallower Sonesta reef area but was subsequently scattered by storms, including Hurricane Lenny (1999). Its components — wing, propellers, fuselage sections, tail, and cockpit — now range from about 14 meters (shallowest wing tip) to 22 meters (cockpit). Despite being dispersed, the individual pieces are still distinctive and covered in encrusting corals.

The **NAMC YS-11** (a 13-tonne Japanese-built turboprop that had belonged to Air Aruba) was sunk in 2004 by the Aruba Water Sports Association after the aircraft was abandoned at Queen Beatrix International Airport. It rests intact and upright, with its cockpit at 16 meters and its tail at 27 meters. Passenger seats and four doors were removed before sinking, making the interior easy to penetrate — divers can swim through the fuselage its entire length from the rear doors to the cockpit.

Note: Some sources previously cited a "Beech 18" as one of the Sonesta aircraft. Multiple operator sources (Pure Diving Aruba, Palm Beach Divers) confirm the two aircraft are a Convair 240 and an Air Aruba YS-11; no Beech 18 is documented at this site.

## Site Information

- **Location**: In front of Renaissance Island, western Aruba
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 27 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–25 meters
- **Current**: Generally light
- **Best Time**: Year-round

## Marine Life

Documented species at this site include barracuda (large individuals under the YS-11 wings), octopus, moray eels, pufferfish, flounders, angelfish, silversides, stingrays, and occasional spotted eagle rays. Banded coral shrimp have been reported in the YS-11 cockpit. Both aircraft are colonized by encrusting sponges and soft corals.

## Dive Profile

A typical dive covers both aircraft in sequence. Begin at the YS-11: descend to the cockpit at 16 meters, explore the fuselage interior (penetration is straightforward with good buoyancy — the hull is clear of obstructions), exit at the tail at 27 meters, then ascend to the Convair debris field at 14–22 meters. Manage bottom time carefully as the combined depth profile can accumulate quickly. Safety stop at 5 meters.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive only. Approximately 300 meters offshore. Giant stride or back roll. Deploy SMB for safety stop and boat pickup.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Swim through the YS-11 fuselage from rear to front — it is large enough for comfortable passage with good buoyancy control
- A torch helps in the darker interior of the YS-11
- The Convair components are scattered — take a compass bearing from the YS-11 to locate the main Convair sections
- This site is often paired with Antilla as a two-tank day

## Safety Considerations

Interior penetration of the YS-11 should not be attempted without proper buoyancy control — the cleared fuselage is straightforward, but panicking inside a confined space is a documented dive incident pattern. The YS-11 tail section at 27 meters approaches recreational limits; monitor depth and air consumption carefully.

## Photography

The intact YS-11 is one of the most dramatic aircraft wreck subjects in the Caribbean — the cockpit window frames and instrument panel interior make compelling subjects. The scattered Convair propellers and fuselage sections partially covered in coral offer excellent macro and wide-angle subjects.
