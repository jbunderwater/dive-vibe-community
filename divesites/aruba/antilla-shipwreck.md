## Antilla Shipwreck

This OSM polygon entry represents the same SS Antilla — a 398-foot German freighter scuttled May 10, 1940 — as the curated "SS Antilla Wreck" entry. The coordinates here correspond to the OSM polygon centroid of the wreck outline; both entries refer to a single dive site.

## Overview

The Antilla is Aruba's signature wreck dive and, by widespread consensus, the largest wreck accessible to recreational divers in the Caribbean. Built in 1939 in Hamburg for the Hamburg America Line (4,363 GRT), the freighter was scuttled by her German crew on May 10, 1940, as Dutch marines attempted to board her following Germany's invasion of the Netherlands. Captain Schmidt's crew opened the seacocks and set fires; a post-sinking inspection confirmed the hull went down intact, with the break amidships caused by a 1953 storm rather than any explosion.

She lies on her port side with the shallowest steel visible a few meters below the surface and the propeller resting at approximately 18 meters. The wreck hull is visible above the waterline at low tide in places. The sheer length — nearly 122 meters — means divers typically choose one section per dive: bow, midship cargo holds, engine room, or stern.

The orange cup corals, tube sponges, and anemones that encrust the hull have taken decades to establish and are one of the site's most striking visual features. Documented marine life includes green turtles, moray eels, stingrays, scorpionfish, octopus, schools of silversides, angelfish, and bluehead wrasse. Pelicans rest on the surface sections and dive for silversides.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Malmok Bay, northwestern Aruba
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 18 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–30 meters
- **Current**: Generally light
- **Best Time**: December to April (dry season, calmest seas)

## Marine Life

Consistently documented species at this site: green sea turtles, moray eels, stingrays, octopus, scorpionfish, occasional yellow frogfish, large schools of silversides and glassy sweepers, angelfish, yellowtail snappers, and damselfish. The scale and structural variety of the wreck support a broad range of reef fish.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the hull (5–8 meters at shallowest). Divers typically work along the exterior toward either the bow or stern, with the propeller marking the deepest point at 18 meters. Interior penetration of cargo holds and engine room is possible with proper training and a torch, but conditions are silty and structural deterioration is significant. Safety stop at 5 meters.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive only. Pickup typically from Hadicurari pier. Giant stride or back roll. Deploy SMB at safety stop for pickup.

## Tips and Recommendations

- A torch is essential for interior sections and to reveal the true colours of the encrusting corals
- Do not attempt penetration without wreck training and proper equipment
- Some interior sections are structurally unsound — stay aware of overhead environments
- Diveable by Open Water level, but experience with wrecks significantly enhances the dive
- Morning dives provide the best natural light on the hull exterior

## Safety Considerations

The wreck has deteriorated over 85+ years. Some overhangs are unstable and some interior sections are not safe to enter. Dive within your experience level.

## Photography

One of the most photogenic wide-angle wreck subjects in the Caribbean. The density of encrusting corals and scale of the hull allow for compelling compositions in multiple sections. Torch photography inside the holds reveals brilliant oranges and reds.
