## Kelleston Drain

Kelleston Drain is Tobago's most iconic advanced drift dive — a deep channel in the northeast where the Guiana Current funnels through with enough force to carry divers on an exhilarating, manta-ray-haunted passage that represents the pinnacle of Tobago diving for experienced divers.

## Overview

The "drain" is a natural channel formed between reef structures in the Speyside area, through which the Guiana Current flows with particular intensity. Depths reach 40 meters in the channel floor, and the walls and coral formations on either side are covered with some of the Caribbean's most impressive encrusting life — black coral, sea fans, and massive sponge structures thrive in the current-fed environment. Kelleston Drain is not a site to explore at leisure; it is a drift dive where buoyancy control, current reading, and experience are prerequisites. Visibility is often exceptional, reaching 30 meters or more, and cold upwelling water can drop temperatures below 22°C.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Speyside, northeast Tobago
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Drift dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 40 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–35 meters
- **Current**: Strong; this is a drift dive
- **Best Time**: October to February (peak manta season and upwelling)

## Marine Life

Kelleston Drain is celebrated for manta ray encounters — the current concentrates plankton and the mantas follow. Multiple sources confirm manta ray sightings at the site, with peak encounters typically from October to February during the upwelling season, though manta encounters can occur year-round. Nurse sharks and Caribbean reef sharks are confirmed at the site. Large barracuda and large green moray eels are present. The most distinctive biological feature is the giant brain coral measuring approximately 10 feet (3 metres) high by 16 feet (5.3 metres) wide, which multiple sources (ERIC Tobago, destinationtnt.com) describe as the largest brain coral in the western hemisphere or Caribbean. The brain coral colony is noted as extremely fragile despite its size.

## Dive Profile

Entry is timed with current direction. Drop into the current at the channel entrance and drift through at depth, following the guide's signals. The channel floor at 35–40 meters should be visited briefly before ascending to the walls and mid-channel zone. Divers ascend as a group on SMBs when the guide signals the exit point. Live-boat pickup only — do not attempt to return to the entry point against the current.

## Entry and Exit

Only with operators who know the site, the current timing, and the safe entry and exit points. Live-boat pickup mandatory. All divers must deploy SMBs at the signal. Never attempt independently.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Only dive Kelleston Drain with an operator who has a proven track record at this specific site
- Time your trip for October to February for the highest probability of manta ray encounters
- A 5mm wetsuit minimum is recommended — upwelling water can reach 20–22°C
- Drift diving experience is a genuine prerequisite — the current is not negotiable
- This dive is one of the Caribbean's great experiences for appropriately qualified divers — don't rush the planning

## Safety Considerations

Strong current is the defining characteristic and primary safety consideration. All divers must be experienced drift divers. SMBs are mandatory and must be deployable in current. The 40-meter depth limits NDL; plan conservatively and ascend before limits are reached. Cold water, strong current, and significant depth make this one of the Caribbean's most demanding recreational dives.

## Photography

Kelleston Drain offers unparalleled Caribbean wide-angle photography. Manta rays in strong current, reef sharks in the channel passage, and coral walls swept by current create images of power and scale rarely achievable elsewhere in the Caribbean. Camera setup must be pre-completed before entry — there is no opportunity to adjust settings in the drift.
