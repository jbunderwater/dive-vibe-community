## Maverick Wreck

The M.V. Maverick — originally named the Scarlet Ibis — is a former Trinidad-Tobago inter-island ferry deliberately sunk in April 1997 to create an artificial reef, now resting upright at 30 metres off Crown Point with the top deck at around 18 metres.

## Overview

The vessel originally served as the MV Scarlet Ibis, providing the inter-island shipping link between Trinidad and Tobago in the 1960s and 1970s alongside her sister ship the MV Bird of Paradise. After retirement, the ferry was renamed the Maverick and deliberately sunk in April 1997 off Crown Point on Tobago's southwestern coast. Sources disagree on the vessel's exact length: visittobago.gov.tt cites 198 feet 9 inches, while dive operators and PADI commonly describe it as 185 feet — both refer to the same vessel. The wreck rests upright on a flat sandy seabed at approximately 30 metres (100 feet), with the top deck at 18 metres (60 feet). The superstructure has developed fan coral encrustation and attracts large schools of Creole wrasse, striped grunts, and mangrove snappers. A resident green moray eel and visiting cobia are reliable encounters. The Maverick is the most frequently visited wreck in Tobago and is used for Advanced Open Water, Deep Diver, and Wreck Diver certifications. Visibility averages 15–25 metres. Water temperature is 26–28°C.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Crown Point, southwestern Tobago
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–25 meters

## Marine Life

Large schools of Creole wrasse, brown chromis, striped grunts, and mangrove snappers swirl around the superstructure. A resident great barracuda holds position at the wreck, and cobia make regular visits. A large green moray eel is a consistent resident. The car deck interior provides sheltered habitat for reef fish communities. The fan coral encrustation on the superstructure supports invertebrate communities.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the top deck at 18 metres and explore the superstructure before proceeding to the car deck level and the sandy bottom at 30 metres. The wreck's upright position makes orientation straightforward. NDL limits at 30 metres are restrictive — plan for 20–25 minutes at the deepest sections before ascending to the superstructure for remaining bottom time. Safety stop at 5 metres.
