# Staghorn Crest

Extensive staghorn coral formations blanket the reef crest above a soft-coral-draped wall, creating one of Sipadan's healthiest and most colourful shallow reef environments

## Overview
Staghorn Crest is named for the dense fields of staghorn coral (Acropora) that dominate the reef crest before the wall drops away. This site showcases Sipadan's reef health at its finest — the staghorn formations are extensive and pristine, creating a complex three-dimensional habitat that teems with reef fish. The shallow coral gardens transition to a wall below where soft corals take over, providing a striking contrast between the hard coral plateau and the soft coral wall. Turtles graze among the staghorn fields, and the healthy reef supports a rich community of butterflyfish, angelfish, and damselfish. The moderate depth and generally manageable currents make this an accessible and visually rewarding dive.

## Site Information
- **Location**: Sipadan Island, Sabah, Malaysia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15-30 metres
- **Current**: Mild to moderate
- **Best Time**: April to December for best visibility and conditions

## Marine Life
The staghorn coral fields are the foundation of the ecosystem here. These branching formations provide shelter for thousands of small fish — damselfish, chromis, and anthias swarm above the coral, creating clouds of colour. Butterflyfish species feed among the branches — look for raccoon, lined, and saddled butterflyfish. Emperor and regal angelfish patrol their territories. Green and hawksbill turtles are commonly seen grazing on sponges and algae in the coral gardens, and their relaxed behaviour suggests regular feeding in this area. Below the reef crest, the wall is decorated with soft corals, small gorgonians, and crinoids. Whitetip reef sharks occasionally pass along the wall edge. Trumpetfish, cornetfish, and longnose hawkfish are found among the coral formations.

## Dive Profile
Begin on the reef crest at 5-10 metres, exploring the staghorn coral fields and their associated marine life. Work gradually toward the wall edge where the hard coral transitions to soft coral on the wall face. Descend along the wall to 15-20 metres to see the soft coral growth and check for sharks along the wall edge. Return to the reef crest for an extended shallow phase — the rich coral gardens and fish life make the safety stop one of the most enjoyable parts of the dive. The shallow maximum depth allows long bottom times.

## Entry and Exit
Boat dive from Mabul or Kapalai island resorts. Giant stride entry onto the reef crest area. The moderate conditions and shallow profile make entry and exit straightforward. Deploy an SMB during ascent for boat pickup.

## Tips and Recommendations
- Spend ample time on the reef crest — the staghorn coral gardens are the highlight and are best appreciated slowly
- Excellent buoyancy is essential in the coral gardens — the staghorn formations are brittle and easily broken by fin strikes
- Look for hawkfish perched on sea fans and gorgonians along the wall — they hold still for photographers
- Sipadan permits (120 per day) are required and must be arranged through your resort
- The shallow profile makes this an ideal late-afternoon dive when no-decompression limits are reduced from earlier deep dives

## Safety Considerations
The shallow depth and moderate currents make this one of Sipadan's most forgiving sites, but care is still needed. The brittle staghorn coral formations can cause cuts if a diver makes contact, and the damage to the coral is disproportionate — a single fin kick can destroy years of growth. Maintain a safe distance above the reef at all times. An SMB and signalling device are standard equipment. The nearest hyperbaric chamber is in Kota Kinabalu.

## Photography
The staghorn coral fields photographed from above with natural light create stunning reef-scape images that showcase Sipadan's reef health. Wide-angle with a slow shutter for ambient light works well in the shallow, bright conditions. Turtles grazing in the coral gardens provide strong foreground subjects. The transition zone where hard coral gives way to soft coral on the wall edge makes a compelling composition. Macro opportunities include the numerous small reef fish, hawkfish portraits, and invertebrates in the coral branches. Butterflyfish pairs make colourful subjects against the coral backdrop.
