# South Point

Sipadan's premier hammerhead shark site, where strong currents sweep the island's southern tip and deep-water encounters with scalloped hammerheads reward early morning dives

## Overview
South Point marks the southern extremity of Sipadan Island, where prevailing currents collide against the reef wall to create powerful upwellings and current-swept conditions that attract pelagic predators. This is Sipadan's best site for scalloped hammerhead shark sightings, typically spotted in deeper water below 30 metres during early morning dives when they rise from the depths. The wall features dramatic overhangs and small cave formations along its face. Strong currents make this site demanding but rewarding — the same forces that challenge divers are what draw the big animals.

## Site Information
- **Location**: Sipadan Island, Sabah, Malaysia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 40 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15-40 metres
- **Current**: Strong to very strong — consistently one of the most current-exposed sites
- **Best Time**: April to December; early morning for hammerhead sightings

## Marine Life
Scalloped hammerhead sharks are the main draw, typically seen in groups below 30 metres during early morning dives when the water is cooler and the sharks move to shallower depths. Grey reef sharks patrol the wall edge and are seen on most dives. Schooling chevron barracuda pass through regularly, though not in the massive numbers seen at Barracuda Point. Bumphead parrotfish move along the reef crest. Eagle rays are spotted gliding along the wall. Green and hawksbill turtles are abundant. The wall overhangs shelter whitetip reef sharks resting during the day. Schools of bigeye trevally and fusiliers add to the constant movement of fish life along the current-swept wall.

## Dive Profile
Typically dived as a deep drift dive along the southern wall. Negative entry and rapid descent to 30-35 metres to maximize the chance of hammerhead encounters in the deeper water. Scan the blue water below and along the wall edge. Rise gradually along the wall, checking overhangs for resting whitetips and exploring small cave formations. The upper wall and reef crest between 5 and 15 metres offer turtle encounters and schooling fish during the safety stop phase. Current strength often increases around the point itself — be prepared for a fast drift.

## Entry and Exit
Boat dive from Mabul or Kapalai island resorts. Giant stride entry with negative descent due to strong surface currents. The divemaster will time entry based on current direction. Deploy an SMB well before surfacing — currents at the southern tip can carry divers rapidly away from the island. Boat pickup in open water is common at this site.

## Tips and Recommendations
- Request the earliest dive slot for the best hammerhead probability — they are most commonly seen before 8 AM in deeper water
- Carry a reef hook for holding position on the wall edge while watching the blue water for pelagic activity
- Stay close to the wall when current is strongest — the overhangs provide shelter from the full force
- Sipadan permits (120 per day) must be secured in advance through your resort
- Nitrox is strongly recommended to extend bottom time at depth where the hammerheads are encountered

## Safety Considerations
South Point regularly experiences the strongest currents on Sipadan. Down-currents along the wall are a real hazard and can pull divers below planned depths rapidly. Monitor depth constantly and be prepared to inflate your BCD and fin away from the wall if caught in a downward pull. The deep profile required for hammerhead encounters demands careful gas management and no-decompression limit awareness. An SMB, whistle, and GPS marker or mirror are essential. Never fight the current. The nearest hyperbaric chamber is in Kota Kinabalu.

## Photography
Wide-angle with ambient light for hammerhead silhouettes against the blue water — strobes rarely reach the sharks at the distances they are typically observed. Grey reef sharks along the wall edge make more approachable subjects for strobe-lit wide-angle. The overhangs with resting whitetips and soft corals provide strong compositions. Eagle rays passing along the wall are dramatic but often fast-moving subjects. The reef crest offers turtle portraits during decompression.
