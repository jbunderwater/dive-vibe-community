# B-25 Mitchell Bomber

WWII B-25D serial 41-30118, nicknamed "Green Dragon" -- shot down by anti-aircraft fire on 4 August 1943 while attacking Madang Airfield. Intact except for the missing port engine, with four nose guns still loaded with ammunition, an accessible cockpit, dorsal turret, and tail gun.

## Overview

This B-25D Mitchell medium bomber, serial number 41-30118 and nicknamed "Green Dragon," was shot down by Japanese anti-aircraft fire on 4 August 1943 during a low-level attack on Madang Airfield. The aircraft came down off Wangat Island, where it rests at 15-25 meters depth. The wreck is remarkably intact -- the port engine is missing (likely torn away by the anti-aircraft hit or on impact), but the rest of the airframe is well-preserved. Four nose-mounted machine guns are still in place, complete with ammunition. The cockpit is accessible and retains structural detail. The dorsal turret and tail gun positions are identifiable. A large gorgonian sea fan has grown between the distinctive twin tail fins, adding a striking natural contrast to the wartime metal. Visibility is typically 8-10 meters, more limited than open-water sites, which adds to the atmospheric quality of the dive.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Off Wangat Island, Madang Province, Papua New Guinea
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 25 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 8-10 meters
- **Current**: Mild
- **Best Time**: Year-round

## Marine Life

The aircraft has become an artificial reef over eight decades. The gorgonian sea fan growing between the twin tail fins is the most distinctive marine growth on the wreck. Coral and sponge encrustation covers much of the airframe. Small reef fish shelter around the fuselage, wings, and engine nacelles. The surrounding waters off Wangat Island support the typical Madang marine community.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the aircraft at 15-25 meters. The visibility is typically limited to 8-10 meters, so the wreck may not be visible from the surface -- follow a guide or descent line. Begin at the nose section where the four machine guns are still mounted, complete with ammunition belts. Move aft to the cockpit area, which can be peered into for structural details. Continue past the dorsal turret position along the fuselage to the distinctive twin tail fins, where the large gorgonian sea fan makes a dramatic photographic subject. Note the missing port engine -- the starboard engine remains in place. The depth range of 15-25 meters allows a comfortable dive duration of 30-40 minutes.

## Entry and Exit

Access is by dive boat from Madang-based operators, a short ride to Wangat Island. Entry is by giant stride. The harbour/island location provides sheltered conditions. Surface near the wreck area for boat pickup.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The four nose guns with ammunition are the most historically striking feature -- examine them closely but do not touch
- The gorgonian between the twin tail fins is the signature photograph of this wreck -- position yourself for a wide-angle shot
- Visibility is limited (8-10 meters), so a torch is essential for illuminating details and bringing out colours
- Combine with the nearby Henry Leith wreck for a Wangat Island double-wreck day
- The cockpit is accessible but tight -- approach with care and maintain buoyancy to avoid stirring silt

## Safety Considerations

The ammunition in the nose guns is live ordnance -- observe from a close but safe distance and do not touch. Visibility is limited, making buddy contact and navigation important. A torch is essential. The moderate depth allows comfortable dive times without decompression concerns for most profiles. This is a WWII crash site -- treat it with appropriate respect and do not remove any artifacts.
