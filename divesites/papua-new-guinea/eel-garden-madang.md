# Eel Garden Madang

A shallow, easy passage dive between Pig and Massus Islands in Madang Lagoon -- named for the garden eels that sway in the sandy bottom, with the bonus of a sunken catamaran wreck at modest depth.

## Overview

Eel Garden sits in the passage between Pig Island and Massus Island in Madang Lagoon. The site is best dived on the outgoing tide, when a gentle current carries divers through the pass at a comfortable pace. The namesake garden eels colonize the sandy bottom in the passage, their slender bodies extending from burrows and retracting as divers approach.

The other main feature is the wreckage of a large catamaran resting on the sand at 11-20 meters. The engines and propellers have been removed, but the hull structure remains intact and provides a framework for coral growth and a shelter for fish life. The shallow depth range of 3-14 meters through the pass makes this an easy, relaxed dive -- typically used as a second dive after a deeper site like Barracuda Point.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Passage between Pig Island and Massus Island, Madang Lagoon, Papua New Guinea
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters
- **Current**: Gentle on outgoing tide
- **Best Time**: April to November; best dived on outgoing tide

## Marine Life

The garden eels are the signature species -- dozens of slender spotted eels emerge from the sandy bottom across the passage floor, creating the "garden" effect that gives the site its name. They are easily spooked, so a slow, patient approach is needed to observe them at close range.

Around the catamaran wreckage, small reef fish shelter in the hull structure -- damselfish, cardinalfish, and glassy sweepers. Lionfish and scorpionfish lurk in the wreck's crevices. The reef edges of the passage support healthy hard coral with the usual Madang complement of anemonefish, butterflyfish, and small groupers. Nudibranchs and flatworms can be found on the coral rubble and wreck surfaces for macro enthusiasts.

## Dive Profile

The dive is typically conducted as a gentle drift through the passage on the outgoing tide. Enter up-current and allow the flow to carry you through at 3-14 meters depth. The garden eels are found on the sandy patches between coral outcrops -- approach low and slow to avoid scaring them into their burrows. The catamaran wreck sits on the sand at 11-20 meters toward the deeper end of the passage. Spend time exploring the hull before the current carries you through to the shallower exit of the pass.

The entire dive can be completed well within no-decompression limits, making it ideal as a second or third dive of the day.

## Entry and Exit

Access is by dive boat from Madang-based operators, with a short ride to the passage between Pig and Massus Islands. The boat drops divers at the entrance to the pass on the incoming current side. Entry is by giant stride. Exit at the far end of the passage where the boat will be waiting for pickup. An SMB is still recommended for visibility from the boat.

## Tips and Recommendations

- This site works best as a second dive of the day -- the shallow depth and easy conditions make it a perfect follow-up to a deeper morning dive
- Time the dive for the outgoing tide for the best drift conditions through the passage
- Approach the garden eels slowly and stay low -- they retreat into their burrows if startled, and patience is rewarded with closer views
- A macro lens is ideal for the garden eels and nudibranchs; the catamaran wreck is also compact enough for wide-angle shots
- The shallow depth means long bottom times and no need for Nitrox

## Safety Considerations

This is one of the easiest dives in the Madang area. Current through the passage is gentle on the outgoing tide but should still be respected -- do not fight against it. The shallow maximum depth of 14 meters means decompression obligations are not a concern for normal dive durations. The catamaran wreckage is open and does not present overhead environment risks. Standard buddy diving procedures apply.

## Photography

Macro photography is the strength of this site. The garden eels make compelling subjects but require a patient, low-profile approach -- use a longer focal length macro lens if available to avoid getting too close. The catamaran wreck at 11-20 meters offers compact wide-angle compositions with fish life in and around the hull structure. Nudibranchs and flatworms on the wreck surfaces and coral rubble reward careful searching. Natural light is excellent at these shallow depths, reducing the need for powerful strobes.
