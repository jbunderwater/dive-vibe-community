# Douglas A-20 Havoc

A WWII A-20A attack bomber resting on a sandy bottom at 16-18 meters inside Port Moresby Harbour -- a historically significant aviation wreck, though harbour visibility is typically poor.

## Overview

The Douglas A-20A Havoc was a twin-engine light attack bomber used by the United States Army Air Forces during the New Guinea campaign. This example lies on a sandy bottom at 16-18 meters inside Port Moresby Harbour. The airframe is present and recognizable, though cockpit instruments have been removed (likely salvaged or deteriorated over the decades). The site is historically significant as a tangible relic of the air war over New Guinea, but divers should set expectations accordingly -- Port Moresby Harbour typically suffers from poor visibility, which can significantly limit the experience. When visibility cooperates, the outline of the twin-engine bomber on the sandy bottom is an evocative sight.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Port Moresby Harbour, National Capital District, Papua New Guinea
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 18 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: Variable; often poor due to harbour conditions
- **Current**: Mild to moderate
- **Best Time**: Year-round; visibility may improve during drier months

## Marine Life

The harbour location limits marine life compared to the outer reef sites. The wreck provides structure on an otherwise featureless sandy bottom, attracting resident fish species. Coral growth is modest given the harbour's turbid conditions. Expect small reef fish sheltering around the airframe rather than the spectacular diversity found at Port Moresby's outer reef sites.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the sandy bottom at 16-18 meters. In poor visibility, the aircraft may not be visible from the surface, so descend along a line or follow a guide. Locate the twin-engine layout -- the wing span and engine nacelles define the aircraft's shape. The cockpit area is accessible but instruments are missing. Examine the fuselage and tail section. The shallow-moderate depth allows generous bottom time, but the limiting factor is typically visibility rather than air supply. Ascend with a safety stop at 5 meters.

## Entry and Exit

Access is by dive boat from Port Moresby operators. The site is inside the harbour, so boat transit is short. Entry is by giant stride. Given potentially poor visibility, descend along a line to avoid missing the wreck. Surface near the descent point for pickup.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Manage expectations -- harbour visibility is often poor and can reduce the experience significantly
- A torch is essential, both for navigating in low visibility and illuminating wreck details
- This dive is more rewarding for history enthusiasts than for those seeking spectacular underwater scenery
- Ask your operator about current visibility conditions before committing to this site
- Pair this dive with one of the outer reef sites (Suzies Bommie, The Finger) for contrast

## Safety Considerations

Poor visibility is the primary challenge. Maintain close contact with your buddy and guide. Carry a torch. The shallow depth eliminates decompression concerns. Harbour conditions can include boat traffic overhead -- listen for engines during your safety stop and deploy an SMB. Do not remove artifacts from WWII wrecks.
