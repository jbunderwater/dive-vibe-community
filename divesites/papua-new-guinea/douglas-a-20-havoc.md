# Douglas A-20 Havoc

A WWII A-20A Havoc attack bomber (serial 40-173, "Strawberry Roan") resting on a sandy bottom at 16-18 meters off the eastern side of a coral reef near Loloata Island, Bootless Bay -- approximately 20 km east of Port Moresby. A historically significant aviation wreck that ditched due to engine failure on 22-23 April 1943, with all crew surviving.

## Overview

The Douglas A-20A Havoc (serial 40-173, nicknamed "Strawberry Roan") was a twin-engine light attack bomber used by the United States Army Air Forces during the New Guinea campaign. On 22 or 23 April 1943, the aircraft suffered port engine failure and was deliberately ditched by pilot 2nd Lt. William Langley on the eastern side of a coral reef near Loloata Island in Bootless Bay, approximately 20 km east of Port Moresby. All crew survived. The wreck was discovered on 6 November 1980 by Dik Knight.

The aircraft rests on a sandy bottom at 16-18 meters. The airframe is present and recognizable -- the starboard propeller is bent back (engine was running at impact) while the port propeller is undamaged (engine had been shut down), and the flaps are fully extended, all consistent with a controlled engine-failure ditching. Most cockpit instruments have been removed; the seat, throttles, and joystick controls remain. The Bootless Bay location means water clarity is influenced by nearby mangroves and tidal flow, and visibility can be variable.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Eastern side of coral reef near Loloata Island, Bootless Bay, approximately 20 km east of Port Moresby, Papua New Guinea
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 18 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: Variable; often poor due to harbour conditions
- **Current**: Mild to moderate
- **Best Time**: Year-round; visibility may improve during drier months

## Marine Life

The harbour location limits marine life compared to the outer reef sites. The wreck provides structure on an otherwise featureless sandy bottom, attracting resident fish species. Coral growth is modest given the harbour's turbid conditions. Expect small reef fish sheltering around the airframe rather than the spectacular diversity found at Port Moresby's outer reef sites.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the sandy bottom at 16-18 meters. In poor visibility, the aircraft may not be visible from the surface, so descend along a line or follow a guide. Locate the twin-engine layout -- the wing span and engine nacelles define the aircraft's shape. The cockpit area is accessible but instruments are missing. Examine the fuselage and tail section. The shallow-moderate depth allows generous bottom time, but the limiting factor is typically visibility rather than air supply. Ascend with a safety stop at 5 meters.

## Entry and Exit

Access is by dive boat from Port Moresby or Bootless Bay operators, approximately 20 km east of Port Moresby near Loloata Island. Entry is by giant stride. Given potentially poor visibility, descend along a line to avoid missing the wreck. Surface near the descent point for pickup.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Manage expectations -- harbour visibility is often poor and can reduce the experience significantly
- A torch is essential, both for navigating in low visibility and illuminating wreck details
- This dive is more rewarding for history enthusiasts than for those seeking spectacular underwater scenery
- Ask your operator about current visibility conditions before committing to this site
- Pair this dive with one of the nearby Bootless Bay sites (Suzies Bommie, Di's Delight) for contrast

## Safety Considerations

Poor visibility is the primary challenge. Maintain close contact with your buddy and guide. Carry a torch. The shallow depth eliminates decompression concerns. Harbour conditions can include boat traffic overhead -- listen for engines during your safety stop and deploy an SMB. Do not remove artifacts from WWII wrecks.
