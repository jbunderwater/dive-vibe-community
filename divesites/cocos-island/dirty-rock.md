# Dirty Rock

Cocos Island's best all-round dive site, a guano-covered rock delivering hammerheads, mantas, dolphins, and the island's widest species variety

## Overview
Dirty Rock (Roca Sucia) is a low rocky head roughly 15 metres long that rises 3–5 metres above the surface, perpetually coated in seabird guano that gives the site its name. Below the waterline, volcanic pinnacles and boulder formations extend into deep water, with a two-tiered pinnacle on the western side rising from depth to approximately 18 meters. A cleaning station on the southern face at around 27 meters draws hammerhead sharks in large numbers. Dirty Rock consistently produces the widest variety of pelagic species of any Cocos Island dive site, making it many divers' favorite overall.

## Site Information
- **Location**: Central Cocos Island, Costa Rica
- **Entry Type**: liveaboard
- **Site Type**: Pinnacle/seamount
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 35 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15-30 meters
- **Current**: Moderate to strong
- **Best Time**: June to December for peak hammerhead activity; year-round for general species diversity

## Marine Life
Dirty Rock offers the broadest species lineup at Cocos Island. Scalloped hammerhead sharks appear in schools of hundreds at the cleaning station on the southern face, joined by barberfish and eagle rays. Galapagos sharks and silky sharks patrol the pinnacles. Massive schools of bigeye jacks and horse-eye jacks swirl in the current. Whitetip reef sharks rest in fissures between boulders. Marble rays, eagle rays, and whale sharks are regular visitors. Green sea turtles and hawksbill turtles feed along the rocks. Leather bass and large groupers inhabit the deeper structure. Bottlenose dolphins regularly join divers during safety stops. The cleaning station at approximately 27 metres on the southern face draws the densest hammerhead activity.

## Dive Profile
Descent is along the mooring line to the main rock formation. The cleaning station at 27 meters on the southern face is the primary destination for hammerhead viewing. The two-tiered western pinnacle rising to 18 meters offers a multi-level dive profile. Shallower areas around the rock base at 10-15 meters are productive for rays, turtles, and reef life. Plan bottom time carefully as multiple depth levels encourage profiles that consume air quickly.

## Entry and Exit
Negative entry from the panga onto the descent line. Current can be strong but is generally more manageable than Bajo Alcyone. Exit via ascent on the mooring line with pickup by the panga. Safety stops at 5 meters often feature dolphin encounters.

## Tips and Recommendations
- Plan multiple dives here; the species variety rewards repeat visits
- Explore both the southern cleaning station at depth and the shallower western pinnacle
- Afternoon dives can produce additional schooling activity around the rock
- Keep an eye on the blue water during safety stops for dolphins and mantas passing through

## Safety Considerations
Current strength varies significantly and can shift direction during a dive. Maintain visual contact with the rock formations and your group. The cleaning station depth of 27 meters means careful air management is needed if you also want to explore the shallower pinnacle. Carry an SMB and signaling devices. All divers should be comfortable with negative entries and current diving.

## Photography
Dirty Rock is Cocos Island's most versatile photography site. Wide-angle for hammerhead schools, jack tornadoes, and manta fly-bys. The dolphin encounters during safety stops provide unique split-level opportunities. Morning light on the western pinnacle produces the best ambient conditions.
