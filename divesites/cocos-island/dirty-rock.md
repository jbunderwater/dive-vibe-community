# Dirty Rock

Cocos Island's best all-round dive site, a guano-covered rock delivering hammerheads, mantas, dolphins, and the widest species variety in the archipelago

## Overview
Dirty Rock (Roca Sucia) is a low rocky head approximately 15 meters long that rises around 5 meters above the surface, perpetually coated in seabird guano that gives the site its name. Below the waterline, volcanic pinnacles and boulder formations extend into deep water, dropping to 35 meters. A cleaning station at around 25 meters draws hammerhead sharks in large numbers, with barberfish (black-nosed butterflyfish) servicing the sharks in the manner of a car wash. Dirty Rock consistently produces the widest variety of pelagic species of any Cocos Island dive site, making it many divers' favorite overall.

## Site Information
- **Location**: Central Cocos Island, Costa Rica
- **Entry Type**: liveaboard
- **Site Type**: Pinnacle/seamount
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 35 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15-30 meters
- **Current**: Moderate to strong
- **Best Time**: June to December for peak hammerhead activity; year-round for general species diversity

## Marine Life
Dirty Rock offers the broadest species lineup at Cocos Island. Scalloped hammerhead sharks appear in schools at the cleaning station at 25 meters. Galapagos sharks patrol the pinnacles. Large schools of bigeye jacks swirl around the rock. Whitetip reef sharks rest in fissures between boulders. Marble rays, eagle rays, and whale sharks are regular visitors. Green and hawksbill sea turtles feed along the rocks. Leather bass and Yellow Amarillo snappers inhabit the structure. Bottlenose dolphins regularly join divers during safety stops. A cave section holds lobsters at depth.

## Dive Profile
Descent is along the mooring line to the main rock formation. The cleaning station at 25 meters is the primary destination for hammerhead viewing, where barberfish mark the area and hammerheads circle in predictable patterns. Shallower areas around the rock at 10-15 meters are productive for rays, turtles, and reef fish. Plan bottom time carefully as multiple depth levels encourage profiles that consume air quickly.

## Entry and Exit
Negative entry from the panga onto the descent line. Current can be strong but is generally more manageable than Bajo Alcyone. Exit via ascent on the mooring line with pickup by the panga. Safety stops at 5 meters often feature dolphin encounters.

## Tips and Recommendations
- Plan multiple dives here; the species variety rewards repeat visits
- Explore the cleaning station at 25 meters and the shallower sections of the rock on the same dive
- Afternoon dives can produce black triggerfish and convict tang spawning activity
- Keep an eye on the blue water during safety stops for dolphins and mantas passing through

## Safety Considerations
Current strength varies significantly and can shift direction during a dive. Maintain visual contact with the rock formations and your group. The cleaning station depth of 25 meters means careful air management is needed if you also want to explore the shallower rock sections. Carry an SMB and signaling devices. All divers should be comfortable with negative entries and current diving.

## Photography
Dirty Rock is Cocos Island's most versatile photography site. Wide-angle for hammerhead schools, jack swarms, and manta fly-bys. The dolphin encounters during safety stops provide unique split-level opportunities. Morning light on the pinnacle produces the best ambient conditions.
