# Viking Rock

A helmet-shaped rock at the edge of Wafer Bay with steep walls, powerful currents, and tiger sharks on the deeper ledges

## Overview
Viking Rock is a small helmet-shaped rock formation at the northern limit of Wafer Bay on Cocos Island. Below the surface, the rock drops away as a steep vertical wall plunging to approximately 60 meters, with huge stepped ledges cutting into the wall face. The site is known for powerful currents that sweep around the exposed rock, making it one of the more demanding dives at Cocos. Tiger sharks, hammerhead sharks, and eagle rays patrol the deeper wall sections, while the shallower areas between 10 and 20 meters offer the best overall diving with whitetip reef sharks, rays, and pufferfish among the volcanic rock formations.

## Site Information
- **Location**: Northern limit of Wafer Bay, Cocos Island, Costa Rica
- **Entry Type**: liveaboard
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 40 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15-25 meters
- **Current**: Strong to very strong
- **Best Time**: June to December for peak pelagic activity; best attempted in calmer current windows

## Marine Life
Tiger sharks are the headline species at Viking Rock, cruising the deeper wall and stepped ledges at 25-40 meters. Scalloped hammerhead sharks pass along the wall in schools, particularly on the deeper sections. Eagle rays glide along the wall face. The shallower zone at 10-20 meters is productive for whitetip reef sharks resting in wall recesses, marble rays, and pufferfish. The stepped ledges at depth provide shelter for larger species and create natural observation points where divers can tuck in behind the rock to watch passing pelagics.

## Dive Profile
The best diving is concentrated in the 10-20 meter range along the wall, where the stepped ledges and volcanic rock formations hold the most life. Deeper excursions along the wall to 30-40 meters bring tiger shark and hammerhead encounters but consume air rapidly, especially when fighting current. The wall continues well beyond recreational limits, so depth awareness is essential. The helmet shape of the rock means current splits around it, creating eddies on the lee side where divers can shelter. Work the sheltered side first, then drift along the current-exposed face if conditions allow.

## Entry and Exit
Negative entry from the panga directly onto the descent line. The exposed position at the bay entrance means surface conditions are often rougher than at sheltered Chatham Bay sites. Descent must be immediate. Exit requires ascending along the wall to 5 meters for a safety stop, deploying an SMB for panga pickup. In strong current, divers may end the dive well away from the entry point.

## Tips and Recommendations
- Focus your dive in the 10-20 meter range where the wall features and marine life are richest
- Use the stepped ledges on the lee side as shelter from current while scanning the blue for tiger sharks
- Save this site for a day when you are comfortable with Cocos current conditions — it is not a good first-day dive
- Reef hooks are useful for holding position on the wall in strong current
- Nitrox is recommended if planning deeper excursions along the wall

## Safety Considerations
Viking Rock has some of the most powerful currents at Cocos Island. The exposed position at the edge of Wafer Bay channels current directly over and around the rock. Getting swept off the wall into open water is a real risk — maintain contact with the rock structure at all times. The wall drops well beyond recreational limits, and the combination of current, depth, and reduced visibility at depth demands strict discipline. This site is strictly for experienced current divers with excellent buoyancy control. Carry an SMB, whistle, and signaling mirror.

## Photography
The vertical wall provides dramatic depth perspective for wide-angle compositions. Tiger sharks on the deeper ledges are the prize shot but require fast reactions and comfort at depth in current. The 10-20 meter zone offers better light for ambient shooting of whitetip sharks and eagle rays against the wall. Strobes are essential for wall detail at any depth. Secure all camera equipment firmly for the rough entry and potential drift exit.
