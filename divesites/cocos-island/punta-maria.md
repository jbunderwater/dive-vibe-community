## Punta Maria

Punta Maria is a submerged seamount located 500 meters off Cocos Island's southwestern coast. A long spine of rock forms the top of an underwater mountain, with one side descending steeply as a wall and the other shelving more gradually. The site is one of Cocos's premier locations for Galapagos shark sightings — large individuals (3 to 3.5 meters) use the prominent cleaning station here regularly. Strong currents sweeping around the southwestern point generate the upwellings that attract a wide range of powerful pelagic species.

## Overview

Punta Maria's seamount structure creates a concentrated focal point for marine life in otherwise deep, open water. The cleaning station at the top of the ridge, accessible via a shot line, draws Galapagos sharks as its primary clientele — an unusual distinction compared to the hammerhead-dominated sites elsewhere at Cocos. The steep drop-off on one side plunges into deep blue water where silky sharks, manta rays, and passing hammerhead schools can be spotted. The shallower spine and adjacent pinnacle host dense schools of snapper, bigeye soldierfish, and hunting packs of whitetip reef sharks.

## Site Information

- **Location**: 500 meters southwest of Cocos Island
- **Entry Type**: liveaboard
- **Site Type**: Pinnacle/seamount
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 37 meters
- **Optimal Viewing Depth**: 24-30 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15-20 meters (reduced visibility common due to strong currents)
- **Current**: Strong; among the strongest at Cocos Island
- **Best Time**: June to November for peak current-driven pelagic aggregations

## Marine Life

Galapagos sharks are the defining species at Punta Maria, with large individuals (easily reaching 3-3.5 meters) attending the cleaning station in numbers that rival the hammerhead aggregations at Alcyone. Silky sharks patrol the drop-off, and schooling hammerheads occasionally sweep through from the deeper water. Manta rays arrive on the current. On the adjacent smaller pinnacle, whitetip reef sharks are abundant and hunt in coordinated packs. Large schools of snapper and bigeye soldierfish mass along the rockface. Eagle rays move in formation along the wall. The strong current that makes the site demanding is also what delivers the continuous parade of marine life.

## Dive Profile

Descend the shot line to the top of the seamount ridge at around 24 meters, then work along the spine or descend the wall to the 30-37 meter range. The primary cleaning station sits near the top of the structure. Current direction determines whether to drift along the spine or hold position on the line. The adjacent smaller pinnacle — a short swim when conditions allow — is worth visiting for the whitetip shark concentrations. Manage depth carefully; the wall drops away steeply and strong current can accelerate a descent. Ascend the shot line for a controlled safety stop.

## Entry and Exit

Entry is from liveaboard zodiacs. Surface conditions at this southwestern exposure can be rough, with swell and wind combining with tidal current. The zodiac driver follows bubbles and SMBs carefully at this site. Deploy your SMB before leaving depth for the safety stop.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Position at or near the shot line during high current — the Galapagos sharks come to the cleaning station regardless of flow
- Watch the deep blue on the drop-off side for passing hammerhead schools and silky sharks working the edge
- The adjacent small pinnacle is worth the effort — it concentrates whitetips in photographic range
- Currents here can be the strongest encountered during a Cocos itinerary; confirm briefing instructions before descending
- Nitrox extends bottom time at the optimal 24-30 meter depth

## Safety Considerations

Punta Maria demands sound current management. The exposed southwest position means conditions change quickly and can be significantly more challenging than predicted. The shot line is essential — do not free-descent or free-ascend at this site without deliberate planning. An SMB, whistle, and signaling device are mandatory. If swept off the structure, deploy an SMB immediately and stay on the surface for zodiac recovery.

## Photography

Wide-angle is essential. The Galapagos shark encounters at the cleaning station — large, slow-moving sharks in relatively clear water — are excellent photographic subjects. The drop-off provides a deep blue background for dramatic silhouette shots. Strobe use is effective at cleaning station depths where the sharks approach predictably. The smaller adjacent pinnacle surrounded by whitetips offers a chaotic but rewarding wide scene.
