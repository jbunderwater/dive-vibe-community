# Punta Maria

A remote seamount off the southwestern coast where Galapagos sharks rule and silky sharks emerge from the deep blue

## Overview
Punta Maria is an underwater mountain located approximately 500 meters off the southwestern coast of Cocos Island. The main seamount rises from a deep sandy seafloor to around 25 meters, with two additional nearby pinnacles creating a complex of submerged peaks. This is the premier Galapagos shark site at Cocos Island — large Galapagos sharks are the dominant predators here, often approaching divers more boldly than at other sites. The remote southwestern position means Punta Maria receives less traffic than the marquee northern sites, and its exposure to open ocean currents brings a steady parade of pelagic visitors.

## Site Information
- **Location**: 500 meters off southwestern coast of Cocos Island, Costa Rica
- **Entry Type**: liveaboard
- **Site Type**: Pinnacle/seamount
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 37 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15-25 meters
- **Current**: Moderate to strong
- **Best Time**: June to December for peak shark activity; year-round for Galapagos sharks

## Marine Life
Large Galapagos sharks are the main attraction and the reason divemasters return to this site. They patrol the seamount in numbers, often circling divers with evident curiosity. Schools of burrito grunts and blue-striped snappers blanket the pinnacle structure. Cortez rainbow wrasse add color to the volcanic rock. Silky sharks appear from the blue water beyond the seamount. Manta rays pass through on current lines. Scalloped hammerhead sharks visit, though generally in smaller numbers than at Bajo Alcyone or Dirty Rock. Tiger sharks have been sighted here with increasing regularity.

## Dive Profile
Descent is along the mooring line to the seamount top at approximately 25 meters. The dive centers on the main pinnacle, with excursions to the two adjacent pinnacles if current and air supply permit. Most of the action occurs at 20-30 meters around the seamount crown where Galapagos sharks circle. The deep sandy bottom at 37 meters is worth a brief visit to scan for pelagics in the blue but consumes air and bottom time quickly. Ascent back along the mooring line.

## Entry and Exit
Negative entry from the panga onto the mooring line. The southwestern exposure can produce choppy surface conditions. Descent must be immediate — the mooring line is essential for reaching the seamount efficiently against current. Exit via mooring line ascent with safety stop at 5 meters and panga pickup.

## Tips and Recommendations
- Nitrox is strongly recommended given the 25-30 meter working depth
- Galapagos sharks here are bolder than at other Cocos sites; remain calm and hold your position
- Check between the three pinnacles for different species concentrations
- Reef hooks are useful for holding position on the exposed seamount top
- This site is sometimes available when northern sites are blown out by weather

## Safety Considerations
The southwestern exposure brings strong open-ocean currents and can create difficult surface conditions. Mooring line descent is essential — free descents risk missing the seamount entirely. The 25-meter minimum working depth means air management is critical, especially for divers also visiting the deeper sandy areas. Galapagos sharks here are more inquisitive than at other sites; do not make sudden movements or attempt to touch them. Carry an SMB, whistle, and signaling mirror. The nearest recompression chamber is approximately 36 hours away on the mainland.

## Photography
Wide-angle is the priority for Galapagos shark portraits and silky shark fly-bys in the blue water. The schooling burrito grunts and snappers on the pinnacle create excellent foreground subjects with sharks behind. Strobes are essential at the 25-meter working depth. The relatively compact seamount means encounters happen at close range, favoring rectilinear wide-angle lenses over fisheye for shark portraits.
