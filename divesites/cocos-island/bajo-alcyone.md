# Bajo Alcyone

The world's most famous hammerhead shark aggregation site, a submerged seamount battered by converging currents

## Overview
Bajo Alcyone is a submerged flat-topped seamount approximately one kilometre off the coast of Cocos Island, named by the Cousteau Society after their research vessel Alcyone, which first explored and documented the site. The seamount extends roughly 600 metres in length and is swept by strong converging ocean currents that create a nutrient-rich upwelling, attracting extraordinary concentrations of pelagic life. This is widely regarded as the premier hammerhead shark diving site in the world, where schools of scalloped hammerheads numbering in the hundreds gather at the cleaning stations. It is also the most challenging dive at Cocos Island, demanding advanced skills, strong fitness, and comfort in extreme current.

## Site Information
- **Location**: 1 mile off southern coast of Cocos Island, Costa Rica
- **Entry Type**: liveaboard
- **Site Type**: Pinnacle/seamount
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 40 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15-30 meters
- **Current**: Strong to extreme, often unpredictable
- **Best Time**: June to December for largest hammerhead aggregations; January to May for better visibility

## Marine Life
Bajo Alcyone delivers the densest shark encounters at Cocos Island. Hundreds of scalloped hammerhead sharks patrol the seamount, passing within metres of divers holding position on the rocks. At depths between approximately 18 and 35 metres, barberfish (blacknosed butterflyfish, Johnrandallia nigrirostris) operate cleaning stations where hammerheads queue for parasite removal alongside king angelfish. Silky sharks, Galapagos sharks, and whitetip reef sharks are regular visitors. Devil rays, manta rays, and eagle rays cruise the current lines. Whale sharks make appearances, as do large schools of yellowfin tuna and bigeye jacks hunting in the blue water surrounding the seamount.

## Dive Profile
This is a rapid negative entry descent to approximately 25 metres, hand-over-hand down a mooring line in extremely strong current. Divers settle behind boulders on the seamount top and hold position to watch hammerhead schools pass. Converging currents arrive from multiple directions across the seamount, requiring constant awareness of position. Bottom time is typically 25–35 minutes at depth before ascending the line. Air consumption is high due to exertion against current.

## Entry and Exit
Negative entry from the liveaboard panga directly onto the descent line. There is no time for surface adjustment. Divers must be fully equipped and ready to descend immediately upon entering the water. Exit requires ascending the mooring line against current and surfacing near the panga for pickup.

## Tips and Recommendations
- This is the most demanding dive at Cocos Island; save it until you are comfortable with conditions
- Bring reef hooks or be prepared to wedge behind boulders to hold position
- Nitrox is strongly recommended to extend bottom time at 25-35 meters
- Stay low and behind the rock formations to avoid being swept off the seamount
- Carry a safety sausage and audible signaling device in case of separation from the group
- Morning dives tend to produce the largest hammerhead schools

## Safety Considerations
Bajo Alcyone has the strongest and most unpredictable currents of any Cocos dive site. Divers must be able to execute a rapid negative entry and descend immediately. Getting swept off the seamount means a blue-water ascent and potential long surface drift. Divemaster briefings are critical and instructions must be followed exactly. This site is strictly for experienced divers with excellent buoyancy control, fitness, and comfort at depth in strong current. Minimum 50 logged dives is typical, though 100+ is recommended.

## Photography
Wide-angle is essential here. The hammerhead schools passing overhead or alongside the seamount are the signature shot. Fisheye lenses in the 10-17mm range work best. Strobes help fill shadows but ambient light shooting can capture the scale of the schools. Holding a camera steady while bracing against current is the main challenge. Forget macro at this site.
