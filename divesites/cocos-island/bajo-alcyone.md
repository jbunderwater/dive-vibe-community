## Bajo Alcyone

Bajo Alcyone is the most celebrated dive site at Cocos Island and one of the most iconic shark dives on the planet. This submerged seamount on the island's eastern side rises to within 25 meters of the surface, topping out around 25-30 meters before descending steeply into the blue. The site is defined by its permanent shot line, which divers hold onto as they hang mid-water watching scalloped hammerheads pass by in groups that regularly exceed one hundred animals.

## Overview

Bajo Alcyone (Spanish for "Below Alcyone") delivers Cocos Island's signature experience: dense aggregations of scalloped hammerhead sharks descending through the thermocline, sometimes in schools numbering several hundred individuals. The currents that batter this exposed seamount are precisely what draw the sharks — they use the upwellings as cleaning stations and congregation points. Manta rays and mobula rays frequent the site for the same reason, gliding through the current with effortless grace while hammerheads wheel overhead and below.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Eastern Cocos Island, Pacific
- **Entry Type**: liveaboard
- **Site Type**: Pinnacle/seamount
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 40 meters
- **Optimal Viewing Depth**: 25-35 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15-30 meters
- **Current**: Strong to very strong — a permanent shot line is in place
- **Best Time**: June to November for densest hammerhead aggregations

## Marine Life

Bajo Alcyone is defined by its scalloped hammerhead shark aggregations, with schools regularly in the dozens and sometimes exceeding one hundred animals. Manta rays and mobula rays use the cleaning stations here, often arriving in groups. Silky sharks, whitetip reef sharks, and yellowfin tuna are regular fixtures. Large schools of bigeye jacks circle in the current. During the wet season (June–November), the hammerhead aggregations are at their most intense; the dry season (December–May) brings increased manta ray and silky shark activity.

## Dive Profile

The site requires descending the shot line, which is permanently moored to the seamount. Divers descend to the 25-35 meter viewing area and hold position on or near the line, watching the hammerheads pass as the current flows. The top of the seamount sits around 25 meters; divers can work shallower for photography in better light. Depth management is critical — the wall drops away steeply and the current can carry divers deeper quickly. Plan a controlled ascent up the shot line for the safety stop.

## Entry and Exit

All access is from liveaboard zodiacs. Conditions on the surface can be rough, with swell and current making entry and exit demanding. Divers must be prepared for a drift on ascent if the current picks up. Deploying an SMB at the start of the safety stop is essential. The zodiac driver will track bubbles and SMBs to retrieve divers.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Hold the shot line — this is not a free-drift dive; staying put lets the hammerheads come to you
- Aim for the 25-30 meter range where lighting is better and hammerhead density is highest
- Shoot early in the dive at depth before moving shallower for schooling shots in better light
- Expect strong downcurrents as well as horizontal flow — control your buoyancy carefully
- A wetsuit of at least 5mm is recommended due to cold thermoclines at depth

## Safety Considerations

Bajo Alcyone is strictly for experienced advanced divers. The combination of strong currents, deep exposure, and remote location requires solid buoyancy control and current-handling experience. The permanent shot line is essential — never leave it without a deliberate plan to manage the drift. Cold thermoclines below 30 meters can cause rapid heat loss. Always dive with an SMB, whistle, and signaling mirror for surface pick-up. Nitrox is widely used here to extend bottom time at the optimal depths.

## Photography

Wide-angle is the only lens choice at Bajo Alcyone. Shoot upward to silhouette hammerhead schools against the light filtering down. Morning dives typically offer the best light angles. The shot line can be used as a brace for stability while framing large schools. A strobe is less useful in open water here — rely on ambient light and shoot in RAW for maximum dynamic range.
