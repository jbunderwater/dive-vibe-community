# Silverado

A tiny shallow pinnacle once famous as the world's premier silvertip shark cleaning station, now a calm reef dive with a bittersweet history

## Overview
Silverado is a small submerged rock near Chatham Bay, located close to the waterfall that tumbles down Cocos Island's jungle cliffs. The site consists of a 2-meter-high pinnacle rising from a sandy bottom at just 13 meters, with a cleaning station that historically attracted silvertip sharks in large numbers — the site was named for them. For years, Silverado was considered the world's premier location to observe silvertip shark cleaning behavior at close range. Since approximately 2007, silvertip sharks have largely disappeared from the site, possibly displaced by increasing populations of blacktip, Galapagos, and tiger sharks. Today Silverado remains a pleasant shallow dive with reef life and occasional surprises, but it carries the weight of what has been lost.

## Site Information
- **Location**: Near Chatham Bay waterfall, Cocos Island, Costa Rica
- **Entry Type**: Liveaboard
- **Site Type**: Pinnacle (small submerged rock)
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 13 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15-25 meters
- **Current**: Mild to moderate (calm by Cocos standards)
- **Best Time**: Year-round

## Marine Life
The cleaning station on the pinnacle still operates, though its clientele has changed. Whitetip reef sharks are the most common visitors, resting on the sandy bottom and circling the rock. Frogfish have been spotted on the pinnacle structure. Reef fish including butterflyfish, angelfish, and damselfish populate the rock. Tiger sharks occasionally cruise through, and their increasing presence at Cocos may explain the silvertip decline. On rare occasions, a silvertip shark still appears at the cleaning station. The sandy bottom around the pinnacle holds garden eels and small rays.

## Dive Profile
This is the shallowest dive at Cocos Island. Descent to the sandy bottom at 13 meters is quick, and the small pinnacle can be circled many times in a single dive. The cleaning station is on the pinnacle itself, and the best strategy is to settle on the sand at a comfortable distance and wait. The shallow depth allows exceptionally long bottom times, even on air. Some divemasters use Silverado as a final dive of the day or a rest dive between more demanding sites.

## Entry and Exit
Negative entry from the panga. Surface conditions near Chatham Bay are typically calm, making this one of the easier entries at Cocos. The shallow depth means descent is brief. Exit via direct ascent with a safety stop at 5 meters, though the 13-meter maximum depth makes decompression concerns minimal.

## Tips and Recommendations
- Approach the pinnacle slowly and settle on the sand to observe the cleaning station without disturbing activity
- Look carefully on the pinnacle structure for frogfish — they blend with the volcanic rock
- This is an excellent site for newer advanced divers to acclimate to Cocos conditions before tackling deeper sites
- Extended bottom times make this a good opportunity for video work
- Ask your divemaster about the history of the silvertip sharks — it is a sobering lesson in marine ecosystem shifts

## Safety Considerations
Silverado is the calmest and shallowest site at Cocos Island, but standard Cocos safety protocols apply. Tiger sharks do visit this site; remain calm, maintain eye contact, and give them space. Despite the gentle conditions, this is still a remote liveaboard destination with no nearby recompression facilities. Carry an SMB and signaling devices even at this shallow depth.

## Photography
The shallow depth and calm conditions make Silverado the easiest site at Cocos for photography. The 13-meter maximum means excellent ambient light throughout the dive. Macro gear is worthwhile here for frogfish and reef life on the pinnacle — one of the only Cocos sites where macro makes sense. Wide-angle works for whitetip portraits on the sandy bottom. If a tiger shark appears, the shallow depth and good light create ideal conditions for ambient-light shark photography.
