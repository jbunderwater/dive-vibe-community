## Davíðsgjá Fissure

Davíðsgjá is a tectonic fissure dive in Þingvellir National Park — less visited than Silfra, it offers the same glacial clarity in a narrower, more atmospheric setting that local divers describe as Silfra's "darker, spookier sibling."

## Overview

Davíðsgjá is a submerged rift fissure in Lake Þingvallavatn within Þingvellir National Park, located approximately 4 kilometres from the famous Silfra fissure (about 10 minutes by car). Like Silfra, it lies on the boundary between the North American and Eurasian tectonic plates and is filled with glacially sourced freshwater of exceptional clarity.

The fissure's top begins approximately 7 metres below the lake surface, reached after a shallow swim from shore. Divers enter the calm, open water of the lake and swim to the fissure entry before descending into the crack. The maximum depth is 21 metres. Further into the fissure the passage branches: a narrow, dark gorge to the left and a wider, more open gorge to the right — a feature that distinguishes Davíðsgjá from the single-channel Silfra system.

The site is considerably less crowded than Silfra and is described by DIVE.IS as a favourite among local Icelandic divers. The visual character differs from Silfra: the fissure walls are darker and the geometry more enclosed, with a more cave-like atmosphere in the narrower branch. However, it is not a cave dive — both branches are open tectonic fissures.

Water conditions differ from Silfra: temperature is typically slightly warmer than the 2–4°C of Silfra (both are near-freezing), and visibility, while often excellent, is more dependent on weather and lake conditions than the spring-fed Silfra. Drysuit is mandatory; no wetsuit diving is permitted.

## Dive Profile

- **Entry**: Shore entry; shallow swim across lake to fissure opening
- **Maximum depth**: 21 m (confirmed by DIVE.IS and PADI)
- **Fissure top**: Approximately 7 m below lake surface
- **Dive duration**: 30–40 minutes typically
- **Visibility**: Typically 100 m+, but more weather-dependent than Silfra

## Requirements

- **Mandatory equipment**: Drysuit (wetsuits prohibited)
- **Certification**: Open Water minimum; drysuit certification or 10 logged drysuit dives required
- **Guided dive**: Required; access through accredited operators only
- **Permit**: Þingvellir National Park dive permit required
- **Winter access**: Road to site may require 4x4; ice-ceiling conditions possible in winter, which requires Ice Diver certification

## Marine Life

Marine life is sparse, as in all Icelandic freshwater dive sites. Brown trout are documented at the site by DIVE.IS — they are commonly encountered swimming near the fissure opening. Þingvallavatn Lake is home to four unique fish species including dwarf char (Salvelinus devarius), but no dive-specific sources reviewed confirm dwarf char sightings specifically within the Davíðsgjá fissure. The primary visual draw is geological: the fissure walls, tectonic geometry, and glacial water colour.

## Photography

The narrower geometry and darker walls of Davíðsgjá create a different photographic character from Silfra — more dramatic shadow and depth contrasts. The branching structure at depth provides compositional variety for wide-angle shooting.

## Safety

Water temperature of 2–5°C requires a high-quality drysuit with adequate thermal underlayers. The shore entry involves swimming across open lake water in full diving equipment, which requires good physical condition. Always dive with a guide and partner. Winter dives with ice overhead require additional certification and equipment.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Þingvellir National Park, Iceland
- **Entry Type**: Shore entry
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 21 meters
- **Protected Status**: UNESCO World Heritage Site (Þingvellir National Park)
