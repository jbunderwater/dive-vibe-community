## Davíðsgjá Fissure

Davíðsgjá (meaning "David's fissure" in Icelandic) is a large volcanic eruption fissure on the northeastern shore of Thingvallavatn Lake in Thingvellir National Park, approximately 10 minutes by car from Silfra. It is often described as the darker, spookier sibling of Silfra — a less-visited but equally dramatic freshwater tectonic dive.

## Overview

Unlike Silfra, which is a continuous drift dive, Davíðsgjá is explored as an out-and-back dive from the shore. The fissure's opening begins approximately 7 meters underwater — meaning divers swim along the lake surface before descending into the crack. The fissure branches into two distinct arms: a narrow, dark gorge to the left and a wide, open gorge to the right. Maximum depth is 21 meters. Visibility is exceptional — typically exceeding 100 meters — though it is more dependent on weather and lake conditions than Silfra. Water temperature is slightly warmer than Silfra (2–6°C) due to the fissure's shallower connection to the lake. Drysuit diving experience is required.

Davíðsgjá is popular with local Icelandic divers but rarely visited by international tourists, making it a less-crowded alternative to Silfra. It can be combined with Silfra as a two-dive day since the sites are close.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Northeast shore of Thingvallavatn Lake, Thingvellir National Park, Iceland
- **Entry Type**: Shore entry
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 21 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 100+ meters (weather-dependent)
- **Water Temperature**: 2–6°C (35–43°F); slightly warmer than Silfra
- **Current**: None to minimal inside fissure
- **Best Time**: April to October (winter access limited by road closures and ice)

## Marine Life

Arctic char and brown trout from Thingvallavatn Lake commonly join divers in Davíðsgjá. Dwarf char inhabit the deeper crevices of the fissure walls. The freshwater environment supports no coral, no saltwater species, and no tropical marine life. The visual draw is geological: monumental round boulders and square lava slabs stacked throughout the fissure, illuminated in the exceptional glacial water clarity.

## Dive Profile

Divers enter at the lake shore and swim approximately 5–10 meters on the surface before the fissure opens beneath them. The descent leads into the branching gorge system. The recommended approach is to explore one branch first (either the narrow dark gorge or the wide open gorge), then return and explore the other. Maximum depth reached in the fissure is 21 meters. Average working depth is 10–15 meters. The open gorge provides the best natural light and visibility; the narrow gorge is darker and requires a torch.

## Entry and Exit

Shore entry directly into Thingvallavatn Lake. There are no steps or platforms — entry requires wading and then swimming across the surface to the fissure opening. Exit is at the same shore point. Drysuit certification or proof of minimum 10 logged drysuit dives within the last two years is required by the operators who run Davíðsgjá dives.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Bring a primary dive torch — the narrow left-arm gorge is significantly darker than Silfra
- Visibility is more variable than Silfra; best after periods of calm weather with no wind disturbing the lake
- The site can be combined with Silfra for a full-day two-dive itinerary with local operators
- The large boulders and scale of the fissure walls are most impressive when explored slowly — budget your air for a complete exploration of both gorge arms

## Safety Considerations

As a freshwater tectonic fissure, Davíðsgjá shares the same cold-water risks as Silfra: a drysuit is essential, buoyancy control must be precise, and stirring sediment quickly reduces visibility for other divers. The fissure's branching structure means the risk of disorientation is slightly higher than in Silfra's linear drift system — diving with a local guide is strongly recommended for first visits. The site is seasonal; in winter, road closures may prevent access and portions of the lake may freeze near shore.

## Photography

The scale of the boulders and fissure walls in Davíðsgjá makes it compelling for wide-angle photography. The open right-arm gorge provides the best natural light. Shoot upward from the fissure floor to convey the monumental scale of the lava formations. Arctic char and trout may appear as subjects when present. A torch is needed for detail shots in the narrower left-arm section.
