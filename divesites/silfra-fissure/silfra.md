## Silfra

Silfra is the primary dive site within the Silfra fissure system in Thingvellir National Park, Iceland — a tectonic crack running along the divergent boundary between the North American and Eurasian plates.

## Overview

Silfra sits within Thingvallavatn Lake, where glacial meltwater from the Langjökull glacier filters through porous lava rock for 30 to 100 years before reaching the fissure. This extended underground filtration produces some of the purest freshwater on Earth, yielding visibility that regularly exceeds 100 meters — widely regarded as the clearest diving water in the world. The water temperature remains a constant 2–4°C year-round, making a drysuit non-negotiable. This site is ranked among PADI's top 10 dive sites worldwide.

The dive flows through four connected sections: Big Crack, Silfra Hall, Silfra Cathedral, and Silfra Lagoon. The full fissure descends to 63 meters, but guided recreational dives are capped at 18 meters for safety.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Thingvellir National Park, Iceland
- **Entry Type**: Shore entry
- **Site Type**: Cave/cavern
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 18 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 100+ meters
- **Water Temperature**: 2–4°C (35–39°F) year-round
- **Current**: Minimal inside fissure
- **Best Time**: Year-round; summer offers neon-green algae bloom in lagoon section

## Marine Life

Silfra is a freshwater site with no coral, fish schools, or tropical species. The dominant life form is the striking neon-green algae that locals call "troll hair," which coats lava walls and sways in the gentle current. Arctic char visit in August and September during mating season. Dwarf char (reaching up to 8 cm) inhabit the deeper crevices year-round but are rarely seen. The endemic freshwater amphipod *Crymostygius thingvallensis* — found only in Thingvellir's rift lakes — may be spotted in the shallower sections. Occasional stickleback and brown trout from the lake enter the fissure.

## Dive Profile

Enter from the purpose-built shore platform and descend into the Big Crack section, where the fissure is at its narrowest and you can simultaneously reach out to touch both the North American and Eurasian plates. The dive progresses downstream through Silfra Hall (widest section, up to 8 meters wide), into Silfra Cathedral (a dramatic 100-meter-long corridor with lava walls dropping vertically), and exits through Silfra Lagoon. The typical dive lasts 30–45 minutes. Average depth is 7–12 meters throughout most of the route.

## Entry and Exit

Access is via a designated concrete entry platform with steps leading into the water. There is no giant stride here — entry is a controlled descent using the steps while wearing full gear. Divers exit at Silfra Lagoon, a separate exit point requiring a short surface swim. Icelandic regulations permit one dive per day per person in Silfra to manage cold exposure risk. All dives must be done with an authorized operator within Thingvellir National Park.

## Tips and Recommendations

- A drysuit and drysuit certification (or documented recent drysuit experience) are mandatory — no wetsuit diving is permitted
- Open Water certification is the minimum required; no Advanced certification needed for standard guided dives
- The water is clean enough to drink — it is perfectly safe to remove your regulator briefly and taste it
- Go early in the morning for better light and fewer people; midday sees heavy snorkeling tour traffic
- Bring a dive torch — the deeper sections under Silfra Hall benefit from supplemental lighting

## Safety Considerations

The primary hazard is cold. At 2–4°C, heat loss is rapid and divers must manage their drysuit buoyancy carefully. The one-dive-per-day limit exists specifically to prevent cold-related incidents. The cave sections beneath Silfra Hall reach 45 meters and are strictly off-limits — they involve long swim-through tunnels and have only been penetrated by a handful of experienced technical divers. Stay at recreational depths and follow the natural flow of the fissure to the lagoon exit.

## Photography

Silfra is one of the most photogenic freshwater dive sites on Earth. The 100-meter+ visibility allows for wide-angle shots showing the full scale of the fissure walls. In the Cathedral section, if there are divers ahead, you will see the famous chandelier effect — rising bubbles catching the light against the blue-lit walls. The Lagoon section in summer produces vivid green troll-hair algae shots. A wide-angle lens (10–17mm fisheye equivalent) works best.
