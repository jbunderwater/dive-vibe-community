## Strytan Hydrothermal Chimney

Strytan is the world's only known hydrothermal chimney accessible to recreational scuba divers — a 55-metre-tall mineral cone of anhydrite and saponite (magnesium silicates) rising from the floor of Eyjafjörður fjord in North Iceland.

## Overview

Strytan (also spelled Strýtan) is located in Eyjafjörður fjord near the village of Hjalteyri, approximately 20 km south of Akureyri in North Iceland. It is the first protected underwater area in Iceland (designated 2001) and is entirely separate from the freshwater Silfra sites — approximately 300 km north, it is a saltwater marine dive requiring a full-day excursion from Akureyri.

The chimney is a remarkable geological structure: a 55-metre-tall cone of consolidated minerals, with its top at approximately 15 metres depth and its base at 65–70 metres (sources vary: strytan.is gives 65 m, Wikipedia and DIVE.IS give 70 m). It was built over approximately 10,000 years — since the last ice age — by a geothermal spring that vents hot freshwater into the cold ocean. The minerals dissolved in the geothermal water precipitate upon contact with seawater, gradually building the structure. Vent water temperature sources disagree slightly: strytan.is and alertdiver.eu record 72°C; xray-mag.com reports 75°C; Wikipedia gives approximately 76°C. The vented water is freshwater with a high pH (~10.2, highly alkaline). Divers can hold a bare hand in the warm plume close to the vents — the surrounding ocean is 4–8°C.

Strytan was discovered in 1997 by Icelandic divers Erlendur Bogason and Árni Halldósson, who reported it to scientists; GEOMAR investigated with a submersible the same year. The site was made Iceland's first underwater nature reserve in 2001, and the Strýtan Divecenter at Hjalteyri acts as its official protector.

A second site, Arnarnesstrýtur (Little Strýtan), is a cluster of smaller hydrothermal chimneys within the protected area. The chimneys at Arnarnesstrýtur rise from 23 metres to 15 metres and cover an area of approximately 400 by 1,000 metres. DIVE.IS describes Little Strýtan as "probably the most densely packed area of different varieties of marine life in Iceland." Both sites are dived from the same boat.

## Marine Life

Strytan and Arnarnesstrýtur host the richest marine life in Iceland's diving. The following species have been documented at the site by multiple sources:

- **Atlantic wolffish** — present year-round; Arnarnesstrýtur is specifically noted for an abundance of wolffish monitored by discoverer Erlendur Bogason; peak breeding season is August
- **Lumpsucker / lumpfish** — observed guarding eggs from May onward; breeding behaviours visible in summer
- **Cod and pollock** — schools present around the chimney structure
- **Starry rays** — documented at the site
- **Nudibranchs** — multiple species including Flabellina
- **Anemones, mussels, bryozoans, sponges, hydroids, brittlestars, polychaete worms** — colonising the chimney's mineral surface

The best season for marine life is May through September, when wolffish and lumpfish breeding behaviours are observable.

## Dive Profile

- **Entry**: Boat only from Strýtan Divecenter at Hjalteyri
- **Maximum depth**: 40 m typical dive to view the upper chimney section
- **Chimney top**: ~15 m depth
- **Chimney base**: ~65–70 m depth (beyond recreational range)
- **Water temperature**: 4–8°C in Eyjafjörður; ~72–76°C at the vents
- **Visibility**: Typically 3–10 m in winter/poor conditions; up to 40 m in good conditions
- **Current**: Variable; can be strong in the fjord

## Requirements

- **Difficulty**: Advanced — cold water, potential strong currents, exceptional buoyancy control essential (chimney is fragile and easily damaged)
- **Certification**: Advanced Open Water or equivalent; drysuit experience required
- **Buoyancy**: Exceptional buoyancy control is mandatory — touching or damaging the chimney is prohibited
- **Operator**: Dives can only be booked through the Strýtan Divecenter at Hjalteyri

## Site Information

- **Location**: Eyjafjörður fjord, near Akureyri, North Iceland (NOT near Silfra/Reykjavik)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Pinnacle/seamount
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 40 meters
- **Protected Status**: Iceland's first underwater nature reserve (designated 2001)
