## Strytan Hydrothermal Chimney

Strýtan is the world's only accessible hydrothermal chimney — a 55-meter-tall limestone cone built over approximately 10,000 years by a freshwater hot spring emitting 75°C water into the cold salt water of Eyjafjörður fjord in North Iceland. It is located near Akureyri, not near Silfra or Thingvellir.

## Overview

**Important location note**: Strýtan is situated in Eyjafjörður fjord on the north coast of Iceland, near Iceland's second-largest city Akureyri — approximately 300 km north of Silfra and Thingvellir. It is operated by the Strýtan Divecenter at the old fish factory in Hjalteyri on the western shore of the fjord. This site was added to the Silfra Fissure destination grouping as it is Iceland's other world-class dive site, though it is geologically and geographically entirely separate.

Strýtan was discovered by divers Erlendur Bogason and Árni Halldósson in 1997 and became the first protected underwater area in Iceland in 2001. The chimney rises from the seafloor at 65 meters depth to within 15 meters of the surface. Dives are conducted along a shot line to 25 meters where a horizontal guide line leads to the chimney, then divers spiral upward around the cone. The best vantage point is from 40 meters depth, looking up at the full profile of the structure. This is an Advanced-only site due to depth, currents, and drysuit requirements.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Eyjafjörður fjord, near Hjalteyri and Akureyri, North Iceland (NOT near Silfra/Thingvellir)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive (Zodiac from Strýtan Divecenter at Hjalteyri)
- **Site Type**: Hydrothermal chimney / pinnacle
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 40 meters (chimney base at 65m; typical dive to 25-40m)
- **Chimney Top Depth**: 15 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–40 meters
- **Water Temperature**: 4–8°C ambient fjord water; vent fluid at 75°C creates visible halocline and thermocline
- **Current**: Can be strong; variable
- **Best Time**: Year-round (site is accessible all year)

## Marine Life

Strýtan supports significantly more marine life than the freshwater Silfra fissures. Large schools of cod and pollock circle the chimney. Wolffish inhabit the surrounding seafloor. Lumpsucker fish (locally known as "rognkjálki") are a distinctive Iceland species found here. Starry rays rest on the sandy bottom around the base. Macro subjects include colorful Flabellina nudibranchs, crustaceans, sponges, starfish, and anemones. The warm vent water and mineral-rich substrate create a localized productive ecosystem unlike anywhere else in Iceland.

## Dive Profile

A standard Strýtan day typically consists of two dives: the first at Arnarnesstrýtan (Little Strýtan — shallower, smaller chimneys, max 25m, approximately 5 minutes by Zodiac), and the second at the main Strýtan chimney (10 minutes by Zodiac). At Strýtan, divers descend along the shot line to 25 meters, follow the horizontal guide line to the chimney base, then spiral upward around the cone to the top at 15 meters. Looking upward from 40 meters provides the most dramatic view of the full chimney scale. The vent fluid at the top creates a visible shimmering halocline where fresh hot water meets cold salt water.

## Entry and Exit

Back-roll entry from a Zodiac at the surface mooring buoy. Descent is along a steel shot line. The mooring system protects the chimney from anchor damage. All dives are guided by Strýtan Divecenter staff. Surface interval is taken at the dive center between the two dives of the day.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Advanced certification is required; drysuit certification plus minimum 10 logged drysuit dives (within the last two years) is the prerequisite
- Book directly through Strýtan Divecenter at Hjalteyri or through Magmadive — this is a specialist site operated by the site's discoverer
- Good buoyancy control is essential: the chimney is a protected structure and contact must be avoided
- A dive torch is useful for illuminating the chimney surface and identifying macro life
- Allow 2–3 days if combining with other North Iceland diving (e.g., Arnarnesstrýtan on the same day)

## Safety Considerations

Strong currents can develop in Eyjafjörður and must be monitored. The advanced depth (dives routinely reach 25–40m) and cold water (4–8°C) make this a physically demanding site. Drysuit buoyancy control at 40 meters must be precise for safe ascent. The vent fluid at the chimney top reaches 75°C — divers must follow guide instructions to stay clear of the active vent openings. No independent diving is permitted; all dives are guided.

## Photography

Strýtan rewards wide-angle photography. The full-chimney profile shot, looking up from 40 meters, captures the complete 55-meter height of the structure with schools of cod and pollock swirling around it. The halocline at the vent top creates a distinctive shimmering lens effect visible in photographs and video. Macro photographers will find Flabellina nudibranchs, wolffish faces, and lumpsucker fish as distinctive North Atlantic subjects. Strobe lighting recommended given depths and ambient light levels.
