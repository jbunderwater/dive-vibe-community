# Chandelier Cave

Five interconnected chambers adorned with ancient stalactites and stalagmites, making this one of the world's most accessible and visually stunning cave dives

## Overview
Chandelier Cave is a system of five connected limestone chambers located inside a Rock Island cliff face in Malakal Harbor, just minutes from Koror. The cave formed during the last Ice Age when sea levels were over 100 meters lower, allowing the slow formation of stalactites and stalagmites that now hang above and below the waterline. Each chamber contains an air pocket where divers can surface and marvel at the formations in open air. The crystal-clear water, shallow depth (maximum 15 meters), and absence of current make this one of the most accessible cave dives anywhere in the world. Despite its ease of access, the experience of swimming through chambers glittering with ancient formations under dive-torch light is genuinely otherworldly. Chandelier Cave has been a staple of Palau diving since the earliest dive operators began taking visitors here in the 1970s.

## Site Information
- **Location**: Malakal Harbor, Rock Islands, Palau, Micronesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Cave/cavern
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 15 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15-30 meters (50-100 feet)
- **Current**: None
- **Best Time**: Year-round; sheltered location makes this an excellent bad-weather alternative

## Marine Life
Marine life is not the primary attraction here, but the cave entrance and surrounding reef host a variety of species. Cardinalfish and glassy sweepers school in the shadowed entrance area. Small shrimp and crabs hide in crevices throughout the chambers. Occasional mandarin fish are found near the entrance rubble. Outside the cave, the surrounding Malakal Harbor reef has anemonefish, moray eels, lionfish, and reef fish typical of Palau's inshore waters. The cave itself is a geological rather than biological attraction.

## Dive Profile
Divers enter through a submerged opening in the cliff face at approximately 3 meters depth and descend to a maximum of about 10-15 meters before swimming horizontally into the first chamber. Each of the five chambers connects via short passages at varying depths between 5-12 meters. Divers can surface in the air pockets of each chamber to rest and observe the stalactite formations above the waterline. Total dive time is typically 40-60 minutes with minimal air consumption due to the shallow depth and calm conditions. The dive retraces the same route for exit.

## Entry and Exit
A short boat ride from Koror (10-15 minutes) brings divers to the Rock Island entrance. Entry is a gentle giant stride or back roll into calm, protected water. The cave entrance is clearly visible as a dark opening in the limestone cliff. Exit is through the same entrance. No current and sheltered conditions make this a relaxed entry and exit.

## Tips and Recommendations
- Bring a powerful dive torch and a backup light -- the formations are spectacular under direct illumination
- Surface in each air pocket to see the stalactites from above, but do not touch the formations
- Exhale slowly when surfacing in air pockets to avoid disturbing the ancient air trapped inside
- This dive pairs well with a morning deep dive, as the shallow profile makes it an ideal second dive
- Keep your group small to minimize silt disturbance and maximize the experience

## Safety Considerations
While this is a true cave dive with overhead environments, the shallow depth, calm conditions, and multiple air pockets make it significantly less hazardous than most cave diving. However, divers should still be comfortable in enclosed spaces and carry at least one torch (two recommended). Buoyancy control is critical to avoid contacting and damaging the fragile stalactite formations. Do not touch or break any formations. Silt can reduce visibility quickly if fins contact the bottom -- use a frog kick or modified flutter kick. No cave certification is required, but comfort in overhead environments is essential.

## Photography
Chandelier Cave is a spectacular photography venue. Wide-angle lenses capture the chamber scale and formations, while a torch-wielding buddy provides dramatic rim lighting. Shoot upward at stalactites with a diver for scale. In the air pockets, remove your regulator and use a dry camera to photograph the above-water formations. Strobes can backscatter in the enclosed environment, so off-camera lighting or a single strobe aimed carefully works best. The contrast between dark water and illuminated formations creates striking images.
