# Siaes Tunnel

A dramatic natural swim-through tunnel cutting 30 meters through the reef, emerging onto a shark-patrolled outer wall with spectacular light effects

## Overview
Siaes Tunnel is a natural tunnel carved through the reef on Palau's western barrier reef, near Siaes Corner. The tunnel entrance begins on the reef top at around 5-8 meters, and the passage extends roughly 30 meters through the reef structure before opening onto the outer wall face. Swimming through the tunnel is a dramatic experience -- the passage narrows and widens, with shafts of light filtering through cracks and openings in the ceiling. The exit onto the outer wall delivers a classic Palau moment: emerging from the dimness of the tunnel into brilliant blue water with the wall dropping away below and sharks cruising in the blue. Despite the cave classification, Siaes Tunnel is a genuine swim-through with natural light visible throughout, making it accessible to advanced open-water divers who are comfortable in overhead environments. It is typically dived in combination with Siaes Corner.

## Site Information
- **Location**: Rock Islands, Palau, Micronesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Cave/cavern
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 12 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15-30 meters (50-100 feet)
- **Current**: Light to moderate inside; moderate to strong on outer wall
- **Best Time**: Year-round; best light effects with overhead sun

## Marine Life
Inside the tunnel, lobsters and large crabs hide in crevices. Soldierfish and squirrelfish school in the darker sections. Whitetip reef sharks sometimes rest on the tunnel floor. Outside on the wall, grey reef sharks patrol the blue. Spotted eagle rays pass the tunnel exit. Sea fans and soft corals decorate the outer wall face. Schools of fusiliers and butterflyfish animate the wall. Napoleon wrasse are seen cruising the reef top near the tunnel entrance. Nudibranchs and flatworms provide macro subjects on the tunnel walls.

## Dive Profile
Descend to the reef top at 5-8 meters and locate the tunnel entrance. Swim through the passage single-file, maintaining good buoyancy to avoid contact with ceiling and walls. The tunnel gently descends to about 10-12 meters at the exit point on the outer wall. After emerging, divers can drift along the wall or head to Siaes Corner if conditions and air permit. The tunnel itself takes only 5-10 minutes to transit, leaving ample time for wall exploration. Safety stop at 5 meters on the reef top on return.

## Entry and Exit
Boat dive from Koror (approximately 50 minutes) or from liveaboards. Entry by giant stride with descent to the reef top. The tunnel entrance is marked by the guide. Exit is typically a drift pickup after the wall portion, with SMB deployment at 5 meters. If conditions are calm, return to the boat anchor may be possible.

## Tips and Recommendations
- Carry a dive torch to illuminate the tunnel walls and spot critters hiding in crevices
- Stay single-file through the tunnel and maintain buoyancy to avoid silt and ceiling contact
- Pause at the tunnel exit and look out into the blue before emerging -- sharks are often visible
- Combine this dive with Siaes Corner for a tunnel-to-corner experience in one dive
- The tunnel is at its most photogenic when sunlight penetrates the cracks overhead -- midday is best

## Safety Considerations
While this is a swim-through rather than a dead-end cave, it is still an overhead environment. Divers should be comfortable in enclosed spaces. The passage is wide enough for single-file transit but does not allow turning around in the narrower sections. Maintain good buoyancy and finning technique to avoid silting. Carry at least one torch (two recommended). Do not attempt to surface inside the tunnel. The outer wall can have strong currents -- be prepared for the transition from sheltered tunnel to exposed wall.

## Photography
The tunnel offers dramatic through-the-looking-glass compositions. Shoot toward the exit opening for a framed view of the blue water and wall beyond, ideally with a diver silhouetted in the opening. Wide-angle or fisheye is essential inside the tunnel. Use off-camera lighting or a torch to illuminate the walls and create dramatic shadows. Outside on the wall, switch to pelagic-oriented shooting for sharks and rays against the blue. The contrast between the dark tunnel interior and the bright exit creates compelling images without any artificial lighting.
