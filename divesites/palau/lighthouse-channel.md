# Lighthouse Channel

A sheltered, shallow channel near Koror known for twilight mandarin fish sightings and an abundance of macro critters on rubble and coral heads

## Overview
Lighthouse Channel is a sheltered, shallow channel near the Koror lighthouse, offering a very different Palau diving experience from the dramatic walls and current-swept corners that dominate most itineraries. The channel features a sandy bottom with scattered coral heads and rubble at a maximum depth of just 5 meters, with gentle tidal currents providing a mild drift. The site's reputation rests primarily on its dusk dives: mandarin fish (*Synchiropus splendidus*) emerge from rubble hideouts at sunset to perform a brief mating display, and this is one of Palau's most reliable locations for observing this behaviour. By day, the sheltered conditions and shallow depth make it an excellent site for macro photography and checkout dives. Lighthouse Channel demonstrates that world-class diving in Palau does not require deep water or strong current.

## Site Information
- **Location**: Near Koror lighthouse, Koror, Palau, Micronesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Drift dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 5 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 10–20 meters (30–65 feet)
- **Current**: Light (gentle tidal drift)
- **Best Time**: Year-round; dusk dives for mandarin fish; night dives for critters

## Marine Life
Mandarin fish are the primary attraction, documented at this site type in Palau by multiple sources; guides know the specific rubble areas where they emerge at dusk. Lionfish are common on the coral bommies. Blue-spotted stingrays rest on the sand. Nudibranchs decorate the substrate. Anemonefish, gobies, and blennies populate every available coral head. At night, octopus and cuttlefish become active along the channel. The shallow rubble and coral-head habitat supports a broad range of small invertebrate life. Note: specific critter species beyond those listed (e.g., thorny seahorses, ornate ghost pipefish, Spanish dancers) appear in general Palau literature but have not been confirmed by three independent sources specifically at this channel — divers should regard those as possible rather than certain.

## Dive Profile
A simple, relaxed dive. Descend to the sandy bottom at 3–5 meters and drift gently with the tidal current along the channel, exploring coral heads and rubble patches. For mandarin fish, arrive at the designated rubble area before dusk and settle near the coral heads where the fish emerge — guides know the specific spots. The display lasts approximately 15–20 minutes around sunset. Night dives follow the same profile with torches. Bottom time is essentially unlimited given the shallow depth, with dives commonly lasting 60–80 minutes.

## Entry and Exit
Short boat ride from Koror (10–15 minutes). Entry by giant stride into calm, shallow water. The gentle drift carries divers along the channel. Exit is back to the boat, which follows the group or repositions for pickup. Surface conditions are always calm in this sheltered location.

## Tips and Recommendations
- Book a dusk dive specifically for mandarin fish — they emerge for only 15–20 minutes around sunset
- Ask your guide exactly where the mandarin fish rubble patches are — not all coral heads at this site produce sightings
- Bring a macro lens and patience for finding nudibranchs and small critters
- This is an excellent night dive site — ask your operator to schedule one here
- Perfect first or last dive of the day due to the shallow profile and proximity to Koror

## Safety Considerations
This is one of Palau's safest dive sites. The shallow depth, calm conditions, and sheltered location minimise all typical hazards. The gentle current is easy to manage for all experience levels. The main consideration is avoiding contact with coral heads and rubble to protect the habitat and your own skin. Lionfish are present — avoid touching corals and rubble carelessly. During night dives, carry a backup torch and maintain buddy contact.

## Photography
Lighthouse Channel is a macro photography destination. A 60mm or 100mm macro lens is ideal for mandarin fish and nudibranchs. Use a snoot or focused beam for dramatic lighting on small subjects. For mandarin fish, manual focus and patience are essential — the fish are fast and the display brief. Night photography offers vivid subjects against the dark background.
