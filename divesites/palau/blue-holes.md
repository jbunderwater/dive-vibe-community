# Blue Holes

Four openings in the reef top lead down into a cathedral-like cavern where sunbeams create one of Palau's most photographed underwater scenes

## Overview
Blue Holes is a cavern dive located just north of Blue Corner on the western barrier reef of Ngemelis Island. Four vertical openings in the reef top at 1-3 meters depth drop into an expansive limestone chamber at approximately 25 meters. The cavern's fame rests on its extraordinary light effects -- shafts of sunlight stream through the four holes, illuminating the dark interior like spotlights in a cathedral. The chamber opens onto the outer wall at 25-30 meters, where divers exit to drift along the Blue Corner wall. This combination of cavern and wall diving, often done as a two-site combo with Blue Corner, makes Blue Holes one of the most popular and visually stunning dives in Palau. Despite the 40-meter maximum depth, the cavern itself is open and spacious with no true overhead environment, making it accessible to confident intermediate divers.

## Site Information
- **Location**: Rock Islands, Palau, Micronesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Cave/cavern
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 40 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20-40 meters (65-130 feet)
- **Current**: Light to moderate inside cavern; moderate to strong on outer wall
- **Best Time**: Year-round; best light effects with overhead sun between 10am and 2pm

## Marine Life
Inside the cavern, mandarin fish can be found in the rubble at the base of the holes, particularly active at dusk. Whitetip reef sharks sometimes rest on the sandy cavern floor. Lobsters and large crabs hide in crevices along the cavern walls. Schools of cardinalfish and sweepers hover in the darker sections. Exiting onto the outer wall, the marine life shifts dramatically to pelagic species -- grey reef sharks, Napoleon wrasse, spotted eagle rays, and schools of barracuda and jacks. Soft corals, sea fans, and black coral trees decorate the wall. Turtles are frequently seen cruising along the drop-off.

## Dive Profile
Divers descend through one of the four holes in the reef top, dropping into the main chamber at around 25 meters. The cavern is roughly 20 meters wide with a sandy bottom at 25-30 meters. After exploring the light effects and interior life, divers exit through the large opening in the wall face at approximately 25 meters. From here, the dive continues as a wall drift, either heading south toward Blue Corner or north along the reef. The wall drops vertically to over 300 meters, so depth control on exit is critical. Safety stop at 5 meters on the reef top.

## Entry and Exit
Boat access from Koror (45 minutes) or from liveaboards anchoring in the area. Entry is a giant stride followed by a descent through one of the four reef-top openings. The largest hole is typically used for entry. Exit is a drift pickup after ascending from the wall section -- deploy an SMB from 5 meters.

## Tips and Recommendations
- Visit between 10am and 2pm for the best sunlight effects through the holes
- Carry a dive torch to explore the darker sections and illuminate the cavern walls
- This dive pairs perfectly with Blue Corner as a two-site drift -- ask your operator
- Hover at mid-depth inside the cavern to appreciate the full light-beam effect
- Check the rubble zone at the base of the holes for mandarin fish, especially late afternoon

## Safety Considerations
Although classified as a cavern rather than a true cave dive, divers should be comfortable in overhead environments. The large chamber and multiple exits mean there is always natural light visible. Watch your depth on exit -- the wall drops away steeply, and it is easy to descend beyond planned limits. Air management is important given the depth and duration of a combined Blue Holes/Blue Corner dive. Silt can be stirred in the cavern, so maintain good buoyancy and finning technique.

## Photography
Blue Holes is one of Palau's premier underwater photography sites. The sunbeam effect through the four holes is best captured with a wide-angle or fisheye lens, exposing for the ambient light and using a slow shutter speed. Position yourself near the cavern floor looking up for the most dramatic beam shots. A diver silhouetted in one of the holes adds compelling scale. On the outer wall, wide-angle is essential for capturing the reef topography and passing pelagics.
