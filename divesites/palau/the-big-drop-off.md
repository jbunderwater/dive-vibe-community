# The Big Drop Off

A vertical wall draped in giant sea fans and cascading soft corals at Ngemelis Island, offering dramatic wall diving in more sheltered conditions than Blue Corner

## Overview
The Big Drop Off is a stunning wall dive on the eastern side of Ngemelis Island, featuring a sheer vertical face that plunges hundreds of meters into the deep. What sets this site apart from Palau's other famous walls is the extraordinary density and quality of its soft coral and sea fan growth. Giant gorgonian fans over two meters across extend from the wall, while soft corals in purple, orange, and red cascade down the face in vivid displays. The reef top at 1-2 meters is a healthy hard coral plateau teeming with reef fish. Because the site is on the more sheltered eastern side of Ngemelis, conditions are typically calmer than at Blue Corner, making this an excellent wall dive for confident intermediate divers. The Big Drop Off regularly appears on lists of Palau's top five dives and is a highlight of every Rock Islands diving itinerary.

## Site Information
- **Location**: Rock Islands, Palau, Micronesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 18 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20-40 meters (65-130 feet)
- **Current**: Light to moderate
- **Best Time**: Year-round; more sheltered than western wall sites

## Marine Life
The wall is adorned with some of the largest gorgonian sea fans in Palau, along with dense growths of soft coral, whip corals, and sponges. Hawksbill and green sea turtles are seen regularly, feeding on sponges along the wall face. Grey reef sharks and whitetip reef sharks patrol the wall edge. Napoleon wrasse cruise by at regular intervals. Schools of fusiliers, surgeonfish, and pyramid butterflyfish animate the water column. Lionfish perch in overhangs. Leaf scorpionfish hide on coral ledges. Manta rays are occasional visitors, particularly during plankton blooms. The reef top hosts anemonefish, chromis, and damselfish in abundance.

## Dive Profile
Divers descend from the reef top over the wall edge, typically exploring between 5-18 meters where the soft coral and fan growth is most spectacular. The wall is nearly vertical, making depth control straightforward but requiring awareness as the bottom drops away to extreme depth. Swim along the wall face, looking into overhangs and crevices. The reef top at 1-2 meters is an excellent safety stop area with plenty to see among the hard corals. Most dives are 50-60 minutes at relaxed pace.

## Entry and Exit
Boat dive from Koror or liveaboard. Entry by giant stride onto the reef top. Depending on current, this can be a return-to-boat dive or a gentle drift with SMB pickup. The sheltered location means surface conditions are usually calm, making entry and exit straightforward.

## Tips and Recommendations
- Focus your dive between 5-18 meters where the soft coral growth is densest and most colorful
- Look carefully at the large sea fans for pygmy seahorses -- they are present but tiny and well camouflaged
- Morning light illuminates the wall face beautifully, making early dives ideal for photography
- This is an excellent second dive after Blue Corner, as the calmer conditions and shallower profile allow longer bottom times
- Hover off the wall rather than hugging it to appreciate the full scale of the fan and coral growth

## Safety Considerations
While this is one of Palau's more accessible wall dives, the sheer drop still demands respect. Monitor your depth and avoid descending beyond planned limits. Currents are usually mild but can pick up without warning. Maintain good buoyancy to avoid damaging the delicate soft corals and fans. Stay clear of the large sea fans -- contact with them causes lasting damage. Carry an SMB for drift exits.

## Photography
The Big Drop Off is a soft coral and sea fan photographer's dream. Wide-angle is essential to capture the scale of the fans against the deep blue background. Position yourself slightly off the wall and shoot along the face to show the cascading corals in context. Backlighting sea fans with a strobe reveals their structure. Macro shooters should search the large fans for pygmy seahorses and the wall crevices for nudibranchs. The reef top in shallow water offers excellent natural-light photography of hard coral formations.
