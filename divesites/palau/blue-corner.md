# Blue Corner

Arguably the most famous dive site on Earth, where ocean currents converge on a submerged reef promontory to deliver non-stop pelagic action

## Overview
Blue Corner is a submerged reef plateau jutting out into open ocean southwest of Ngemelis Island, consistently ranked among the top dive sites in the world by virtually every dive publication. The site's magic lies in its position at the convergence of deep ocean currents that funnel nutrients and attract an extraordinary density of large marine life. Divers typically hook into the reef at around 15 meters and hang in the current like human flags, watching the parade of grey reef sharks, Napoleon wrasse, eagle rays, massive schools of barracuda and bigeye jacks, and the occasional bull shark cruising past at arm's length. The reef top is a garden of hard corals alive with surgeonfish, triggerfish, and fusiliers. Blue Corner has been a mandatory stop on Palau liveaboard itineraries since the 1980s and remains the benchmark against which all other current-swept wall dives are measured.

## Site Information
- **Location**: Rock Islands, Palau, Micronesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20-40 meters (65-130 feet)
- **Current**: Strong to very strong, variable direction
- **Best Time**: Year-round; strongest currents and best pelagic action October to April

## Marine Life
Blue Corner is defined by its large pelagic residents. Grey reef sharks patrol the corner in groups of ten to thirty, often approaching within a few meters of hooked-in divers. Napoleon wrasse weighing over 50 kilograms are regulars. Spotted eagle rays glide past in small formations. Massive tornado-like schools of bigeye jacks and barracuda are a signature sight. Dogtooth tuna and bluefin trevally hunt along the wall edge. Bull sharks appear occasionally in deeper water. The reef top hosts dense populations of titan triggerfish, surgeonfish, unicornfish, and fusiliers. Whitetip reef sharks rest in crevices along the wall. Hawksbill and green sea turtles are seen on most dives.

## Dive Profile
The dive begins with a descent to the reef plateau at around 12-15 meters. Most guides lead divers to the corner's edge where the current is strongest and the pelagic action is best. Divers clip reef hooks to dead coral and inflate their BCDs slightly to hover above the reef, watching sharks and rays pass in the blue. After the hook phase, divers typically drift along the wall, which drops vertically to well over 300 meters. The wall section between 15-30 meters features overhangs, small caves, and dense soft coral growth. Ascent is made along the wall, with a safety stop at 5 meters on the reef top.

## Entry and Exit
Access is exclusively by dive boat, either from Koror-based day boats (approximately 45-minute ride) or liveaboards anchoring nearby. Entry is by giant stride into the blue. The boat captain reads the current direction to determine drop point. Exit is typically a drift pickup -- deploy your SMB during your safety stop so the boat can track and collect you.

## Tips and Recommendations
- Bring a reef hook and practice using it beforehand -- this is essential gear at Blue Corner
- Carry your hook clipped to a 1-meter line attached to your BCD D-ring for hands-free hovering
- Dive the incoming current for the best shark and pelagic encounters
- Morning dives tend to have the strongest current and most active marine life
- Keep your camera ready during descent -- the action often starts before you reach the reef

## Safety Considerations
Strong, unpredictable currents are the primary hazard at Blue Corner. Down-currents along the wall can rapidly push divers to dangerous depths, and current direction can shift mid-dive. Carry a signaling device and SMB at all times. Stay close to your group and guide. This site is not appropriate for newly certified divers. Hooks should only be attached to dead coral or rock to prevent reef damage. Monitor air consumption carefully, as the excitement can cause rapid breathing.

## Photography
Blue Corner is a wide-angle paradise. A fisheye or rectilinear wide-angle lens captures the shark parades, eagle ray fly-bys, and barracuda tornados that define this site. Shoot upward against the blue water for dramatic silhouettes. The reef top offers macro opportunities with nudibranchs and small critters, but most photographers come here for the big stuff. Video shooters should use a steady hand or tray -- the current can make stable footage challenging.
