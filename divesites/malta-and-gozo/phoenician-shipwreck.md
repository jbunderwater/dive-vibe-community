# Phoenician Shipwreck

A 2,700-year-old Phoenician trading vessel off Xlendi, Gozo — the oldest known shipwreck in the central Mediterranean.

## Overview

This late 7th-century BC Phoenician shipwreck is the oldest known shipwreck in the central Mediterranean and one of only nine Phoenician wrecks found worldwide. Located approximately 900 metres off Xlendi Bay, Gozo, at 110 metres depth, it was discovered in 2007 via a sonar survey by a University of Malta team.

The cargo includes approximately 50 Tyrrhenian-style amphorae, around 20 lava grinding stones (quern-stones sourced from Sicily), bowls, urns, and jugs. Some ceramics may have been produced on Gozo itself. Archaeological excavation was conducted between 2017 and 2021, during which a human tooth was recovered for carbon dating and DNA analysis. This was the first maritime archaeological survey to explore shipwrecks with divers beyond 100 metres depth.

Heritage Malta manages this protected archaeological site.

## Site Information

- **Location**: ~900m off Xlendi Bay, SW Gozo
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 110 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: Variable at depth
- **Current**: Variable
- **Best Time**: May to October

## Marine Life

At 110 metres, marine life is limited to deep-water species. The amphorae scatter provides shelter for small organisms.

## Dive Profile

Extreme technical dive at 110 metres. The cargo scatter of amphorae and grinding stones is the primary feature. No hull structure survives — the site presents as an archaeological debris field. Bottom time is measured in single-digit minutes.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive only. Heritage Malta permit mandatory — this is a protected archaeological site. Technical diving qualifications with deep trimix or CCR experience required.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Heritage Malta permit mandatory — this is a protected archaeological site
- The oldest known shipwreck in the central Mediterranean (~2,700 years old)
- One of only nine Phoenician wrecks found worldwide
- No hull survives — the site is an amphorae and cargo scatter
- No artefact removal permitted under any circumstances

## Safety Considerations

Extreme technical dive at 110 metres. CCR or very heavy trimix mandatory. Heritage Malta permit absolutely required. This is a protected archaeological site of global significance. No artefacts may be touched, moved, or removed. Full technical support team required.

## Photography

The amphorae scatter on the seabed is the primary photographic subject — 2,700-year-old ceramics in situ. The lava grinding stones from Sicily provide scale. This is an archaeological documentation opportunity of the highest order. Requires extremely powerful lighting.
