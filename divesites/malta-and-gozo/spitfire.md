# Spitfire

A WWII Supermarine Spitfire Mk.Vc discovered in 2021 off Gozo at 70 metres — lost during the Allied build-up to Operation Husky.

## Overview

This Supermarine Spitfire Mk.Vc (believed to be serial ES182, built at Castle Bromwich Aircraft Factory) was assigned to the 308th Fighter Squadron, 31st Fighter Group, USAAF. On 30 June 1943, the aircraft was en route from Korba, Tunisia, bound for Xewkija airfield on Gozo when it missed its approach. Radio contact was lost at that point and neither aircraft nor pilot, 2nd Lt John B Steven Jr, was found at the time.

The wreck was initially detected during a commercial sonar survey in 2020 and the discovery was announced in April 2021 following a confirming side-scan sonar survey. The aircraft lies at 70 metres depth on a sandy seabed off the coast of Gozo, sitting upright. During the violent ditching, the rear fuselage folded forward — the tail section is visible upside down behind the port wing trailing edge. The wooden Dowty Rotol propeller blades disintegrated on water impact; only the hub remains. Heritage Malta manages the site.

The loss occurred during the build-up period to Operation Husky — the Allied invasion of Sicily in July–August 1943.

## Marine Life

Limited marine life at 70 metres on the sandy bottom. Some encrustation on the airframe surfaces. No site-specific marine life reports are documented. The wider Malta/Gozo waters support grouper, moray eels, octopus, bream, barracuda, and Mediterranean corals.

## Dive Profile

Technical descent to 70 metres. The upright Spitfire is recognisable by its distinctive elliptical wing shape. Note the folded tail section behind the port wing — evidence of the violent ditching. The wooden propeller blades have disintegrated; only the hub remains. Bottom time is very limited.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive only from Gozo operators. Heritage Malta permit required. Technical diving qualifications mandatory.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Heritage Malta permit required
- The folded-forward tail tells the story of the violent ditching
- The wooden propeller blades disintegrated on impact — only the metal hub remains
- The pilot (2nd Lt Steven Jr) was USAAF, not RAF — Americans flew Spitfires from Malta during the Sicily campaign
- Lost during the build-up to Operation Husky (the Allied invasion of Sicily, July 1943)
- The serial ES182 identification is based on available records but is described in sources as "believed to be" — the specific aircraft has not been conclusively confirmed

## Safety Considerations

Technical dive at 70 metres requiring trimix and decompression planning. Heritage Malta permit mandatory. The site is offshore from Gozo — ensure adequate boat support for technical diving operations.

## Photography

The iconic Spitfire elliptical wing shape is immediately recognisable even at depth. The folded tail section provides dramatic visual evidence of the violent ditching. Requires powerful lights at 70 metres — the distinctive wing planform is the signature shot.
