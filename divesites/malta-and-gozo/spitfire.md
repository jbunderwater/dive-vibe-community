# Spitfire

A WWII Supermarine Spitfire Mk.V discovered in 2021 off Gozo at 70 metres — lost during the buildup to Operation Husky.

## Overview

This Supermarine Spitfire Mk.Vc (serial ES182) was flown by 2nd Lt John B Steven Jr, USAAF, 31st Fighter Group, 308th Fighter Squadron. On 30 June 1943, while returning from a combat mission in Tunisia, the aircraft missed its approach to Xewkija airfield on Gozo and was lost.

The wreck was discovered in April 2021 via a side-scan sonar survey off the coast of Gozo at 70 metres depth on a sandy seabed. The aircraft sits upright but the rear fuselage folded forward during the violent ditching — the tail is visible upside down behind the port wing trailing edge. The wooden Dowty Rotol propeller blades disintegrated on impact with the water. Heritage Malta manages the site.

The loss occurred during the buildup period to Operation Husky — the Allied invasion of Sicily in July–August 1943.

## Marine Life

Limited marine life at 70 metres on the sandy bottom. Some encrustation on the airframe surfaces.

## Dive Profile

Technical descent to 70 metres. The upright Spitfire is immediately recognisable by its distinctive elliptical wing shape. Note the folded tail section behind the port wing — evidence of the violent ditching. The wooden propeller blades have disintegrated. Bottom time is very limited.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive only from Gozo operators. Heritage Malta permit required. Technical diving qualifications mandatory.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Heritage Malta permit required
- The folded-forward tail tells the story of the violent ditching
- The wooden propeller blades disintegrated on impact — only the hub remains
- The pilot was USAAF, not RAF — Americans flew Spitfires from Malta during the Sicily campaign
- Lost during the buildup to Operation Husky (invasion of Sicily)

## Safety Considerations

Technical dive at 70 metres requiring trimix and decompression planning. Heritage Malta permit mandatory. The site is offshore from Gozo — ensure adequate boat support for technical diving operations.

## Photography

The iconic Spitfire elliptical wing shape is immediately recognisable even at depth. The folded tail section provides dramatic visual evidence of the violent ditching. Requires powerful lights at 70 metres — the distinctive wing planform is the signature shot.
