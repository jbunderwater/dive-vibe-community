# HMS St. Angelo

A WWII auxiliary tug mined in 1942 outside Valletta — sitting upright at 54 metres in an active shipping channel, with a telegraph and ship's wheel hub still in place.

## Overview

HMS St. Angelo was an auxiliary tugboat (approximately 24 metres long, 150 tons) named after Fort St. Angelo — the Royal Navy's headquarters in Grand Harbour, Valletta, which the tug regularly served. The vessel transported personnel and supplies from the Fort St. Angelo naval base to warships moored in Grand Harbour, and also performed rescue, towing, and minesweeping duties.

On 30 May 1942, at approximately 14:15 hrs, HMS St. Angelo struck a mine while engaged in a minesweeping operation about three-quarters of a mile off the entrance to Grand Harbour. Four of her crew were killed: Leading Seaman Joseph Debattista, Able Seaman Vincent Farrugia, Stoker Paul Grima, and Stoker Joseph Said.

The wreck lies approximately 1.5 km off the Grand Harbour entrance at 54 metres depth on a sandy bottom, sitting upright and in reportedly good condition. Interior features including the telegraph and the hub of the ship's wheel remain in place, along with the generator, sweeping cables, and other fittings. The site is in the main maritime channel with busy commercial shipping traffic in and out of Valletta — one of Malta's more hazardous dive locations.

## Site Information

- **Location**: ~1.5 km off Grand Harbour entrance, Valletta
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 54 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–30 meters
- **Current**: Variable; active shipping channel
- **Best Time**: May to October (check harbour closure schedule)

## Marine Life

The intact upright wreck provides shelter for grouper, moray eels, and octopus — confirmed by multiple dive operators. The sandy surroundings host bream and other Mediterranean species. Four crew members are buried with the wreck — treat with respect.

## Dive Profile

Descend via shotline to the deck of the upright wreck at approximately 45–50 metres. The compact 24-metre vessel can be surveyed in a single pass. Notable features include the telegraph, wheel hub, generator, and sweeping cables. Maximum seabed depth 54 metres. Gas management critical at this depth.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive only. The site is in an active maritime shipping channel — dive boats must coordinate with port authorities and maintain appropriate flags and signals. The harbour is sometimes closed to large vessel traffic on specific dates, making those days safer for diving here.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The site is in an active shipping channel — listen for engine noise throughout the dive and during safety stops
- The telegraph and ship's wheel hub are the most distinctive interior features
- Named after Fort St. Angelo in Grand Harbour, the Royal Navy's Malta headquarters, which it regularly serviced
- Four Maltese crew members were killed — treat the site with appropriate respect
- Check harbour closure schedules — diving is considerably safer when large vessel traffic is suspended

## Safety Considerations

Advanced/technical certification required for 54-metre depth. The site's location in an active shipping channel is the primary hazard — large vessels transit this area continuously. Dive boats must display appropriate signals. Deploy SMB well before surfacing. Listen for engine noise during decompression stops. Dive only when harbour conditions are appropriate.

## Photography

The upright, well-preserved wreck makes an excellent wide-angle subject. The compact size allows the entire vessel to be captured in a single frame with a wide lens. The telegraph and wheel hub are compelling close-up subjects. Shoot upward to silhouette the vessel against ambient light.
