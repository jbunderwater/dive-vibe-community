# Carolita

The locally used name for Xlighter X127 — a WWI-built Royal Navy lighter at Marsamxett Harbour.

## Overview

Carolita is the local dive community name for the wreck of Xlighter X127, a British water lighter originally built as a landing craft in 1915 for the Royal Navy. The vessel sank on 6 March 1942 during WWII operations in Marsamxett Harbour off Manoel Island. The wreck was unidentified for decades, earning the name "Carolita" or "Coralita" from Malta's diving community. A 2003 survey identified the vessel, confirmed as X-127 in 2006.

The wreck lies at a steep angle on a harbour slope — bow at 5 metres and stern at 22–25 metres. Despite decades underwater, she is in exceptionally good condition. Visibility is consistently poor due to the harbour location. A Land Rover wreck lies along the coast nearby.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Marsamxett Harbour, off Manoel Island
- **Entry Type**: Shore entry
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 3–8 meters (consistently poor)
- **Current**: Light
- **Best Time**: Year-round

## Marine Life

Octopuses and groupers inhabit the wreck. The harbour environment limits marine diversity.

## Dive Profile

Shore entry and swim to the wreck on the harbour slope. The steep angle — bow at 5 metres descending to stern at 25 metres — provides a dramatic dive profile. The excellent preservation allows inspection of the lighter's construction details.

## Entry and Exit

Shore entry from the Manoel Island area. Sheltered harbour conditions.

## Tips and Recommendations

- 'Carolita' is the local name — the wreck is actually Xlighter X127
- Visibility is always poor — this is a harbour site
- The steep angle from 5m to 25m is dramatic
- A Land Rover wreck is nearby along the coast

## Safety Considerations

The shallow bow (5m) is suitable for beginners. The 25m stern section requires more experience. Poor visibility means torch and buddy contact are essential.

## Photography

The steep angle and moody harbour visibility create atmospheric images. A torch illuminates the well-preserved hull details.
