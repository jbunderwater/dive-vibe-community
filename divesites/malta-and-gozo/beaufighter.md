# Beaufighter

A WWII Bristol Beaufighter twin-engine fighter resting inverted on the sandy seabed off Dragonara Point, Sliema, at 37–38 metres.

## Overview

The Beaufighter (serial T5174) was a Bristol Beaufighter twin-engine, two-seat heavy fighter of No. 272 Squadron RAF. On 17 March 1943 at 11:25 hours, nine Beaufighters from 272 Squadron took off to escort nine Beaufort torpedo bombers of No. 39 Squadron on a shipping strike off Point Stelo. Shortly after takeoff, Beaufighter T5174 — crewed by Sergeant Donald Frazee (pilot) and Sergeant Sandery (observer) — began to vibrate violently and lost speed rapidly. With a safe landing out of the question, Sgt Frazee ditched the aircraft at approximately 100 mph into a slight swell approximately 800 metres off Dragonara Point, Sliema. Both crew members were picked up by a Maltese water-taxi shortly after and survived.

The aircraft lies inverted on a clean, white sandy seabed at 37–38 metres depth, approximately 800–900 metres offshore. Most of the fuselage is buried in sand, but the wings and main fuselage sections are largely intact. Both undercarriage frames are locked in position with degraded rubber tyres visible. The port-side propeller remains attached to its engine. The tail section lies separately approximately 5 metres from the main structure. The wreck has continued to degrade over decades and recent diver reports note that primarily the wing undersides and engines remain recognisable.

## Site Information

- **Location**: ~800–900 m off Dragonara Point, Sliema, Malta
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 38 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–30 meters
- **Current**: Light to moderate
- **Best Time**: May to October

## Marine Life

Moray eels have been documented taking up residence in the four 20 mm Hispano cannon ports along the fuselage. Triggerfish have been observed around the wreck. The surrounding sandy seabed is otherwise relatively bare.

## Dive Profile

Descend via the boat's shotline to the sandy bottom at 37–38 metres. Orient to the engines and the attached port-side propeller first, then follow the wing sections. The separate tail section lies approximately 5 metres from the main structure. The small footprint of the aircraft can be covered in a single dive. Gas management is critical at this depth.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive only from Sliema or St. Julian's operators. The site is approximately 800–900 metres offshore from Dragonara Point.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The port propeller still attached to the engine is the most distinctive feature
- Look for moray eels in the four cannon ports along the fuselage
- The tail section lies approximately 5 metres from the main wreck — do not miss it
- The wreck continues to deteriorate — conditions may differ from older guides
- Serial T5174 and crew names (Frazee and Sandery) are confirmed from RAF records

## Safety Considerations

Advanced certification required due to 38-metre depth. No penetration is possible due to the inverted position and degradation. Monitor gas consumption carefully and carry an SMB.
