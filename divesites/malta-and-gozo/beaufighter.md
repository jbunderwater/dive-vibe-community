# Beaufighter

A WWII Bristol Beaufighter twin-engine fighter resting inverted on the sandy seabed off St. Julian's Point at 38 metres.

## Overview

The Beaufighter is a Bristol Aeroplane Company twin-engine, two-seat heavy fighter (length 12.6m, wingspan 17.6m) that crashed on 17 March 1943 after mechanical problems shortly after takeoff from Malta. Both crew members ditched successfully and survived. The aircraft lies approximately 900 metres offshore from St. Julian's Point, Sliema, inverted on a sandy seabed at 35–38 metres depth.

The wreck is heavily degraded after decades underwater. The port-side propeller remains attached to its engine, and the wing underside sections and both undercarriage frames are visible. Recent diver reports indicate continued deterioration, with mainly the wing undersides and engines remaining recognisable. The surrounding sandy seabed supports limited marine life.

## Site Information

- **Location**: St. Julian's Point, Sliema
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 38 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–30 meters
- **Current**: Light to moderate
- **Best Time**: May to October

## Marine Life

Marine life is sparse on the surrounding sandy seabed. Small fish shelter among the remaining metal structures. The wreck's degraded surfaces host some sponge growth and encrustation.

## Dive Profile

Descend via the boat's shotline to the sandy bottom at 35–38 metres. The aircraft lies inverted — orient yourself to the engines and propeller first, then follow the wing sections. The wreck's small footprint (12.6m length) allows a complete circuit within bottom time limits at this depth. Gas management is critical at 38 metres. Ascend with adequate reserve for a safety stop at 5 metres.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive only from Sliema or St. Julian's operators. The site is approximately 900 metres offshore. Conditions are generally calm but check weather before departure.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The port propeller still attached to the engine is the most photogenic feature
- Bring a torch — details are hard to see on the dark sandy bottom
- The wreck is small and can be covered in a single dive
- Note the undercarriage frames — evidence of a wheels-up ditching
- The wreck continues to deteriorate — manage expectations for structural detail

## Safety Considerations

Advanced certification required due to 38-metre depth. This is not a WWII combat loss — the crew survived a mechanical failure. No penetration possible due to inverted position and degradation. Monitor air consumption carefully and carry an SMB.

## Photography

Wide-angle shots of the propeller and engine are the main subjects. The inverted fuselage silhouette against the sandy bottom can make a dramatic composition with backlighting. A torch brings out colour on the encrusted metal surfaces.
