# HMS Hellespont

A WWII paddle tug sunk in 1942 during Axis air raids on Grand Harbour, later scuttled offshore in an upright position at 41 metres.

## Overview

HMS Hellespont was a Robust-class paddle tug (steam-powered paddle steamer, 46 metres long) damaged on 6–7 April 1942 during Luftwaffe and Italian air raids while moored inside Grand Harbour, Valletta. The bombing destroyed the first 15 metres of the bow section, which was never recovered. After the war, the wreckage was raised to clear the harbour and scuttled approximately 1.5 km from the harbour entrance off Rinella.

Discovered by divers on 15 May 1999, the wreck sits upright on a sandy bottom at 35–41 metres depth. The vessel is well preserved except for the missing bow section — the paddle wheels and remaining superstructure are clearly identifiable. Strong currents are often present at this exposed offshore location.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Off Rinella, ~1.5 km from Grand Harbour entrance
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 41 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–30 meters
- **Current**: Often strong
- **Best Time**: May to October

## Marine Life

The upright wreck structure hosts groupers, moray eels, and octopus. The paddle wheel structures provide shelter for smaller fish. Conger eels inhabit the interior spaces.

## Dive Profile

Descend via shotline to the deck at approximately 35 metres. The paddle wheels amidships are the most distinctive feature. Explore the preserved stern section, then note where the bow section ends abruptly — destroyed by the 1942 bombing. Maximum depth at the seabed is 41 metres. Monitor currents throughout.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive only from Valletta or Sliema operators. The site is approximately 1.5 km offshore from Grand Harbour. Currents can be strong — check conditions.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The paddle wheels are the signature feature — unique among Malta's wrecks
- Note the abrupt end where the 15-metre bow section was destroyed by bombing
- Strong currents are common — bring a reef hook and be prepared to abort
- Deep Diver certification minimum recommended

## Safety Considerations

Advanced certification required for 41-metre depth. Strong currents are frequently present — carry appropriate current management equipment and an SMB. The site's offshore location means surface support is essential. Monitor gas consumption carefully.

## Photography

The paddle wheels are a unique wide-angle subject. The abrupt bow termination — where the bombing damage begins — tells the wreck's story in a single frame. Shoot with ambient light from above for dramatic silhouettes.
