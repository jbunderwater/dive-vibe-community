# HMS Southwold bow

The bow section of a Type II Hunt-class destroyer mined in 1942, lying on its side at 68 metres — separated from the stern by 300 metres.

## Overview

HMS Southwold was a Type II Hunt-class destroyer (1,050 tons, 85.3 metres long, launched 29 May 1941) that struck a British mine on 24 March 1942 approximately 2.5 km off Marsaskala on Malta's east coast. The ship was attempting to pass a line to HMS Breconshire when the explosion flooded the engine room and destroyed electrical systems. One officer and four ratings were killed; survivors were rescued by HMS Dulverton.

The wreck lies in two sections separated by approximately 300 metres. The bow section (~40 metres long) lies on its starboard side at 68 metres depth. The stern section (~28 metres long) sits upright at 73 metres. Both sections show excellent coral growth and significant weapons systems remain visible. Heritage Malta has managed the site since May 2019.

## Site Information

- **Location**: ~2.5 km off Marsaskala, east Malta
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 68 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–30 meters
- **Current**: Variable
- **Best Time**: May to October

## Marine Life

Excellent coral growth covers both sections. The wreck structures attract grouper, amberjack, and other pelagic species. The debris field between the sections hosts smaller reef species.

## Dive Profile

Technical dive requiring two separate descents to properly explore both sections. The bow section at 68 metres lies on its starboard side — explore the weapons systems and hull structure. The stern at 73 metres sits upright. The 300-metre separation between sections means most divers choose one per dive. Bottom time is extremely limited.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive only. Heritage Malta special permit required since May 2019. Technical diving qualifications mandatory.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Heritage Malta special permit required
- The wreck is in two sections 300 metres apart — plan which section to visit
- The bow at 68m is slightly shallower than the stern at 73m
- Ironically struck a British mine, not an enemy one
- Five crew were killed — treat as a war grave

## Safety Considerations

Technical dive at 68–73 metres requiring trimix and decompression planning. Heritage Malta permit mandatory. Five crew were lost — treat with respect. The two sections require separate dive planning.

## Photography

The coral-encrusted hull sections make dramatic wide-angle subjects. Weapons systems provide scale and historical context. The separated bow and stern tell the story of the mine strike that broke the ship apart.
