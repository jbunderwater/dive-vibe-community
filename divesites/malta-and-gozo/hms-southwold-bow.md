# HMS Southwold bow

The bow section of a Type II Hunt-class destroyer mined in 1942, lying on its starboard side at 68–70 metres — separated from the stern by 300 metres.

## Overview

HMS Southwold was a Type II Hunt-class destroyer (85 metres long, 9.6-metre beam, completed 9 October 1941, built by J. Samuel White and Company, East Cowes) that struck a mine on 24 March 1942 approximately 1.5 miles from Marsaskala on Malta's east coast. The ship was attempting to tow the damaged oil tanker HMS Breconshire to port when the mine detonated beneath her engine room. The structural damage proved fatal — the hull split in two. One officer and four crewmen were killed in the explosion; survivors were rescued.

The wreck lies in two sections separated by approximately 300 metres. The bow section (~40 metres long) lies on its starboard side at approximately 68–70 metres depth. The stern section (~28 metres long) sits upright at approximately 72 metres. Both sections show significant growth and weapons systems remain visible. Heritage Malta has managed the site since May 2019 — diving requires a special permit and approved dive centre.

## Site Information

- **Location**: ~1.5 miles from Marsaskala, east Malta
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 68 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–30 meters
- **Current**: Variable
- **Best Time**: May to October

## Marine Life

Both sections of the wreck attract marine life. Grouper, amberjack, and other species have been observed around the wreck structure. The debris field between sections supports additional marine life. No site-specific scientific surveys are documented; observations are from dive operator reports.

## Dive Profile

Technical dive requiring two separate descents to properly explore both sections. The bow section at 68–70 metres lies on its starboard side — explore the weapons systems and hull structure. The stern at approximately 72 metres sits upright. The 300-metre separation between sections means most divers choose one section per dive. Bottom time is extremely limited at these depths.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive only. Heritage Malta special permit required since May 2019 — must dive with an approved operator. Technical diving qualifications mandatory.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Heritage Malta permit required — dive through an approved operator only
- The wreck is in two sections 300 metres apart — plan which section to visit
- The bow at 68–70m is slightly shallower than the stern at ~72m
- The ship struck a mine while on an allied mercy mission to tow HMS Breconshire — not sunk by enemy action directly
- Five crew were killed — treat with appropriate respect

## Safety Considerations

Technical dive at 68–72 metres requiring trimix and decompression planning. Heritage Malta permit mandatory. Five crew were lost — treat with respect. The two sections require separate dive planning and gas management.

## Photography

The coral-encrusted hull sections make dramatic wide-angle subjects. Weapons systems provide scale and historical context. The separated bow and stern together document the mine strike that broke the ship apart.
