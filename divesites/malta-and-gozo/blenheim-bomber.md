# Blenheim Bomber

A WWII Bristol Blenheim light bomber resting upright on sand and reef off Malta's east coast at 42 metres.

## Overview

The Bristol Blenheim is a twin-engine light bomber (approximately 13 m in length, 17.2 m wingspan, crew of three) built by the Bristol Aeroplane Company for the RAF. On 13 December 1941, the aircraft was on a mission to Kefalonia, Greece. Italian aircraft attacked the plane and damaged the port engine, forcing the pilot to ditch in the sea near Malta. The crew survived with minor injuries.

The wreck lies approximately 500 metres offshore from Xrobb l-Għaġin on Malta's east coast at 39–42 metres depth, upright on sand and small reef areas. The wings and radial engines are mostly intact — the starboard engine retains a bent propeller while the port engine propeller is missing. The cockpit cover is absent and the rear fuselage has separated, lying inverted and partially buried in sand several metres from the main structure. The wreck has deteriorated significantly in recent years; one source describes the current state as primarily "the skeleton of the wreck."

## Site Information

- **Location**: ~500 m offshore from Xrobb l-Għaġin, east coast Malta
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 42 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–30 meters
- **Current**: Light to moderate, can be strong
- **Best Time**: May to October

## Marine Life

No site-specific marine life reports found. The wider Malta/Gozo waters support grouper, moray eels, octopus, bream, barracuda, and Mediterranean corals.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the main wreck structure at 39–42 metres. The intact wing section and engines are the best-preserved parts. Note the bent starboard propeller and absent port propeller. The separated rear fuselage lies inverted several metres away and is partially buried. Bottom time is limited at this depth.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive only from east coast operators. The site is approximately 500 metres offshore from Xrobb l-Għaġin.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Compare the two engines: starboard has a bent propeller, port propeller is missing
- The separated rear fuselage is easy to miss — ask your guide for its location
- The wreck is deteriorating — features described in older guides may no longer be visible
- The open cockpit (no canopy) provides a window into the aircraft's history

## Safety Considerations

Advanced certification required for 42-metre depth. The wreck has been significantly degraded — avoid contact with fragile remaining structures. Monitor gas consumption and plan decompression. Currents can be present. Carry an SMB.
