# Blenheim Bomber

A WWII Bristol Blenheim light bomber resting upright on sand and reef off Malta's east coast at 42 metres.

## Overview

The Bristol Blenheim is a twin-engine light bomber (length ~13m, wingspan 17.2m, crew of three) lost on 13 December 1941 during a mission to Kefalonia, Greece. Italian aircraft attacked the plane and damaged the port engine, forcing the pilot to ditch near Malta. The crew survived with minor injuries.

The wreck lies approximately 500 metres off Xrobb l-Għaġin on Malta's east coast at 39–42 metres depth, upright on sand and small reef areas. The wings and radial engines are mostly intact — the starboard engine retains a bent propeller while the port engine propeller is missing. The cockpit cover is absent and the pilot's seat has been removed. The rear fuselage has separated and lies inverted, partially buried in sand several metres from the main structure. The wreck has deteriorated significantly in recent years.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Xrobb l-Għaġin, east coast Malta
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 42 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–30 meters
- **Current**: Light to moderate
- **Best Time**: May to October

## Marine Life

The wreck structure provides limited shelter for reef fish. The surrounding sand and reef areas host typical Mediterranean species including grouper, moray eels, and octopus.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the main wreck structure at 39–42 metres. Start with the intact wing section and engines — note the bent starboard propeller and missing port propeller. The separated rear fuselage lies inverted several metres away and is partially buried. Bottom time is limited at this depth.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive only from east coast operators. The site is approximately 500 metres offshore from Xrobb l-Għaġin.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Compare the two engines — starboard has bent propeller, port propeller is missing
- The separated rear fuselage is easy to miss — ask your guide for its location
- The wreck is deteriorating — features described in older guides may no longer be visible
- The open cockpit (no canopy, no seat) provides a window into the aircraft's history

## Safety Considerations

Advanced certification required for 42-metre depth. The wreck has been significantly degraded — avoid contact with fragile remaining structures. Monitor gas consumption and plan decompression. Carry an SMB.

## Photography

The intact wing section with engines provides the best wide-angle composition. The bent starboard propeller makes an evocative detail shot. The separated tail section is worth documenting as it shows the violence of the ditching.
