# Zonqor Point Wrecks

Three scuttled wrecks on a single shore dive — two WWII-era tugs and a Cold War patrol boat, transforming barren sand into a thriving artificial reef.

## Overview

Zonqor Point Wrecks is a multi-wreck shore dive site on the north side of Marsaskala Bay in southeast Malta, where three intentionally scuttled vessels rest in close proximity at 20–24 metres depth on what was previously barren sand.

**Tug St. Michael** (built 1944 in Canada, 20m long) served the Royal Navy in WWII then worked Malta's Grand Harbour. Scuttled 16 May 1998, she sits upright on sand approximately 15 metres from the reef. Her cabin was destroyed by a winter storm in 2025.

**Tug Number 10** (built late 1940s at Malta Drydocks, 16m long) was scuttled the same day alongside St. Michael. She sits upright with her bow resting against the reef base, approximately 50–60 metres behind St. Michael.

**Patrol Boat P33** (Bremse-class, built 1971–72 in East Germany, 23m long) was scuttled 31 July 2021 between the two tugs. She is disintegrating rapidly and now lies in pieces.

The three wrecks have transformed the barren seabed into a productive artificial reef ecosystem.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Zonqor Point, Marsaskala, SE Malta
- **Entry Type**: Shore entry
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 22 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 10–30 meters (avg 20m)
- **Current**: Light; sheltered from NW winds
- **Best Time**: May to October

## Marine Life

Octopuses, groupers, cuttlefish, moray eels, hermit crabs, giant tun snails, and various fish schools. The wrecks are partially covered with sponges and corals, creating an artificial reef oasis on previously barren sand.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Three wrecks on a single shore dive — unique in Malta
- Shore accessible with a 5-minute surface swim
- P33 is disintegrating rapidly — don't rely on structural integrity
- Rocky entry path between salt pans — watch your footing
- Sheltered from NW winds — good alternative when north coast sites are blown out
- St. Michael's cabin was lost to a 2025 storm — the wreck is changing
