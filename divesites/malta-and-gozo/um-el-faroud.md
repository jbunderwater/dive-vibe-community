# Um El Faroud

Malta's largest and most iconic shore-accessible wreck — a 110-metre Libyan oil tanker intentionally scuttled off Wied iż-Żurrieq in 1998.

## Overview

The Um El Faroud is Malta's flagship wreck dive, one of the most impressive shore-accessible wreck dives in the Mediterranean. The vessel was a Libyan gas tanker (built 1969, 110 metres long, 10,000 tons) that suffered a catastrophic gas explosion in Malta's Grand Harbour on 3 February 1995, killing nine dockyard workers. Declared a total write-off after the explosion, she was cleaned and intentionally scuttled off the south coast near Wied iż-Żurrieq on 2 September 1998, prepared for safe diving with all doors removed and entry holes cut. A memorial brass plaque bearing the names of the nine workers lost in the 1995 explosion is prominently displayed on the bridge, making this dive a tribute as well as an exploration. The wreck broke in two during a severe winter storm in 2005–2006, and the two sections have shifted so that they now present separate but adjacent exploration objectives. She rests upright on a sloping seabed, with the funnel at 15 metres, main deck at 25 metres, and propeller and rudder at 36 metres.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Wied iż-Żurrieq, south coast of Malta
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive (shore entry also possible but boat recommended)
- **Site Type**: Wreck (intentional scuttle)
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 36 metres
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–30 metres
- **Water Temperature**: 14–26°C
- **Current**: Light to moderate; can be strong on certain wind/wave states
- **Best Time**: May to October

## Marine Life

The Um El Faroud's enormous hull surface has become a productive artificial reef. Large groupers patrol the wreck's exterior, particularly around the bridge and engine room superstructure. Barracuda and pelagic jacks sweep past the stern section. Moray eels and conger eels inhabit the interior passageways. Octopus occupy openings throughout the hull. Schools of bream and amberjack move around the wreck's perimeter. The propeller at 36 metres attracts schools of barracuda and is a popular dive destination for experienced divers. Squid are often seen at night near the wreck.

## Dive Profile

The wreck's scale means two or more dives are needed for thorough exploration. Standard plan: descend to the main deck at 25 metres, explore the bridge superstructure and memorial plaque, penetrate accessible sections (engine room, passageways), then ascend to the funnel at 15 metres for a partial safety stop. The propeller and rudder section at 36 metres is a deeper sub-objective requiring careful gas management. The bow section, shifted by the storm, provides a second complementary dive.

## Entry and Exit

Accessible by boat from Wied iż-Żurrieq; some operators also offer shore entry. A permit to dive to 30 metres minimum is required. A permanent line marks the wreck location from surface. Descend the line to the deck level. Deploy an SMB before ascending — the area receives dive boat traffic.

## Tips

- Allocate two separate dives to cover both the main hull/stern and the separated bow section properly
- Find the memorial plaque on the bridge — reading the names of the nine workers who died in 1995 is a moving experience
- The propeller and rudder at 36 metres are a highlight; visit early in the dive before air consumption climbs
- Advanced buoyancy control and a torch are essential for wreck penetration — many passages are accessible but can silt up if disturbed

## Safety

Minimum advanced OW certification with wreck or deep dive experience. Advanced/penetration wreck diving requires wreck specialty certification and a torch. Monitor gas consumption carefully at 25–36 metres. Deploy an SMB before surfacing — diving activity is concentrated in this area. Strong wind can make the approach difficult; check weather before departure.

## Photography

The Um El Faroud is one of the most photographed wrecks in the Mediterranean. Wide-angle shots looking along the massive hull, the funnel at 15 metres with blue water above, and the enormous propeller at 36 metres are iconic subjects. The memorial plaque is a compelling macro or medium-distance subject. Inside the wreck, a torch and wide-angle lens with strobes capture the atmospheric passages.
