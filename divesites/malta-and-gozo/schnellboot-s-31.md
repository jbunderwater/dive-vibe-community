# Schnellboot S-31

A WWII German E-boat lost while laying mines outside Valletta in 1942 — torpedoes still loaded and anti-aircraft guns still standing at 65 metres.

## Overview

Schnellboot S-31 was a German fast motor torpedo boat (E-boat) approximately 33 metres long with a 5-metre beam, constructed with an aluminium frame covered in mahogany planking. Deployed to the Mediterranean in September 1941, she was part of the Axis forces besieging Malta.

On 10 May 1942, while laying mines near Grand Harbour Valletta, S-31 collided with a loose mine — possibly one of her own that had broken free. Thirteen crew were rescued but thirteen were lost, making this a **war grave**.

Discovered in September 2000, the wreck lies at approximately 65 metres depth. The wooden casing has rotted away, leaving the aluminium frame exposed. The hull is broken in two sections. Remarkably, original weaponry remains intact: three engines with triple propellers and rudders at the stern, twin torpedo tubes at the bow with torpedoes still loaded and ready for launch, and anti-aircraft machine guns still standing in their mountings. Heritage Malta manages the site since 2019.

- **Entry Type**: Boat dive

- **Typical Visibility**: 15–30 meters
- **Current**: Variable
- **Best Time**: May to October

## Marine Life

The exposed aluminium framework provides shelter for Mediterranean deep-water species. Limited marine growth on the aluminium structure compared to steel wrecks.

## Dive Profile

Technical descent to 65 metres. The exposed aluminium skeleton reveals the E-boat's construction. The bow torpedo tubes — still loaded — are the primary objective. The three engines with triple propeller arrangement at the stern demonstrate the vessel's high-speed design. The standing anti-aircraft guns are found amidships. Bottom time is limited.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive only. Heritage Malta permit required since 2019. Technical diving qualifications mandatory.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Heritage Malta permit required
- 13 crew were lost — this is a war grave
- The loaded torpedo tubes at the bow are extraordinary — still ready for launch after 80+ years
- The wooden hull has rotted away — you're seeing the aluminium skeleton
- The triple propeller arrangement shows this was a very fast vessel (Schnellboot = fast boat)

## Safety Considerations

Technical dive at 65 metres requiring appropriate gas mixtures and decompression planning. Heritage Malta permit mandatory. This is a war grave — 13 crew died. The loaded torpedoes pose no modern hazard but should not be disturbed. No artefact removal.

## Photography

The loaded torpedo tubes are the most dramatic subject — possibly unique in recreational diving worldwide. The standing anti-aircraft guns provide powerful historical images. The exposed aluminium skeleton against the sandy bottom creates distinctive compositions unlike typical steel wrecks.
