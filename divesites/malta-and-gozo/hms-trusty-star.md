# HMS Trusty Star

A WWII naval minesweeper drifter lost to a mine in 1942, discovered in 2016 at 85–90 metres off Valletta.

## Overview

HMD Trusty Star (His Majesty's Drifter) was originally a fishing vessel named FV Elie Ness, built in 1919 by Ouse Shipbuilding for the Royal Navy. The steel-hulled vessel (~26 metres long, 5.5-metre beam) was powered by a triple expansion steam engine. Requisitioned by the Admiralty in 1939, she was converted to a minesweeper.

On 10 June 1942, while clearing mines outside Grand Harbour, Trusty Star hit a mine. One Maltese seaman was injured but the crew largely escaped. The wreck was discovered in September 2016 and opened to divers on 1 May 2019.

She lies approximately 3 km off Fort St. Elmo at 85–90 metres depth, quite intact but covered in silt, resting at a 45-degree angle on her starboard side on a sandy seabed. Heritage Malta manages the site.

## Site Information

- **Location**: ~3 km off Fort St. Elmo, Valletta
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 90 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: Variable at depth
- **Current**: Variable
- **Best Time**: May to October

## Marine Life

The silt-covered wreck supports limited marine life at this depth. Some encrustation and deep-water species present.

## Dive Profile

Extreme technical dive at 85–90 metres. The vessel lies at a 45-degree angle on her starboard side — orient to the hull and explore along its 26-metre length. The intact condition rewards close inspection of the minesweeping vessel's features. Bottom time is measured in minutes at this depth.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive only. Heritage Malta permit required since May 2019. Technical diving qualifications with deep trimix or rebreather experience mandatory.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Heritage Malta permit required
- Designation is HMD (His Majesty's Drifter), not HMS
- Originally a fishing vessel — look for the converted minesweeping equipment
- The wreck is silted — maintain good buoyancy to avoid reducing visibility
- She survived the mining with only one casualty — most crew escaped

## Safety Considerations

Extreme technical dive at 85–90 metres. CCR or heavy trimix mandatory. Heritage Malta permit required. The wreck's silty covering means careful buoyancy control is essential. Full technical support team required.

## Photography

The intact hull at a dramatic 45-degree angle makes a compelling subject. Silt disturbance is the main photography challenge — approach carefully and shoot before disturbing the area. Powerful lights required at 85+ metres.
