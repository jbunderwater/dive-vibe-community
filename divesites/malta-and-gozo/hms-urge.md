# HMS Urge

A WWII U-class submarine lost with all hands in 1942 — discovered in 2019, now a designated war grave at 130 metres. **This is NOT a recreational dive site.** The depth (130 m) places it firmly in extreme technical diving territory and no regular diver access is permitted.

## Overview

HMS Urge was a U-class British submarine (58.22 metres long, 4.9-metre beam) that departed Grand Harbour on 27 April 1942 bound for Alexandria, Egypt. She never arrived. While still on the surface shortly after exiting Grand Harbour, she struck a German naval mine. The resulting explosion destroyed the bow section and the submarine sank rapidly. All 32 crew members, 11 naval passengers, and a British war correspondent (44 people in total) were lost — there were no survivors.

The wreck was located in October 2019 by researchers from the University of Malta's Maritime Archaeology Programme, working alongside the grandson of HMS Urge's commanding officer. The submarine was definitively identified in May 2021 when Heritage Malta divers confirmed the vessel's name on the conning tower. She lies approximately 2 miles off Malta's coast at 130 metres depth, standing upright on the seabed with her deck gun still facing forward. The bow shows heavy damage consistent with the mine explosion. The hull is otherwise reported in remarkable condition.

HMS Urge is a designated **war grave** under Maltese, British, and international legislation. A memorial to the 44 lost was unveiled at Fort St Elmo in Malta in 2022 by the President of Malta and the British High Commissioner.

## Marine Life

Marine life has colonised the hull over 80+ years. No site-specific marine life surveys have been published. The wider Malta/Gozo waters support grouper, moray eels, octopus, bream, barracuda, and Mediterranean corals, but species at 130 metres are primarily deep-water adapted fauna.

## Dive Profile

At 130 metres, this is among the deepest wrecks in Maltese waters. **This depth is beyond recreational and most technical diving limits.** Access has been limited to scientific expedition dives with Heritage Malta and the University of Malta. No regular public dive access is offered. The upright submarine with its forward-facing deck gun is visible by ROV footage; the damaged bow documents the fatal mine strike. Any expedition requires CCR or very heavy trimix, extensive decompression, and full technical support infrastructure.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive — expedition only. As a designated war grave, this site is protected under Maltese, British, and international legislation. Unauthorised diving is not permitted. No standard Heritage Malta dive permit process is available for this site as of 2026.

## Tips and Recommendations

- **This is a war grave — 44 crew and passengers are lost aboard. The site warrants solemn respect.**
- At 130 metres, this exceeds recreational and standard technical diving limits
- The wreck was identified from the conning tower name in May 2021
- A 2022 memorial at Fort St Elmo in Valletta commemorates the 44 lost
- Wreck photography and virtual tours are available via Heritage Malta's Underwater Virtual Museum

## Safety Considerations

Extreme technical expedition dive at 130 metres. CCR or very heavy trimix mandatory. Full technical expedition support required. The site is a protected war grave — no artefact disturbance or removal permitted under any circumstances. Not a site for individual or small-group technical dives.

## Photography

ROV and expedition footage of the upright submarine with deck gun is available via Heritage Malta. The damaged bow section documents the fatal mine strike. Any in-person photography requires expedition-level planning and lighting systems capable of operating at 130 metres.
