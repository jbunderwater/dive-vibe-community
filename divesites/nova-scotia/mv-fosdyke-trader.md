## MV Fosdyke Trader

The MV Fosdyke Trader is a small 1944-built Empire F-type coaster (43 m / 142 ft, 411 GRT) that grounded on the south coast of Seal Island, Nova Scotia on November 17, 1991, while operating under the name *Mon Ami*. All five crew were rescued after deliberately beaching the vessel in deteriorating gale conditions. The hull has since settled on the exposed rocky shoreline of Seal Island, which sits at the entrance to the Bay of Fundy approximately 35 km southwest of Yarmouth. Seal Island itself is a remote, wave-battered bird sanctuary with no permanent population, making this one of the more remote wreck dives in Nova Scotia.

**Important:** At the time of the wreck the vessel had been sold by Fosdyke Trader Ltd and was registered under the name *Mon Ami* in Tennessee. Some sources still use the original name.

## Overview

MV Fosdyke Trader (operating as *Mon Ami*) is a wreck dive site on the exposed south shore of Seal Island, Nova Scotia. The vessel was built in 1944 by Henry Scarr Ltd in Hessle, UK as *Empire Fathom* for the British Ministry of War Transport, and changed names several times before the 1991 grounding. The wreck lies in the tidal zone and adjacent shallows on an exposed rocky coast. Access requires calm weather windows and a boat capable of open-water passage from Yarmouth or nearby ports. The Bay of Fundy influence creates strong tidal flows in the area.

## Wreck History

*Empire Fathom* was laid down in 1944 as a small Empire F-type coaster (142 ft 2 in / 43.33 m length, 411 GRT, 190 NRT). Postwar, she passed through several owners and names before being acquired in 1990 by an American owner and renamed *Mon Ami*, registered in Tennessee. On November 17, 1991, en route to Miami and experiencing compass problems that had already forced a return to Halifax days earlier, she was caught in a gale off Seal Island, lost her anchor, and was intentionally beached on the south coast of Seal Island by her crew to prevent sinking at sea. All five crew were rescued. The vessel was subsequently abandoned. It has since been referred to by its former name MV Fosdyke Trader in maritime records.

## Marine Life

Regional marine life in the southwestern Nova Scotia coastal waters includes Atlantic wolffish, cunner, Atlantic cod, rock crab, sea urchin, and lobster. Kelp beds are present in shallower rocky areas. No site-specific species reports were found for the Fosdyke Trader wreck; the remote, exposed nature of Seal Island limits diving frequency.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access only from Yarmouth or Clark's Harbour (approximately 35 km). The island is a provincially designated Important Bird Area — no overnight stays are permitted without authorization. Conditions off the south coast of Seal Island are highly exposed to Atlantic swell; this site should only be approached in very settled weather.

## Safety Considerations

The extreme remoteness and exposure of Seal Island makes this a serious undertaking. Rescue services are distant; carry a full emergency kit aboard the boat. Bay of Fundy tidal cycles generate significant current in the surrounding waters. Cold Atlantic water (4–12°C) requires a drysuit. The exposed coastline requires a capable offshore vessel.
