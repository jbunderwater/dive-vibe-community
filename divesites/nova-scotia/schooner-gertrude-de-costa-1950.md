## Schooner Gertrude de Costa (1950)

The *Gertrude de Costa* was a wooden motor-assisted schooner of more than 30 metres in length that sank in Halifax Harbour in 1950 following a collision with an oil tanker named the *Island Connector*. Seven seamen were lost with the vessel. The wreck rests near the South End Container Terminal area of Halifax Harbour in approximately 28 metres of water. Divers have explored the wreck and report features including an engine, modern gauges and a fire extinguisher consistent with mid-20th-century Canadian coasting trade — the two masts lie on the seabed adjacent to the hull. A serious hazard: **unexploded ordnance has been found on the deck**; origin is unknown but the hazard is documented.

**Location note:** The site is in Halifax Harbour near the South End Container Terminal, not the Shubenacadie Channel.

## Overview

The *Gertrude de Costa* is a tragic WWII-era merchant-era wooden schooner resting in Halifax Harbour at approximately 28 metres. The collision that sank her in 1950 killed seven of her crew. The wreck's proximity to harbour operations and the presence of unexploded ordnance on deck make it a site requiring careful planning and local knowledge. The cold Atlantic water (4–15°C) preserves wooden structure relatively well, and the vessel retains recognizable hull form and masts.

## Wreck History

The *Gertrude de Costa* was a two-masted wooden motor schooner active in Canadian coastal trade. In 1950 she collided with the oil tanker *Island Connector* in Halifax Harbour, sinking with the loss of seven seamen. Her length was over 30 metres; both masts came to rest alongside the hull on the seabed. Divers exploring the wreck have noted equipment suggesting she was motorized — an engine, instrument panel, and fire extinguisher were visible. **Unexploded ordnance of unknown origin has been reported on deck** — a significant hazard that has not been cleared as of available records.

## Safety Considerations

The presence of **unexploded ordnance (UXO) on deck is a documented hazard.** Do not touch, move, or disturb any materials that could be ordnance. This is a serious risk specific to this site. Do not penetrate the wreck without confirming UXO clearance status with local authorities. Halifax Harbour vessel traffic also requires a properly flagged dive boat.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access only. Contact local Halifax dive operators for current access logistics. Harbour authority requirements and UXO status should be confirmed before diving.
