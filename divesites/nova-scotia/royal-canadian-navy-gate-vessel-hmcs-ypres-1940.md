## Royal Canadian Navy gate vessel HMCS Ypres (1940)

HMCS Ypres began her career as a Battle-class naval trawler, launched June 16, 1917, and commissioned November 13, 1917 by Polson Iron Works in Toronto. She is NOT a minesweeper — that label in earlier records was an error. By 1938, after a refit, she was re-designated "Gate Vessel 1" and stationed at Halifax to operate the harbour boom defences: the anti-submarine net stretched across the harbour entrance. On May 12, 1940, the British battleship HMS Revenge, departing Halifax to escort troop transports, rammed the HMCS Ypres when the gate was opened too late. The collision rolled the trawler nearly onto her side, flooding her through open deck hatches, and she sank at approximately 21:25. No lives were lost; HMS Revenge and sister gate vessel HMCS Festubert rescued all 18 crew. The wreck rests in approximately 27 meters in Halifax Harbour.

**Important:** The OSM name says "gate vessel HMCS Ypres (1940)" — the 1940 date refers to the sinking, not the vessel's build date (1917). The vessel was NOT a minesweeper at the time of sinking.

## Overview

HMCS Ypres is a small trawler-type vessel sunk by friendly-fire accident in Halifax Harbour in May 1940. The wreck represents an important piece of WWII-era Halifax harbour history — the boom defences were a critical element of the anti-submarine campaign in Atlantic convoy operations. The vessel is relatively compact (trawler-class), resting at approximately 27 meters in Halifax Harbour cold water.

## Wreck History

HMCS Ypres was a Battle-class trawler built by Polson Iron Works, Toronto, launched June 16, 1917, commissioned November 13, 1917. Named after the Second and Third Battles of Ypres, she patrolled Canada's east coast for submarine activity in WWI. After a period as a training vessel (1923–1932), she was refitted in 1938 and redesignated Gate Vessel 1, tasked with operating the anti-submarine gate boom at Halifax Harbour entrance. On May 12, 1940, HMS Revenge was departing Halifax to escort convoy troop transports. A miscommunication about departure timing meant the gate was opened late; HMS Revenge struck the small trawler, rolling her nearly onto her side. Water flooded through open deck hatches and she sank within minutes at approximately 21:25. All 18 crew survived. The incident is documented in British and Canadian naval records.
