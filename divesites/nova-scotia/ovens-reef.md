## Ovens Reef

Ovens Reef is a shallow rocky reef in Lunenburg County, Nova Scotia, in the waters adjacent to The Ovens Natural Park — the site of Nova Scotia's 1861 gold rush. The nearby Ovens Point shore dive (operated from the park's campground, a 5-minute walk to entry) offers sandy bottom with rocky ridges to 13 metres, lobsters, and good visibility at slack high tide (typically 9–12 metres / 30–40 ft). The park's sea caves — Tucker's Tunnel, Indian Cave, and Cannon Cave — are visible from the surface and add geological interest. This area is designated a Novice to Intermediate dive zone by local operators.

## Overview

Ovens Reef sits near The Ovens Natural Park on the South Shore of Nova Scotia, a privately held 180-acre coastal reserve in Lunenburg County. East Coast Scuba & Watersports lists the nearby Ovens Point as a primary dive site: a sandy bottom with rocky ridges, maximum depth 13 m / 43 ft, with good visibility at high slack tide. Best diving occurs when the ocean is calm; rough conditions generate surge in the shallows near the caves. On-site washrooms and camping facilities are available through the park.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Rose Bay, Lunenburg County, Nova Scotia (near The Ovens Natural Park)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 14 meters
- **Water Temperature**: 4–15°C (winter–summer)

## Marine Life

Lobsters are reported at the Ovens Point / Ovens Reef area. Rocky ridges support encrusting invertebrates and kelp. No site-specific species list beyond lobsters was confirmed in available sources.

## Entry and Exit

Shore entry is possible from Cunard's Beach (Ovens Point) within The Ovens Natural Park — approximately a 5-minute walk to the water. The park charges a fee for access and provides parking. Boat access to the offshore reef is also an option. Dive only in calm ocean conditions; the area experiences surge in swells.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Best visibility at slack high tide (9–12 m / 30–40 ft is typical)
- Ocean must be calm; surge near the cave formations can be dangerous in swell
- Cold Atlantic water (4–15°C) requires a drysuit outside peak summer
- Carry a surface marker buoy (SMB) for boat safety
