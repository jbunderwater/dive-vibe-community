## British tanker Kars (1942)

The MT Kars was a large British oil tanker sunk by a German U-boat in 1942 while carrying 12,700 tons of aviation spirit and fuel oil — one of the most significant Allied tanker losses near Halifax in the WWII Battle of the Atlantic. The stern section was towed into Halifax Harbour and beached near McNabs Island; wreckage remains at approximately 28 metres.

## Overview

The Kars was a 8,888 GRT motor tanker built in March 1939 by Blythswood Shipbuilding Co. Ltd. in Scotstoun, Glasgow, and operated by Western Oil Shipping Co. Ltd. of London. She measured 495.5 feet (151 m) in length. On 22 February 1942 at 22:57, U-96 (commanded by Kptlt. Heinrich Lehmann-Willenbrock, famous as the U-boat in the film *Das Boot*) torpedoed the Kars at approximately 44°15'N, 63°25'W — roughly 25 miles southeast of Halifax — while she was en route from Trinidad to Halifax to Belfast carrying aviation fuel and oil. The hull broke in two around 25 February; the bow section sank in open water. The stern was towed to Halifax and beached near McNabs Island on 27 February 1942.

Casualties were heavy: sources conflict slightly, with uboat.net recording 46 dead out of 47 aboard (master, crew, and gunners), and the Royal Museums Greenwich noting a similar figure. Only one or two survivors were rescued.

The wreck location in the OSM record (44.626°N, 63.536°W) corresponds to the beached stern section near McNabs Island in the outer Halifax Harbour channel — a separate position from the torpedo attack site. Whether the wreck at these coordinates is accessible or has deteriorated into scattered debris is not confirmed by any recreational dive source.

## Safety Considerations

Cold water year-round (4–15°C); drysuit required. Halifax Harbour has strong tidal currents, particularly in the outer channel. This site is not on any commercial dive charter itinerary found — contact local operators (East Coast Scuba, Dartmouth) before attempting to access this site. The exact condition of the wreck at these coordinates has not been publicly documented by any dive source found.
