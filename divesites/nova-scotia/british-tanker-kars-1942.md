## British tanker Kars (1942)

The British motor tanker *Kars* was an 8,888-ton tanker torpedoed by German submarine U-96 on February 22, 1942, approximately 25 miles south-southeast of Halifax. The ship caught fire and broke in two; the forward section sank at sea while the stern section was towed into Halifax Harbour and beached at McNabs Cove on February 27, 1942, where it was declared a total loss. Of the 47 people aboard, 46 perished — one of the costliest single merchant-ship disasters in Canadian waters during WWII. The wreck at these coordinates represents the beached stern section in approximately 28 meters of water near McNabs Island, Halifax Harbour.

**Location note:** The site is in Halifax Harbour near McNabs Island, not the Shubenacadie Channel.

## Overview

The stern section of the British tanker *Kars* rests near McNabs Island in Halifax Harbour, a site laden with WWII maritime history. The vessel was carrying 12,700 tons of aviation spirit and fuel oil when struck. Halifax Harbour's cold water (4–15°C) preserves metal structure well, but visibility and currents reflect harbour conditions. This is a boat dive site in restricted inner-harbour waters.

## Wreck History

Built in 1939 by Blythswood Shipbuilding Co Ltd in Glasgow, the *Kars* (8,888 GRT) was a British motor tanker operated in wartime convoy service. On February 22, 1942, while approximately 25 miles SSE of Halifax (44°15'N, 63°25'W), U-96 — under the command of Heinrich Lehmann-Willenbrock — struck her with a single G7e torpedo. The *Kars* was loaded with 12,700 tons of aviation spirit and fuel oil, which immediately caught fire. The ship broke apart three days later: the forward section sank, while the damaged stern was towed to Halifax and beached at McNabs Cove on February 27. Of 47 persons aboard (46 crew and armed guards, plus the master), 46 perished; two survivors were picked up by HMCS Melville, though one subsequently died. The vessel was declared a total loss and abandoned at the cove.
