## Nova Scotia

Cold-water Atlantic diving with historic shipwrecks spanning centuries and rocky reef ecosystems rich in marine life, all accessible from Canada's eastern seaboard.

## Description

Nova Scotia's waters hold documented dive sites ranging from 12 to 38 meters depth, spread along hundreds of kilometers of rugged Atlantic coastline. The province is renowned for its shipwreck heritage, with wreck sites in our database including vessels from the 1800s through the mid-20th century, such as the Norwegian barque Tordenskjold (1879), the British tanker Kars (1942), and several wartime aircraft. Cold North Atlantic waters support dense kelp forests, plumose anemones, and thriving lobster populations across reef sites. Diving here demands cold-water experience and proper thermal protection, but rewards divers with excellent visibility and uncrowded sites.

### Diving Opportunities

- **Boat Diving**: Boat-accessible sites make up the vast majority of Nova Scotia diving, reaching depths of 12-38 meters across reefs, ledges, and wrecks scattered along the coast.
- **Wreck Diving**: Wreck sites spanning over 150 years of maritime history, including sailing vessels, wartime barges, naval ships like HMCS Ypres, fighter aircraft, and the famous Marco Polo clipper ship at 35 meters.
- **Shore Diving**: Documented shore entry at Fox Point Beach (12m depth), though additional shore dives exist around the Halifax area at sites like Point Pleasant Park, The Dingle, and Ferguson's Cove.
- **Reef Diving**: Reef and ledge sites featuring cold-water rocky reef ecosystems with kelp forests, urchin barrens, and abundant invertebrate life.

### Accessibility

- **Getting There**: Halifax Stanfield International Airport (YHZ) is the main gateway, with direct flights from major Canadian and US cities. The Trans-Canada Highway (Highway 104) connects Nova Scotia to New Brunswick. Most dive sites along the coast require 30 minutes to 1.5 hours of driving from Halifax.
- **Dive Operators**: Several established operators serve the province, including Torpedo Rays Scuba Adventures (PADI 5 Star IDC Centre, Halifax), East Coast Scuba & Watersports (PADI 5 Star Dive Centre, Bedford), Nautilus Aquatics (Halifax), and Causeway Diver Supply (northeastern Nova Scotia).
- **Accommodation**: Halifax offers the full range from hotels to vacation rentals. Coastal towns like Lunenburg, Peggy's Cove area, and Cape Breton have seasonal accommodations closer to dive sites.
- **Transportation**: A rental car is essential for reaching most dive sites. Boat charters are arranged through local dive operators for the 53 boat-access sites.
- **Facilities**: Dive shops in Halifax provide air fills, equipment rental, and guided dives. Divemaster-led shore dives run approximately CAD $125-$203.

### Marine Life & Environment

- **Water Conditions**: Cold North Atlantic water requires a drysuit or heavy wetsuit (minimum 14mm). Visibility varies seasonally -- winter offers the best clarity due to reduced runoff, while summer brings warmer water (up to 15-18C at the surface) and more biological activity. Visibility regularly reaches 15+ meters at offshore sites. Currents can be significant, particularly around headlands and in the Bay of Fundy region.
- **Marine Biodiversity**: Rich cold-water ecosystems support large Atlantic lobsters, plumose and tube anemones, nudibranchs, sea urchins, crabs, ctenophores, and dense kelp forests. Late summer (August-September) occasionally brings unusual visitors like seahorses and triggerfish carried north by the Gulf Stream. Grey seals are common at many sites.
- **Conservation**: Nova Scotia has several marine protected areas. Divers should respect lobster fishing regulations and seasonal closures. The province's shipwrecks are protected under heritage legislation -- artifact removal is prohibited.

## Additional Information

- **Best Time to Visit**: June through October for the warmest water temperatures and calmest seas. July through September is peak season with the best combination of visibility, marine life activity, and comfortable surface conditions. Winter diving (November-March) offers superior visibility for those with proper cold-water experience and equipment.
- **Currency**: Canadian Dollar (CAD)
- **Language**: English, French
- **Safety**: A drysuit is strongly recommended for all diving. Cold-water exposure is the primary hazard -- hypothermia risk is real even in summer. Strong currents exist at many sites, particularly tidal areas near the Bay of Fundy. Open Water certification is the minimum requirement, but many of the 20 Advanced-rated sites demand wreck or deep diving specialties. Boat traffic is a concern at harbour sites like Africville. The nearest hyperbaric chamber is in Halifax.
