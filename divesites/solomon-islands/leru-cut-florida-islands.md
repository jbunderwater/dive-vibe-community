## Leru Cut Florida Islands

Leru Cut is one of the most visually dramatic dive sites in the Solomon Islands — a narrow volcanic chasm carved through an island in the Florida Islands group, creating a spectacular swim-through passage where sheer walls rise 30 meters above on each side and the sky is reduced to a thin strip of light far overhead.

## Overview

Despite being listed under cave-type diving, Leru Cut is technically a canyon or cut — an open-sky chasm rather than an enclosed cave. The slot is narrow enough that divers can almost touch both walls simultaneously, yet deep enough that the bottom sits at around 20 meters. The defining feature is the extraordinary light: at certain times of day, shafts of sunlight pour vertically into the chasm from the open sky above, creating shimmering light columns and mirror-like reflections that give the site an otherworldly quality. The walls themselves are draped in soft corals, sponges, and crinoids that thrive in the sheltered, low-flow environment. It is frequently cited by experienced Solomon Islands divers as one of the most memorable dives in the archipelago.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Florida Islands, Solomon Islands
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Cave/cavern
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–30 meters
- **Current**: Minimal inside the cut
- **Best Time**: April to November; mid-morning for optimal light

## Marine Life

The sheltered nature of the cut produces a distinct ecosystem. The walls are encrusted with sponges, ascidians, and a variety of soft corals that benefit from the calm conditions. Crinoids in multiple colour forms cling to the wall surfaces. Reef fish — including cardinalfish, damselfish, and small wrasse — occupy the crack in large numbers, using the narrow walls as protection. At the base of the cut, sea cucumbers, urchins, and various invertebrates populate the sand. Occasionally a turtle or whitetip reef shark is encountered entering or exiting the chasm. The site lacks the large pelagic life of open-water passages but compensates with intimate reef encounters and remarkable scenery.

## Dive Profile

The dive begins outside the cut, where the boat drops divers at one end of the chasm. Divers descend to the sandy bottom at around 20 meters and swim through the cut at depth, taking in the full vertical scale of the walls before ascending slowly through the water column to appreciate the progressive light changes as the sky opens above. The swim-through is not long — the entire cut can be traversed in a single breath-controlled pass — but the experience rewards slow exploration. Most divers swim through once and return to explore the wall detail more carefully on the way back. Total dive time is typically 50–60 minutes including time on the reef outside the cut.

## Entry and Exit

Access is by boat from operators based in the Florida Islands or from Honiara-based operators making the crossing. The cut is narrow and entry must be sequential rather than in a group — enter one or two divers at a time to avoid congestion in the chasm. Exit at the far end of the cut, ascend on the outside, and the boat picks up divers there.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Time the dive for mid-morning when sunlight enters the cut at its most dramatic angle — the light shafts are the defining experience
- Move slowly through the cut — rushing misses the detail on the walls and the subtle light transitions
- Torch or video light is useful for illuminating wall detail but is not essential in the upper portions
- Buoyancy must be precise — the walls are close and the corals and sponges are fragile
- Excellent for underwater video — the cinematic quality of light in the cut photographs and films exceptionally well

## Safety Considerations

Leru Cut is an overhead environment in the sense that open-sky ascent is not always immediately available. Divers must be comfortable with the psychological aspect of enclosed-feeling spaces, even though the cut is open to the sky. Buoyancy control is critical to avoid damaging the wall life. The narrow width means that divers should not enter in large groups and must communicate clearly about direction of travel. Currents inside the cut are minimal. Always dive with a guide familiar with the site.

## Photography

Leru Cut is among the most photographed dive sites in the Solomons. Wide-angle photography looking upward from the base of the chasm — with the narrow strip of sky visible far above and light shafts cutting down through the water — produces genuinely striking images. A fisheye or ultra-wide lens captures the towering wall perspective. Video of the light shafts in motion is exceptional. Macro photographers will find sponges, ascidians, and crinoids in unusual colour forms on close inspection of the walls.
