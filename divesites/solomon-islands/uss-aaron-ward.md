# USS Aaron Ward

A Gleaves-class destroyer (DD-483), 348 feet long, sunk stern-first on 7 April 1943 during a Japanese air attack (Operation I-Go) near Nggela Sule, Solomon Islands. The wreck sits upright at approximately 63–70 metres — an extremely deep technical dive to one of the most significant naval wrecks of the Guadalcanal campaign.

## Overview

On 7 April 1943, Japanese aircraft launched Operation I-Go, a major air offensive targeting Allied shipping in the Solomons. USS Aaron Ward was escorting LST-449 near Tinete Point, Nggela Sule, when she was attacked by dive bombers and sank. The same air raid sank HMNZS Moa and USS Kanawha at nearby Tulagi.

Note: USS Aaron Ward (DD-483) was heavily damaged — but survived — the Naval Battle of Guadalcanal on 12–13 November 1942. She was repaired, returned to service in early 1943, and was sunk five months later in the April 1943 air attack described above. The sinking event is Operation I-Go, not the November 1942 surface battle.

The wreck now sits upright on the seabed at approximately 63–70 metres (deck at ~63m, deepest point ~70m) with a slight list. Her guns, torpedo tubes, and depth charge racks are visible on deck. The 348-foot hull sits complete and upright, making for an impressive sight in the deep water. At 70 metres this is firmly in the realm of technical diving.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Near Tinete Point, Nggela Sule, Iron Bottom Sound, Solomon Islands
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 70 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–30 metres
- **Water Temperature**: 27–28°C (cooler at depth)
- **Current**: Variable

## Dive Profile

This is an extremely deep technical dive. Descend on a shot line to the wreck at 63–70 metres. The upright orientation allows a systematic tour of the deck features — guns, torpedo tube mounts, and depth charge racks are visible. The superstructure and bridge area can be examined externally. Bottom time at 70 metres is extremely limited even on trimix, so dive planning must prioritize which features to visit. Extended decompression stops are mandatory.

## Tips and Recommendations

- This dive is exclusively for experienced technical divers with appropriate deep-diving certifications, training, and equipment
- Trimix is required at this depth — air and Nitrox are not suitable
- Plan for extensive decompression obligations; carry sufficient bailout gas
- Pre-plan exactly which features you want to see — bottom time is measured in minutes, not tens of minutes
- The wreck's historical significance as a victim of Operation I-Go (7 April 1943), which also sank HMNZS Moa and USS Kanawha the same day, makes it one of the most important WWII wrecks in the Solomon Islands

## Safety Considerations

This is a war grave — do not disturb or remove any artefacts. At 70 metres, this dive is well beyond recreational limits and carries significant risks including nitrogen narcosis on air, oxygen toxicity, and extended decompression obligations. Only attempt this dive with proper technical diving training (trimix certified), appropriate equipment (twinsets, stage/bailout cylinders, decompression gases), and an experienced technical dive operator familiar with the site. Mandatory decompression stops will be lengthy. Current conditions must be assessed carefully.
