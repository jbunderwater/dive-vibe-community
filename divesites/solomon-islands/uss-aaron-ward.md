# USS Aaron Ward

A Bristol-class destroyer (DD-483), 348 feet long, sunk stern-first on 13 November 1942 during the First Naval Battle of Guadalcanal. The wreck sits upright at 70 metres with a slight starboard list -- an extremely deep technical dive to one of the most significant naval wrecks of the Pacific War.

## Overview

On 13 November 1942, during the chaotic night surface action known as the First Naval Battle of Guadalcanal, the USS Aaron Ward was hit and sank stern-first. The destroyer now sits upright on the seabed at approximately 70 metres with a slight list to starboard.

The wreck is well preserved at this depth. Her guns, torpedo tubes, and depth charge racks are visible on the deck. The 348-foot hull sits complete and upright, making for an impressive sight in the deep water. However, at 70 metres this is firmly in the realm of technical diving and is one of the deepest diveable wrecks in the Solomon Islands.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Iron Bottom Sound, near Guadalcanal/Tulagi, Solomon Islands
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive (WWII destroyer)
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced (extreme depth -- technical diving only)
- **Maximum Depth**: 70 metres
- **Typical Visibility**: 15-30 metres
- **Water Temperature**: 27-28°C (cooler at depth)
- **Current**: Variable

## Dive Profile

This is an extremely deep technical dive. Descend on a shot line to the wreck at 70 metres. The upright orientation with slight starboard list allows a systematic tour of the deck features -- guns, torpedo tube mounts, and depth charge racks are visible. The superstructure and bridge area can be examined externally. Bottom time at 70 metres is extremely limited even on trimix, so dive planning must prioritize which features to visit. Extended decompression stops are mandatory.

## Tips and Recommendations

- This dive is exclusively for experienced technical divers with appropriate deep-diving certifications, training, and equipment
- Trimix is required at this depth -- air and Nitrox are not suitable
- Plan for extensive decompression obligations; carry sufficient bailout gas
- Pre-plan exactly which features you want to see -- bottom time is measured in minutes, not tens of minutes
- The wreck's historical significance as a First Naval Battle of Guadalcanal casualty makes it one of the most important WWII wrecks in the Solomon Islands

## Safety Considerations

This is a war grave -- do not disturb or remove any artefacts. At 70 metres, this dive is well beyond recreational limits and carries significant risks including nitrogen narcosis on air, oxygen toxicity, and extended decompression obligations. Only attempt this dive with proper technical diving training (trimix certified), appropriate equipment (twinsets, stage/bailout cylinders, decompression gases), and an experienced technical dive operator familiar with the site. Mandatory decompression stops will be lengthy. Current conditions must be assessed carefully -- aborting mid-dive is extremely complicated at these depths.
