# Kyusyu Maru

A Japanese transport ship beached on the coast of Guadalcanal during the fierce fighting of 1942, now lying on her port side at a 45-degree angle. The wreck spans from 3 metres at the shallowest point down to the stern propellers at 46 metres, making it a deep and rewarding dive for advanced divers.

## Overview

On 15 October 1942, the Kyusyu Maru was beached approximately 18 kilometres west of Honiara during the Guadalcanal campaign. The vessel came to rest on her port side at roughly 45 degrees, creating a dramatic profile against the sloping seabed. The depth range of 3 to 46 metres means the uppermost hull sections are accessible in very shallow water, while the stern with its propellers sits at the deepest point.

The hold is a time capsule of wartime cargo -- divers can see rifle rounds, burnt biscuits, china, and cattle bones among the contents. The stern propellers at 46 metres are a highlight for those with the training and gas planning to reach them.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Guadalcanal coast -- approximately 18 km west of Honiara
- **Entry Type**: Shore entry
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 46 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15-30 metres
- **Water Temperature**: 27-28°C
- **Current**: Generally mild close to shore

## Dive Profile

The shallowest parts of the wreck begin at just 3 metres, allowing an initial orientation on the uppermost hull sections. Work down along the 45-degree hull to the hold area where the wartime cargo is visible -- rifle ammunition, burnt biscuits, broken china, and cattle bones scattered among the debris. For experienced divers, continue deeper along the hull toward the stern where the propellers rest at 46 metres. This deep section demands strict gas management and bottom time planning. Ascend gradually along the hull, using the shallower sections for decompression or safety stop time.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The shallow upper hull (3-10 metres) is worth exploring in its own right and can be done as a separate, easier dive
- A torch is essential for examining the hold contents and the shadowed port side
- The stern propellers at 46 metres require deep diving certification and careful gas planning -- do not attempt on a standard open water certification
- The cargo remnants in the hold are historically fascinating but should not be touched or disturbed
- This wreck pairs well with nearby Guadalcanal shore sites for a multi-dive day

## Safety Considerations

This is a war grave -- do not remove any artefacts. The 46-metre maximum depth at the stern propellers places this firmly in advanced/deep diving territory. Plan gas supply carefully and consider using enriched air (Nitrox) for the shallower portions. The 45-degree angle of the hull can be disorienting -- maintain situational awareness of your depth at all times. Corroded metal edges are present throughout; maintain proper buoyancy to avoid contact with the structure.
