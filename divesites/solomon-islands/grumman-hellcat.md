# Grumman Hellcat

A fully intact Grumman F6F-3 Hellcat fighter (serial number 25389) resting at just 11 metres near Queue Island, south of Kolombangara. Shot down on 16 September 1943 by friendly fire from Pappy Boyington, this is one of the best-preserved WWII aircraft wrecks in the Pacific and an ideal dive for all experience levels.

## Overview

On 16 September 1943, this F6F-3 Hellcat (serial 25389) was shot down by friendly fire -- the shooter was none other than the famous Marine ace Gregory "Pappy" Boyington. The aircraft went down near Queue Island, south of Kolombangara in the New Georgia group.

The Hellcat is fully intact at just 11 metres depth. The cockpit is open, and the instrument panel is readable -- gauges and dials can be examined in detail. The flaps are in the down position, consistent with a forced landing attempt. The aircraft sits upright on the seabed in excellent condition, making it one of the most photogenic WWII aircraft wrecks anywhere in the Solomon Islands.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Near Queue Island, south of Kolombangara, New Georgia group
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive from Gizo
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive (WWII fighter aircraft)
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 11 metres
- **Typical Visibility**: 15-30 metres
- **Water Temperature**: 27-28°C
- **Current**: Generally mild

## Dive Profile

Descend to the aircraft at 11 metres. The Hellcat sits intact on the seabed -- take time to circle the entire airframe and appreciate how complete it is. The open cockpit is the highlight: the instrument panel with readable gauges, the control stick, and the cockpit framing are all clearly visible. Note the flaps-down position on the wings. The engine cowling and propeller at the front, and the tail section at the rear, are all worth examining. The shallow depth allows extended bottom time -- there is no rush on this dive.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The 11-metre depth and intact condition make this one of the best WWII aircraft wreck dives for beginners and photographers alike
- Bring a macro lens or close-focus capability for the cockpit instruments -- the readable gauges are extraordinary
- Wide-angle shots of the complete airframe with natural light at 11 metres produce stunning images
- The Pappy Boyington connection makes this wreck historically significant beyond its physical condition
- Combine with other Gizo-area dives for a full day of diving

## Safety Considerations

This is a war grave -- do not touch, sit on, or remove any part of the aircraft. The shallow depth makes this an extremely safe dive. Maintain buoyancy clearance above the aircraft to avoid damaging the fragile instrument panel and cockpit fittings. Be careful not to fin-kick sand onto the wreck.
