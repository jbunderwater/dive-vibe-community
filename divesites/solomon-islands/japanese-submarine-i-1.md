# Japanese Submarine I-1

The wreck of a Junsen Type 1 cruiser submarine -- 319 feet long and displacing 2,135 tons -- lying in Kamimbo Bay, approximately 40 kilometres west of Honiara. One of the most historically significant submarine wrecks accessible to recreational divers in the Pacific.

## Overview

In January 1943, the Japanese submarine I-1 was rammed three times by the New Zealand corvette HMNZS Kiwi in Kamimbo Bay during a resupply mission to Guadalcanal. The submarine sank in relatively shallow water. After the war, salvage operations destroyed the front third of the hull, but the remaining aft two-thirds section is remarkably intact.

The surviving section includes the dive planes, rudder, and propeller, all clearly identifiable. The interior is penetrable in places, though this requires proper wreck penetration training and equipment. The wreck spans a depth range of 5 to 30 metres, with the shallowest sections encrusted in coral growth and the deeper stern areas well preserved.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Kamimbo Bay, Guadalcanal -- approximately 40 km west of Honiara
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15-30 metres
- **Water Temperature**: 27-28°C
- **Current**: Generally mild in Kamimbo Bay

## Dive Profile

Descend to the wreck and orient yourself at the break point where the forward section was removed by post-war salvage. Work aft along the remaining hull, examining the submarine's external features -- the dive planes, hull plating, and deck structures. Continue to the stern where the rudder and propeller are clearly visible at the deeper end of the wreck around 30 metres. The shallower portions of the wreck start at around 5 metres, allowing for extended exploration time before working deeper. The penetrable sections of the hull offer views of internal machinery and compartments.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Allow at least 40 minutes of travel time from Honiara by dive boat
- The break point from the salvage operation provides an interesting perspective on the submarine's internal structure
- The propeller and rudder at the stern are the photographic highlights
- Carry a torch for examining the interior sections and shadowed hull areas
- Penetration requires wreck diving certification, proper training, and redundant equipment

## Safety Considerations

This is a war grave -- do not disturb or remove any artefacts. The broken forward section has sharp, corroded edges. Interior penetration should only be attempted by divers with proper wreck training and equipment. Maintain good buoyancy control to avoid disturbing silt inside the hull. The depth range of 5-30 metres makes gas management straightforward, but plan your deeper exploration first while air supply is full.
