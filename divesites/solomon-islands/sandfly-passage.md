## Sandfly Passage

Sandfly Passage is a current-swept channel in the Florida Islands area of the Solomon Islands that delivers large pelagic encounters in a high-energy environment. Strong tidal flow through the passage concentrates sharks, barracuda, and schooling trevally, making it a standout site for advanced divers seeking open-water action.

Sandfly Passage is located in the Florida Islands, north of Guadalcanal, near Tulagi. The corrected coordinates (-9.03, 160.08) reflect this location.**

## Overview

Sandfly Passage connects water between islands in the Florida Islands group north of Guadalcanal, generating strong tidal currents that bring nutrient-rich water and attract pelagic predators. The site is typically visited by Florida Islands-based operators and liveaboards working the area around Tulagi. The reef structure along the passage walls provides a backdrop for the pelagic action, with hard coral gardens on the shallower sections giving way to sea fans and sponge-encrusted walls as depth increases. The advanced rating reflects both current strength and the open-water nature of the pelagic encounters.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Florida Islands, Solomon Islands (north of Guadalcanal, near Tulagi)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Drift dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–40 meters
- **Current**: Strong (tidal-dependent)
- **Best Time**: April to November

## Marine Life

Sharks are the headline attraction at Sandfly Passage. Grey reef sharks are the most commonly encountered species, patrolling the current-swept zone in numbers during peak flow. Whitetip reef sharks are present in calmer sections. Schools of great barracuda assemble in the mid-water column, often in dense formations of 50 or more fish. Bigeye trevally and dog-tooth tuna make charging passes through the passage. Eagle rays soar through periodically, using the current effortlessly. The reef structures alongside the main current zone support the usual assemblage of Solomon Islands reef life — parrotfish, Napoleon wrasse, grouper, moray eels, and dense schools of anthias and fusiliers that fuel the food chain drawing in the larger predators.

## Dive Profile

The dive strategy depends on tidal state and current direction. Strong incoming or outgoing flow pushes divers along the passage wall, and a good guide positions the entry to drift divers past the aggregation points where sharks and barracuda are most concentrated. In strong current, divers may deploy a reef hook to hold position in a prime viewing spot without having to fin against the flow. Depths of 15–25 meters cover the most productive zones. The maximum depth of 30 meters is available for divers with the gas management to use it, but the majority of life is encountered at shallower depths. Dives run 45–60 minutes depending on current speed.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive from Gizo-based operators or liveaboards. Entry is timed to the tidal window recommended by the local guide. The boat follows the group and picks up divers at the end of the drift or at a pre-agreed exit point. Deploy an SMB before ascending to ensure boat crew visibility.

## Tips and Recommendations

- A reef hook is a valuable piece of equipment here — it allows stationary observation of shark activity without burning energy fighting the current
- Follow the guide's tidal timing closely; arriving outside the productive window can result in slack, featureless diving
- Streamline all equipment before entry
- Blue-water photography setups — wide-angle with strobe — are ideal for the shark and barracuda encounters
- Combine with other Florida Islands sites such as HMNZS Moa, USS Kanawha, or Hundred Thousand Dollar Point for a full dive day in the Tulagi area

## Safety Considerations

Strong current requires solid drift diving skills. Divers should be comfortable with fast-moving water, aware of the risks of separation, and capable of deploying an SMB accurately. The open-water nature of the site means divers can be carried away from the reef if they lose their reference. Establish buddy protocols before entry and agree on procedures for separation. A dive guide with local knowledge is not optional here — incorrect tidal timing can result in genuinely dangerous current speeds. Carry a whistle and SMB.

## Photography

Sandfly Passage's open-water encounters favour wide-angle and fisheye lenses. Shooting upward at circling grey reef sharks against the light-filtered surface is the defining photographic subject. Dense barracuda schools offer particularly compelling wide-angle subjects, especially when a shaft of light cuts through the formation. Video divers will find the passage produces dramatic footage with minimal effort if the timing is right. The reef structure alongside the passage provides complementary coral and fish life for macro photographers during slack periods.
