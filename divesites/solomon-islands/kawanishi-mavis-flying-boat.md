# Kawanishi Mavis Flying Boat

Japanese Kawanishi H6K "Mavis" flying boats sunk at their moorings near Ghavutu and Tanambogo islands when US aircraft attacked on 7 August 1942 -- the opening day of the Guadalcanal campaign. The best-preserved example (designated Mavis No 2) lies at approximately 30 metres, nearly fully intact, and is one of 10 to 13 aircraft wrecks scattered across this area.

## Overview

On 7 August 1942, US forces launched the invasion of Guadalcanal and the surrounding islands. The Japanese seaplane base near Ghavutu and Tanambogo was among the first targets, and the Kawanishi H6K "Mavis" four-engine flying boats moored in the harbour were destroyed by US aircraft before they could take off. Between 10 and 13 aircraft wrecks from this attack are scattered across the seabed in the area.

The best-preserved aircraft, known as Mavis No 2, lies at approximately 30 metres depth. The flying boat is nearly fully intact -- cockpit instruments are still visible, a reconnaissance camera remains in place, the wing struts are complete, and the distinctive twin tail fins extend upward from the fuselage. The aircraft's large size (the H6K had a wingspan of over 40 metres) makes it an impressive sight on the seabed.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Near Ghavutu and Tanambogo islands, Florida Islands, Solomon Islands
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive from Tulagi
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive (WWII flying boat aircraft)
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: ~30 metres
- **Typical Visibility**: 15-30 metres
- **Water Temperature**: 27-28°C
- **Current**: Variable

## Dive Profile

Descend to Mavis No 2 at approximately 30 metres. The aircraft's large wingspan makes it immediately recognizable on the seabed. Begin at the cockpit area where instruments are still visible in the panel -- a torch is essential here. The reconnaissance camera is mounted in the fuselage. Work along the wing struts to appreciate the aircraft's enormous scale, then move to the tail section where the twin tail fins are a distinctive feature of the H6K design. If air supply and bottom time permit, your guide may be able to show you additional aircraft wrecks from the same attack scattered across the nearby seabed.

## Tips and Recommendations

- A torch is essential for examining cockpit instruments and interior details
- The reconnaissance camera is a unique artefact rarely seen on aircraft wrecks -- ask your guide for its exact location
- Wide-angle photography captures the aircraft's impressive scale; macro shots of the instrument panel tell the human story
- The area contains 10-13 aircraft wrecks total -- experienced divers may want to arrange multiple dives to explore other examples
- At 30 metres, bottom time on air is limited; Nitrox extends your exploration time significantly

## Safety Considerations

This is a war grave -- do not touch, disturb, or remove any artefacts including instruments and equipment. The 30-metre depth requires advanced certification and careful gas planning. The airframe structure may have sharp corroded edges; maintain buoyancy clearance from the wreck at all times. The area can experience variable currents. Carry an SMB for ascent.
