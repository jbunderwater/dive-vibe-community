## HTMS Sattakut

A former US Navy Pacific War landing craft deliberately sunk in 2011 — Koh Tao's most accessible wreck dive and a growing artificial reef.

## Overview

The HTMS Sattakut was originally commissioned as USS LCI(M)-739 (Landing Craft Infantry, Mortar variant), built at Commercial Iron Works in Portland, Oregon, and launched in early 1944. During World War II, the vessel earned three Battle Stars serving in three major Pacific operations: the capture of the southern Palau Islands (September–October 1944), the assault and occupation of Iwo Jima (February 1945), and the assault on Okinawa Gunto (March–June 1945). After the war, the vessel was transferred to the Royal Thai Navy in 1946 and renamed HTMS Sattakut — hull number 742 in Thai service — in honour of a Thai military victory in the Franco-Thai War of 1941. She served until decommissioning in 2007.

On 18 June 2011, Thailand's Department of Marine and Coastal Resources deliberately sank the cleaned and stripped vessel as an artificial reef. The initial scuttling placed the wreck on its side; a salvage team righted it to its current upright position in August 2011. Sources report the vessel's length at approximately 48–49 metres with a 7-metre beam, and displacement of around 246 tonnes light / 419 tonnes loaded. The keel rests at 27–30 m on a sandy bottom; the bridge and wheelhouse sit at approximately 18–19 m depth, and two cannon guns remain on the hull. Since sinking, the structure has been colonised by one-spot snappers, brown sweetlips, giant groupers, banded sea kraits, scorpionfish, and schools of barracuda.

## Site Information

- **Location**: West of Koh Tao, approximately 800 m offshore, adjacent to Hin Pee Wee pinnacle
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate (wreck penetration requires advanced/wreck certification)
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters (keel at 27–30 m; bridge at 18–19 m)
- **Typical Visibility**: 2–15 m (variable; can be poor)
- **Current**: Mild to moderate
- **Best Time**: May to August

## Marine Life

Confirmed species at the wreck include one-spot snappers in large schools, brown sweetlips, giant groupers, banded sea kraits, scorpionfish near doors and portholes, and Durban dancing shrimp on the interior staircases. Barracuda, batfish, and lionfish are documented around the superstructure. Moray eels inhabit interior spaces. Whale sharks are occasional visitors to the area, not residents of the wreck itself.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the bow or stern (guide selects based on current orientation) and perform a systematic circuit of the hull. Start at the superstructure at 18–19 m and work down to the keel at 27–30 m. Multiple penetration entry and exit points are accessible for qualified wreck divers. Ascend via the mast structure and transition to the adjacent Hin Pee Wee pinnacle for extended air use and a safety stop.

## Entry and Exit

Boat from Koh Tao (approximately 15–20 minutes). Entry via back roll. Mooring lines are attached to the wreck. SMB deployment recommended for open water ascent away from the mooring.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Plan your dive to start at depth and ascend progressively to make the most of no-decompression time
- The wreck pairs naturally with Hin Pee Wee pinnacle in a single dive
- Bring a torch for examining the interior spaces — Durban dancing shrimp on the staircases are a highlight
- Nitrox extends bottom time on the keel at 27–30 m
- Visibility can be poor (2–10 m is typical); adjust expectations and plan accordingly

## Safety Considerations

Swim-throughs and internal penetration should only be attempted by divers with wreck diving training or certification. Do not penetrate beyond the light zone without a line and appropriate training. Scorpionfish on the hull are well-camouflaged — do not touch any surface. Always dive with a buddy and carry redundant lights for any interior exploration.

## Photography

Wide-angle captures the full hull profile and accompanying fish schools. One-spot snapper schools around the superstructure create compelling school shots. Macro work on the encrusting soft corals and the cannon guns. The bow profile with a diver in frame provides the classic wreck portrait.
