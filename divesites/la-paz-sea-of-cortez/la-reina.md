## La Reina

La Reina is a rocky islet with a lighthouse at the northern tip of Isla Cerralvo, known for a resident sea lion colony, large gorgonians, and seasonal giant Pacific manta ray encounters.

## Overview

La Reina (also written La Reyna) is a small rocky islet topped by a lighthouse just north of Isla Cerralvo in the southern Sea of Cortez. The site experiences strong tidal currents that sweep nutrients through the channel, keeping the water clear and feeding productive reef communities. La Reina hosts a resident colony of approximately 40 California sea lions (confirmed by Cabo Private Guide and Cortez Club; colony size consistent across sources). The site features a submerged canyon decorated in vibrant sponges and rich invertebrate communities. Giant Pacific manta rays (Mobula birostris) are a notable seasonal draw, with the Cortez Club and La Paz Divers documenting groups of eight or more individuals during the mid-June through mid-October window. After a reported hiatus of over 15 years, mantas have returned to La Reina in recent seasons. Maximum depth is confirmed at 26 meters (approximately 80 feet) by multiple operator sources.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Northern tip of Isla Cerralvo, Sea of Cortez
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Coral reef / rocky reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 26 meters

## Marine Life

The rocky reef structures support large gorgonian sea fans and brain corals. Moray eel species documented at La Reina include zebra moray, green moray, and jeweled moray (per Baja Diving and Cortez Club). Fish life includes schools of barracuda, tuna, skipjack, snappers, and colorful tropical reef species. Sea turtles, hawkfish, and butterflyfish are regularly sighted. The resident sea lion colony (~40 animals) provides interaction opportunities. Giant Pacific manta rays are the seasonal highlight, documented arriving mid-June through mid-October. Occasional pelagic passes by hammerhead sharks, wahoo, dorado, and yellowtail are reported.

## Dive Profile

Dives range from the surface to approximately 26 meters (80 feet), including a submerged canyon section with extensive sponge coverage. Visibility frequently exceeds 100 feet. Currents can be strong and variable — they keep the water clean but require drift diving readiness. The site suits intermediate divers comfortable with current. Cabo Trek notes the boat crossing from La Paz takes approximately 90 minutes.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access from La Paz — approximately 90 minutes depending on departure point. Cabo Trek and local La Paz operators offer dedicated Cerralvo / La Reina dive days.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Mid-June through mid-October is the documented peak period for giant Pacific manta ray encounters at this site.
- Visit during incoming tide for strongest manta ray activity in channel currents.
- Strong currents mean buoyancy control is important — be prepared to drift.
- Visibility over 100 feet makes this a strong photography site for wide-angle manta shots.

## Safety Considerations

Currents at La Reina can be strong (described by the Underwater Photography Guide as "wicked"). Dive with a local guide familiar with tidal timing. A surface marker buoy is essential. The longer crossing from La Paz means conditions should be assessed before departure.
