## Daharat Abida

Daharat Abida (also documented as "Dahrat Abid") is the southernmost named dive site in Sudan's Red Sea, lying roughly 220 km south of Port Sudan and about 30 km from the Eritrean border. It is a small, uninhabited, bird-populated island of only a few hundred metres in length, marking the end of the "deep south" liveaboard route and visited by only a handful of specialized operators.

## Overview

Daharat Abida/Dahrat Abid sits at the far end of Sudan's deep south circuit, reached only by liveaboards running dedicated extreme-south itineraries (multiple independent operator accounts, including Cassiopeia Safari's Red Sea Diving Safari routes, describe it as the southernmost site they visit, close to the Eritrean border). The island has a shallow balcony/plateau on one side, with soft and hard corals and reef fish, and a wall that drops steeply into deep water. Divers report exceptional visibility and a strong likelihood of large hammerhead shark schools, along with whitetip, silvertip, and grey reef sharks. A wreck lies on the island's north/northwest side; sources note there is little to see of the wreck itself underwater today, with a lifeboat (reported name "Aris Pireus") nearby as one visible marker. One earlier draft of this page mentioned "3 wrecks" on the northwest side — this specific detail could not be confirmed by a second independent source and has been removed pending verification.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Southernmost named site in Sudan's Red Sea, roughly 220 km south of Port Sudan, ~30 km from the Eritrean border
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Expert
- **Maximum Depth**: 35 meters

## Marine Life

Independent trip reports and operator descriptions consistently mention large hammerhead shark schools at this site — one magazine account describes roughly 100 hammerheads passing at depth near the wall, while another operator account describes a school of around 50; the discrepancy likely reflects natural variation between visits rather than a fixed number, so treat any specific count as anecdotal rather than guaranteed. Whitetip, silvertip, and grey reef sharks are also reported. Barracuda, jacks, snapper, and wrasse are mentioned around the reef. Soft and hard corals cover the shallow balcony area. Claims of silky sharks or manta rays specific to this site were not found in the sources reviewed and have been removed; regional deep-south fauna (silky sharks, mantas) may be present but are not confirmed for this specific site.

## Dive Profile

Diving is generally done along the wall in the 15-30 metre range, where hammerheads are most often encountered, with the wall itself continuing well beyond recreational limits (400m+ per some accounts). Safety stops are typically done over the shallow balcony area. Strong currents are common in the deep south generally; specific current conditions at this site vary by report. Given the remote location and depth range, this site is appropriately rated for experienced divers only.

## Entry and Exit

Access is exclusively via specialized deep-south liveaboard expeditions — only a small number of operators run itineraries this far south. Entry and exit are coordinated with the liveaboard crew and depend on sea conditions. High-visibility surface marker buoys are strongly recommended given the remoteness.

## Requirements & Tips

- **Advanced or Expert certification recommended**, with comfort at 25-35m depths and in open-water shark encounters
- **Exposure suit**: Reports describe warm water (around 28-30°C in season), though conditions vary seasonally
- **Redundant signaling equipment**: SMB, whistle, or mirror recommended given the remote location
- **Book only with operators experienced on Sudan's deep-south route** (e.g., extreme-south liveaboard itineraries)

## Safety Considerations

This is remote-area diving with minimal emergency support — evacuation from this far south takes considerably longer than in Sudan's more-visited northern sites. Divers should be comfortable with open-water shark encounters, current conditions, and depths approaching 35 metres. Always dive with an experienced buddy and maintain conservative air and no-decompression limits given the distance from medical support.
