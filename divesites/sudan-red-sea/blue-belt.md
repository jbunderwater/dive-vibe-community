## Blue Belt

Blue Belt, also known as the Toyota Wreck, is a deep Sudanese Red Sea cargo wreck whose current diveability is disputed — some sources report it has slid past 60m and is no longer accessible.

## Overview

The Blue Belt was a Saudi Arabian general cargo vessel, reportedly built around 1950 and displacing about 2,399 gross registered tons, sailing from Jeddah to Port Sudan with a cargo of roughly 190 Toyota vehicles and spare parts (hence the "Toyota Wreck" nickname) when she struck Sha'ab Suedi reef on 2 December 1977, about 75km north of Port Sudan. Adrex.com attributes the grounding to a navigation error compounded by poor winter weather; a secondary, unverified theory involving vehicle smuggling also circulates but has no independent confirmation.

**Current status is uncertain.** Two independent sources (Adrex.com and Cassiopeia Safari) report that the wreck, already lying overturned on a steep slope, slid further down the reef face past 60m in recent years and is "no longer permitted to be dived" due to its depth and unstable condition. Other dive-booking listings (Divechamp, Divescover, Good Dive) continue to advertise Blue Belt as an active advanced/technical dive. Given this conflict, divers should confirm current access status directly with a Port Sudan liveaboard operator before planning a trip around this site.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Sha'ab Suedi Reef, ~75km north of Port Sudan
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Expert
- **Maximum Depth**: 90 meters
- **Access Status**: Disputed — see Overview

## Wreck History

The 103m Blue Belt (~2,399 GRT) struck the reef on 2 December 1977 and settled overturned on a sloping reef face, keel up, bow toward the reef, carrying about 190 Toyota vehicles. These specific vessel dimensions and cargo figures are corroborated by two sources (Adrex.com, Divechamp); the 1950 build year appears in both but the two write-ups share enough identical phrasing that they may not be fully independent, so treat the build year as reported-but-not-fully-corroborated.

## Marine Life

When the site was diver-accessible, reports describe shoals of big-eye trevally and snappers, lunartail groupers, and white-tip reef sharks patrolling near the hull. No more recent, site-specific marine-life reports were found; if the wreck is now inaccessible at depth, current sightings cannot be confirmed.

## Dive Profile

If access is confirmed available, this is a deep technical wreck dive requiring mandatory decompression planning, redundant gas, and deep-diving certification (TDI/IANTD Advanced Nitrox & Decompression or equivalent). Given the reported depth and instability, do not attempt this dive without direct, current confirmation from an experienced Port Sudan operator that the site remains accessible and safe.

## Entry and Exit

Historically reached only via liveaboard from Port Sudan, with descent along the reef slope to the wreck. No shore access exists.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Confirm current accessibility with your liveaboard operator before including this site in a trip plan
- If diveable, this is for technical deep-diving certified divers only — not a recreational-limits dive
- Carry redundant depth computers, decompression gas, and backup lighting if the dive proceeds

## Safety Considerations

Sources agree this wreck, if still accessible, sits well beyond recreational depth limits with an unstable structure. Hazards include nitrogen narcosis, mandatory decompression obligations, and entanglement risk on the collapsing hull. Do not attempt this dive without technical certification and a guide with current, first-hand knowledge of the wreck's condition.
