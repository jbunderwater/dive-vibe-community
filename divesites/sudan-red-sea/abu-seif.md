## Abu Seif

Abu Seif (also spelled Abu Sheif) is a shallow reef dive on Sudan's northern liveaboard routes, named directly in Scuba Diving magazine's "Five Great Dive Sites in Sudan's Red Sea" and referenced by Blue Force Fleet's route materials. It stands out from Sudan's typically deep wall and pelagic sites for its shallow, relaxed profile.

## Overview

Per Scuba Diving magazine, Abu Sheif features cracks and crevices in coral formations just below the surface, excellent natural lighting, and a relaxed pace that allows extended bottom time. The magazine describes sea goldies near the surface and anemone meadows with resident clownfish deeper in the crevice system. Blue Force Fleet's route materials independently list "Abu Sheif" among northern-route stops, corroborating the site's existence and shallow character, though without additional marine-life specifics beyond the crevice topography.

Although conditions here are genuinely mild by Sudan liveaboard standards, this destination's policy sets Advanced as the floor difficulty for all Sudan Red Sea sites, since access is exclusively by remote liveaboard with no local emergency infrastructure — divers should still hold at least an Advanced certification to be aboard these itineraries, even though this particular dive itself is easy.

Sea goldies are reported near the surface and anemones with clownfish partnerships in deeper crevice sections (Scuba Diving magazine). No other species are confirmed for this specific site in available sources — moray eels, additional reef fish, or pelagic claims from the prior version of this page were not corroborated and have been removed.

## Dive Profile

The dive centers on exploring a shallow crack-and-crevice coral system just below the surface, per Scuba Diving's description. The shallow depth (max 12m recorded) supports longer bottom times than Sudan's deeper wall sites. No specific current, drift pattern, or exact bottom-time figures are given in sources — treat any such numbers as unconfirmed.

## Entry and Exit

Access is by liveaboard dive boat only. Sources describe entry as easy given the shallow, sheltered topography ("you could spend hours exploring the cracks and crevices sprawling mere feet below the surface"). No specific exit procedure detail beyond this is confirmed in sources.
