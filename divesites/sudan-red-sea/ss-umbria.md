## SS Umbria

SS Umbria is a scuttled Italian cargo ship at Wingate Reef near Port Sudan, deliberately sunk by her own crew in June 1940 to keep her wartime cargo out of British hands.

## Overview

The SS Umbria was built as SS Bahia Blanca in Hamburg (launched 30 December 1911) and originally ran cargo and passenger routes between Europe and Argentina. Purchased by the Italian government in 1935, she was renamed Umbria. On 9 June 1940, British warships HMS Grimsby and HMS Leander forced her to anchor at Wingate Reef near Port Sudan on the pretext of a contraband search. When her captain, Lorenzo Muiesan, learned that Italy was about to enter the war against Britain, he ordered the ship scuttled overnight (9–10 June 1940) rather than let her war cargo fall into British hands. She sank without explosion or major structural damage, which is why so much of her cargo and fittings remain intact and identifiable today. She now lies on her port side and is regarded as one of the best-preserved wreck dives in the world.

**Reported figures differ across sources and are presented here rather than resolved into a single number.** Wikipedia gives her length as 154.9 m and tonnage as 10,076 GRT; other dive-industry sources (PADI, dive operators) describe her simply as "over 150 m" without giving tonnage. Maximum depth is also reported inconsistently: Wikipedia and several dive operators cite a maximum depth of 38 m (at the bow), while PADI's site profile lists a shallower 5–35 m range. Divers should expect the wreck to span roughly the mid-30s in maximum depth, with much of the structure well within recreational limits.

Cargo reports are broadly consistent across sources: roughly 360,000 bombs, several hundred cases of detonators, other munitions, large quantities of cement (now solidified in the holds), and a small number of Fiat 1100 cars on the car deck. Exact bomb-count figures vary slightly by source (e.g., "6,000 tons of bombs" vs. "360,000 bombs" as a unit count — these are not necessarily contradictory since they describe different measures), so treat any single precise figure as approximate rather than exact.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Wingate Reef, near Port Sudan, Sudan Red Sea
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced (destination minimum for this remote liveaboard region; much of the wreck itself is at moderate, near-recreational depths)
- **Maximum Depth**: Reported as 38 meters by most sources (Wikipedia, several dive operators); PADI lists a shallower range of 5–35 meters. Treat 38 m as the conservative maximum.

## Wreck History

Umbria was scuttled deliberately by her own crew, not sunk by enemy action — there was no battle, torpedo, or mine involved. This is a well-documented event corroborated by Wikipedia and multiple independent dive-history sources (cassiopeiasafari.com, PADI). She rests on her port side largely intact, with her cargo holds, engine room, and parts of the car deck still accessible to divers. Both wartime cargo (munitions, cement) and pre-existing ship's fittings remain in place because the sinking was slow and controlled rather than violent.

## Marine Life

Marine life reports specific to this wreck (not just the general region) are corroborated by more than one source: snapper are repeatedly noted around the stern/rudder area, along with featherstars/crinoids, barracuda, and butterflyfish encrusting the structure. Soft and hard coral growth covers much of the hull. Beyond these repeatedly-cited species, other individual sources mention sharks, turtles, and reef fish in the vicinity, but since these are not corroborated across multiple independent site-specific accounts, they should be treated as less certain than the snapper/crinoid/barracuda/butterflyfish sightings.

## Dive Profile

Much of Umbria sits at depths accessible to divers with solid buoyancy control and wreck-diving awareness, with parts of the superstructure near the surface and the deepest sections around the high-30s in meters. Penetration into the holds and engine room is possible for divers with appropriate wreck training and equipment (dive light, line, redundant air) — penetration is never inherently "safe," and divers without wreck certification should stay on the exterior or in open, well-lit swim-throughs only. Silt and cargo debris inside the holds can reduce visibility quickly if disturbed.

## Entry and Exit

Access is via liveaboard dive boat operating out of Port Sudan; there is no shore access to Wingate Reef. Entry is typically a giant stride or back roll from the tender or mothership. Descents follow a mooring or shot line down to the wreck.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Wreck diving training is recommended before penetrating the holds or engine room
- Bring a primary and backup dive light for interior sections
- Maintain careful buoyancy near silty cargo areas to preserve visibility for other divers
- Note the conflicting depth figures (5–35 m per PADI vs. up to 38 m per other sources) and dive conservatively to your training level

## Safety Considerations

Penetration of the holds and engine room is possible for trained wreck divers but is never without risk — entanglement, silt-outs, and disorientation are real hazards inside an intact but decades-old structure. Divers without wreck certification should limit themselves to open exterior sections and well-lit swim-throughs. Always dive within your training and certification limits, and check gear and lights before penetrating any enclosed space.
