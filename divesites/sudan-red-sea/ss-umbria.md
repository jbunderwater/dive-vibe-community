## SS Umbria

The SS Umbria is one of the world's most celebrated wreck dives — an Italian cargo ship deliberately scuttled by her own captain at Wingate Reef on 9 June 1940, moments after Italy entered WWII, to keep her secret wartime cargo out of British hands.

## Overview

Built in Hamburg and launched on 30 December 1911 as SS Bahia Blanca, the 154.9m, 10,076-gross-registered-ton vessel was acquired by the Argentine government in 1918 and used on Atlantic cargo routes before Italy purchased and renamed her Umbria in 1935. On 3 June 1940 she arrived at British-controlled Port Said carrying a covert wartime cargo: roughly 6,000 tons of bombs, 600 cases of detonators, about 100 tons of assorted weapons, over 2,000 tons of cement, and three Fiat 1100 cars. British warships HMS Grimsby and HMS Leander forced her to anchor at Wingate Reef near Port Sudan on the pretext of a contraband search. When Captain Lorenzo Muiesan learned by radio that Italy had joined the war, he requested a muster drill and, with his crew, deliberately scuttled the ship on 9 June 1940 to keep her cargo from British seizure.

Umbria now lies on her port side at a maximum depth of 38 metres, with much of her wartime cargo still in the holds and Fiat cars still visible on the car deck. The wreck is regarded as one of the best and most accessible wreck dives anywhere, with her engine room, kitchen and dining areas explorable and encrusted in soft corals and crinoids.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Wingate Reef, near Port Sudan, Sudan Red Sea
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 38 meters

## Wreck History

Ship name, build date, dimensions, tonnage, cargo and cause of sinking are corroborated across Wikipedia and multiple dive-industry sources (PADI, Cassiopeia Safari): built 1911-12 in Hamburg as Bahia Blanca; 154.9m, 10,076 GRT; renamed Umbria after 1935 Italian purchase; deliberately scuttled by Capt. Lorenzo Muiesan on 9 June 1940 at Wingate Reef to prevent capture of her bomb and detonator cargo. (The site's prior write-up stated 10 June — the corroborated date is 9 June 1940.)

## Marine Life

Dense schools of sweepers and snapper are commonly reported around the wreck, with the superstructure and holds encrusted in soft corals and crinoids. No further site-specific species list beyond these commonly-reported groups was independently confirmed in this review.

## Dive Profile

The wreck sits in 4-38m of water on her port side, allowing multi-level exploration: shallower sections and the exposed hull are accessible to divers with solid buoyancy control, while the engine room, cargo holds (with the still-intact bomb cargo, visible but never to be touched or entered without wreck training) and car deck require wreck diving experience and careful route planning. Multiple dives are typically needed to see the full wreck.

## Entry and Exit

Reached exclusively by liveaboard from Port Sudan; entry is a standard giant stride or back-roll onto the wreck's shallow sections, descending along the hull.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Bring a dive light — interior spaces and cargo holds are dark even in good visibility
- Do not touch or disturb ordnance still present in the holds
- Wreck-diving training is recommended for anyone planning to enter enclosed spaces
- Multiple dives across a liveaboard itinerary allow full exploration of the site

## Safety Considerations

Ordnance (bombs and detonators) remains in the wreck's holds; do not handle or disturb it. Interior penetration should only be attempted with proper wreck-diving training and equipment — it is never "completely safe," even on a well-known, relatively benign wreck like Umbria. Maintain good buoyancy near silty interior sections to preserve visibility for yourself and other divers.
