# Dari Laut

The wreck of an old abandoned floating casino resting on a sandy slope, its box-frame structure now heavily encrusted with sponges, gorgonias, featherstars, and anemones -- an unusual wreck with strong critter appeal.

## Overview

Dari Laut is the wreck of an old abandoned floating casino that now rests on a sandy slope in the Anilao area. The structure sits with its shallowest point at approximately 12 meters and its deepest sections at around 25 meters. Unlike traditional ship wrecks, the Dari Laut has a box-frame construction that is now heavily colonized by marine life -- sponges, gorgonia fans, featherstars, and anemones cover the framework, creating a thriving artificial reef. The unusual origin and structure of this wreck give it a character quite different from typical wreck dives.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Anilao, Batangas, Philippines
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters (wreck deepest at ~25 m, bottom at ~30 m)
- **Typical Visibility**: 10-20 meters
- **Water Temperature**: 27-29°C
- **Current**: Variable
- **Best Time**: November to June (dry season)

## Marine Life

The Dari Laut's encrusted framework supports a rich community of marine life. Batfish school around the structure. Clownfish occupy anemones that have colonized the frame. Crocodilefish lie camouflaged on flat surfaces. Moray eels inhabit the framework's many recesses. Nudibranchs are found on the sponge-covered surfaces. The gorgonias, featherstars, and anemones that blanket the structure provide habitat for a wide range of invertebrates, making this a productive critter dive as well as an interesting wreck exploration.

## Dive Profile

Enter from the boat and descend to the wreck structure. The shallowest part of the frame is at approximately 12 meters, making it accessible and allowing for extended bottom time. Explore around and through the box-frame structure, examining the encrusted surfaces for critters. The deepest sections reach around 25 meters, with the sandy bottom at approximately 30 meters. The open framework allows natural light to penetrate throughout, making orientation straightforward. Work from deep to shallow to manage bottom time efficiently.

## Entry and Exit

Access by boat from Anilao-area resorts. Enter by giant stride and descend to the wreck. Deploy an SMB during your safety stop for boat pickup.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The open box-frame structure is well-lit and easy to navigate -- less intimidating than enclosed wreck penetration
- Check the anemones for clownfish and the flat surfaces for crocodilefish
- The shallowest sections at 12 meters make for an excellent extended safety stop area
- Bring a torch to illuminate the sponge-covered framework and bring out colours

## Safety Considerations

The box-frame structure is relatively open, but be aware of potential entanglement points if carrying camera equipment or trailing hoses. The structure rests on a sandy slope, so buoyancy control is important to avoid disturbing silt. The maximum depth of 30 meters at the bottom limits bottom time on air. As with any wreck, do not enter enclosed spaces without appropriate training and equipment.

## Photography

The Dari Laut offers a mix of wide-angle and macro opportunities. The encrusted framework makes for unusual wide-angle compositions -- the box-frame silhouetted against blue water with marine growth is distinctive. Batfish schooling around the structure are good wide-angle subjects. Macro targets include nudibranchs on the sponges, clownfish in anemones, and crocodilefish on flat surfaces. A wide-angle lens captures the wreck's unusual structure best, but a macro lens rewards careful exploration of the encrusted surfaces.
