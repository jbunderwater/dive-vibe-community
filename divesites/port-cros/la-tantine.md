# La Tantine

A small but historically obscure wreck — a tugboat resting at 48 metres near the islet of Bagaud, famously named for the conger eels that once densely inhabited it.

## Overview

La Tantine (also known as "La Barge aux Congres" — the Conger Barge) is a small wreck located approximately 500 metres from the islet of Bagaud, within the broader maritime zone of Port-Cros National Park. The wreck is a tugboat approximately 20 metres long, 6 metres wide, and 3 metres high, lying flat on a sandy bottom at 48 metres. A crane was mounted at its stern; only the folded crane structure rises significantly from the seabed.

The history of this wreck is genuinely unresolved. According to multiple sources including the official Port-Cros National Park dive trail documentation and the Provence-Alpes-Côte d'Azur tourism authority, the exact sinking date and cause are unknown. What is consistently reported is that the vessel was involved in a towing operation around the 1960s, transporting construction materials (reportedly bricks) for the Manoir hotel on Port-Cros island, and that movement of the cargo during rough weather destabilised the vessels and caused the tug to sink. No official register record has been located, and no source provides a specific sinking date, vessel name, or owner.

The wreck earned its popular name from the very large population of conger eels that once inhabited it. Sources confirm by the 1990s the population numbered many large individuals, but that feeding cessation subsequently reduced this population significantly. Current dive operator reports indicate only 2–3 large conger eels remain on the wreck.

## Site Information

- **Location**: ~500 m from Islet of Bagaud, Hyères Bay, Var, France
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 48 meters
- **Wreck Dimensions**: ~20 m long, 6 m wide, 3 m high (sources: provence-alpes-cotedazur.com, hyeres-plongee.com)
- **Typical Visibility**: 10–25 metres
- **Water Temperature**: 13–25°C
- **Current**: Often strong; the small wreck size makes it challenging to locate
- **Best Time**: May to October

## Marine Life

The wreck's defining species are conger eels — the source of its common name. Sources confirm 2–3 large conger eels currently inhabit the wreck (a decline from historical numbers). Moray eels occupy the lower recesses. Scorpionfish and red mullet are recorded at the site. Regional Mediterranean species may visit, but no site-specific reports of groupers, gorgonian fan communities, or schooling pelagics were confirmed for this specific wreck during the 2026-05-25 QA review.

## Dive Profile

The small wreck means a thorough exterior circuit takes approximately 10 minutes at depth. Descend directly on a line — the combination of 48-metre depth, small target, and strong current make a descent line essential. Explore the exterior hull, crane structure, and accessible stern openings. No-decompression limits on air at 48 metres are approximately 15 minutes; Nitrox extends this modestly. Begin ascent with substantial reserve.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access only. A permanent mooring or descent line is essential. The small wreck is difficult to locate even with GPS in current — dive with an operator who knows the site. Ascend on the line; deploy SMB from the 5-metre safety stop before surfacing.

## Tips

- This is one of the harder locates in the Hyères wreck circuit — dive with an operator who has GPS coordinates for this specific wreck
- Nitrox is advisable at 48 metres to make sensible use of limited bottom time
- Approach the stern openings quietly — the remaining conger eels inhabit these spaces
- Carry a torch: the enclosed stern area is dark even in clear Mediterranean water

## Safety

Advanced depth and frequently strong current. Minimum advanced certification required; deep dive experience strongly preferred. The small target increases the risk of missing the wreck and drifting in current — never free-descend at this depth without a line. Gas management must be conservative. Do not penetrate interior spaces without wreck training and dedicated lighting.

## Photography

The folded crane structure rising above the flat hull is the most photogenic feature. Conger eels in the stern openings, illuminated with a torch, are the signature subject. Artificial lighting is essential at 48 metres.
