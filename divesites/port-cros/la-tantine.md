# La Tantine

A small but historically obscure wreck — a tugboat resting at 48 metres near the islet of Bagaud, famously named for the conger eels that once densely inhabited it.

## Overview

La Tantine (also known as "La Barge aux Congres" — the Conger Barge) is a small wreck located approximately 500 metres from the islet of Bagaud, within the broader maritime zone of Port-Cros National Park. The wreck is a crane pontoon (ponton grue) — a wooden, unpowered barge approximately 15–20 metres long and 6 metres wide — lying flat on a sandy bottom at 48 metres. A crane structure at the stern rises 2–3 metres above the sandy bottom. The vessel had no engine or propeller; it was towed during operations.

The history of this wreck is genuinely unresolved. According to multiple sources including the official Port-Cros National Park dive trail documentation and the Provence-Alpes-Côte d'Azur tourism authority, the exact sinking date and cause are unknown. What is consistently reported is that the vessel was involved in a towing operation around the 1960s, transporting construction materials (single-row perforated bricks and a motorised pump) for the Manoir hotel on Port-Cros island. During towing, cargo displacement destabilised the tow, causing the barge to sink. No official register record has been located, and no source provides a specific sinking date, vessel name, or owner.

Note: Some sources (including miopalmoplongee.com) describe the vessel as "un remorqueur doté d'une grue" (a tugboat equipped with a crane), while sun-plongee.com and provence-alpes-cotedazur.com describe it as a wooden pontoon without motor or propeller. The "ponton grue" / crane-barge description is the more widely supported characterisation, consistent with its towed status.

The wreck earned its popular name from the large population of conger eels that once inhabited it. By the 1990s the population was very high, but a reduction of human feeding activity has since decreased it. Current dive operator reports note 2–3 large conger eels (sun-plongee.com, provencemed.com) to "more than a dozen exceeding 2 metres" (hyeres-plongee.com) — sources differ on current population; the eels are well-hidden beneath the hull and require careful approach to observe.

## Site Information

- **Location**: ~500 m from Islet of Bagaud, Hyères Bay, Var, France
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 48 meters
- **Vessel Type**: Crane pontoon / ponton grue (no engine or propeller; wooden hull)
- **Wreck Dimensions**: 15–20 m long, 6 m wide; crane extends ~2–3 m above bottom (sources: provence-alpes-cotedazur.com, hyeres-plongee.com, sun-plongee.com)
- **Typical Visibility**: 10–25 metres
- **Water Temperature**: 13–25°C
- **Current**: Often strong; the small wreck size makes it challenging to locate
- **Best Time**: May to October

## Marine Life

The wreck's defining species are conger eels — the source of its common name. Sources report 2–3 large conger eels (sun-plongee.com, provencemed.com) to "more than a dozen exceeding 2 metres" (hyeres-plongee.com); sources differ and the population may vary seasonally. The eels hide beneath the hull and require divers to position low to the sand and illuminate the underside of metal debris. Moray eels (mostelles) and rocklings occupy the lower recesses. Scorpionfish and red mullet are recorded at the site. Cargo remnants (single-row bricks, a motorised pump with metal wheels) remain visible on the port side. No site-specific reports of groupers, gorgonian fan communities, or schooling pelagics were confirmed for this specific wreck during QA review.

## Dive Profile

The small wreck means a thorough exterior circuit takes approximately 10 minutes at depth. Descend directly on a line — the combination of 48-metre depth, small target, and strong current make a descent line essential. Explore the exterior hull, crane structure, and accessible stern openings. Lie close to the sandy bottom to illuminate the underside of the hull to find the conger eels. Cargo remnants (bricks, pump) are visible on the port side. No-decompression limits on air at 48 metres are approximately 15 minutes; Nitrox extends this modestly. Begin ascent with substantial reserve.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access only. A permanent mooring or descent line is essential. The small wreck is difficult to locate even with GPS in current — dive with an operator who knows the site. Ascend on the line; deploy SMB from the 5-metre safety stop before surfacing.

## Tips

- This is one of the harder locates in the Hyères wreck circuit — dive with an operator who has GPS coordinates for this specific wreck
- Nitrox is advisable at 48 metres to make sensible use of limited bottom time
- Approach the stern openings quietly — the remaining conger eels inhabit these spaces
- Carry a torch: the enclosed stern area is dark even in clear Mediterranean water

## Safety

Advanced depth and frequently strong current. Minimum advanced certification required; deep dive experience strongly preferred. The small target increases the risk of missing the wreck and drifting in current — never free-descend at this depth without a line. Gas management must be conservative. Do not penetrate interior spaces without wreck training and dedicated lighting.

## Photography

The folded crane structure rising above the flat hull is the most photogenic feature. Conger eels in the stern openings, illuminated with a torch, are the signature subject. Artificial lighting is essential at 48 metres.
