# La Tantine

A small wooden ponton-crane that sank around 1960 during a towing operation, lying flat on sand at 48 metres north of Port-Cros — notable more for its conger eel history than its structure.

## Overview

La Tantine (also called *La Barge aux Congres* — "the conger barge") is a non-motorised wooden vessel, approximately 15–20 metres long and 6 metres wide, resting flat on a sandy bottom at 45–49 metres depth approximately 500 metres from the island of Bagaud (Port-Cros). The precise circumstances of its sinking are unknown. Local sources indicate it sank during a towing operation around 1960, possibly while transporting building materials (brick remnants and a motor pump with metal wheels remain visible on the port side) to the Manoir hotel at Port-Cros. The vessel was not self-propelled — it had no engine — and whether it sank in heavy weather due to shifting cargo, or for another reason, is undocumented.

The wreck itself is of modest structural interest (largely collapsed at 48 metres), but was famous in the 1990s for an extraordinarily dense population of conger eels that would actively approach divers. Overfishing in the surrounding area has since reduced this population significantly; current reports suggest only a handful of individuals remain.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Hyères Bay, Port-Cros maritime zone, Var, France
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 48 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 10–25 metres
- **Water Temperature**: 13–25°C
- **Current**: Light to moderate; can be stronger on occasion
- **Best Time**: May to October

## Marine Life

The wreck's namesake is the conger eel — in its 1990s heyday the site hosted a remarkable density of large congers that would swim directly toward divers, making it one of the most distinctive wreck encounters in the region. Local dive operators report that this population has substantially declined due to broader area fishing pressure and now numbers only a few individuals. Moray eels, red mullet, and occasional spiny lobsters are also recorded at the site. Forkbeard (Phycis phycis) are noted by one operator. The sandy bottom and collapsed wooden structure do not support the gorgonian and anthias communities typical of the deeper steel wrecks in the same area.

## Dive Profile

This dive requires a planned descent directly to the wreck at 48 metres. Bottom time is severely limited by no-decompression limits at this depth — expect 15–20 minutes maximum on air. Explore the wreck's most accessible exterior features and any open interior sections before beginning a staged ascent. Plan a safety stop at 5 metres. Nitrox 32 or 36% significantly improves no-decompression time at this depth.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access only. A permanent mooring or descent line is essential at this depth. Descend the line directly to the wreck. Do not free-ascend in open water at this depth without a line — use the ascent line and SMB together. Coordinate ascent and safety stop timing with your dive buddy before entry.

## Tips

- Nitrox qualification is strongly advisable for this depth — the difference in no-decompression time at 48 metres between air and EAN36 is substantial
- Carry a dive torch — wreck interiors are dark and the encrusting organisms are far more colourful under artificial light
- Pre-plan your wreck circuit so the limited bottom time is spent efficiently on the most interesting features
- The wreck's orientation and major landmarks should be briefed before descent

## Safety

This is an advanced dive with depth-related risks. Never dive La Tantine without proper advanced certifications (at minimum a PADI Advanced OWD with deep dive; ideally deep specialty or tech training). Monitor gas carefully — begin ascent with substantial reserve. Current can affect the ascent line; ensure it is secured. A torch is essential.

## Photography

The encrusted metalwork and resident fish life create compelling subjects at La Tantine. Wide-angle shots of groupers and dentex against the wreck structure, and macro work on the sponge and gorgonian encrustation, both work well. Artificial lighting is essential — at 48 metres, no usable ambient colour remains. A torch for focus assists and a powerful strobe are needed.
