# Le Donator Wreck

One of the most celebrated shipwrecks on the French Mediterranean coast — a 78-metre cargo ship lying upright at 35–52 metres with extraordinary gorgonian colonisation and dense fish life.

## Overview

The *Donator* — officially named *Prosper Schiaffino* at the time of its sinking — is one of the most famous wreck dives in the western Mediterranean. The vessel was built in 1931 at Bergen Mekaniske Verksted in Bergen, Norway (78.28 metres long, 11.94 metres wide, 1,698 gross register tons). It was launched as *Donator*, renamed *Petite Terre* in October 1933 when sold to a French company for banana transport, and renamed again *Prosper Schiaffino* in June 1939 when purchased by the Algerian navigation company Société Schiaffino, which converted it into a wine carrier.

On 10 November 1945, at 13:15, a drifting mine tore the vessel almost in two south of Porquerolles island while it was carrying wine from Algeria, hugging the coast to shelter from the Mistral wind. The ship sank in under four minutes. Casualty accounts conflict across sources: the French Wikipedia article and multiple secondary sources state 5 deaths; en.bormesplongee.fr records 2 missing sailors and 27 survivors rescued (no deaths stated); ulysseplongee.fr states 3 victims. Sources agree the sinking was rapid and lifeboats could not be launched. The wreck lies upright on a sandy bottom between the islands of Porquerolles and Port-Cros, at approximately 42°59'57"N / 6°16'43"E, within the Hyères Bay Natura 2000 zone. Deck depth is 35 metres; the propeller reaches 51–52 metres.

The ship later appeared in Jacques-Yves Cousteau's documentary work and is among the best-documented wrecks in the Mediterranean.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Between Porquerolles and Port-Cros, Hyères Bay, Var, France
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 52 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 10–25 metres
- **Water Temperature**: 13–25°C
- **Current**: Strong current possible; check conditions before each dive
- **Best Time**: May to October

## Marine Life

The wreck is densely colonised by red gorgonian sea fans (*Paramuricea clavata*) and yellow gorgonians covering the superstructures — among the finest gorgonian growth recorded on any Mediterranean wreck. Schools of swallowtail sea perch and anthias swarm around the mast and bridge structures. Dentex, groupers, two-banded sea bream, and red mullet are abundant. Large conger eels and moray eels inhabit the interior passageways. Scorpionfish and nudibranchs are found on encrusted surfaces.

## Dive Profile

Descend directly to the deck at 35 metres and begin exploration of the major features: the large steering wheel at the stern castle, the wine cargo holds (still recognisable), the engine room access, the galley and crew quarters (including a bathtub). The propeller and rudder are at 51–52 metres — plan the deepest point first. The mast (which until January 2000 rose to 25 metres depth) and the upper superstructure provide excellent subjects during the shallower portion of the ascent. A spare propeller is located aft. Nitrox is strongly recommended. Begin ascent with substantial reserve.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access from dive operators based in La Londe, Le Lavandou, and Hyères. A descent line to the wreck is essential given the depth and possible current. Giant stride entry; descend directly to the wreck. Ascend via the line and complete a safety stop at 5 metres before surfacing. Deploy an SMB from the safety stop depth.

## Tips

- The steering wheel is one of the most memorable features of this wreck — large, intact, and deeply atmospheric at depth
- Penetration of the interior (galley, crew quarters, holds) is feasible for experienced wreck divers with proper training and lighting — do not penetrate without wreck certification and a torch
- Ask the dive operator about current conditions before descending — the channel position makes this site sensitive to tidal flow
- The gorgonians on the mast section at 35 metres are outstanding — allocate ascent time here deliberately

## Safety

Advanced diving with depth-related risks. Minimum recommended certification: advanced open water with deep dive experience; deep specialty or wreck specialty strongly preferred. Nitrox 32–36% strongly recommended for safe bottom time management at 35–52 metres. Strong current can develop — abort if conditions deteriorate. Never penetrate the wreck without proper wreck training and a torch.

## Photography

Le Donator is one of the most photogenic wrecks in France. The gorgonian-covered mast section at 35 metres, shot wide-angle with a diver for scale, is an iconic image. The steering wheel is a powerful close-focus wide-angle subject. Macro work on gorgonian polyps and encrusted metal shows exceptional detail. At 35–52 metres, artificial lighting is essential — a powerful strobe and a torch are needed.
