# Wrack 'Sagona' (Le Grec)

An alternate buoy access point to the Sagona (Le Grec) wreck — a Scottish-built cargo ship that struck a mine in December 1945 and lies in two to three sections at 35–47 metres south of Porquerolles.

## Overview

This entry marks an alternate access buoy to the *Sagona*, the cargo ship universally known by divers as "Le Grec." Built in 1912 at the shipyards in Dundee, Scotland (54 metres long, 8.5 metres wide, 808 tons), the vessel was purchased in 1943 by the Greek shipping company Zanatti SS. Co. Ltd. and operated under the Panamanian flag transporting wine.

On 3 December 1945, the *Sagona* struck a floating mine south of Porquerolles — approximately 23 days after the nearby *Prosper Schiaffino* (Donator) met the same fate in the same mine field. The explosion tore off the bow; 2 crew were killed and 1 went missing. The wreck now lies on sandy bottom between 35 and 47 metres depth, with the main hull (stern and middle) at 36–47 metres.

**Note on wreck sections**: Bormes Plongée and European Diving School describe the wreck in two sections; Tribloo describes "three blocks approximately 60 metres apart." The layout should be confirmed with the operating dive centre before each dive.

The name "Le Grec" (The Greek) came from a post-sinking survey: French Navy divers found Greek-language documents aboard — the ship's papers and crew were Greek-registered at time of sinking.

## Site Information

- **Location**: South of Porquerolles, Hyères Bay (alternate buoy), Var, France
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 47 meters
- **Deck/access depth**: approximately 35 metres
- **Typical Visibility**: 10–25 metres
- **Water Temperature**: 13–25°C
- **Current**: Moderate to strong; variable with tidal state
- **Best Time**: May to October

## Marine Life

Red and yellow gorgonian sea fans colonise the gangways and accessible hull surfaces. The holds contain recognisable barrel stave debris from the wine cargo. Fish communities occupying the wreck include groupers, barracudas, moray eels, and dentex. Conger eels inhabit the deeper interior spaces. Schools of swallowtail sea perch swarm the superstructure.

## Dive Profile

The main hull (middle and stern) at 36–47 metres is the primary objective from this buoy position: explore the winch, propeller, holds with barrel remnants, and the port-side mast. The separated bow section can be the objective of a second dive on the same excursion. Plan the deepest objective first; nitrox is strongly recommended. Begin ascent with generous reserves.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access from La Londe, Le Lavandou, or Hyères operators. A descent line is essential. Confirm before the dive which wreck section the line leads to (main hull or bow). Ascend via the line; deploy SMB at 5 metres before surfacing.

## Tips

- Confirm with the operator which section the mooring buoy serves — the main hull and bow are separate objectives
- The barrel stave debris in the holds is one of the wreck's most evocative details
- Nitrox planning: EAN36 at 47 metres extends no-decompression time from approximately 20 to 30 minutes
- The twin-wreck experience with the nearby Donator (just a few hundred metres away) is a compelling area-level itinerary

## Safety

Advanced depth and current conditions. Monitor gas at all times — the multi-section layout can encourage overextended dives. Current in the Hyères channel should be assessed before each descent. Two SMBs recommended.

## Photography

The gorgonian-covered gangways and machinery are outstanding wide-angle subjects. The barrel stave debris in the holds, photographed with a torch, tells a story. Macro work on gorgonian polyps and encrusting sponges shows exceptional detail. Full artificial lighting is essential at 36–47 metres.
