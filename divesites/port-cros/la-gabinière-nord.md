## La Gabinière Nord

La Gabinière Nord is the northern wall dive of the La Gabinière islet, more sheltered than the exposed southern and western aspects, within Port-Cros National Park.

## Overview

La Gabinière Nord is the northern face of the La Gabinière islet, situated within Port-Cros National Park — Europe's oldest marine protected area, established in 1963. While the southern and western exposures of La Gabinière are the most celebrated for current-driven pelagic action, the northern wall offers a different character: more sheltered from prevailing swell and easterly winds, with a steeply dropping rock face descending to approximately 40 metres.

The northern aspect receives less direct current than the southern aspects, which concentrates fish life differently. Groupers are present — the islet's total brown grouper population exceeds 300 individuals per current park monitoring — though typically encountered in smaller concentrations on the northern face than on the current-swept eastern and southern aspects. The wall is draped with the same Mediterranean invertebrate life found around the islet: red and yellow gorgonian sea fans, dense sponge coverage, and yellow cluster anemones. Moray eels occupy crevices throughout. Shoals of seabream, wrasse, and bogue move along the wall, with barracuda from the large school occasionally passing through.

Access is by boat from Hyères, La Londe-les-Maures, or Le Lavandou dive centres. The northern wall is typically combined with other La Gabinière dive sites on multi-dive days, often as a second dive on the same mooring trip. Mooring buoys are available around the islet; anchoring is prohibited within the national park. CaPeL registration required (maximum 40 simultaneous divers at the islet).

## Dive Profile

- **Entry**: Boat only
- **Depth range**: Surface to 40 m along the wall
- **Conditions**: More sheltered than southern aspects; reduced current
- **Visibility**: Typically 15–25 m in summer
- **Best season**: May–October

## Marine Life

Brown groupers are present along the northern wall. Moray eels and conger eels inhabit deeper crevices. Shoals of seabream, bogue, wrasse, and mullet cruise the mid-water column. Red and yellow gorgonian fans, sponges, and anemones cover rock faces. Nudibranch diversity is noted on the encrusted rock surfaces.
