## Port-Cros

Europe's oldest marine national park, established in 1963 on France's Cote d'Azur, where decades of strict protection have produced exceptional grouper populations, large barracuda schools, and Mediterranean wrecks in waters with outstanding visibility.

## Description

Port-Cros documents dive sites across the island's protected waters and surrounding area, with depths ranging from 8 to 53 meters. The destination features a diverse mix of reef sites (9), historic wrecks (6), and wall dives (3), with the vast majority accessed by boat (16 sites). As France's first national park to include marine territory, Port-Cros offers some of the most biodiverse Mediterranean diving available, with the iconic Gabiniere islet accounting for the majority of dives in the park.

### Diving Opportunities

- **Boat Diving**: Sites accessed by boat from Hyeres, Le Lavandou, or Cavalaire, with crossing times of approximately 30-60 minutes depending on departure point
- **Shore Diving**: Shore-accessible sites within the protected waters
- **Reef Diving**: Reef sites including La Gabiniere (east drop-off and shallows), Cape Montremian, and La Tourelle de la Dame, ranging from 10 to 45 meters
- **Wreck Diving**: Wrecks including the renowned Donator (a 78-meter cargo ship at 47-52 meters) and Le Grec, both considered among the finest wreck dives in the Mediterranean. Depths range from 25 to 53 meters, suitable for advanced and technical divers
- **Wall Diving**: Wall dives along the island's volcanic drop-offs, with gorgonian fans and dense fish aggregations

### Accessibility

- **Getting There**: The nearest airport is Toulon-Hyeres Airport (TLN), approximately 15 minutes from the ferry terminal at Port Saint-Pierre in Hyeres. Marseille Provence Airport (MRS) and Nice Cote d'Azur Airport (NCE) are also options, both approximately 1.5-2 hours by car. TLV-TVM ferries depart from Hyeres to Port-Cros, with a crossing time of approximately 1 hour. Summer schedule offers multiple daily departures; winter is reduced to one departure daily (except Tuesdays and Thursdays)
- **Dive Operators**: Multiple centres operate in the Hyeres area including Divin'Giens (Presqu'ile de Giens), Mio Palmo Plongee (ports of Hyeres and Cavalaire), Saint P'Hyeres Plongee, Aqualonde Plongee, Ulysse Plongee, Saphir Plongee, and Lavandou Plongee. Dune Port-Cros is a PADI centre based closer to the island
- **Accommodation**: Port-Cros island itself has very limited accommodation (one hotel). Most divers stay in Hyeres, Le Lavandou, or on the Presqu'ile de Giens, which offer hotels, holiday rentals, and campgrounds at various price points
- **Transportation**: Rental car recommended to reach the ferry port from airports. Within Port-Cros, the island is car-free and explored on foot
- **Facilities**: Dive centres on the mainland offer full equipment rental, air and Nitrox fills, PADI and FFESSM (French federation) courses. The island itself has minimal facilities -- bring food and water for day trips

### Marine Life & Environment

- **Water Conditions**: Water temperatures range from 13 to 25 degrees C, with summer peaks of 22-25 degrees C (June-September) and winter lows around 13-15 degrees C. Visibility regularly exceeds 20 meters in summer, among the best in the western Mediterranean. Some sites experience moderate to strong currents, particularly at exposed points like La Gabiniere
- **Marine Biodiversity**: Decades of protection have allowed exceptional recovery of marine populations. Large dusky groupers (merou brun) are the park's signature species, with individuals exceeding 1 meter regularly seen at La Gabiniere. Mediterranean barracuda form large schools in open water. Other common species include dentex, moonfishes, sea bream, conger eels, moray eels, octopus, and dense gorgonian and sponge gardens on the walls. Eight species of cetaceans have been recorded in park waters, within the Pelagos Sanctuary for Mediterranean Marine Mammals. Occasional loggerhead sea turtle sightings have increased in recent years
- **Conservation**: Port-Cros National Park was created in 1963 as the first European national park to protect marine territory. The park now covers 120,000 hectares of sea, with 30% designated as core protection zones. Diving requires registration and adherence to a mandatory diving charter (enforceable since 2016). Mooring on designated buoys is compulsory at all dive sites -- anchoring on the seabed is prohibited. Fishing is prohibited within 100 meters of dive moorings. These strict regulations have enabled measurable recovery of fish populations over the past decades

## Additional Information

- **Best Time to Visit**: May to September for the best water temperatures and visibility. June and September offer a good balance of conditions with fewer crowds than July-August. Wreck dives to the Donator and Le Grec are available year-round but most comfortable in summer. Winter diving is possible but ferry schedules are limited
- **Currency**: Euro (EUR)
- **Language**: French. English is understood at most dive centres but French is helpful for briefings and logistics
- **Safety**: Strong currents at La Gabiniere and other exposed sites require attention -- follow operator briefings closely. Deep wrecks (47-53 meters) require appropriate certification and experience. The park's mandatory dive registration system means operators track all divers and conditions. Most sites are suitable for CMAS 1-star / PADI Open Water and above, but deeper sites and wreck penetrations require CMAS 3-star / PADI Advanced or higher
