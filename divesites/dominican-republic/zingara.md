# Zingara

*A deliberately scuttled Caribbean freighter resting at 35–36 metres off Sosua, the deepest and most popular wreck dive on the Dominican Republic's north coast.*

## Overview

The Zingara is a small coastal freighter that transported coffee and fruit around the Caribbean before being salvaged from Puerto Plata and deliberately sunk to create a dive attraction. It rests upright on a sandy bottom approximately 15 minutes by boat east of Sosua Beach, between Playa Chiquita and Playa Laguna.

Sources disagree on the vessel's length (ranging from 30 metres to 48 metres across various databases) and the exact year of sinking (1992 or 1993, with most primary sources stating one or the other). The Adrex dive article and the scubadivesosua.com operator both report 30 metres and 1992; sosuabeachdr.com states 36 metres and 1993. Given this conflict, the vessel's length is best described as approximately 30–36 metres. The sinking year is reported as 1992–1993. No independent documentation of the vessel's original name or registry was found in sources consulted.

The wreck sits with its deepest sections at 35–36 metres, placing it at the limit of recreational diving limits and making it an Advanced-level dive. The mooring line extends from the surface down to the chimney. The stern and engine room are penetrable for appropriately trained divers. A large school of grunts is reported as a near-permanent inhabitant of the interior.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Between Playa Chiquita and Playa Laguna, approximately 15 minutes east of Sosua Beach, north coast Dominican Republic
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 36 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15+ metres; conditions vary by season
- **Current**: Medium current expected; variable by season
- **Best Time**: May to October when the Atlantic is calmer on the north coast

## Marine Life

Sources confirm the following specific to this wreck: a resident green moray eel, large barracuda, Caribbean parrotfish, French grunts (large school inside the hull), French angelfish, snappers, bluehead wrasse, reef squid, green moray eels, and barracudas. Occasional shark sightings are reported. The engine block remains in one piece inside the engine room. Corals and sponges have colonised the exposed exterior over three decades of submersion.

## Dive Profile

Descend the mooring line from the surface to the chimney at around 30 metres, then drop to the deepest sections of the hull at 35–36 metres. Spend the initial bottom time exploring the deeper hull and superstructure, then work shallower as air depletes. The upper sections and adjacent reef allow decompression stop at 5 metres. Bottom time at 36 metres is limited under recreational no-decompression limits — plan conservatively and consider nitrox.

Operators typically include a multi-level component: the wreck itself at depth, then a transit to the adjacent reef at 15–20 metres to extend the dive to 40–50 minutes total.

## Entry and Exit

Dive boats from Sosua-area operators serve this site (including Superior Dive Sosua and Aqua Adventures). Entry via giant stride or back roll. Deploy an SMB before your ascent. The north coast can have choppier surface conditions than the southern Caribbean shore; choose days with settled conditions.

## Tips

- Nitrox is strongly recommended to maximise bottom time at this depth
- The mooring line is a reliable reference for ascent
- Do not penetrate enclosed areas without advanced wreck training, redundant lighting, and a reel
- The wreck is often combined with a Sosua Bay reef dive for a two-tank day

## Safety

Maximum depth of 35–36 metres requires careful dive planning within Advanced Open Water limits. Nitrox significantly improves the margin at this depth. The north coast of the Dominican Republic can have stronger currents and larger swells than the south coast — confirm conditions before departing. Always dive with a buddy.

## Photography

The encrusted exterior and resident school of grunts inside the hull are the primary photographic subjects. A torch is essential for illuminating sponge colours inside the wreck. Position near the chimney with the blue water column above for classic wreck portraits.
