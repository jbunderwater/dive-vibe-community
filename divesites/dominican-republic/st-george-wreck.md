# St. George Wreck

*A deliberately scuttled 73-metre cargo freighter off Bayahibe, one of the Dominican Republic's most impressive wreck dives for advanced divers.*

## Overview

The St. George was originally built in 1962 at the Ardrossan shipyard in Strathclyde, Scotland, under the name M.V. Norbrae. The vessel spent two decades transporting wheat and barley between Norway and the Americas before being decommissioned and left abandoned in Santo Domingo harbour. In 1999, following the devastation of Hurricane George, the ship was purchased, renamed in honour of that hurricane, and deliberately sunk on June 12, 1999, approximately 800 metres offshore from Bayahibe to create an artificial reef.

The wreck lies on a sandy bottom with its deepest sections at 44 metres and the top of the superstructure (chimney) at 15 metres. At 73 metres in length, it is a substantial wreck offering multiple penetration options across the mid-section at 25–40 metres. Visibility in these waters ranges from 15–25 metres. Water temperatures average 26–28°C.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Offshore Bayahibe, La Romana province, southeastern Dominican Republic (approximately 800 m from Coral Point Diving)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 44 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–25 metres (50–80 feet)
- **Current**: Variable; can be moderate
- **Best Time**: May to November for generally calmer southern coast conditions

## Marine Life

The wreck supports a growing artificial reef community after more than two decades on the seabed. Eagle rays have been observed in the water column above the wreck. Barracuda patrol the superstructure. A green moray eel is a known resident of the ship's chimney. Schools of Caribbean reef fish, including queen angelfish, move through the open areas of the hull. Encrusting sponges and corals now cover exposed metal surfaces. Sources do not confirm lionfish specifically at this wreck; sightings should not be assumed.

## Dive Profile

This is a deep wreck requiring careful dive planning. Descend the mooring line or descent line to the top of the superstructure at 15 metres, then work down along the hull. The most penetrable and photographable sections lie between 25 and 40 metres. Bottom time is limited at this depth on air — a typical dive lasts around 30 minutes. Nitrox (32%) is strongly recommended to maximise bottom time and reduce decompression loading. Plan an extended safety stop at 5 metres (minimum 3–5 minutes). Ascend conservatively.

## Entry and Exit

Dive operators from Bayahibe — including Coral Point Diving, Dressel Divers, and Pro Dive International — run regular trips to this site. Entry via giant stride or back roll. Deploy an SMB before ascending in open water.

## Tips

- Plan this dive conservatively; narcosis at 35–44 metres can impair judgment
- Nitrox 32% or 36% is the standard choice for divers visiting the St. George
- Pair with a shallower Bayahibe reef or the Atlantic Princess wreck as a second dive the same day
- Do not attempt wreck penetration at this depth without advanced wreck training, redundant lighting, and a reel

## Safety

The 44-metre maximum depth places this at the outer limit of recreational scuba. Know your personal nitrogen narcosis susceptibility before diving here and ascend immediately if impaired. Maintain a gas reserve of at least 100 bar before beginning ascent. Never attempt to penetrate the lower decks at maximum depth. Always dive with a buddy and agree on turn-around signals in advance.

## Photography

Natural light is dim at 35+ metres; a capable strobe is essential for revealing sponge and coral colours on the hull. Wide-angle shots of the hull silhouette against the water column are the signature image from this site. The chimney, with its resident moray, is a classic portrait subject if you can position safely without stirring up silt.
