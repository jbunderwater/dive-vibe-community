# Saona Island

*Pristine fringing reefs in a national park off the southeastern tip of Hispaniola, with outstanding turtle and stingray encounters in the region's most important turtle breeding area.*

## Overview

Saona Island (Isla Saona) lies at the southeastern tip of the Dominican Republic within the Cotubanamá National Park (formerly Del Este National Park). The island is surrounded by fringing reefs, seagrass beds, and sandy lagoons. Dive sites around Saona Island sit at 6–12 metres over flat to gently sloping terrain. Visibility reaches 20–30 metres and water temperatures are 27–29°C year-round. Saona Island is identified by Pro Dive International as the most important sea turtle breeding ground in the Dominican Republic. The eastern side of the island is exposed to stronger Atlantic currents, which bring in pelagic life; the western, leeward side features protected shallower reefs.

Day trips to Saona Island by speedboat from Bayahibe take approximately 45 minutes. The island sees heavy snorkel and tourist traffic by midday — morning dive departures offer quieter conditions.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Isla Saona, Cotubanamá National Park, southeastern Dominican Republic
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Coral reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 12 metres
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–30 metres (65–100 feet)
- **Current**: Minimal on the western leeward side; stronger on the eastern side
- **Best Time**: Year-round; morning departures avoid peak tourist traffic

## Marine Life

Sources confirm the following marine life at and around Saona Island: southern stingrays (congregating in sandy lagoons in significant numbers), hawksbill and green sea turtles (feeding on sponges and seagrass — the island is the country's most important turtle breeding area), eagle rays, queen conch (protected within the national park), large groupers, parrotfish, French grunts, Nassau groupers, spotted moray eels, spiny lobsters, nurse sharks, puffer fish, trunkfish, angelfish, and barracuda. The diverse reef structure supports populations across multiple depth zones.

## Dive Profile

Saona Island dives are shallow and relaxed. Descend to 5–8 metres over the reef crest and explore at a slow pace. The seagrass lagoon side of the island at 3–6 metres is noted as productive for turtle observations. No safety stop is required given the maximum depth, but spending the final minutes in the shallows over the reef top extends the dive. Bottom times are long — expect 60–80 minute dives.

## Entry and Exit

Day trips depart from Bayahibe (approximately 45 minutes by speedboat) or La Romana. Dive and snorkel combined trips are common. Conditions are reliably calm given the island's leeward position on most dive sites. Entry via boat ladder or giant stride.

## Tips

- Arrive early — Saona Island sees heavy snorkel and speedboat tourism by midday; morning dives have better conditions and less competition for space with wildlife
- The seagrass lagoons on the south/west side of the island are the best areas for turtle and stingray encounters
- Multiple dive sites exist around the island — ask your operator which section is least trafficked that day
- The national park status affords some protection to the marine life — respect the no-take rules for conch and lobster

## Safety

The main hazard at Saona is boat traffic from the high volume of tourist speedboats visiting the island on day trips. Dive boats time dives to avoid the busiest snorkel traffic. Stay close to the reef and avoid surfacing in open areas without an SMB. Fire coral is present on the reef.

## Photography

Crystal-clear, shallow water with abundant sunlight creates ideal wide-angle conditions. Large groups of stingrays in the sandy lagoons are a distinctive subject. Sea turtle portraits in shallow, bright water photograph well in natural light. The dense reef fish population provides easy subjects for wide-angle reef portraits.
