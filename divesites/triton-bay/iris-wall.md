## Iris Wall

Iris Wall is a deep, current-swept wall dive along the Iris Strait, where the outer waters of Triton Bay meet the open Ceram Sea — a site characterized by towering black coral forests, large pelagic encounters, and some of the strongest nutrient-driven fish aggregations in the region.

## Overview

Named for the Iris Strait that separates the southern islands of Triton Bay from the open ocean, Iris Wall sits on the exposed outer edge of the bay system in West Papua, Indonesia. This geography gives the site a distinctly different character from the sheltered inner-bay reefs: stronger and less predictable currents, reduced visibility from oceanic upwelling, and the persistent possibility of large pelagic animals sweeping through from open water. The wall itself drops from a reef crest at 5 to 8 meters, falling sharply to beyond 35 meters — and significantly deeper in places, with the wall appearing to continue into the abyss. From 15 meters downward, large black coral trees (Antipatharia) become a defining feature, their dark branches spreading meters wide and hosting cleaning stations visited by a parade of reef fish. This is a site for experienced divers who are comfortable managing depth, current, and open-water conditions simultaneously.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Triton Bay, West Papua, Indonesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 35 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 8-20 meters (can drop with oceanic upwelling)
- **Current**: Strong; direction variable with tidal state and seasonal flow
- **Water Temperature**: 26-30°C (thermoclines possible at depth)
- **Best Time**: October to April; morning dives typically calmer

## Marine Life

The black coral forests below 15 meters are the signature feature — each tree supports a micro-ecosystem of pygmy seahorses, longnose hawkfish perched on branch tips, crinoids, and tiny crustaceans. Large barrel sponges anchor to the wall from 20 meters onward, some of them wide enough to hide a diver inside. Schooling dogtooth tuna and large wahoo patrol the open water at mid-depth. Whitetip reef sharks are common residents resting on ledges at 25 to 30 meters. The strong current periodically sweeps eagle rays and occasionally manta rays along the wall face. The shallower reef crest above the wall supports dense soft corals and a full complement of Indo-Pacific reef fish including Napoleon wrasse, bumphead parrotfish, and dense anthias clouds.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the reef crest at 5 to 8 meters and immediately assess current direction and strength before committing to the wall. In moderate current, descend along the wall face to 20 to 25 meters where the black coral trees begin to dominate, then work slowly deeper toward 30 to 35 meters, monitoring bottom time carefully. At this depth, no-decompression limits narrow quickly — plan to spend no more than 8 to 10 minutes below 30 meters before beginning a staged ascent. Ascend along the wall, pausing at key depths to observe cleaning stations and barrel sponge residents before a 5-minute stop at 5 meters. In strong current, treat this as a drift dive and ascend with an SMB when bottom time is exhausted.

## Entry and Exit

Access by liveaboard tender or day-boat. In any significant current, the boat will maintain a surface watch and track divers' positions throughout the dive. Carry and deploy an SMB before ascending from depth — the open-water nature of this site means you could surface well away from the vessel. Night diving at Iris Wall is not recommended given the current exposure and depth.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Begin the dive with a full tank — gas management at 30 to 35 meters is unforgiving
- Check for thermoclines below 25 meters and be prepared for a temperature drop of several degrees
- Look carefully at the tips of black coral branches for pygmy seahorses and longnose hawkfish
- Scan the blue water throughout the dive — pelagics can appear at any depth on this site
- Pair Iris Wall with a shallower, calmer site in the afternoon to balance the intensity of the experience
- Nitrox is highly recommended if your liveaboard operator supplies it — it significantly improves no-decompression margins at this depth

## Safety Considerations

Iris Wall is one of Triton Bay's most demanding sites. The combination of a 35-meter maximum depth, strong variable currents, and an exposed outer-bay location requires diver experience, good gas management, and full situational awareness. Always plan the dive conservatively — target 25 to 28 meters for the bulk of the dive and treat 35 meters as an absolute maximum. Carry both a primary and backup SMB. Be prepared for reduced visibility from upwelling and the possibility of sudden current changes. Divers with fewer than 50 logged dives and limited current experience should consider this site beyond their current level.

## Photography

The black coral forests of Iris Wall are among the most photogenic features in Triton Bay. Wide-angle shots at 20 to 25 meters capture the scale of the trees against the blue-black water of the wall below. The density of the coral branches creates natural framing for fish portraits. At 30 meters and deeper, light levels drop significantly — bring powerful strobes and shoot RAW for post-processing latitude. Wide-angle reef scenes work best in the 0 to 15 meter range where ambient light is sufficient. A fisheye lens shooting upward from beneath a black coral tree, with reef fish silhouetted against the surface light above, produces some of the most dramatic compositions available anywhere in West Papua.
