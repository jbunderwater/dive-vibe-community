# Zero plane

A WWII Japanese aircraft resting in 3-10 meters of water — locally called the "Zero plane," though its actual type (Zero fighter or Betty bomber) is disputed

## Overview
The "Zero plane" wreck is a World War II-era Japanese military aircraft lying in the shallows off Yap. The site takes its common name from the famous Mitsubishi A6M Zero fighter, but the aircraft's actual identity is uncertain: Manta Ray Bay Resort's own published account notes it "was said to be a Japanese Zero, however...a two-seater...claimed it's a Betty," referring to the Mitsubishi G4M twin-engine bomber. The Zero was a single-seat fighter; the Betty was a two-seat (and crew) bomber. No definitive independent identification has been confirmed in publicly available sources. What is confirmed is that the aircraft is genuine WWII wreckage with medium-caliber bullet holes visible in the wings and instrumentation still in the cockpit. The body, wings, tail section, and landing gear are largely present though "rearranged" by decades of wave action and coral growth. The tail section reaches the deepest point at approximately 10 meters. The extremely shallow depth makes this accessible to snorkelers as well as divers.

## Site Information
- **Location**: Yap, Federated States of Micronesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 10 meters (body at ~3m, tail at ~10m)
- **Typical Visibility**: Variable (shallow lagoon water)
- **Current**: Light
- **Best Time**: Year-round; morning for best light penetration

## Marine Life
The wreck is described as "encrusted with life" with several types of coral formations growing over and around it. The bullet-scarred wings and fuselage sections have become artificial reef structure. Specific species attributable to this site from sources are limited — the site is primarily visited for its historical interest rather than marine life diversity. General shallow reef fauna of Yap (anemonefish, damselfish, small reef fish) can be expected on the coral growth.

## Dive Profile
Descend to the aircraft at 3-5 meters where the main fuselage and wings rest. The tail section is at the deepest point, around 10 meters. The whole aircraft is present ("body, both wings, tail section, and landing gear") though rearranged by wave action and coral growth. Take time to identify aircraft components beneath the coral and look for the bullet holes in the wings and instruments in the cockpit. The entire site can be explored in a single dive with extended bottom times at these depths. The wreck is visible from the surface in good conditions, making it workable as a snorkel.

## Entry and Exit
Boat access from Colonia, short ride. Gentle entry into calm, shallow water directly over the wreck site. The boat anchors or moors nearby. Exit is straightforward -- surface and swim to the boat.

## Tips and Recommendations
- Approach this site with historical awareness and respect — it is WWII wreckage and a piece of Pacific War history
- Do not touch, remove, or disturb any wreck components, which are protected by Micronesian law
- Morning light provides the best illumination of the wreck details in the shallow water
- Bring a dive torch to peer into shaded areas of the wreckage
- This pairs well as a final dive of the day or an easy afternoon dive after deeper morning dives
- Note the medium-caliber bullet holes in the wings — evidence of the aircraft's combat history

## Safety Considerations
The extreme shallow depth and calm conditions make this one of Yap's safest dive sites. The primary hazard is the wreck structure itself -- corroded metal can have sharp edges, so maintain neutral buoyancy and avoid contact. Lionfish shelter in the wreckage and should not be approached closely. Be mindful of fire coral that has colonized parts of the structure. No penetration is possible or appropriate given the fragile, corroded state of the aircraft.

## Photography
The shallow depth means abundant natural light, making this an excellent natural-light photography site. Wide-angle lenses capture the overall wreck scene with clear blue water above. Shoot low and wide to get the full wreck profile with the surface visible above. Close-up shots of coral growth on identifiable aircraft components -- propeller, cockpit frame, landing gear -- tell a powerful story. Macro subjects include the anemonefish and lionfish that have colonized the wreckage. Overunder (split) shots are possible in calm conditions, showing the wreck beneath and Yap's coast above. The historical context makes every image from this site inherently compelling.
