# Land's End

A wild, current-swept point where reef systems converge and big pelagics gather -- grey reef sharks, eagle rays, and tuna in unpredictable open-ocean conditions

## Overview
Land's End marks the outermost point of Yap's reef system, where the reef terminates and open ocean begins. This is where currents from multiple directions converge, creating upwellings that concentrate plankton and attract large pelagic species. The diving here is exhilarating and unpredictable -- grey reef sharks gather in numbers, eagle rays pass in formation, and yellowfin tuna blast through in pursuit of baitfish. The exposed location means conditions can change rapidly, with currents strengthening or shifting direction mid-dive. This is Yap's most adventurous dive site, reserved for experienced divers comfortable in challenging open-water conditions.

## Site Information
- **Location**: Yap, Federated States of Micronesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 32 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20-40 meters (65-130 feet)
- **Current**: Strong and unpredictable
- **Best Time**: Year-round; best on incoming tides

## Marine Life
Grey reef sharks are the star attraction, often congregating in groups of ten to twenty or more at the point where currents converge. Eagle rays cruise past regularly, sometimes in groups of five or six. Yellowfin and dogtooth tuna hunt along the reef edge. Large Napoleon wrasse are resident. Barracuda form large schools in the current. Whitetip reef sharks rest in crevices below the current line. On the wall itself, healthy gorgonians and soft corals provide habitat, though the big draw here is the open-water pelagic action rather than the reef structure. Occasional hammerhead sharks have been reported, though sightings are not reliable.

## Dive Profile
This is a current-dependent dive that requires flexibility. The guide will assess conditions and choose the entry point accordingly. Typically, divers descend quickly to the reef at 15-20 meters and work toward the point, using the reef as shelter from the main current. At the point, hook in at 20-25 meters and watch the pelagic show in the blue water. The wall drops beyond 32 meters, but the action is concentrated at the current convergence zone. Ascent is away from the point into calmer water. Air consumption tends to be high due to the physical demands of the current.

## Entry and Exit
Boat access from Colonia, longer ride to this remote outer reef location. Negative entry is standard due to current. The boat captain monitors conditions and may abort the dive if currents are too strong. Pickup is coordinated via SMB in calmer water away from the point.

## Tips and Recommendations
- A reef hook is essential at this site -- you will need to anchor yourself at the point to enjoy the pelagic action
- Check your air frequently as current diving increases consumption significantly
- Stay close to your guide who will read the current and choose the best approach to the point
- Carry a loud surface signaling device in addition to your SMB in case of separation from the boat
- This is best as a first dive of the day when you have maximum energy and no-decompression time

## Safety Considerations
Land's End is Yap's most demanding dive site. Currents are strong, unpredictable, and can change direction during the dive. Downcurrents at the reef point are a real hazard -- if caught, swim horizontally away from the wall rather than fighting upward. Separation from the group is the primary risk in strong current. Carry a safety sausage, whistle, and mirror. The boat captain will abort the dive if surface conditions indicate unsafe currents. Do not override the captain's judgment. Only dive this site with an experienced local guide.

## Photography
Wide-angle is the only viable option. The subject matter -- sharks, rays, tuna -- demands fast shutter speeds (1/250 or higher) and the ability to shoot quickly as animals pass. Stabilizing in current is the main challenge, so a reef hook frees both hands for camera work. Ambient light exposures with the deep blue water background produce dramatic images. Strobes are useful for filling in color on passing marine life but keep recycle time short for rapid-fire shooting. Some of Yap's most dramatic shark images come from Land's End on high-current days.
