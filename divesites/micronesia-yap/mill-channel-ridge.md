# Mill Channel: Ridge

The Tzimoulis Ridge — a 9-meter coral formation spanning Mi'l Channel that concentrates tidal current and attracts some of Yap's largest manta aggregations

## Overview
The Ridge in Mi'l Channel — known formally as Tzimoulis Ridge — is a coral formation approximately 9 meters (30 feet) deep that runs across the channel, intensifying the tidal current and creating ideal conditions for manta ray cleaning. As many as thirty mantas have been sighted simultaneously from this ridge, one of the highest confirmed aggregation counts in Yap. Rather than a single station, the ridge functions as a broad cleaning zone where mantas hover and are serviced by small cleaner wrasses. The current sweeps clear ocean water through the channel on incoming tides, providing excellent visibility at this shallow working depth. The dive is primarily a stationary observation rather than a true drift, with divers positioning at the ridge edge.

## Site Information
- **Location**: Mi'l Channel (Tzimoulis Ridge), Yap, Federated States of Micronesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Drift dive / cleaning station
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 9 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 9-27 meters (30-90 feet; variable with tidal conditions)
- **Current**: 0-8 km/h; intensifies at the ridge
- **Best Time**: Year-round; incoming tides for best manta sightings and visibility

## Marine Life
Manta rays are the primary draw, with up to thirty individuals confirmed at this site simultaneously — one of the highest single-site manta counts reported in Yap. On the ridge itself, jacks, black snappers, and small reef fish that serve as cleaner species are abundant. Whitetip reef sharks and grey reef sharks patrol the channel edges. The site is primarily visited for the manta aggregations; the ridge coral itself provides context rather than being the main attraction.

## Dive Profile
Descend to the ridge at approximately 9 meters and position yourself at the ridge edge as directed by your guide. The key technique is to stay low near the edges of the ridge to avoid blocking the mantas — if divers crowd the center of the ridge, the mantas retreat. The shallow depth allows extended observation time. Visibility varies with tidal state; the best conditions come at the top of the incoming tide when clear ocean water has flushed through the channel. Total dive time can exceed 60 minutes at this depth.

## Entry and Exit
Boat drop upcurrent with a negative entry to reach the ridge quickly. The boat follows your bubbles during the drift. Deploy your SMB well before the end of the drift to allow the boat captain to position for pickup. Exit in calmer water at the channel mouth.

## Tips and Recommendations
- Carry a reef hook to anchor yourself if you want to pause at a good vantage point along the ridge
- Keep your eyes on the blue water off the ridge edge for passing mantas and pelagics
- Stay streamlined to reduce drag in the current and conserve air
- This pairs well with a cleaning station dive on the same trip for a complete Mill Channel experience
- Bring a current-rated SMB and reel with at least 20 meters of line

## Safety Considerations
Currents can be strong and increase rapidly with tidal changes. Drift diving experience is essential. Stay with your guide and group, as separation in current is difficult to recover from. Do not fight the current -- if swept off the ridge, ascend safely and deploy your SMB. Be aware that downcurrents are possible along the ridge wall face.

## Photography
The drift provides opportunities for action shots of mantas and sharks in open water. Wide-angle is the only sensible choice. The challenge is stabilizing yourself in current for sharp images. Higher shutter speeds (1/200 or faster) help freeze motion. Video shooters will appreciate the cinematic quality of drifting alongside marine life. The ridge corals in the foreground with blue-water backgrounds make for compelling compositions.
