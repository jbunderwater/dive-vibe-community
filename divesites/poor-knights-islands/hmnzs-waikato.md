# HMNZS Waikato

New Zealand's first Leander-class frigate, 113.4 metres long, scuttled off Ngunguru Bay near Tutukaka on 25 November 2000. Now resting on her port side in two sections with the shallowest point at 12-14 metres and the deepest at approximately 30-32 metres. Large purpose-cut access openings make this a popular wreck penetration training site.

> **Note on location**: The HMNZS Waikato lies off Ngunguru Bay near Tutukaka (actual coordinates approximately -35.645, 174.550), not at the Poor Knights Islands (23 km offshore). It is commonly combined with Poor Knights trips by Tutukaka-based operators but is a separate dive site.

## Overview

HMNZS Waikato was built by Harland and Wolff in Belfast, Ireland, launched on 18 February 1966, and commissioned into the Royal New Zealand Navy on 5 September 1966. At 113.4 metres long, 12.5 metres beam, and 5.5 metres draft, she was the first Leander-class frigate built specifically for the RNZN.

After decommissioning, the Waikato was prepared as a dive attraction with large openings cut into the hull for diver access to interior spaces including control rooms, engine compartments, and helicopter hangars. She was deliberately sunk on 25 November 2000 off Ngunguru Bay in a sinking time reported as 2 minutes 40 seconds.

The wreck now lies at approximately 45 degrees to the sand, broken into two sections. The shallowest part of the wreck is at 12-14 metres, with the sand at 28-32 metres, making it accessible across a range of depths.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Off Ngunguru Bay, Tutukaka Coast, Northland, New Zealand (not at Poor Knights Islands)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters (deepest sections reach 32m)
- **Minimum Depth**: 12 meters (shallowest wreck surface)
- **Typical Visibility**: 8-30 meters
- **Water Temperature**: 17-22 degrees C
- **Current**: Variable; exposed location
- **Best Time**: September to February

## Marine Life

The wreck structure attracts schooling fish that shelter within and around the hull. Kelp growth on the exposed starboard surfaces adds to the reef-like character of the shallower sections.

## Dive Profile

Descend the mooring line to the wreck. The starboard side is at 12-15 metres, providing an accessible starting point. Explore along the hull, noting the break between bow and stern sections where internal deck levels are exposed. The 4.5-inch gun emplacements at the bow, shell storage areas, and the propeller at the stern are key features. Large openings purpose-cut into the hull allow penetration for qualified wreck divers. The ship's 45-degree tilt can be disorienting, particularly combined with kelp movement from swell.

## Entry and Exit

Access is by dive boat from Tutukaka marina. The site is more exposed than the HMNZS Tui, so surface conditions can be rougher. Entry by giant stride. The mooring line provides a reliable descent and ascent reference.

## Safety Considerations

Advanced Open Water certification is recommended. The exposed location means sea conditions and visibility can change; follow the operator's assessment of conditions. The wreck's tilt combined with kelp movement from swell can cause disorientation — maintain visual reference to fixed points. Internal penetration requires wreck diving certification. Loose metal edges exist where the hull has separated; maintain good buoyancy throughout. Adequate thermal protection is essential.
