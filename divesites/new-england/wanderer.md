# Wanderer

The last square-rigged whaleship to depart New Bedford, lost to a gale on the Middle Ground shoal near Cuttyhunk Island in August 1924.

## Overview

The *Wanderer* was a 116-foot wooden whaling bark (27.5-foot beam, 16-foot draft), launched in 1878 from a Mattapoisett, Massachusetts shipyard — the last whaleship built in that town. She made 23 whaling voyages over a 46-year career. By 1924, she was owned by Captain Antone Edwards, an Azorean immigrant who had crewed on New Bedford whalers since 1898.

On August 25, 1924, a steam tug towed the *Wanderer* into Buzzards Bay to begin what would be her final voyage. On August 26, 1924, newspapers reported 60-mph winds and 15-foot waves within Buzzards Bay. The gale snapped one of the Wanderer's two anchor chains and dragged the vessel roughly seven miles across the bay until she struck the Middle Ground shoal near Cuttyhunk Island. The crew of 15 abandoned ship in two whaleboats: seven reached Cuttyhunk safely, and eight others disappeared overnight before being rescued the following morning by the lightship stationed near Sow and Pigs Ledge. No lives were lost.

Salvagers removed provisions, whaling gear, sails, and the ship's golden eagle figurehead (now at the New Bedford Whaling Museum) before a second storm completed the hull's destruction. The *Wanderer* is widely regarded as the symbolic end of New Bedford's whaling era.

**Depth note**: Multiple sources conflict on the claim of "25 metres" for this dive site. Wreckhunter.net lists the maximum depth at the wreck site as only 20 feet (~6 metres). The vessel grounded in shallow shoal water and is completely broken up. The prior 25m depth figure in our data has been corrected to ~6m. Any diver planning a visit should verify current conditions with local operators.

## Dive Profile

The wooden hull has completely broken up over the course of 100 years, with small artifacts remaining wedged between rocks. The dive is shallow (maximum ~20 feet) and, per Wreckhunter.net, considered an easy dive technically — though Buzzards Bay conditions and current must be respected. Local operators should be consulted for current wreck state and navigation.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access required. New Bedford-area or Cuttyhunk-area operators. Local knowledge of Buzzards Bay currents and Elizabeth Islands shoals is important. The wreck lies on the west side of Cuttyhunk Island (coordinates per Wreckhunter: 41-25-18N / 70-56-36W).

## Tips and Recommendations

- Read about the *Wanderer* and New Bedford's whaling history before diving — the context transforms the site
- The wooden structure is entirely broken up — what remains are scattered timbers and artifacts in the rocks
- Buzzards Bay currents can be tricky — plan around favorable tidal conditions
- Verify current site conditions with a local dive operator before visiting

## Safety Considerations

Shallow site, but Buzzards Bay tidal currents are variable and can be strong near island passages. Cold water year-round below any thermocline — 7mm wetsuit recommended. Deploy SMB before surfacing.
