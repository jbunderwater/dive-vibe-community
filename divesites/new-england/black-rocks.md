# Black Rocks

Dark metamorphic outcrops off the Plum Island/Newburyport coast, one of the signature shallow-water reef dive clusters in northern Massachusetts.

## Overview

Black Rocks is a group of dark metamorphic rock outcrops in the shallow nearshore waters off the northern Massachusetts coast, in the area between Plum Island and the Merrimack River mouth near Newburyport. The site's name reflects the dark appearance of the local bedrock — typical of the Precambrian and Paleozoic formations that underlie much of coastal Essex County. At 10 metres maximum depth, this is an archetypal northern Massachusetts shallow reef dive: dense kelp fronds waving over dark angular rock, lobsters tucked into crevices, and tautog holding position on the harder edges. The site sits in close proximity to Badgers Rock, Lunts Rock, and Half Tide Rocks — a cluster of named reef sites that Cape Ann-area dive operators collectively refer to as productive lobster ground.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Off Plum Island/Newburyport coast, northern Massachusetts
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 10 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 3–8 meters (10–26 feet)
- **Current**: Moderate tidal current; influenced by Merrimack River outflow
- **Water Temperature**: 5–20°C depending on season
- **Best Time**: June to October

## Marine Life

The dark rock substrate at Black Rocks is particularly productive for American lobsters, which find the angular crevices between boulders ideal for denning. Tautog hold territory on the exposed rock faces. Cunner hover in the kelp at mid-depth. Encrusting organisms including anemones, sponges, and bryozoans cover shaded surfaces. Sea urchins are abundant on rock faces. Atlantic mackerel and pollock patrol above the reef in summer. Winter flounder rest on the surrounding sand.

## Dive Profile

Access via boat from Newburyport, Plum Island, or Cape Ann. Descend to the rocky bottom at 8–10 metres and navigate the perimeter of the rock cluster. Work from the shallowest tops down to the base where the rocks meet the sand — the lobsters concentrate at the sand-rock interface. The 10-metre maximum allows generous bottom time. Plan around slack tide for the Merrimack River tidal outflow. Complete a 3-minute safety stop at 5 metres.

## Entry and Exit

Boat charter required. The shoal-studded northern Massachusetts nearshore requires a captain familiar with local navigation hazards. Anchor in sand adjacent to the rocks. Deploy SMB before ascending.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The sand-rock interface at the base of the boulders is prime lobster territory — inspect carefully
- Combine with Badgers Rock and Lunts Rock for a productive multi-site northern Massachusetts day
- Kelp growth peaks in late spring; by fall the kelp is denser and the bottom darker
- Merrimack River outflow can affect visibility after heavy rain — check conditions before departure
- Drysuit or 7mm wetsuit essential throughout the year

## Safety Considerations

Cold water year-round — minimum 7mm wetsuit or drysuit. The Merrimack River tidal influence can create directional current near the river mouth. Rocky bottom creates boat navigation hazards. Deploy SMB before surfacing.

## Photography

The dark rock and dense kelp create excellent contrast for wide-angle reef photography. American lobster portraits in their boulder crevices are a classic northern Massachusetts subject. Natural light is strong at 10 metres on clear summer days.
