# Brenton Reef

The historic LV-39 lightship wreck off Newport, Rhode Island — a deep cold-water dive at 55 metres requiring technical certification.

## Overview

The LV-39 was a 119-foot 6-inch (36.4-metre) wooden lightship of 387 gross tons, built approximately 1875. Her confirmed station assignments were: Vineyard Sound MA (1875–1876), Five Fathom Bank NJ (1876–1877), Relief MA (1877–1897), Relief NY (1897), and Brenton Reef RI (1897–1935) — a total career of approximately 60 years. In August 1905 she was struck by the battleship USS Iowa, which carried away part of her stem and damaged head stays; no lives were lost. After retirement in 1935 her post-service history is not fully documented in surviving records, and sources conflict on whether she served as a restaurant, a clubhouse vessel, or some other purpose. She sank in 1975 while under tow to a shipyard in Beverly, Massachusetts. The wreck was first dived on July 5, 2004 by Bob Foster, David Caldwell, and Heather Knowles. She rests upright with a port list at 55 metres (180 feet), providing approximately 6–7 metres (20–25 feet) of relief. This is a deep technical dive requiring trimix or advanced technical certification and extensive cold-water wreck diving experience.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Brenton Reef, off Brenton Point, Newport, Rhode Island
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 55 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 5–15 meters (variable)
- **Current**: Variable; can be strong

- **Best Time**: June to September
- **Certification Required**: Advanced/technical diving with trimix experience

The wreck structure itself — hull, deck, and penetrable lower decks — is the primary draw rather than biological diversity.

## Dive Profile

This dive exceeds recreational limits significantly. Technical divers descend to the wreck at 55 metres. Interior penetration is possible for divers with proper technical training. The wreck provides approximately 6 metres of vertical relief, with the deck accessible at 50 metres and the hull keel at 55 metres. Multiple decompression stops are required: significant stops between 30 and 9 metres, with extended stops at 6 and 3 metres for 30–60+ minutes total decompression time. Total dive time including decompression: 90–150 minutes depending on bottom time and gas plan. Rebreather divers have more bottom time flexibility.

## Entry and Exit

Technical charter required — Northern Atlantic Dive Expeditions (NADE) specializes in New England technical wreck diving. Controlled descent on a drop line to the wreck. Navigate carefully in reduced visibility and cold water. Return to ascent line for multi-stage decompression. Carry all required decompression gas on the diver, not on the boat.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Prerequisites: Advanced trimix or closed-circuit rebreather certification with 100+ technical dives minimum
- Drysuit and advanced thermal protection are mandatory — water at depth is near 7°C year-round
- Multiple independent gas sources and a comprehensive decompression plan are essential
- Work with NADE or a comparable technical charter that knows Rhode Island deep wreck operations
- The historical significance of the LV-39 is remarkable — do background reading before the dive

## Safety Considerations

This site carries significant risk for improperly trained divers. Depth significantly exceeds recreational limits. Cold water impairs judgment and motor function. Strong currents can sweep decompression divers off their stops. Only divers with demonstrated technical competency and current wreck diving experience should attempt this dive. Never dive beyond training certification limits.

## Photography

Technical wreck photography at 55 metres requires advanced skills and technical lighting setups. The lightship's hull and deck structures are compelling documentary subjects. Video systems with wide-angle lenses and dual video lights produce the best results in the limited ambient light.
