# SS Pendleton

The stern section of a WWII T2 tanker that broke apart in a famous 1952 nor'easter off Chatham, now resting in approximately 12 metres off Monomoy Island.

## Overview

The SS Pendleton was a Type T2-SE-A1 oil tanker measuring 504 feet in length (10,448 GRT), built in 1942. She was carrying approximately 120,000 barrels of heating oil (sources report between 120,000 and 122,000 barrels) when she broke in two during a violent nor'easter on February 18, 1952, approximately six miles off the coast of Chatham, Cape Cod. The captain and seven crewmen were in the bow section; 33 crewmen were in the stern.

The rescue that followed is one of the most celebrated in U.S. Coast Guard history. Four Coast Guardsmen — Coxswain Bernard Webber, Ervin Maske, Andrew Fitzgerald, and Richard P. Livesey — rode out in CG-36500, a 36-foot wooden motor lifeboat designed to carry 12 people, to reach the stern section. They rescued 32 of the 33 stern crewmen; one man, cook George C. Meyers, was crushed to death between the rescue boat and the Pendleton hull during the rescue. Eight crewmen from the bow section also perished. The four Coast Guardsmen each received the Gold Lifesaving Medal. The 2016 Disney film *The Finest Hours* dramatised the rescue.

The stern section grounded off Monomoy Island near Pollock Rip Shoal and now rests in approximately 40 feet (~12 metres) of water. The site is managed by the Massachusetts Board of Underwater Archaeological Resources as an Exempted Site. Strong tidal currents, cold water, and limited visibility make this a challenging dive despite the relatively shallow depth.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Monomoy Island, Chatham, Cape Cod, Massachusetts
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 12 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 2–5 metres (5–15 feet)
- **Current**: Very strong — slack tide mandatory
- **Water Temperature**: 10–15°C (50–59°F) summer; colder off-season
- **Best Time**: June to September (slack tide windows essential)

## Marine Life

No site-specific marine life reports found. Cold-water New England species typical of Pollock Rip Shoal and Monomoy Island apply.

## Dive Profile

Access is via charter boat from Hyannis or Chatham-area operators. Dives are conducted on the narrow slack tide window — typically 1–2 hours around the tide change. Descent via a reference line to the hull debris in 9–12 metres. Monitor current constantly — outside slack windows, current can exceed 2–3 knots making navigation impossible. Bottom time 25–35 minutes depending on air consumption. Ascend on the reference line with a 3-minute safety stop at 5 metres.

## Entry and Exit

Charter boat required. Northern Atlantic Dive Expeditions and Cape Ann-area operators run trips to Monomoy-area wrecks. Descent on reference line. Current recovery is essential — always stay close to the line. Be prepared to abort if conditions deteriorate between dive and surface.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Dive only on slack tide — conditions outside the window are dangerous even for experienced divers
- A 7mm wetsuit is the minimum; a drysuit is preferable in all but peak summer conditions
- Bring a dive light for examining hull structures in the low visibility
- The historical context of this wreck makes it worth reading *The Finest Hours* before diving
- Pollock Rip Shoal area has several other Cape Cod wrecks nearby for a multi-site day

## Safety Considerations

This is a challenging site despite the shallow depth. Tidal currents at Pollock Rip are among the strongest on the Eastern seaboard — never dive outside the slack window. Cold water accelerates air consumption; plan conservatively. Carry SMB and be prepared to surface away from the boat if current develops. Always dive with an experienced buddy familiar with Cape Cod tidal conditions.
