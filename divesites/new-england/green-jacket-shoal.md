# Green Jacket Shoal

Rhode Island's largest ship graveyard — a 33-acre shoal in the Providence River holding the remains of 29 vessels abandoned over more than a century of industrial maritime history.

## Overview

Green Jacket Shoal is a 33-acre shoal in the Providence River between Providence and East Providence, Rhode Island, located east of Fox Point approximately 7.4 miles above the river mouth. Marine archaeologist David Robinson identified 29 separate vessels in the graveyard, including 2 sidewheel steamships, 17 scow barges, 4 sailing ships, 3 harbor steamers, and a schooner barge. Notable wrecks include the *Bay Queen*, a 182-foot sidewheeler that operated 1865–1902, and the *Mount Hope*, a 193-foot sidewheel passenger ferry. The Providence Dry Dock and Marine Railway Company operated at nearby Bold Point, East Providence, from 1884 to 1919, using Green Jacket Shoal as a vessel parking and disposal ground during that era. The shoal was designated a Marine Protected Area in 2018 in recognition of its archaeological and cultural heritage significance.

Guided boat tours of the shoal are offered by the Providence River Boat Company, led by marine archaeologist David Robinson, making this one of the few New England wreck sites that can be appreciated from the surface. The shallow, low-visibility Providence River environment makes conventional recreational scuba diving here limited.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Providence River, between Providence and East Providence, Rhode Island
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 5 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 1–4 meters (upper Providence River; turbid from river sediment)
- **Current**: Moderate upper-bay tidal current
- **Water Temperature**: 8–22°C depending on season
- **Best Time**: Late summer for lowest turbidity; always check Providence River visibility

## Marine Life

Upper Providence River species. Striped bass are the dominant gamefish in the Providence River area in warmer months. Bluefish pass through seasonally. American eels inhabit the wreck structures. Cunner, tautog, and sea bass use the hard structure as habitat. Winter flounder on surrounding soft bottom. The wood-and-iron wreck structures support encrusting organisms including mussels, barnacles, and hydroids.

## Dive Profile

The wrecks of Green Jacket Shoal lie in generally shallow water throughout the 33-acre site. Providence River visibility is characteristically low (1–4 metres) due to tidal turbidity and freshwater input. Divers should use a guideline and maintain close buddy contact. Boat access from Providence River launch facilities. Plan around slack tide. Surface support should be aware of boat traffic in the Providence navigation channel.

## Entry and Exit

Private boat or charter required. Providence area boat launches provide access. The Marine Protected Area designation (2018) applies — do not disturb any archaeological material. Deploy SMB before surfacing.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Consider the Providence River Boat Company's guided surface tours as an introduction to the site's history — their marine archaeologist guide (David Robinson) provides context unavailable elsewhere
- Low visibility is endemic to this site — excellent buoyancy control and a guideline are essential
- The Marine Protected Area designation means all wreck material must be left in place
- 7mm wetsuit or drysuit recommended throughout the season
- Do not attempt to penetrate wreck structures — the wood-hulled vessels are in varying states of deterioration

## Safety Considerations

Low visibility in the Providence River requires close buddy proximity and guideline use. Upper bay tidal current at peak flow. Deteriorating wooden wreck structures present entanglement and collapse risk — do not enter interior spaces. Providence River boat traffic from the commercial channel. Deploy SMB before surfacing.

## Photography

Low visibility limits wide-angle work. Close-focus macro documentation of encrusting organisms on the wooden hull structures, and wreck detail shots with strobes, are the practical approach. The historical vessels reward patient, methodical photography.
