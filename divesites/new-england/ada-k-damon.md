# Ada K. Damon

The remains of an 1875-built Grand Banks fishing schooner wrecked in December 1909, now a fragmentary intertidal archaeological site at Crane Beach, Ipswich — not a dive site.

## Overview

The Ada K. Damon was an 84-foot, two-masted Grand Banks fishing schooner built in 1875 by Ebenezer Burnham in Essex, Massachusetts. She was converted to a sand-hauling vessel in 1909 for what would be her only run in that role. On December 26, 1909, during a severe northeast gale (known as the "Great Christmas Snowstorm"), her anchor chain parted and she was driven ashore at Steep Hill Beach on Crane Beach, Ipswich. All five crew members survived. The vessel was declared a total loss and salvage attempts failed. The wreck became buried by shifting barrier beach sands and remained largely hidden for decades.

**Important access note**: This site is NOT a dive site or snorkel site. The Trustees of Reservations, who manage Crane Beach, explicitly prohibit snorkeling and mask use on the beach. The wreck is an archaeological site accessible only on foot at low tide, and the removal of any artifact requires a Massachusetts BUAR permit.

Hurricane Teddy (September 2020) broke the hull apart and scattered timbers over 18 acres of beach. As of 2021 only scattered beams and a cast iron galley stove remained as fixed features. A ~50-foot hull section re-emerged in February 2024 from shifting sands along with the rudder, anchor chain, and galley stove — all at beach level, not submerged.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Crane Beach, Ipswich, Massachusetts (base of Steep Hill)
- **Entry Type**: beach
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 2 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 1–3 meters (tide-dependent; best at dead low)
- **Current**: Mild tidal surge; watch for incoming tide
- **Water Temperature**: 5–22°C depending on season
- **Best Time**: Summer low tides, June to September

## Marine Life

Confirmed on exposed wreck timbers (from 2024 on-site observation): blue mussels, barnacles, crabs, seaweed, and snails colonize the exposed wood. No fish species have been confirmed at this specific site. The site is not submerged and is not accessible to divers or snorkelers.

## Visiting the Site

This is strictly a low-tide walking visit, not a dive or snorkel. Access is through Crane Beach (parking fees apply; advance passes required on weekends). Walk south along the beach toward the base of Steep Hill. The wreck location varies with seasonal sand movement — look for exposed dark timbers at the tide line. Inspection time is limited to roughly 1–2 hours around dead low tide before the returning flood covers the area. Do not attempt to snorkel or use masks — this is explicitly prohibited by the Trustees of Reservations.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Consult a detailed tide chart before visiting — the site is accessible on foot only within about an hour of low tide
- Sand movement is seasonal; the wreck may be deeply buried or newly exposed depending on recent storm activity
- Wear rubber boots or water shoes — the beach substrate near the wreck can be soft and wet
- This is an archaeological site managed by the Trustees of Reservations — do not remove any material; violation is a criminal offense under Massachusetts law
- Admission is required; advance passes required on summer weekends (Trustees of Reservations website)

## Safety Considerations

The primary hazard is the returning tide. Exit the wreck area before the flood tide returns. Snorkeling and mask use are explicitly prohibited at Crane Beach. Do not attempt to wade beyond wading depth — the barrier beach coastline has strong tidal currents as the tide fills.

## Photography

The exposed hull timbers, partially silted and encrusted, make excellent documentary subjects in natural light. Low-angle wide shots at dead low tide with the Crane Estate dunes in the background provide historical context. Close-up shots of the iron fasteners, marine growth, and wooden grain convey the passage of 115 years.
