# Ada K. Damon

The remains of a fishing schooner converted to a sand hauler, lost on December 26, 1909, during the Great Christmas Snowstorm, exposed in the intertidal zone at Steep Hill Beach on Crane Beach, Ipswich.

## Overview

The Ada K. Damon was an 84-foot double-masted fishing schooner, built in 1875 by H.A. Burnham in Essex, Massachusetts, measuring 84 feet in length with a 23.3-foot beam and weighing 94 tons. Originally a Grand Banks fishing vessel, she was converted to a sand-hauling schooner in 1909 by her owner, Captain A.K. Brewster, who had sold his Maine farm to invest in the vessel. On December 26, 1909 — during the severe Great Christmas Snowstorm — on her very first sand-hauling voyage, her anchor chain parted in strong winds and intense seas west of Plum Island. She was driven ashore and grounded on Steep Hill Beach at the southern end of Crane Beach in Ipswich, Massachusetts. All five crew members survived. Unable to free the vessel, Captain Brewster ultimately abandoned her as a total loss.

The wreck has been periodically buried and re-exposed by shifting sands over more than 115 years. Hurricane Teddy in September 2020 broke apart the previously intact hull, scattering timbers along the beach. The Commonwealth of Massachusetts holds title to the wreck under BUAR (Board of Underwater Archaeological Resources), which has designated it a "sentinel site" for monitoring how coastal resources change due to natural processes and climate impacts. The Trustees of Reservations manage the Crane Beach property. This is an archaeological site rather than a conventional scuba wreck — the maximum depth of 2 metres means the site is experienced primarily by wading and snorkeling rather than diving.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Crane Beach, Ipswich, Massachusetts (base of Steep Hill)
- **Entry Type**: beach
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 2 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 1–3 meters (tide-dependent; best at dead low)
- **Current**: Mild tidal surge; watch for incoming tide
- **Water Temperature**: 5–22°C depending on season
- **Best Time**: Summer low tides, June to September

## Marine Life

The shallow intertidal and subtidal zone at Crane Beach supports typical New England estuarine species characteristic of this beach type. No site-specific species surveys of the Ada K. Damon wreck area have been published; the marine life noted below reflects the general Crane Beach intertidal environment. Barnacles and blue mussels colonize any hard substrate. Green crabs, hermit crabs, and periwinkles are present throughout the intertidal zone. The wreck timbers, when exposed, provide hard substrate in an otherwise sandy environment.

## Dive Profile

This is not a conventional scuba dive. Visit at low tide and wade to the exposed wreck remains at the base of Steep Hill. The hull is largely flattened, with wooden planking, ribs, and iron fasteners visible when the sand cooperates. Inspection time is limited to roughly 1–2 hours either side of low tide before the returning flood makes the area unsafe for wading. Bring snorkeling gear if any underwater inspection is desired.

## Entry and Exit

Enter from Crane Beach (parking fee applies at the Crane Estate). Walk south along the beach toward the base of Steep Hill. The wreck location varies slightly depending on seasonal sand movement — look for exposed dark timbers at the tide line. Exit well before the tide returns; the shallow beach floods quickly and the current increases substantially as the tide fills. Crane Beach parking is managed by the Trustees of Reservations.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Consult a detailed tide chart before visiting — the site is only accessible within about an hour of low tide
- Sand movement is seasonal; the wreck may be deeply buried in summer and more exposed in late fall after storm activity
- Wear water shoes or waders — the beach substrate is soft and the water is cold
- This is an archaeological site managed by the Trustees of Reservations — do not remove any material
- Combine with a walk along the Crane Estate dunes for an excellent natural and historical half-day trip
- Early morning low tides on calm days provide the best conditions for photography

## Safety Considerations

The primary hazard is the returning tide — the beach floods rapidly and the current increases quickly as the tide fills. Always know your exit window before wading in. Water remains cold throughout the year; hypothermia is a genuine risk for those who linger too long in the shallows without adequate protection. The beach is exposed to northeast swell — conditions can change with weather.

## Photography

The exposed hull timbers, partially silted and encrusted, make excellent documentary subjects in natural light. Low-angle wide shots at dead low tide with the Crane Estate dunes in the background provide historical context. Close-up shots of the iron fasteners, marine growth, and wooden grain convey the passage of 115 years.
