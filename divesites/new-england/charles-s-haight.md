# Charles S. Haight

A WWII Liberty ship wrecked in 1946 on the Flat Ground inside the Dry Salvages off Rockport — her triple-expansion steam engine remains the primary identifiable feature.

## Overview

The Charles S. Haight was a Liberty ship of type EC2-S-C1, laid down August 15, 1944, launched September 23, 1944, and completed October 3, 1944, by J.A. Jones Construction Co. in Brunswick, Georgia, at a cost of $974,111.

**Dimensions**: Wikipedia lists her as 441 feet 6 inches in length (57-foot beam), matching the standard Liberty ship hull. The Rockport harbormaster and Wreckhunter.net both list 422 feet — this discrepancy between sources has not been resolved. Gross tonnage: 7,176 GRT (Wikipedia) or 7,198 (Rockport source). The vessel had a triple-expansion steam engine producing 2,500 horsepower driving a single screw.

On the evening of April 1, 1946, the Haight was returning in ballast from Newport, England, to New York. With poor visibility, rough seas, and no radar, southeasterly winds drove her inshore. At 12:07 AM on April 2, 1946, she grounded on the Flat Ground inside the Dry Salvages, 1.5 miles east of Rockport. She was declared a Constructive Total Loss the following day and ultimately sold for $500 on December 30, 1947.

**Depth note**: Multiple sources confirm the wreck site maximum depth is approximately 30 feet (~9 metres), with a minimum of about 10 feet — the engine block is visible above water at low tide. The prior depth figure of 21 metres in this site record was significantly overstated and has been corrected.

The most recognizable feature today is the large triple-expansion steam engine. Scattered steel beams and plates across the Flat Ground are marked by Red Nun buoy "2." The ship's binnacle was recovered and now resides at the Sandy Bay Yacht Club. The site is managed by Massachusetts BUAR as an Exempted Site.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Flat Ground (inside Dry Salvages), Rockport, Massachusetts (42° 40' 38" N / 70° 35' 03" W)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 9 meters
- **Engine Block**: Visible above water at low tide
- **Typical Visibility**: ~6 metres (~20 feet)
- **Current**: Moderate to strong tidal current between rock outcrops
- **Water Temperature**: 7–20°C depending on season
- **Best Time**: June to October; spring offers better wreck definition (less algae)

## Marine Life

No site-specific marine life reports found. Cold-water New England species typical of Cape Ann rocky ledge substrate apply. By late summer, algae mat covers much of the steel, making wreck and rock difficult to distinguish.

## Dive Profile

Boat access from Rockport. The engine block is the primary navigation target and provides the most relief on the site. At low tide, portions are visible from the surface. Plan dives around slack tide. Descend to the engine structure and explore surrounding scattered debris. Ascend with 80 bar/1,200 psi in reserve. 3-minute safety stop at 5 metres.

## Entry and Exit

Boat charter required from Rockport or Gloucester. The Flat Ground and Dry Salvages area requires a skilled local captain — submerged rock hazards are significant. Anchor carefully. Red Nun "2" marks the wreck area. Deploy SMB before surfacing.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The engine block is the hero feature — ask your captain to put you directly on it
- Spring and early summer offer better wreck definition before late-summer algae develops
- The binnacle at Sandy Bay Yacht Club is worth seeing before your dive for context
- Combine with nearby California and USS Grouse wrecks for a productive three-site Cape Ann day

## Safety Considerations

Tidal current between the Dry Salvages rock outcrops can be fast and variable. Plan dives strictly around slack tide. Cold water year-round — 7mm wetsuit or drysuit. Deploy SMB clearly. A competent local captain is non-negotiable.
