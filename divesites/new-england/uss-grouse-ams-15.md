# USS Grouse (AMS-15)

A WWII wooden minesweeper that grounded on the Little Salvages off Rockport in September 1963 and was destroyed — her scattered metal remains rest near the California wreck.

## Overview

USS Grouse (AMS-15) was a YMS-1-class minesweeper of the YMS-135 subclass, built with a wooden hull for the U.S. Navy during World War II. She was laid down August 29, 1942, launched February 20, 1943, and commissioned as USS YMS-321 on October 25, 1943, by builder Al Larson Boat Building of Terminal Island, California.

During WWII, YMS-321 conducted sweeping and patrol duties in the Guam-Saipan-Tinian area, served as a convoy escort, and patrolled around Iwo Jima in April 1945 — exploding two floating mines. After the war, the vessel was redesignated AMS-15 on February 18, 1947, and named USS Grouse. She was later reclassified MSC(O)-15 on March 1, 1955. After decommissioning in 1957, she returned to active duty in November 1958 as a Naval Reserve training vessel based at Portsmouth, New Hampshire.

On the night of September 21, 1963, while on a training mission, Grouse struck the Little Salvages — a string of sharp submerged shoals off Rockport, Massachusetts. There were no injuries to the crew. Despite five tow attempts, the Navy was unable to free the grounded ship.

**Regarding her destruction**: Sources conflict slightly on the exact sequence. Wikipedia states she was "destroyed by explosives" on September 28, 1963, and struck from the Naval Vessel Register the same day. Other sources (Shipwrecks and Sea Dogs blog) state she was also covered in gasoline and set ablaze during low tide on September 29, burning until the tide extinguished the fire. The prior markdown stated "fire" only — the Wikipedia primary record specifically says "explosives." It is likely that both methods were used in sequence. Her name was struck from the Naval Vessel Register September 28, 1963.

The metal fittings, machinery, and scattered debris now rest in approximately 20 metres of water near the Dry Salvages area. The site is managed by Massachusetts BUAR as an Exempted Site.

**Location note**: The site is near the Little Salvages and Dry Salvages off Rockport — it is NOT at Graves Ledge (as erroneously noted in the OSM data).

## Site Information

- **Location**: Little Salvages, off Rockport, Massachusetts (near Dry Salvages)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 5–10 metres (15–30 feet)
- **Current**: Moderate; plan around slack tide
- **Water Temperature**: 7–20°C depending on season
- **Best Time**: June to October

## Marine Life

No site-specific marine life reports found confirming species at this particular wreck. Harbor seals from the nearby Dry Salvages colony are documented at the Dry Salvages area generally and may be encountered. Cold-water New England species typical of Cape Ann rocky substrates apply.

## Dive Profile

Access via boat charter from Rockport or Cape Ann area. The wooden hull has entirely deteriorated; metal machinery, fittings, and scattered debris are the identifiable remains. The site lies near the California wreck, making the two a natural pairing on a multi-site charter. Plan slack tide windows carefully. Maximum depth ~20 metres. Ascend with adequate reserve and complete a 3-minute safety stop at 5 metres.

## Entry and Exit

Boat charter required from Cape Ann area operators. The Little Salvages area is navigational hazard territory — a local captain with knowledge of these shoals is essential. Deploy SMB clearly before ascending from depth.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Best combined with the California wreck nearby for a productive two-wreck Cape Ann charter
- The destruction of the Grouse (explosives and fire) left scattered debris rather than defined hull structure — expect a debris field
- Ask your charter captain for current information on wreck condition and currents
- A competent local captain who knows the Salvages is mandatory

## Safety Considerations

Rocky shoal area with significant navigation hazards for boats. Cold water year-round — 7mm wetsuit or drysuit required. Tidal current can be significant — plan dives around slack. Deploy SMB clearly before surfacing.
