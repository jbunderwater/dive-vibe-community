# Avery Ledge

A submerged rocky ledge at the northern edge of the Dry Salvages group off Rockport, Massachusetts, productive for lobster and tautog.

## Overview

Avery Ledge lies immediately west of the Dry Salvages and California wreck site cluster off Rockport, Massachusetts — one of the most productive dive areas on the North Shore of Massachusetts. The ledge system sits in roughly 12–18 metres of water at the northern fringe of the Salvages shoal complex, where the rocky substrate gives way to the sandy approaches of Ipswich Bay. Like the neighboring California and Charles S. Haight sites, Avery Ledge benefits from the cold, nutrient-rich Gulf of Maine water that flows through this area, supporting dense invertebrate life and good populations of tautog and American lobster. Local Cape Ann divers frequently combine Avery Ledge with the nearby California wreck as a two-site charter, taking advantage of the proximity and the complementary diving character of reef and wreck.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Off Rockport, Massachusetts, adjacent to the Dry Salvages
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Rocky reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 18 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 5–12 meters (15–40 feet)
- **Current**: Moderate tidal current; plan around slack
- **Water Temperature**: 7–20°C depending on season
- **Best Time**: June to October

## Marine Life

Avery Ledge supports the characteristic Cape Ann cold-water community. American lobsters inhabit the deeper crevices and rock overhangs throughout the ledge system — the Salvages area is known for exceptional lobstering. Tautog (blackfish) patrol the ledge edges and congregate around the larger rock structures. Cunner hover in loose groups over the encrusted surfaces. Pollock school in midwater above the ledge. Sea urchins carpet the rocky substrate in the 8–15 metre range. Striped bass move through the area in late summer. Nudibranchs and encrusting bryozoans cover shaded surfaces throughout the year.

## Dive Profile

Access via boat charter from Rockport or Gloucester. Descend to the rocky ledge at 10–18 metres and navigate the ledge edge from shallow to deeper sections. The most productive marine life zone is typically in the 10–15 metre range where crevice density is highest and lobsters most numerous. The ledge makes an excellent pairing with the California wreck, which lies only a short distance to the east. Plan slack tide windows carefully using Cape Ann tidal predictions. Ascend with adequate reserve and complete a 3-minute safety stop at 5 metres.

## Entry and Exit

Boat charter required from Cape Ann Divers (Gloucester) or other Rockport-area operators. Local knowledge of the Dry Salvages area is essential — submerged rocks create navigation hazards for boats throughout this zone. Anchor in sand adjacent to the ledge. Deploy SMB before ascending from depth.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Combine with the California wreck for an excellent two-site Cape Ann charter day
- The Salvages area is famous for large lobsters — inspect all dark crevices carefully
- Plan dives around slack tide; Cape Ann tidal currents are significant around the Salvages rocks
- Bring a dive light for crevice investigation
- Cape Ann Divers in Gloucester offers the best local knowledge for this area

## Safety Considerations

Submerged rocks in the Dry Salvages area create significant boat navigation hazards — a local captain is essential. Tidal current can be strong between the rock outcrops. Cold water year-round below 5 metres — 7mm wetsuit or drysuit recommended. Deploy SMB clearly before surfacing away from the boat.

## Photography

The ledge's rocky topography provides excellent wide-angle subjects — crevice faces encrusted with anemones, sea urchins, and sponges reward careful framing. American lobsters in their dens respond well to patient, non-intrusive approaches. Tautog portraits against the kelp-draped rock are achievable with care.
