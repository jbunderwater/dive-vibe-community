# Avery Ledge

A submerged rocky ledge off Rockport, Massachusetts, marked by a navigational buoy at the northern approach to the Dry Salvages shoal complex.

## Overview

Avery Ledge is a submerged rocky feature off Rockport, Massachusetts, located approximately one mile west of the Dry Salvages — the rocky shoal group that represents one of the most productive dive areas on the North Shore of Massachusetts. Multiple sources confirm Avery's Ledge as a nautical reference landmark: the Dry Salvages are consistently described as lying "a mile east of Avery's Ledge," and a flashing navigational buoy marks the ledge for mariners approaching Rockport Harbor from the north. The ledge sits at approximately 12–18 metres maximum depth at the northern fringe of the Salvages shoal complex, where rocky substrate transitions toward the sandy approaches of Ipswich Bay. While the Dry Salvages themselves are a documented dive destination with confirmed seal encounters and lobster habitat, Avery Ledge is primarily documented as a nautical waypoint rather than a named dive site in local diving guides.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Off Rockport, Massachusetts, west of the Dry Salvages
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 18 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 5–12 meters (15–40 feet)
- **Current**: Moderate tidal current; plan around slack
- **Water Temperature**: 7–20°C depending on season
- **Best Time**: June to October

## Marine Life

Cold-water temperate species typical of the Cape Ann offshore area inhabit rocky ledge habitat in this region. No site-specific marine life surveys have been documented for Avery Ledge itself. The adjacent Dry Salvages area is documented to host a seal colony and is noted as offering "some of the best lobster diving in New England" in rocky channels and cuts.

## Dive Profile

Access via boat charter from Rockport or Gloucester. The ledge sits at the outer edge of the Salvages complex in 12–18 metres of water. Boat navigation in the Dry Salvages area requires local knowledge given the submerged rocks. Plan slack tide windows carefully using Cape Ann tidal predictions. Ascend with adequate reserve and complete a 3-minute safety stop at 5 metres.

## Entry and Exit

Boat charter required from Cape Ann Divers (Gloucester) or other Rockport-area operators. Local knowledge of the Dry Salvages area is essential — submerged rocks create navigation hazards for boats throughout this zone. The site is exposed to the open Atlantic Ocean and can be subject to shifting winds and currents. Deploy SMB before ascending from depth.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Consider pairing with the Dry Salvages proper for a documented two-site Cape Ann charter day
- The Salvages area has a long haul from harbor — plan to depart early if combining sites
- Plan dives around slack tide; Cape Ann tidal currents are significant around the Salvages rocks
- Bring a dive light for crevice investigation
- Cape Ann Divers in Gloucester offers the best local knowledge for this area

## Safety Considerations

Submerged rocks in the Dry Salvages area create significant boat navigation hazards — a local captain is essential. Tidal current can be strong between the rock outcrops. Cold water year-round below 5 metres — 7mm wetsuit or drysuit recommended. The site is exposed to the open Atlantic Ocean. Deploy SMB clearly before surfacing away from the boat.

## Photography

Rocky ledge habitat in the Gulf of Maine at 12–18 metres. The encrusted rock surfaces typical of this coastal zone reward patient macro photography. Strobes needed for color accuracy at depth.
