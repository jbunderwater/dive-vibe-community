# City of Taunton

The abandoned hull of an 1890s Fall River Line freight steamship, visible at low tide on the western shore of Mount Hope Bay in Somerset, Massachusetts.

## Overview

The *City of Taunton* was a freight steamship built in 1892, one of the vessels that served the Fall River Line — the legendary overnight steamship route that connected Providence, Rhode Island, to New York City via Fall River, Massachusetts. The Fall River Line operated from the 1840s until 1937 and was famous for its lavish sidewheel steamers, but the *City of Taunton* was a working freight vessel in this fleet. The ship was abandoned circa 1930 and left to settle on the western shore of Mount Hope Bay at Brayton Point in Somerset, Massachusetts. Mount Hope Bay is a tidal arm at the head of Narragansett Bay. The wreck is visible at low tide, making it one of the few New England wreck sites that requires almost no diving equipment to experience. At just 4 metres maximum depth, this is primarily an archaeological and historical visit site rather than a conventional scuba dive.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Brayton Point, Somerset, Massachusetts (western Mount Hope Bay)
- **Entry Type**: Shore (wading at low tide; very limited scuba)
- **Site Type**: Wreck
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 4 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 1–3 meters (tidal bay; best at dead low)
- **Current**: Mild tidal current; Mount Hope Bay
- **Water Temperature**: 8–22°C depending on season
- **Best Time**: Summer low tides, June to September

## Marine Life

Shallow upper bay estuarine species. Juvenile striped bass patrol the shallows around the wreck structure in summer. Green crabs pick at the wreck debris. Mummichog and Atlantic silverside school in the tidal shallows. Juvenile bluefish appear in warm months. The wreck provides the only hard substrate for some distance in the otherwise soft-bottom bay — mussels attach to exposed hull sections, and barnacles colonize any higher structure.

## Dive Profile

Not a conventional scuba dive. Access at low tide by wading from Brayton Point shore. The hull ribs and structural remains are identifiable with visible planking and ironwork. Maximum water depth directly on the wreck rarely exceeds 4 metres. Brief snorkeling or wading inspection is more practical than scuba. Visit during dead low tide for maximum wreck exposure and clearest water.

## Entry and Exit

Shore entry from Brayton Point, Somerset. The site is on or near the property boundary of what has historically been industrial land at Brayton Point (a former power plant site). Access conditions may vary — check local information before visiting. Exit well before the tide returns. The shallow beach floods relatively quickly on the flood tide.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Consult tide tables — the wreck is most visible within an hour either side of dead low tide
- Bring rubber boots or water shoes — the bottom is soft mud and shell debris
- Combine with a visit to the nearby Fall River area to appreciate the broader Fall River Line maritime history
- The site has archaeological significance — do not disturb any material
- The former Brayton Point power plant site adjacent to the wreck may affect public access — verify conditions locally before visiting

## Safety Considerations

The primary hazard is the returning tidal flood — mount hope Bay tidal range is approximately 1.5 metres and the shallow shore floods relatively quickly. Always know your exit window. Cold water in the bay except midsummer; waders or wetsuit are appropriate for extended inspection. The soft mud bottom can be slippery.

## Photography

The exposed wooden hull ribs and ironwork in natural low-tide light provide documentary photography opportunities. Contextual shots including the bay and Somerset shoreline convey the industrial maritime history of Mount Hope Bay. Macro subjects on encrusted wreck material.
