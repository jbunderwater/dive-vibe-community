# SS Stavronikita

A 365-foot (111-metre) Greek freighter sitting bolt upright in 40 metres of water, rated by multiple dive publications as the Caribbean's premier wreck dive and one of the top ten wreck dives in the world.

## Overview

The SS Stavronikita — known locally as "The Stav" — is a Greek cargo freighter built in Denmark in 1956. On 26 August 1976, while carrying cement from Ireland to the Caribbean, the vessel caught fire. Six crew members died in the incident; the remaining 24 drifted for four days before rescue. The stricken ship was towed to Barbados, where the Parks and Beach Commission purchased her at auction for US$30,000 in October 1977 with the aim of creating an artificial reef. On 21 November 1978, a US Navy demolition team sank her using explosive charges off Barbados's west coast near the Coral Reef Club. She settled upright on a sandy bottom in approximately 40 metres of water. The forward masts rise to within 6 metres of the surface; the main deck at the bow sits at around 23–24 metres and at the stern at approximately 29–30 metres; the propeller rests at roughly 39–40 metres. Multiple dive publications have rated the Stavronikita among the world's top wreck dives.

## Site Information
- **Location**: West coast, near Coral Reef Club between Holetown and Bridgetown
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 40 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–30 metres
- **Current**: Light to moderate; can be strong
- **Best Time**: December to April (calmest seas, best visibility)

## Marine Life

Decades of submersion have transformed the Stavronikita into a thriving artificial reef. The hull, masts, and superstructure carry dense sponge encrustation, gorgonians, sea fans, and large barrel sponges around the crane derricks. Schools of blue chromis, Spanish hogfish, and grunts swirl through the superstructure. Groupers and coneys inhabit the holds and deeper sections. Barracuda patrol the upper decks. Hawksbill turtles are regularly spotted resting on the wreck. The forward masts — coated with sponges and corals — teem with fish and make a dramatic focal point in the blue water above.

## Dive Profile

The wreck requires careful multilevel planning. The deck slopes from approximately 23–24 metres at the bow to 29–30 metres at the stern; the deepest point is the propeller at approximately 39–40 metres. Most operators recommend descending directly to the stern deck and working forward along the superstructure, visiting the bridge, corridors, and cargo holds before ascending along the masts. The forward mast rising to 6 metres from the surface serves as an ideal landmark for safety stops. Bottom time at the deepest sections is limited — plan 20–25 minutes below 30 metres before beginning the ascent. At least three dives are needed to explore the full extent of the wreck.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive from west coast operators — a short run from Holetown. Usually offered as the first (deeper) dive of a two-tank trip, paired with a shallower reef or the Lord Combermere wreck. Mooring line available; descent via the line or along the masts.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Plan the dive carefully: the bow deck is significantly shallower than the stern — a multilevel profile maximises bottom time
- The bridge, cargo holds, and crane derricks are the most impressive features
- A torch is essential for illuminating sponge and coral growth in the corridors and holds
- The forward mast in the blue water above 20 metres is the iconic photographic image
- Do not attempt penetration into enclosed holds without proper wreck training and equipment
- The mast top at 6 metres makes an excellent safety stop — spend decompression time watching the fish aggregations around the rigging

## Safety Considerations

Advanced wreck dive requiring careful depth and air management. The stern and propeller at 39–40 metres exceed standard recreational limits for many certification levels — plan and adhere to your maximum depth. Silt in enclosed sections can reduce visibility rapidly; maintain excellent buoyancy. Currents can be strong. Do not enter enclosed holds without wreck diving certification. Always deploy DSMB on the ascent.
