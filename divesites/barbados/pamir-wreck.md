# Pamir Wreck

A 160-foot (49-metre) former freighter sunk in 1985 off Barbados's north coast near Speightstown — sitting upright and intact at 15 metres, with an accessible air pocket inside and dense schools of Bermuda chub and blue chromis swarming the hull.

## Overview

The Pamir is a former inter-island freighter and the sister ship of the Friars Craig (see separate entry). She was deliberately sunk in 1985 as an artificial reef off the northwest coast of Barbados near Almond Beach Village in St Peter parish — some distance north of Carlisle Bay. Sources cite her length at approximately 160 feet (49 metres). She rests upright on the sandy bottom in 9–15 metres of water, sitting intact close to the reef edge. The wreck has developed productive colonisation: gorgonians and whip corals have established on the hull, and large fish schools congregate around the structure. The upright position makes navigation straightforward. Visibility averages 15–25 metres. Water temperature is 26–28°C.

## Marine Life

The Pamir is particularly noted for its fish aggregations. Hundreds of Bermuda chub and blue chromis school around the wreck, creating dramatic fish-in-motion photographs. Gorgonians and whip corals cover sections of the hull. Garden eels inhabit the sandy bottom around the wreck. The surrounding reef edge attracts the reef fish community typical of Barbados's north coast. Hawksbill turtles patrol the area.

## Dive Profile

Descent to the wreck deck at 5–8 metres and systematic exploration from bow to stern, dropping to the sandy base at 15 metres. The upright position makes navigation intuitive. Bottom time is very comfortable at this depth. Safety stop at the upper deck.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive from north coast Barbados operators. The Pamir is typically paired with its sister ship Friars Craig (further south) for two-tank north/south wreck days, or combined with nearby reef sites.

## Tips and Recommendations

The Bermuda chub schools are the visual highlight — approach slowly to avoid scattering them. Dive Hightide, operating from the north coast, includes the Pamir regularly on dive programs and knows current wreck conditions. Combine with a nearby reef site for a two-tank day.

## Safety Considerations

Standard wreck diving precautions apply. Do not enter enclosed spaces without wreck training. DSMB required. Light currents are possible on the north coast.

## Photography

The dense chub and chromis schools swarming the Pamir hull are the defining photographic image — wide-angle shots of the school against the hull in the blue water are possible at this depth. Gorgonian close-ups and garden eel portraits on the sandy bottom are secondary subjects.
