## Conch dive site

The SS Conch was one of the world's first purpose-built oil tankers, wrecked on the Akurala reef between Ambalangoda and Hikkaduwa in 1903.

## Overview

The SS Conch was a 3,555-ton steam oil tanker built in 1892 by W. Gray & Co. of Stockton for the Shell Transport and Trading Company — one of the earliest purpose-built oil tankers in the world. On a voyage from Colombo toward Madras with a cargo of petroleum, she struck the Akurala reef, between Ambalangoda and Hikkaduwa on Sri Lanka's south coast. Sources disagree on the exact date: a Sri Lankan maritime-archaeology writeup gives June 2, 1903, while an independent Dutch maritime-heritage register gives June 3, 1903. Both agree the hull broke in two amidships; the Dutch register also notes some evidence has been read as pointing to an explosion, though the primary, better-supported account is that she was driven onto the reef and grounded.

The wreck now lies on her port side, broken into sections scattered across roughly 100 by 35 meters. Reported depths vary by section — around 14 meters near the bow and up to 21 meters at the main wreck — and local dive operators typically describe the dive as around 20 meters. The engine, boilers and propeller are still visible and the site is considered well preserved for the region; interior spaces are penetrable but, as with any wreck penetration, this requires appropriate training and equipment and is never risk-free.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Sri Lanka, Asia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 21 meters

## Marine Life

Operators describe groupers and reef fish colonizing the hull, and cuttlefish have been reported in the wreck's cavities. We found no site-specific report of larger pelagics (sharks, turtles, dolphins) at the Conch itself, despite one aggregator page listing them — that appears to be generic regional boilerplate rather than a site-specific sighting, so it has been left out here.
