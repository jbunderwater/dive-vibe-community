## Conch dive site

The SS Conch, wrecked in 1903 off Akurala near Hikkaduwa, is one of the world's earliest purpose-built oil tankers and one of Sri Lanka's most-dived historic wrecks.

## Overview

The SS Conch was a British steam tanker built by W. Gray & Co. in Stockton in 1892 for Shell Transport & Trading Co. — among the first purpose-built oil tankers in the world. She struck the Akurala reef between Ambalangoda and Hikkaduwa while carrying bulk petroleum from Novorossisk toward Madras, broke in two amidships, and sank. Sources report the sinking date as either June 2, 1903 (PADI, divingsrilanka.com) or June 3, 1903 (archaeology.lk) — the exact day is not settled across sources. The vessel is recorded at 3,555 tons with a triple-expansion engine.

The wreck now lies broken across a debris field roughly 100 by 35 meters, resting on its port side. Reported depths vary by source and by which section of the wreck you're on: PADI and archaeology.lk cite a range of roughly 14-21 meters, while older accounts describe the site resting against the rock at around 22 meters — divers should expect depths across that broader range rather than a single fixed number. Visible structure includes the steam engine, boilers, propeller and shaft, and scattered hull plating. The wreck is penetrable in places, though — as with any wreck penetration — this requires appropriate training and equipment and should not be treated as routine.

PADI's site listing specifically notes groupers, Napoleon wrasse, and queen angelfish among the reef fish using the wreck, and archaeology.lk describes fish sheltering inside a dark, cave-like upturned hull section. Broader "sharks, whales, dolphins, turtles" sightings are listed by PADI as common regional encounters rather than confirmed at this specific wreck, so they are not repeated here as site-specific claims.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Sri Lanka, Asia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 21 meters

## Marine Life

Groupers, Napoleon wrasse, and queen angelfish are specifically reported on the wreck's exterior surfaces by PADI's site listing. Fish shelter inside a dark, cave-like section of upturned hull, per archaeology.lk's account of the wreck. No source-confirmed reports of sharks, turtles, or cetaceans at this specific wreck were found — those species are noted in regional listings as general area sightings, not site-specific.
