## S.S. Chickasaw

The SS Chickasaw is a WWII-era freighter that ran aground just west of South Point on Santa Rosa Island in February 1962 while carrying cargo from Japan. After decades of wave action, very little structure remains above the kelp-covered rocky reef.

## Overview

Built in 1942 as a wartime cargo ship and later returned to civilian service, the Chickasaw was bound for San Pedro, California, from Japan with a cargo of general merchandise — including plywood, shoes, toys, dishes, and at least one 1950s vintage Buick — when she ran aground at 9:18 PM on February 7, 1962, attempting to pass through the narrow channel between Santa Rosa and San Miguel islands in rough conditions. Initial salvage attempts were defeated by high seas for nearly four days before a breeches buoy could be rigged to shore and the crew rescued. The wreck has since been broken apart by decades of wave action in the exposed surf zone west of South Point, and very little identifiable structure remains today.

## Site Information

- **Location**: West of South Point, Santa Rosa Island — exposed south coast
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 12 meters
- **Depth Range**: 3-12 meters (10-40 feet)
- **Typical Visibility**: 6-15 meters (20-50 feet) when conditions allow
- **Current**: Surge and swell common on exposed south coast
- **Best Time**: Late summer on calm days
- **Water Temperature**: 12-16°C (54-60°F)

## Marine Life

Despite the lack of large wreck structure, the rocky reef environment supports abundant California Channel Islands marine life. Large sheephead are frequently observed and the area has historically offered good spearfishing on calm days. Rockfish, kelp bass, and cabezon inhabit the reef surrounding the scattered debris. The kelp forest in this area provides habitat for garibaldi and a variety of invertebrates.

## Dive Profile

Most of the Chickasaw has been returned to the ocean by decades of surge and storm action — the hull has largely disintegrated into the surf line. The dive is best approached as a kelp and reef dive with historical context rather than a traditional wreck exploration. Scattered metal debris marks the site. Divers work the reef in 3-12 meters, with the deeper areas (to 12 meters) offering the best remaining structural fragments.

## Entry and Exit

Charter boat access only from Ventura or Santa Barbara. The south coast of Santa Rosa Island is exposed to prevailing northwest swell. This site is particularly challenging to access due to the location west of South Point. Conditions must be calm and stable — this site sees fewer diving visits than the more accessible north coast wrecks (Aggi). Confirm weather and site conditions with your charter before committing to this location.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Manage expectations — very little intact wreck structure remains after 60+ years in the surf zone
- 7mm wetsuit or drysuit recommended for the cold water
- This site is better suited to divers interested in the reef ecology and history than traditional wreck penetration diving
- Artifact collection prohibited — National Park and Marine Sanctuary protections apply
- Combine with a dive at the Aggi or Goldenhorn if conditions allow access to multiple Santa Rosa wrecks

## Safety Considerations

The exposed south coast location means surge and swell conditions at this site can be significant even on relatively calm days. Monitor conditions throughout the dive and be prepared to abort if surge increases. The rocky reef in shallow water presents collision risk in surge. Plan conservative air limits given the conditions.

## Photography

The kelp forest and rocky reef provide photogenic backgrounds for wide-angle shooting. Large sheephead and other resident fish are relatively approachable. Metal debris fragments make for interesting macro subjects with encrusting invertebrates. Visibility of 6-15 meters can support good photography on favorable days.
