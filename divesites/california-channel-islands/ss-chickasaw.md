## S.S. Chickasaw

S.S. Chickasaw is a historic wreck dive in California Channel Islands, North America.

## Overview

The S.S. Chickasaw is a steel-hulled steam freighter that wrecked off Santa Cruz Island in 1962, and her remains lie on a rocky reef at around 12 meters, one of the shallower and more accessible wreck dives in the northern Channel Islands. The intact boiler and large hull sections are encrusted with orange and pink invertebrates that colour up brilliantly under a dive light, and the surrounding kelp forest filters sunlight down through the canopy in shifting shafts. Garibaldi are abundant here, and the wreck's proximity to Santa Cruz's sea lion haul-outs means playful interactions with the local colony are a regular part of the dive.

## Wreck History

The Chickasaw was a 439-foot (134 m), 6,131-ton steel American-flag freighter built in New Jersey in 1942 as a C-2-type cargo transport. She served as the U.S. Navy transport USS Thurston in both the European and Pacific theaters of World War II before being sold to the Waterman Steamship Company of Mobile, Alabama in 1946, demilitarized, and renamed Chickasaw. On the evening of February 7, 1962, bound for Wilmington, California, from Yokohama, Japan, the Chickasaw encountered 8–10-foot seas, rain, and dense fog south of the northern Channel Islands. The vessel went hard aground on the south side of Santa Rosa Island, just west of South Point, between Cluster Point and South Point. Her cargo at the time included plywood, children's toys, dishes, optical supplies, and general merchandise valued at approximately $1.5 million. The 36 crew and four passengers were rescued without loss of life by the U.S. Coast Guard. Salvage efforts removed the most valuable cargo, along with brass, copper, wiring, and electric motors. By February 18, 1962, the vessel was officially abandoned. In May 1962, a Ventura scrap dealer was arrested by Santa Barbara County sheriff's deputies for stealing remaining electronics and navigational gear from the wreck.

## Marine Life

The remains of the Chickasaw lie in 3–12 meters (10–40 ft) of water along the exposed south shore of Santa Rosa Island. Most of the hull has disintegrated into the surf zone over six decades, but remaining structural sections attract large California sheephead, kelp bass, rockfish, and lingcod. The local environment is cold and nutrient-rich, supporting the full range of northern Channel Islands kelp forest species — garibaldi, cabezon, and various wrasse — as well as invertebrate growth on exposed metal.

## Dive Profile

The site extends from the surf zone down to approximately 12 meters (40 ft). The wreck is highly degraded; large structural sections have collapsed and the most intact elements lie in the shallowest areas. Depth and surge dictate how far productive diving extends on any given day.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive only. Santa Rosa Island lies approximately 34 miles southwest of Santa Barbara. Larger liveaboard vessels (M/V Truth, M/V Peace) are the practical access route. The south-facing coast around South Point is more sheltered from northwest swell than the island's west end, making it more frequently diveable — but the site can still be surgy.

## Tips and Recommendations

Chickasaw is best dived as a secondary site on a Santa Rosa liveaboard trip, in conjunction with the Aggi or Goldenhorn. Conditions on the south coast are often better than the north or west, but check forecasts carefully. Large sheephead are the standout fish encounter here.

## Safety Considerations

Water temperature at Santa Rosa Island is typically 10–16°C (50–61°F). A 7 mm wetsuit with hood and gloves is minimum; drysuit preferred in winter. The shallow, surf-zone character of the wreck means surge is a significant hazard. The remote location requires diver self-sufficiency.
