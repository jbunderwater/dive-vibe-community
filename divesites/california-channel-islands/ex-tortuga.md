## Ex-Tortuga

The Ex-Tortuga (ex-USS Tortuga LSD-26) is a massive Navy landing ship dock that grounded and was scrapped at Cardwell Point on the south shore of San Miguel Island, within the boundaries of Channel Islands National Park and National Marine Sanctuary.

## Overview

The ex-Tortuga (LSD-26) was a 457-foot amphibious landing ship originally named Dry Tortugas, commissioned in 1945. After decommissioning, the vessel was based at Port Hueneme and used as a target ship for Tomahawk cruise missile testing. On December 15, 1987, the ship broke free from its tow during a powerful "Southeaster" storm and went hard aground at Cardwell Point on the south shore of San Miguel Island. The impact caused the 100-foot stern section to break away from the 355-foot forward section, which was impaled on a large boulder. The vessel was subsequently scrapped in place. The remaining structure was later repositioned to serve as an artificial reef within Channel Islands National Marine Sanctuary and National Park boundaries.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Cardwell Point, south shore of San Miguel Island, California Channel Islands
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 5-15 meters (15-50 feet), variable
- **Current**: Strong currents and surge common at this exposed location
- **Best Time**: October to November for optimal conditions
- **Water Temperature**: 10-13°C (50-55°F) year-round

## Marine Life

San Miguel Island's nutrient-rich, upwelling-driven waters produce exceptional marine life density. Around the steel wreck structure, divers encounter wolf eels, giant lingcod, large rockfish species, cabezon, nudibranchs, and sheep crabs. The cold water supports dense invertebrate encrustation on the hull sections. Elephant seals and sea lions are commonly seen near Cardwell Point.

## Dive Profile

The large hull sections of the scrapped ship lie scattered at depth, providing extensive habitat that has been colonized by invertebrates and fish. The massive scale of this 457-foot Navy vessel means dive areas are spread across a wide footprint. Divers typically explore the larger structural sections closest to the mooring or anchor point, working around the steel plates and framing that form the artificial reef.

## Entry and Exit

Access is by charter boat only, with trips originating from Ventura Harbor (~64 miles) or Santa Barbara (~46 miles). San Miguel Island is the most remote of the Channel Islands and requires National Park permits for visits. Weather windows are limited; October and November typically offer the best combination of calm seas and good visibility. This site on the south shore of the island is particularly exposed and conditions can be unforgiving.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Drysuit or 7mm wetsuit required — San Miguel runs 10°F colder than other Channel Islands
- Strong navigation and buoyancy skills essential for this exposed, deep site
- The nearest hyperbaric chamber is approximately 100 miles away — plan conservatively
- This is a protected archaeological and marine sanctuary site — no artifact removal
- Book with charter operators experienced with San Miguel's demanding conditions
- Confirm current site accessibility with your charter operator before the trip

## Safety Considerations

San Miguel Island is fully exposed to open Pacific swells and winds. Conditions deteriorate rapidly and without warning. Strong currents affect the south shore at Cardwell Point. The remote location — with the nearest recompression chamber approximately 100 miles distant — demands conservative diving practices. Always dive with a buddy, carry an SMB, and ensure your charter captain is monitoring conditions throughout the dive.

## Photography

The large steel hull sections offer dramatic wide-angle compositions. Encrusting invertebrates — corynactis anemones, tube worms, and encrusting sponges — provide colorful macro subjects against the rust-stained steel. Torch lighting essential for interior sections and crevices.
