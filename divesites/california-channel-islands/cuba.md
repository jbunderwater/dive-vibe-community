## Cuba

Cuba is a historic wreck dive in California Channel Islands, North America.

## Overview

The Cuba was a Pacific Mail Steamship Company vessel that wrecked on Point Bennett at San Miguel Island in September 1923, and her scattered iron hull sections now rest on a reef swept by the strong currents that make San Miguel notorious among Channel Islands divers. The site sits in around 20 meters and is notable for the wildlife corridor it occupies — San Miguel's Point Bennett hosts the largest pinniped colony in the world, and Steller sea lions and elephant seals regularly interact with divers on the wreck. The exposed northwest position of San Miguel means this dive is only accessible in calm weather windows, which can be rare.

## Wreck History

The SS Cuba was a 306-foot (93.3 m), 3,169-GRT passenger-cargo steamship built by Blohm & Voss in Hamburg in 1897 under the name Coblenz for Norddeutscher Lloyd. She was renamed Sachem in 1917 and Cuba in 1920, eventually operated by the Pacific Mail Steamship Company on the San Francisco–Panama Canal Zone route. On September 8, 1923, at 04:30 hrs, the Cuba was steaming north through the Santa Barbara Channel in dense fog under dead reckoning — her radio was non-functional. Captain Charles J. Holland, exhausted after three days of fog navigation, had handed the watch to Second Officer Rochau. When the ship struck the reef approximately a quarter-mile off Point Bennett at the western tip of San Miguel Island, both propellers were destroyed. All approximately 77 passengers and crew were rescued, along with a cargo that included $2.5 million in silver bullion, mahogany, and coffee. The site has been archaeologically documented by NOAA, Channel Islands National Park, and the Coastal Maritime Archaeology Resources group, and is described as one of the most consolidated and well-organized of the more than 150 historic wrecks in the Channel Islands National Marine Sanctuary.

## Marine Life

The Cuba's triple-expansion steam engines and boilers remain upright on the seafloor, providing structural habitat for wolf eels, large California spiny lobster (known locally as "bugs"), halibut, and an abundance of nudibranchs and other invertebrates. The surrounding reefs off Point Bennett support rockfish and the full complement of Channel Islands kelp forest species. San Miguel's position at the confluence of California and Pacific currents creates nutrient-rich, cold water that drives exceptional invertebrate growth on the hull structure.

## Dive Profile

The wreck rests on the reef at approximately 20 meters (65 ft), though specific depth varies across the scattered debris field. The site is within Channel Islands National Marine Sanctuary and is protected — no artifact removal is permitted. The wreck's consolidated nature means major structural elements are clustered in one area, making navigation straightforward despite the depth.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access only. San Miguel Island lies 64 miles from Ventura Harbor and 46 miles from Santa Barbara — larger dive vessels are required for this crossing. Overnight liveaboard charters (M/V Truth via Channel Islands Expeditions, M/V Peace via Peace Dive Boat) are the practical access option, as the distance prohibits meaningful diving on a single-day trip. San Miguel is only accessible in calm weather windows, which are most reliable in fall.

## Tips and Recommendations

Plan this dive for September–November when northwest winds typically relax and visibility improves. Water at San Miguel is cooler than at the eastern islands — a drysuit is strongly recommended. The intact boiler and engine machinery are the primary targets; bring a dive light to illuminate the encrusted surfaces.

## Safety Considerations

San Miguel Island is subject to some of the strongest winds and swell in the Channel Islands. Water temperature is typically 10–13°C (50–55°F) year-round — drysuit diving is recommended. Currents can be substantial at Point Bennett, and the remote location means self-sufficiency is essential. This is an advanced dive; strong buoyancy control and cold-water experience are required.
