## Equator

The Equator is a small but charming 1949 fishing vessel wreck on the seaward side of Middle Anacapa Island — an accessible dive featuring excellent visibility, colorful reef fish, and a protected archaeological site framed by kelp forest.

## Overview

The Equator was constructed in 1949 and sank the same year after striking a submerged rock approximately 150 yards from shore near East Fish Camp anchorage on the seaward (south) side of Middle Anacapa Island. GPS position: N34°00.398, W119°23.059. Shortly after sinking, salvage operators used explosives to extract the vessel's diesel engine and other valuable components, leaving the remaining hull structure on the bottom. The Equator is now a protected archaeological site within Channel Islands National Park — one of the shallowest and most accessible of the Channel Islands wrecks, suitable for divers of various experience levels.

## Site Information

- **Location**: East Fish Camp, seaward side of Middle Anacapa Island
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 12 meters
- **Depth Range**: 4.5-12 meters (15-40 feet)
- **Typical Visibility**: 7.5-15 meters (25-50 feet) — generally very good
- **Current**: Mild to moderate; occasional current along the outer edge
- **Best Time**: Year-round; summer and autumn offer best visibility and calmest conditions
- **Water Temperature**: 14-18°C (58-64°F)

## Marine Life

The wreck supports colorful resident reef fish typical of Anacapa Island's exceptional diversity. Garibaldi — the California state marine fish — are abundant and conspicuous around the structure. Ghost gobies occupy crevices in the hull. Kelp bass, sheephead, and rockfish inhabit the surrounding reef. Nearby reef sections feature corynactis anemones, sea stars, worms, and various encrusting invertebrates. Sea lions from Anacapa's colony frequently investigate divers in this area.

## Dive Profile

Divers typically descend to the main hull section in 12 meters and work upward through the scattered wreck debris. The site features distinct hull sections and the cavities left by the salvage-extracted engine. Kelp sometimes obscures sections of the wreck from the surface but clears as you descend. The surrounding reef is well worth exploring beyond the immediate wreck footprint. The shallow maximum depth makes this suitable for a multi-dive day trip.

## Entry and Exit

Charter boat only from Ventura Harbor (approximately 14 miles). Anacapa is the most frequently visited of the Channel Islands and has the most regular charter boat service. East Fish Camp is a small anchorage on the seaward south side of Middle Anacapa. Several charter companies from Ventura and Santa Barbara visit this site regularly. GPS coordinates for site: N34°00.398, W119°23.059.

## Tips and Recommendations

- 5-7mm wetsuit recommended
- Garibaldi at Anacapa are extraordinarily bold — a wonderful photography subject
- Kelp can be dense in summer — carry a cutting tool
- "Do not take, touch, or disturb any items on the wreck" — serious federal penalties for violations at this protected archaeological site
- This site pairs well with the Winfield Scott wreck for a two-wreck day trip at Anacapa
- Good site for underwater photography beginners due to the accessible depth and resident fish

## Safety Considerations

The seaward (south) side of Anacapa can be subject to swell and surge, particularly with northwest or south swell. Monitor conditions throughout the dive. Kelp overhead environments require a cutting tool. Visibility is generally good at this site, aiding navigation. Primary hazards are cold water and kelp entanglement.

## Photography

One of the better photography wrecks in the Channel Islands — accessible depth, good visibility, and bold resident fish create excellent conditions. Garibaldi and ghost gobies make ideal subjects against the encrusted hull. Wide-angle work captures the hull sections in ambient light. Macro opportunities on corynactis anemones, nudibranchs, and tube worms covering the metal surfaces. A torch enhances colors in shadowed areas.
