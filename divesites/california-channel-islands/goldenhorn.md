## Goldenhorn

The Goldenhorn was a 268-foot Scottish-built four-masted sailing ship that struck a reef in dense fog on the southwest coast of Santa Rosa Island in September 1892, leaving scattered wreck debris in the surge zone on one of the Channel Islands' most exposed coastlines.

## Overview

The Goldenhorn was a four-masted bark built in Scotland and was carrying coal when she wrecked on the evening of September 12, 1892. Sailing in heavy fog along the southwest coast of Santa Rosa Island, the vessel was unable to maneuver away from the island when the wind failed. She struck a reef on the seaward side of the island and was lost. Her crew of 28 rowed lifeboats to safety across the channel to Santa Barbara. The wreck lies scattered in 4-9 meters of surgy, shallow water on the exposed southwest coast, accessible only during the calmest sea conditions.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Southwest coast of Santa Rosa Island, between Sandy Point and South Point
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 9 meters
- **Depth Range**: 4-9 meters (15-30 feet)
- **Typical Visibility**: 6-15 meters (20-50 feet) when conditions allow diving
- **Current**: Strong surge; exposed seaward coastline — only diveable in calmest seas
- **Best Time**: Late summer on rare flat-calm days
- **Water Temperature**: 12-16°C (54-60°F)

## Marine Life

The shallow, surge-swept reef around the wreck debris supports cold-water Channel Islands species. Calico bass, rockfish, and sheephead navigate the kelp and rocky reef around the scattered hull remnants. Garibaldi are present in the shallower sections. The cold, nutrient-rich water supports a dense invertebrate community on the exposed metal and rock surfaces.

## Dive Profile

Very little structure remains after 130+ years in the surf zone. Scattered iron fastenings, hull fragments, and chain are spread across a shallow rocky reef. The wreck is best explored by following the debris field from the deeper end toward the shallower reef edge. Surge at this site can be significant — divers need strong buoyancy control to avoid being pushed into the reef. Diving here is opportunistic, only possible when seas along the southwest coast are exceptionally calm.

## Entry and Exit

Charter boat access only. The southwest coast of Santa Rosa Island is one of the most exposed coastlines in the Channel Islands — facing directly into prevailing northwest swell. Diving is only feasible on rare days with minimal swell and wind. Most charter trips to Santa Rosa focus on the more sheltered north coast; the southwest wrecks require a dedicated calm-weather visit. Always confirm site accessibility with your charter operator.

## Tips and Recommendations

- 7mm wetsuit or drysuit essential — cold water and surge
- This site demands excellent buoyancy in surge — not suitable for less experienced divers despite the shallow depth
- Manage air carefully — exit before conditions deteriorate
- Very little identifiable wreck structure remains; focus on the reef ecology and the historical context
- Artifact collection prohibited under NPS and California law
- Have a contingency dive site in mind — conditions often make this site undiveable on arrival

## Safety Considerations

The exposed southwest coast of Santa Rosa is unforgiving. Even on apparently calm days, residual swell can make the shallow reef dangerous. Surge at 4-9 meters can be powerful. Plan a conservative dive, maintain contact with your buddy, and be prepared to abort immediately if surge or swell increases. The shallow depth reduces decompression risk but the physical hazard from surge on the reef is significant.

## Photography

Scattered iron hull remnants against kelp-covered reef create moody compositions when visibility allows. Macro subjects — nudibranchs, encrusting anemones — abound on the century-old iron. Wide-angle captures of the reef with hull debris require good timing around surge cycles.
