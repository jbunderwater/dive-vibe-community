## Aggi

The Aggi is one of the most rewarding wreck dives at the Channel Islands — a 265-foot Scottish-built sailing ship driven aground at Talcott Shoal during a 1915 storm, with substantial intact structure spread across a kelp-draped reef at Santa Rosa Island.

## Overview

Built in 1894 in Govan, Scotland as the Aspice, this three-masted, ship-rigged sailing vessel measured 265 feet long and nearly 40 feet wide. While being towed from San Pedro in early May 1915, the vessel broke free during a storm and drove hard aground on Talcott Shoal — a shallow, rocky reef one mile northwest of Sandy Point at the northwest corner of Santa Rosa Island. The crew survived. The wreck was never salvaged and has become a permanent fixture of the shoal. It lies within Channel Islands National Park and is protected under federal law.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Talcott Shoal, northwest of Sandy Point, Santa Rosa Island
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 21 meters
- **Depth Range**: 3-21 meters (10-70 feet)
- **Typical Visibility**: 9-21 meters (30-70 feet) — excellent for the Channel Islands
- **Current**: Potentially strong; northwest winds can develop rapidly
- **Best Time**: Summer through autumn for calm conditions
- **Water Temperature**: 12-16°C (54-60°F); low 60s°F in summer

## Marine Life

The wreck structure shelters a productive reef community. Calico bass, perch, and multiple rockfish species are abundant throughout the hull sections. Spiny lobster — sometimes of notable size — congregate in crevices and under hull sections, making this a popular site during lobster season. The surrounding sand and reef support sheephead, lingcod, and garibaldi. The iron ribs and steel plates are encrusted with invertebrates typical of cold California water.

## Dive Profile

The wreck is scattered between 3 and 21 meters across the reef and sand. Key features include a large hull section draped across the reef, the iron-masted stern, a steam donkey boiler, and a graceful bow section resting in sand at depth. Machinery including capstans, bollards, and anchors mark the wreck footprint. Divers typically start at the deeper stern and bow sections and work shallower, finishing around the boiler and hull.

## Entry and Exit

Access is by charter boat from Ventura or Santa Barbara. This site sits on the exposed northwest corner of Santa Rosa Island and is subject to significant northwest swell and wind chop. Conditions must be genuinely calm to dive safely — the site is only accessible on the best weather days. Verify current accessibility with your charter operator. Anchoring and mooring follow National Park regulations.

## Tips and Recommendations

- 7mm wetsuit or drysuit recommended — water temperature in the low 60s°F with surge
- Dive the deeper sections (bow, stern, boiler) early in the dive and finish shallow
- Surge can be significant even on seemingly calm days at this exposed northwest corner
- Bring a dive light to illuminate hull interiors and lobster crevices
- Artifact collection prohibited — this is a protected NPS archaeological site
- Photography and legal spearfishing (in season, outside MPA zones) are permitted

## Safety Considerations

Talcott Shoal's exposed position on the northwest corner of Santa Rosa Island means conditions change quickly. Surge at depth is common. Northwest winds can build rapidly during afternoon hours. Monitor air supply carefully in surge conditions and plan your ascent conservatively. Carry an SMB and dive with a buddy. The shallow portions (3-5 meters) can have significant wave action.

## Photography

The hull section draped over the reef offers dramatic wide-angle compositions, particularly when kelp frames the structure. The steam donkey boiler is an evocative subject with natural light. Macro opportunities abound on the encrusted iron surfaces — look for nudibranchs, tube worms, and encrusting anemones. Visibility of 9-21 meters makes this one of the better photography wrecks in the Channel Islands.
