## Del Rio

The Del Rio was a commercial fishing vessel that caught fire off Anacapa Island in October 1952 and drifted to the beach at Frenchy's Cove, West Anacapa Island, where it sank — providing a modest wreck site within the kelp reef environment of Channel Islands National Park.

## Overview

The commercial fishing vessel Del Rio caught fire while working offshore from Anacapa Island on October 10, 1952. A neighboring vessel, Sea Ranger, rescued the crew of eleven before the Del Rio drifted, rudderless and burning, toward the beach at Frenchy's Cove on the north side of West Anacapa Island. The US Coast Guard monitored the hulk to determine whether it posed a navigation hazard before it eventually grounded and sank at the cove. The wreck rests in the sheltered north-facing Frenchy's Cove — historically named for Raymond "Frenchy" LeDreau, a long-time resident fisherman who lived on West Anacapa for nearly three decades.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Frenchy's Cove, West Anacapa Island, California Channel Islands
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 12 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 9-30 meters (30-100 feet) — Anacapa is known for excellent visibility
- **Current**: Mild in the protected cove; variable offshore
- **Best Time**: Year-round; summer and autumn best for visibility
- **Water Temperature**: 14-18°C (58-64°F) — warmest of the northern Channel Islands

## Marine Life

Anacapa's north coast is renowned for exceptional marine life diversity. The Del Rio wreck and surrounding reef host sheephead, garibaldi (abundant at Anacapa), bat rays, moray eels, horn sharks, leopard sharks, sea lions, harbor seals, and multiple rockfish species. The kelp forest adjacent to the wreck provides rich hunting and foraging habitat. Black sea bass — now protected — are occasionally spotted. Brightly colored nudibranchs are common on encrusted surfaces.

## Dive Profile

The relatively sheltered position at Frenchy's Cove makes this one of the more accessible wreck sites in the Channel Islands. The wreck sits in a mixed environment of sand, rock, and kelp, with dive depth ranging from the shallows to approximately 12 meters. Divers explore the vessel's remains while enjoying the surrounding kelp reef. The site can be combined with a reef dive along the kelp-fringed north wall of West Anacapa.

## Entry and Exit

Charter boat access from Ventura Harbor — Anacapa is the closest of the Channel Islands to the mainland (approximately 14 miles from Oxnard). Frenchy's Cove is on the north side of West Anacapa and offers reasonable shelter from prevailing northwest swell. Note that the trail and beach landing area at Frenchy's Cove has experienced periodic closures due to trail washout — confirm current access with Island Packers or your charter operator before the trip.

## Tips and Recommendations

- 5-7mm wetsuit recommended — Anacapa's water is the warmest of the northern islands but still cold
- Check current Frenchy's Cove access status before your trip
- This site pairs well with a dive at the Winfield Scott or Equator wrecks on a day trip from Ventura
- Anacapa visibility averages 9 meters (30 feet) year-round and can reach 30 meters (100 feet) on exceptional days
- Artifact collection prohibited — Channel Islands National Park and Marine Sanctuary protections apply
- Sea lions are a highlight at Anacapa — they frequently interact with divers

## Safety Considerations

Frenchy's Cove provides good shelter compared to the exposed south-facing Anacapa coast. Primary hazards are kelp entanglement and cold water. Carry a cutting tool. Monitor air supply carefully when exploring kelp overhead environments. Surge can affect the site when northwest swell wraps around the island.

## Photography

The kelp forest at Anacapa is among the most photogenic in the Channel Islands. The Del Rio wreck provides structural interest while the surrounding reef offers abundant subject matter — garibaldi against kelp, sea lions in open water, nudibranchs on the wreck surfaces. Visibility averaging 9-30 meters creates good conditions for both wide-angle and macro work.
