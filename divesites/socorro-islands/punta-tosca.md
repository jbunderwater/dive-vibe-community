## Punta Tosca

Punta Tosca is on the west coast of Socorro Island, characterized by a series of lava fingers extending from the island into the Pacific. It is regarded as one of the most colorful dives in the otherwise stark Revillagigedo Archipelago and is notable for a wider spectrum of marine life than many of the archipelago's other sites, ranging from macro subjects to megafauna. Nautilus Liveaboards, one of the leading Socorro operators, records more tiger shark sightings at Punta Tosca than at any other site in the islands.

## Overview

The lava finger formations at Punta Tosca create an intricate landscape of channels, ridges, and sandy patches that support a more varied benthic community than the open pinnacles elsewhere in the archipelago. This structure, combined with the site's position on the exposed western side of Socorro Island, draws both resident reef species and pelagic visitors. The site is also recognized as one of the rare locations in the world where divers have encounters with humpback whales in the water — not just surface sightings.

## Site Information

- **Location**: West coast of Socorro Island, Revillagigedo Archipelago (liveaboard only)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 25 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–25 meters (can be reduced by plankton blooms)
- **Current**: Moderate to strong; variable
- **Best Time**: November to June; January–April for humpback whales; November–April for whale sharks

## Marine Life

Tiger sharks are the standout feature that sets Punta Tosca apart — encounters here are the most frequent in the archipelago, though they cannot be guaranteed. Galapagos sharks and silky sharks patrol the site regularly. Giant Pacific manta rays soar through, and the site offers another opportunity to encounter the interactive bottlenose dolphins that range across the islands.

Whale sharks have been recorded at Punta Tosca, particularly from November to April. Humpback whales visit the Socorro area January through March to breed, and Punta Tosca is one of the sites where in-water encounters with humpbacks have been documented — one of very few places in the world where this occurs. Macro life is more abundant here than at other Socorro sites; the lava formations support moray eels, free-swimming morays, crustaceans, and smaller reef species.

## Dive Profile

Divers work along the lava fingers and through the channels, keeping an eye on the blue water for pelagic passes. The site is typically less current-dominated than Cabo Pearce or Roca Partida, which allows for more exploratory diving rather than station-keeping. The relatively shallow maximum depth of 25 meters makes it accessible for extended bottom times. Humpback whale song can often be heard throughout the dive during the January–March season, and divers should look upward for any whale approach from above.

## Entry and Exit

Liveaboard access only. Zodiac tender entry via giant stride. SMB required for ascent and zodiac pickup.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Scan the blue water continuously — tiger sharks, mantas, and dolphins can appear from any direction
- The benthic landscape rewards slower, more exploratory diving than the current-heavy sites
- Visit November–April for the best whale shark probability; January–March for humpback whales
- 5mm wetsuit recommended; water temperature 22–26°C depending on season
- Punta Tosca is considered one of Socorro's best sites for underwater photography

## Safety Considerations

Exposed west-coast location is subject to Pacific swell and variable currents. Tiger shark encounters, while typically non-threatening, require calm, non-erratic diver behavior. SMB and reel mandatory for blue-water ascent. The 30-hour distance from Cabo San Lucas makes emergency medical response extremely limited — Advanced certification and 50+ logged dives are required by all operators.

## Photography

Punta Tosca's reputation as Socorro's most colorful dive makes it rewarding for both wide-angle and close-focus work. The lava finger structure provides foreground interest for wide-angle shots. Tiger sharks and mantas in clear water reward fast-responding systems. Macro opportunities on the lava formations complement the big-animal encounters. Given potential plankton blooms, a strobe setup is useful here when visibility drops.
