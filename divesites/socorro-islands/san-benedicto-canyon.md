# San Benedicto Canyon

A horseshoe-shaped volcanic canyon with two cleaning stations and the highest shark species diversity in the archipelago

## Overview
San Benedicto Canyon — commonly called "El Canyon" — is a horseshoe-shaped volcanic formation on San Benedicto Island, carved by ancient lava flows. The canyon walls create a natural amphitheatre that funnels current and concentrates marine life, particularly sharks. Two separate cleaning stations within the canyon attract both manta rays and a remarkable diversity of shark species. El Canyon is known for producing the highest shark species counts in the Socorro archipelago, with divers reporting up to seven species on a single dive. The site reaches depths of 40 metres at the canyon floor, making it one of the deeper regular dives in the islands.

## Site Information
- **Location**: San Benedicto Island, Socorro Islands, Pacific
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 40 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15-25 metres
- **Current**: Moderate to strong (funnelled by canyon topography)
- **Best Time**: November to June

## Marine Life
The cleaning station at approximately 26-30 metres is the primary marine life aggregation point, serviced by endemic Clarion angelfish. Giant oceanic manta rays (Mobula birostris) visit the station. A distinctive feature of El Canyon noted by multiple sources is the presence of resident silvertip sharks — habituated to divers and notably bold — passing continuously through the cleaning station alongside whitetip reef sharks and Galapagos sharks. Scalloped hammerhead schools patrol the blue water at the canyon mouth and along the walls; multiple sources report hundreds of hammerheads on a single dive. Silky sharks and tiger sharks appear periodically. Bottlenose dolphins pass through the canyon. Large schools of bluefin trevally and bigeye jacks use the canyon as a feeding corridor, as do yellowfin tuna. Humpback whale song is audible during winter months. The volcanic canyon walls harbour giant spiny lobsters, moray eels, and stingrays on the sandy floor.

## Dive Profile
Enter at the canyon mouth and descend along the walls. The canyon floor reaches approximately 40 metres — gas management and no-decompression limit awareness are critical at these depths. Work along the walls, visiting both cleaning stations where mantas and sharks aggregate. The horseshoe shape provides natural shelter from currents in parts of the dive, but current accelerates through the narrower sections. Ascend along the canyon walls and use the shallower rim for your safety stop.

## Entry and Exit
Zodiac tender from the liveaboard. Giant stride entry. Currents can be strong at the canyon mouth and funnelled through the structure. Deploy an SMB during ascent. Nitrox is strongly recommended given the depth profile.

## Tips and Recommendations
- Nitrox is strongly recommended — the 40m depth combined with multi-dive daily profiles makes gas management critical
- Visit both cleaning stations during the dive for maximum manta and shark encounters
- The canyon walls provide current shelter — use the topography to your advantage
- Gloves are prohibited throughout the marine park
- Scan the blue water at the canyon mouth for schooling hammerheads

## Safety Considerations
The 40-metre depth makes this one of the deeper dives in the archipelago — monitor no-decompression limits carefully. Current can accelerate through the canyon's narrower sections. The nearest hyperbaric chamber is approximately 30 hours away. Carry appropriate signalling devices and maintain close buddy contact, especially at depth.

## Photography
The canyon walls provide dramatic framing for wide-angle shots of mantas at cleaning stations and sharks silhouetted against the blue water. The two cleaning stations offer different angles and lighting conditions. The horseshoe shape creates natural leading lines in compositions. Deeper sections require artificial light for colour correction.
