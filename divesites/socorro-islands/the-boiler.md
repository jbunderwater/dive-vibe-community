# The Boiler

One of the world's premier sites for intimate encounters with giant Pacific manta rays at a volcanic pinnacle cleaning station

## Overview
The Boiler is a submerged volcanic pinnacle on the northwest side of San Benedicto Island, widely considered the single best dive site in the Socorro archipelago for manta ray interactions. The site gets its name from the appearance of the ocean surface above the pinnacle — swell and surge breaking over the shallow top make the sea look as though it is boiling. The pinnacle rises from a base at approximately 50 metres (165 feet) to within 6-7 metres (20 feet) of the surface, and is small enough to circumnavigate several times in a single dive. Giant Pacific manta rays congregate here at a cleaning station serviced by endemic Clarion angelfish, and the mantas actively seek out interaction with divers.

## Site Information
- **Location**: San Benedicto Island, Socorro Islands, Pacific
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Pinnacle/seamount
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 45 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15-25 metres (approximately 80 feet typical)
- **Current**: Mild to moderate
- **Best Time**: November to June (peak manta concentration January to April)

## Marine Life
Giant Pacific manta rays (Mobula birostris) with wingspans up to 6.7 metres are the signature encounter. Multiple mantas — often five or more simultaneously — visit the cleaning station where endemic Clarion angelfish remove parasites from their bodies. These mantas are habituated to divers and actively seek interaction, swimming directly overhead and hovering in exhaled bubbles, which they apparently enjoy as a "tickle" against their skin. The encounters are prolonged and intimate, not fleeting passes. Beyond mantas, schools of scalloped hammerhead sharks patrol the deeper water. Silky sharks, dusky sharks, and Galapagos sharks are regular visitors. Bottlenose dolphins frequently pass through. Humpback whales visit from January through March — even when not visible, their song provides an auditory backdrop to the dive. Yellowfin tuna, jacks, and moray eels round out the cast.

## Dive Profile
Descend along the pinnacle and circle the structure, working between the cleaning station zone (10-15m) and the deeper walls (25-40m). The cleaning station on the shallow shelf is the heart of the dive — settle on the volcanic rock at a comfortable depth, remain still, and let the mantas come to you. They typically appear within minutes. For pelagic encounters (hammerheads, sharks), scan the blue water at the deeper limits of the pinnacle. Ascend gradually and use the shallow top of the pinnacle for your safety stop, though be aware of surge near the surface.

## Entry and Exit
Access by zodiac tender from the liveaboard. Giant stride entry. Surface conditions can be rough due to the surge that gives the site its name. Negative entries may be required. Deploy an SMB during ascent for pickup. The pinnacle is relatively small, so maintaining orientation is straightforward.

## Tips and Recommendations
- Remain low and stationary near the cleaning station — never chase or touch the mantas
- Stay away from the actual cleaning station rocks; let the mantas approach you
- No flash photography near the cleaning station — it can disturb cleaning behavior
- Gloves are prohibited throughout the Revillagigedo marine park
- Nitrox recommended for multi-dive days at these depths
- A 5-7mm wetsuit is essential; San Benedicto water temperatures (71-77°F) are often the coldest in the archipelago

## Safety Considerations
Surface surge over the shallow pinnacle top can be disorienting. Maintain awareness of your depth near the top of the structure. Currents are generally milder than at Roca Partida or Cabo Pearce but can pick up. The nearest hyperbaric chamber is in Cabo San Lucas or La Paz (approximately 30 hours transit). Gloves and reef hooks are prohibited.

## Photography
The Boiler offers some of the best manta photography opportunities in the world. Wide-angle is essential — mantas approach to within arm's length and hover overhead. Dramatic belly shots and silhouettes against the surface are signature images. Use natural light where possible to avoid disturbing the mantas' cleaning behavior. The manta rays' mating dance, when witnessed, provides extraordinary footage.
