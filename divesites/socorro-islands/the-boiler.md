## The Boiler

The Boiler is a submerged volcanic pinnacle (guyot) off the northwest coast of Isla San Benedicto. The name comes from the way swell and surge breaking over the summit — which rises to within roughly 6 meters of the surface — makes the water appear to boil. It is one of the most celebrated dive sites in the world for close encounters with giant Pacific manta rays.

## Overview

The Boiler is roughly oval-shaped — about the size of a football field — and rises from a base at approximately 45 meters. The site functions as an active manta ray cleaning station serviced by Clarion angelfish (Holacanthus clarionensis), an endemic species found only in this part of the Pacific. Mantas return daily to be cleaned of parasites, giving divers predictable, prolonged, and remarkably intimate encounters. Up to eight manta rays have been documented simultaneously at The Boiler. The site is also a hydrothermal area, and the warm mineral-rich water is thought to contribute to the abundance of life aggregating here.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Northwest coast of Isla San Benedicto, Revillagigedo Archipelago (liveaboard only)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Pinnacle/seamount
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 45 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–30 meters
- **Current**: Moderate to strong; surge on the summit
- **Best Time**: November to June; January–April peak manta concentration

## Marine Life

Giant Pacific manta rays (Mobula birostris) are the signature species — individuals with wingspans approaching 6 meters (20 feet) are common. The mantas are habituated to divers and actively approach, making eye contact, hovering over diver exhaust bubbles, and circling repeatedly. The sensation of a 6-meter manta arcing overhead at arm's length is the defining experience of The Boiler. Clarion angelfish crowd the mantas in flashes of orange during cleaning sessions.

Beyond mantas, silky sharks patrol the sides of the pinnacle. Schools of scalloped hammerheads pass through in the blue water. Dolphins frequently interact with divers at the surface. Whale sharks appear seasonally. Humpback whales visit January through March — their song is often audible throughout the dive. Lobsters, octopus, and reef fish inhabit the coral-patched summit.

## Dive Profile

Entry by giant stride from a zodiac. Divers descend to 18–25 meters and position themselves near the cleaning station ledges on the southwest corner or northwest ledge. The strategy is to settle motionlessly and wait — mantas typically arrive within minutes. Chasing mantas wastes air and drives them away. After manta time, divers work shallower across the summit to 6–10 meters, extending bottom time in rich fish life. Safety stops are conducted in blue water with SMBs deployed for zodiac pickup.

## Entry and Exit

Liveaboard access only from Cabo San Lucas (approximately 30 hours transit). Zodiac tenders run divers to and from the site. Giant stride entry into open water; SMB required for ascent and pickup.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Stay calm and stationary near the cleaning station — the mantas seek out divers, not the other way around
- Avoid approaching or touching mantas; hovering quietly at depth gets better results and longer encounters
- Stay shallow when possible — the interesting life is at 15–25 meters and air lasts longer
- Clarion angelfish make excellent macro subjects between manta passes
- 5mm wetsuit recommended; water temperature 22–26°C depending on season
- Nitrox recommended for extended multi-level profiles

## Safety Considerations

Open-ocean entry and exit with zodiac pickup requires SMB deployment competency. Surge on the summit can push divers onto barnacle-covered rock — buoyancy control is critical. Currents can strengthen without warning. The closest medical facilities are 30 hours away in Cabo San Lucas. Minimum 50 logged dives and Advanced certification required by all liveaboard operators.

## Photography

The Boiler is one of the world's best locations for manta ray wide-angle photography. Shoot upward with natural light filtering through the surge overhead. A wide-angle lens captures full wingspans at close range as mantas approach within arm's reach. Avoid strobes pointed directly at mantas — side or ambient lighting produces better images and is less disruptive. Clarion angelfish feeding on manta bodies make compelling close-focus wide-angle shots.
