## Hermes

A 165-foot ex-US Coast Guard buoy tender scuttled on May 15, 1984 as an artificial reef, sitting upright on a flat sandy bottom approximately 1.8 km south of Warwick Long Bay Beach.

## Overview

The Hermes was built in 1943 in Pennsylvania as a 165-foot (50 m), 254-ton buoy tender for the US Coast Guard. After being decommissioned, she was sold and converted into a freighter registered in Panama under the name Brava Fogo. In 1983, the aging freighter limped into St. George's Harbour after breaking down while en route from Rhode Island to the Cape Verde Islands, carrying second-hand goods for poor families. Unable to afford repairs, the crew abandoned her. The Bermuda government took possession and sold the vessel to the Bermuda Dive Association for one US dollar. She was cleaned and made diver-safe — hatches removed for diver access — then deliberately scuttled on May 15, 1984, approximately one mile offshore near Horseshoe Bay. Today she sits upright on a flat sandy bottom in approximately 23–24 meters (75–80 feet) of water, her mast pointing toward the surface. She is considered one of Bermuda's most photogenic wrecks due to her intact, upright orientation. (Note: the wreck shifted approximately 200 yards during Hurricane Emily in 1985.)

## Site Information

- **Location**: South Shore, approximately 1.8 km south of Warwick Long Bay Beach
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 24 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–40 meters (65–130 feet)
- **Current**: Light to moderate
- **Water Temperature**: 18–28°C (64–82°F)
- **Best Time**: May–October

## Marine Life

The Hermes hosts one of the most diverse fish communities of any Bermuda wreck. Schools of yellowtail snapper, barracuda, and gray snapper swirl around the hull. Large black grouper patrol the forward cargo hold and engine room. The mast is typically surrounded by baitfish that attract larger predators. Resident spiny lobster and moray eels occupy the internal spaces. The surrounding pristine reef adds parrotfish, angelfish, and occasional sea turtle sightings.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the deck level (approximately 18 meters) and work systematically from bow to stern before dropping to the sandy bottom at approximately 23–24 meters. The forward cargo hold, crew compartments, and engine room are all accessible given the pre-scuttling hatch removal. The mast ascending from the deck is a photogenic landmark. Allow time to circle the hull exterior and observe the surrounding reef.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive from South Shore operators. The Hermes is buoyed and visited by virtually every Bermuda operator. Giant stride entry. Ascend the mast for a natural visual reference during ascent and safety stop.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The Hermes rewards multiple dives — fresh perspectives emerge each time
- Visit the engine room for the largest grouper encounters
- The mast is one of Bermuda's most iconic safety stop venues, surrounded by fish
- Morning dives in summer benefit from the best light angles for natural light photography
- Combine with the nearby Southwest Breaker reef for a comprehensive dive day

## Safety Considerations

The 23–24 meter depth requires standard recreational diving depth management and no-decompression limit awareness. Penetration of the forward hold and engine room is accessible given the removed hatches, but good buoyancy skills are needed to avoid silt disturbance in interior spaces. Monitor your air supply carefully and begin ascent with adequate reserve.

## Photography

The Hermes is widely regarded as Bermuda's best photography wreck. The intact, upright orientation combined with outstanding visibility creates ideal conditions for wide-angle hull compositions. The mast surrounded by schooling fish is a signature shot. Interior rooms offer dramatic ambient light from removed hatches. Macro subjects abound on the hull surfaces.
