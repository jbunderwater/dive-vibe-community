## North Carolina

A 205-foot iron-hulled three-masted barque built in Scotland in 1876, sunk on January 1, 1880 when a refloating attempt went disastrously wrong, its prominent bowsprit and ghostly rows of deadeyes making it one of Bermuda's most atmospheric wreck dives.

## Overview

The North Carolina was a 205-foot (62.5 m), 533-ton iron-hulled three-masted barque built in 1876 at Macmillan & Son Shipyard in Scotland, and owned by Patton, Vickers and Co. of Liverpool. Carrying a cargo of cotton, bark, and fuel from Baltimore to Liverpool, the ship developed a damaged rudder and diverted to Bermuda for repairs. After repairs at St. George's, she departed on December 30, 1879, and promptly ran aground on the western reef on January 1, 1880. A subsequent attempt to refloat her on January 27 ended in disaster when the anchor broke free and crashed through the hull, sinking the vessel immediately. Today the wreck rests in 25–45 feet (8–14 meters) of water, with the bow and stern sections fairly intact but the mid-section collapsed. The bowsprit extends prominently forward, and rows of deadeyes — the triple-holed wooden pulleys used to tension rigging — line the deck railings in ghostly rows. Some researchers have proposed the sinking may have been intentional, though no definitive conclusion has been established.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Outer reef, approximately 8.5 miles west and 5 miles south of Gibbs Hill Lighthouse
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 14 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–30 meters (50–100 feet)
- **Current**: Light to moderate
- **Water Temperature**: 18–28°C (64–82°F)
- **Best Time**: May–October

## Marine Life

The North Carolina's intact structural features provide excellent reef habitat. The bowsprit and rigging attachment points carry dense coral and sponge encrustation. Schools of hogfish and snapper are regularly associated with this wreck. The deck area with its preserved deadeyes hosts resident angelfish and parrotfish. Spiny lobster are common in sheltered spaces beneath the hull plates.

## Dive Profile

Begin at the distinctive bowsprit for orientation, then work systematically along the hull from bow to stern, taking time to examine the rows of deadeyes and imagine the rigging configuration in its operational state. The 14-meter maximum depth allows comfortable bottom time. A safety stop over the surrounding reef provides additional viewing opportunities before surfacing.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive from Bermuda operators. Giant stride or back roll entry. Offshore location — assess weather before departure. Deploy SMB for boat pickup at the end of the dive.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Study the rigging configuration of 19th-century square-rigged barques before your dive — the deadeyes make much more sense with context
- The bowsprit extending forward from the bow is the most dramatic wide-angle photography subject
- Carry a torch to illuminate the encrusted deadeyes and bring out their color for photography
- A knowledgeable dive guide can point out lesser-visible details that make this wreck extraordinary
- Combine with other outer reef wrecks on a multi-dive day — the North Carolina's sailing ship character contrasts well with steam-era wrecks

## Safety Considerations

The 14-meter depth is appropriate for Intermediate divers. Moderate currents can develop on the outer reef — exit the water if current builds significantly. The offshore location means weather monitoring is important. Always deploy an SMB on ascent given the potential for vessel traffic in this area.

## Photography

The North Carolina is considered one of Bermuda's most photogenic wrecks precisely because of the intact bowsprit and deadeye rows. Wide-angle natural light photography at 14 meters captures the sailing ship atmosphere powerfully. The rows of deadeyes against the sandy bottom are a detail that rewards close-focus treatment. Bermuda's excellent visibility makes this site exceptional for underwater photography.
