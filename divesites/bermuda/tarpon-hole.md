## Tarpon Hole

A buoyed South Shore reef site near Elbow Beach named for the large schools of tarpon — some exceeding 2 meters — that cluster here seasonally, feeding on dense shoals of silversides in a maze of massive overhangs, arches, caves, and tunnels.

## Overview

Tarpon Hole is a series of large breakers directly off the western end of Elbow Beach on Bermuda's South Shore. The site's mooring buoy was placed in 2013, making it one of Bermuda's more recently formalized dive sites. The reef is built from a volcanic formation spanning centuries, featuring massive overhangs, caves, tunnels, and long archways that provide a naturally guided exploration route. The site takes its name from the large schools of tarpon — some individuals reportedly exceeding 2 meters (6.5 feet) — that can be found schooling here at certain times of year, feeding on myriads of silversides that pack the tunnel and arch systems. The maximum depth is approximately 17 meters (55 feet), with the upper arch and overhang zone much shallower.

## Site Information

- **Location**: South Shore, directly off western end of Elbow Beach, Bermuda
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 17 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–30 meters (50–100 feet)
- **Current**: Light
- **Water Temperature**: 18–28°C (64–82°F)
- **Best Time**: May–October

## Marine Life

Tarpon Hole is particularly known for ocean tarpon, which school here in significant numbers at certain seasons and can be very inquisitive toward divers. Horse-eye jacks and bar jacks also school in the arches and tunnels. Large gray and mutton snapper are regular residents under the coral arches. The overhangs and tunnel systems support grouper, French angelfish, and butter hamlet. Silversides pack the enclosed spaces in summer, attracting the tarpon that give this site its name.

## Dive Profile

Enter the reef system from the mooring and work through the natural channel network, passing under the arches and through tunnels at various depths from the surface down to 17 meters. The channels and tunnels provide natural navigation through the site. Spend time hovering in the larger enclosed spaces watching the tarpon and jack schools. The safety stop can be conducted at the shallow reef top.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive from South Shore operators. The site is buoyed, simplifying location and mooring. Giant stride or back roll entry. Deploy SMB for surface pickup. South Shore conditions — wind and swell from the southwest — should be assessed before the dive.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The arch swim-throughs are the defining feature — take time to approach from different angles for variety
- Tarpon schools are most commonly encountered at certain seasons — ask local operators about current conditions before booking
- Silversides in the tunnels in summer create spectacular bait-ball conditions
- Morning dives are recommended for the calmest South Shore conditions
- Combine with the nearby Hermes wreck for a contrasting paired dive day
- This newer site is somewhat less impacted than older high-traffic Bermuda sites

## Safety Considerations

The combination of arches and caves means divers can briefly lose visual contact with buddies. Agree on navigation plans before descending and maintain frequent buddy checks. The 17-meter maximum depth is accessible but requires standard recreational depth awareness. Monitor air supply during extended exploration of the cave and tunnel system.

## Photography

The coral arches and caves are natural photographic frames for wide-angle compositions with tarpon or jacks silhouetted against the light. Schools of silversides in Bermuda's clear water create impressive ambient light images. Morning sun angles illuminate the arch interiors most dramatically. Tarpon at close range are among the most spectacular subjects at this site.
