## Montana

A 236-foot Confederate blockade runner that grounded on December 30, 1863, its twin paddlewheel frames and twin boilers resting in 9 meters beside the companion wreck of the Constellation.

## Overview

The Montana was a 236-foot (72 m) paddlewheel steamer built in Glasgow, Scotland, operated as a blockade runner for the Confederacy during the American Civil War — carrying arms, ammunition, and contraband between England, Bermuda, and Confederate ports under various aliases including Nola, Gloria, and Paramount. On December 30, 1863, she struck a reef near the opening of Western Blue Cut on Bermuda's northwest side and sank after an approximately 10-foot breach opened below the waterline. The crew and much of the cargo escaped. Today the two steam boilers and twin paddlewheel frames lying on their sides are the most recognizable features of the wreck, heavily encrusted with soft and hard corals. The paddlewheels nearly reach the surface at low tide. The Montana sits close to the WWII-era Constellation, and the two wrecks are typically dived together as one of Bermuda's famous paired-wreck experiences.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Northwest Bermuda reef, near Western Blue Cut
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 9 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–30 meters (50–100 feet)
- **Current**: Light
- **Water Temperature**: 18–28°C (64–82°F)
- **Best Time**: May–October

## Marine Life

The Montana wreck supports a year-round population of large gray snappers around the boilers and paddlewheel frames. Parrotfish, angelfish, and blue tangs occupy the coral heads surrounding the wreck. The shallow sandy bottom and surrounding reef attract diverse reef fish typical of Bermuda's outer platform.

## Dive Profile

The two prominent steam boilers are the defining navigational features of the wreck. Explore the paddlewheel frames lying on their sides, then visit the bow section which remains relatively intact and heavily coral-encrusted. Cross to the adjacent Constellation wreck on the same dive to cover both historical sites. The 9-meter depth provides ample bottom time within recreational limits.

## Entry and Exit

The site is buoyed under the Bermuda Dive Sites program. Boat dive from any Bermuda operator — this is one of the island's most frequently visited sites. Giant stride entry. Snorkelers can also explore this site given its shallowness.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The paddlewheel frames are the most dramatic structural features — circle them for perspective on 19th-century steam paddle technology
- Swim across to the Constellation wreck to complete the paired-wreck experience
- The boilers at this site are easily distinguishable even for first-time wreck divers
- Morning dives provide the calmest conditions at this northwest offshore site
- The wreck is protected under the Bermuda Dive Sites program with a 500-meter no-fishing zone

## Safety Considerations

At 9 meters, depth hazards are minimal. The primary concerns are the offshore location — weather changes can make the return boat trip uncomfortable. Assess morning conditions carefully. Always stay with the group at this relatively exposed site. The wreck is a protected site — do not disturb any artifacts or structural elements.

## Photography

The paddlewheel frames and twin boilers are iconic photographic subjects that illustrate 19th-century steam paddle technology. Wide-angle natural light photography at 9 meters is highly effective. A split-level shot through the surface in calm conditions captures both the reef environment and the boat above.
