## Pelinaion

A 385-foot Greek cargo steamer that struck St. David's Head reef on January 16, 1940 when the lighthouse was blacked out as a wartime security measure, with its massive triple-expansion engine standing nearly upright in the shallows.

## Overview

The Pelinaion was a 385-foot (117 m), 4,291-ton cargo steamer built at Port Glasgow in 1907. She changed ownership multiple times before being purchased by a Greek shipping company and renamed Pelinaion in 1939. Carrying iron ore from West Africa to Baltimore, she struck the reef off St. David's Head on January 16, 1940. Captain Janis Valikos — on his final voyage before retirement and having passed Bermuda many times — could not locate the island because St. David's Lighthouse had been blacked out as a wartime security measure against German observation. The ship split into two sections on the reef and now lies scattered one mile offshore in depths ranging from 6 to 21 meters (20–70 feet). Most notable is the massive triple-expansion engine standing nearly upright — visible as a shadow from the surface on clear days — along with giant steam boilers and a spare propeller still strapped to the deck. The bow lies in approximately 6 meters of water, accessible to snorkelers.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Off St. David's Head, East End, Bermuda
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–30 meters (50–100 feet)
- **Current**: Light to moderate
- **Water Temperature**: 18–28°C (64–82°F)
- **Best Time**: May–October

## Marine Life

The Pelinaion's scattered remains support diverse reef life across multiple depth zones. The shallow bow section attracts snorkelers and hosts parrotfish and angelfish in the surrounding reef. Larger structural components in deeper water provide habitat for schools of barracuda, snapper, and resident grouper. The prominent propeller at the stern section is a gathering point for marine life. Spiny lobster are consistently found in the wreck's enclosed spaces. Hawksbill turtles occasionally visit the East End reef system.

## Dive Profile

Begin at the massive engine block near the surface for orientation — its scale and upright profile are immediately impressive. Descend along the hull to the deeper sections at 15–20 meters, exploring the triple-expansion engine, steam boilers, propeller, and hull swim-throughs. The bow in approximately 6 meters provides an easy shallow finish before ascending for the safety stop.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive from East End Bermuda operators or operators offering island-wide wreck tours. The St. David's Head location is on the windward East End — weather assessment is particularly important before departing. Giant stride entry and SMB deployment on ascent.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The triple-expansion engine nearly breaking the surface is an extraordinary spectacle visible even from the boat
- The spare propeller still strapped to the deck is an unusual feature unique to this wreck
- The East End wreck area offers a less-crowded alternative to the busier North Shore and northwest sites
- Research the WWII blackout context before diving — it adds poignancy to this preventable tragedy
- A knowledgeable East End guide can point out hull details most easily missed on a solo navigation dive

## Safety Considerations

The East End of Bermuda is more exposed to Atlantic swell than the North Shore. Wave action can make entry and exit difficult in windy conditions. Monitor surface conditions throughout the dive. The 20-meter maximum depth requires standard Intermediate no-decompression management. Always deploy an SMB on ascent.

## Photography

The near-surface engine block creates a dramatic natural light composition visible from the surface and even more powerful when photographed from below. Wide-angle shots of the scattered hull in Bermuda's clear water capture the scale of this large vessel's remains. The spare propeller strapped to the deck and the steam boilers are compelling close-focus subjects.
