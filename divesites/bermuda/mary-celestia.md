## Mary Celestia

A fast Confederate blockade runner sunk at 13 knots in 1864, uniquely preserving one intact paddle wheel and a cargo of Civil War-era artifacts in Bermuda's southern reef.

## Overview

The Mary Celestia was an iron-hulled sidewheel paddle steamer built in Liverpool, England, designed by William C. Miller and Sons and launched in February 1864. She measured approximately 225 feet (68.5 meters) and was capable of 17 knots. She served as a Confederate blockade runner during the American Civil War under various aliases including "Marie Celeste" and "Bijou," running guns, ammunition, and supplies to Wilmington, North Carolina. On September 6, 1864, while piloted by Bermudian pilot John Bristow Virgin and cruising Bermuda's South Shore at approximately 13 knots on a flat calm day, she struck a blind reef ("boiler rock") and sank almost immediately. The cause of the accident on a calm day with an experienced pilot has been discussed but never conclusively determined. The wreck rests at approximately 17 meters (55 feet), and is the only wreck in Bermuda retaining one of its paddle wheels intact. In 2011, a storm removed sand from the bow section; marine archaeologists subsequently uncovered sealed bottles of French wine and two perfume bottles embossed "Piesse and Lubin London." A bottle of the wine was later opened and assessed at the Charleston Wine + Food Festival. The perfume was analyzed and recreated; it is now sold in Bermuda.

## Marine Life

The Mary Celestia's sand patch setting surrounded by reef creates a mixed habitat. The well-preserved paddle wheel, anchor, and bow structure provide hard substrate that attracts encrusting corals and sheltering fish. Resident spiny lobster occupy the structural spaces. South Shore reef fish species typical of the area are present around the wreck. No site-specific marine life census exists; species reflect the general South Shore reef community near Gibbs Hill Lighthouse.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the sandy bottom around the wreck and orient using the prominent intact paddle wheel. Work along the wreck structure taking in the hull iron, engine components, and preserved cargo areas in the bow. The 17-meter depth is comfortable for Intermediate divers. A natural safety stop over the surrounding reef in 5 meters adds time to observe the reef fish community.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive from South Shore operators. The wreck is a protected historic site under Bermuda law. Giant stride or back roll entry. The location near the South Shore may experience afternoon swell — morning dives are typically calmer.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The intact paddle wheel is the defining feature of this wreck — spend significant time examining it
- The bow section where archaeological discoveries were made in 2011 is historically significant
- Photographs of wine bottle discoveries and excavation details are available online for context before your dive
- South Shore can develop significant swell — check marine forecasts and choose morning dive times
- This is a protected wreck — no artifacts may be removed or disturbed

## Safety Considerations

This is a protected heritage site under Bermuda law. Handling, removing, or disturbing any artifacts is illegal and will result in serious consequences. The South Shore location can develop moderate swell and current — abort the dive if conditions deteriorate. Always dive with a buddy and carry an SMB for surface signaling.

## Photography

The Mary Celestia is one of Bermuda's most historically compelling photography subjects. The intact paddle wheel is a unique and immediately recognizable element. The iron hull structure against the white sand bottom photographs beautifully in Bermuda's clear water. A video light or single strobe enhances the detail of artifact-bearing areas near the bow without disturbing the archaeological context.
