## King George

A 171-foot Edwardian dredger built in 1910 and scuttled in 2017, sitting fully upright and intact in 18 meters near North Rock — one of Bermuda's most complete artificial reef wrecks.

## Overview

The King George was a 171-foot hopper dredger built in 1910 at the Lobnitz and Co. yard in Glasgow, Scotland, specifically for the Bermuda government. She arrived in Bermuda on June 28, 1911, and was set to work dredging Town Cut, St. George's — one of three dredgers commissioned to deepen the island's harbour channels for safe navigation. She continued working Bermuda's channels for nearly two decades, with her 30 buckets dredging to a depth of 30 feet, then re-fitted to deepen the channel a further four feet. In 1930 she was replaced by the Lord Cochrane and retired. The Bermuda government took possession of the vessel in 2014, and on April 4, 2017, the King George was towed approximately nine miles off the North Shore near North Rock Beacon and scuttled as a purpose-sunk dive site. She now sits fully upright and intact in approximately 18 meters (60 feet) of water on a sandy bottom, with ladders, catwalks, engines, winches, and forward holds all accessible to divers.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Off North Shore, approximately 9 miles from shore near North Rock Beacon, Bermuda
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive (artificial reef)
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 18 meters (60 feet)
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–30 meters (50–100 feet)
- **Current**: Light to moderate
- **Water Temperature**: 18–28°C (64–82°F)
- **Best Time**: May–October

## Marine Life

As a relatively recently scuttled (2017) artificial reef, the King George is in the early stages of marine colonization. Dive Bermuda at Grotto Bay operators report lobster, octopus, and seahorses at the site. The full complement of North Shore reef species — including snapper, grunts, and passing pelagics — is expected to increase as the wreck matures. No long-established species census exists for this site.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the deck level and work systematically from bow to stern on this upright, intact vessel. The accessible ladders, catwalks, engine room, winches, and forward holds reward a thorough circuit. The intact structure means excellent navigation reference points throughout the dive. Safety stop over the surrounding sandy bottom before ascending.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive from Bermuda operators, particularly East End and North Shore based operators. The site is buoyed and relatively new, so fewer operators run it regularly — confirm with your operator before booking specifically for this site. Giant stride entry. Deploy SMB on ascent.

## Tips and Recommendations

- This is one of Bermuda's newest purpose-sunk wrecks — expect pristine structure and growing marine life
- The fully intact nature means all structural features (ladder runways, catwalks, engine room) are accessible
- North Shore offshore conditions can be variable — assess the morning forecast before departure
- The site is less crowded than older, more famous Bermuda wrecks

## Safety Considerations

The 18-meter depth requires standard Intermediate no-decompression awareness. The North Shore location nine miles offshore means weather monitoring is important before departure. Inside the dredger's holds, maintain good buoyancy to avoid silt disturbance. Deploy SMB before ascending.

## Photography

The fully upright, intact dredger structure provides excellent framing for wide-angle wreck photography. The catwalks and engine room offer industrial composition elements. As coral and sponge colonization grows over time, the photographic interest of this site will increase substantially. Natural light at 18 meters in Bermuda's clear North Shore water is effective.
