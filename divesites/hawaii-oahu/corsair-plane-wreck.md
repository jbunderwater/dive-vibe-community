## Corsair Plane Wreck

The Corsair Plane Wreck is a remarkably intact Vought F4U-1A fighter aircraft resting upright on the sandy seafloor of Maunalua Bay, off Oahu's southeast coast—one of the most photogenic wreck dives in Hawaii.

## Overview

Located approximately 3 miles offshore from Hawaii Kai marina, the Corsair rests upright at a depth of approximately 110–115 feet, its tail pointing toward Koko Head. The aircraft is 33 feet 4 inches long, 16 feet 1 inch high, with a 41-foot wingspan, and has remained largely intact since sinking decades ago. Visibility at the site typically runs 80–100 feet, making photography conditions exceptional.

## Historical Background

Pacific Wrecks records this aircraft as Bureau Number 49668, a Vought F4U-1A operated by VMF-215 of the U.S. Marine Corps. On April 17, 1945, 2nd Lt. William H. Holden was piloting the aircraft on a transit from Molokai Airfield to Ewa Airfield on Oahu when the engine failed. He ditched the aircraft just off Oahu's southeast shore and was rescued. The bent-back propeller blades are consistent with a controlled water landing rather than a crash impact. Note: one dive operator source (oahudiving.com) cites the year as 1946 rather than 1945—the Pacific Wrecks military records database gives April 17, 1945 as the documented date.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Maunalua Bay, 3 miles offshore from Hawaii Kai marina
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 34 meters
- **Visibility**: Typically 80–100 feet
- **Penetration**: Not permitted—exterior-only dive

## Marine Life

The sandy seafloor around the Corsair hosts garden eels, Hawaiian stingrays, frogfish, leaf scorpionfish, and moray eels. The aircraft structure itself provides shelter for octopus and various reef fish. Galapagos sharks and amberjacks have been reported in the water column above the wreck.

## Diving Notes

The depth places this firmly in Advanced territory. Open Water divers can view the aircraft from above at approximately 60 feet; reaching the plane itself requires Advanced certification. Bottom time at depth is approximately 14–16 minutes before ascent. No penetration of the aircraft is permitted.
