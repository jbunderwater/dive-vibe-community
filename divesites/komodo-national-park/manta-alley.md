## Manta Alley

Manta Alley is a rocky channel at the far southern tip of Komodo Island where reef manta rays aggregate at cleaning stations, with multiple mantas common on a single dive during peak season. Strong currents and cold thermoclines make this a challenging but rewarding dive.

## Overview

The site consists of shallow rocky sections and a small channel cut through rock where mantas feed and use cleaning stations. The shallower areas host cleaning stations at 5–15 metres; sandy channels descend to around 20–25 metres, with a maximum depth of 40 metres. Water temperature is notably cooler here than at northern Komodo sites, typically 22–23°C, which can make for cold dives.

**Peak season note: Sources conflict.** Dragon Dive Komodo lists November to June as the best period for southern Komodo including Manta Alley. DiveHappy does not specify a single peak window but notes that cold, plankton-rich conditions that attract mantas are driven by southern ocean currents. Mantas can be seen year-round at this site, though sightings are less reliable during the hottest months. Planning a visit between October and March covers the period most consistently mentioned across sources.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Southern tip of Komodo Island, Komodo National Park, East Nusa Tenggara, Indonesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Drift dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 40 meters
- **Typical Depth**: 5–25 meters (mantas concentrate at shallower cleaning stations)
- **Water Temperature**: 22–23°C (cooler than northern Komodo sites)
- **Current**: Strong; variable direction

## Marine Life

Reef manta rays at cleaning stations are the sole confirmed primary attraction. DiveHappy documented seven mantas on one dive, including a black manta individual. Multiple mantas simultaneously at the channel cleaning stations at 22 metres and in shallower water are confirmed. The cooler, nutrient-rich water supports invertebrate communities typical of southern Komodo. No site-specific confirmation of other pelagic species (trevally, sweetlips, mobula rays) at this exact channel location — these are plausible regional species but not documented specifically at Manta Alley in available sources.
