## Manta Point dive site

Premium manta ray encounter site with long shallow drift and cleaning stations.

## Overview

Manta Point (also called Makassar Reef) is the longest manta dive site in Komodo National Park—nearly 2.5 km of continuous reef—and features approximately 17 documented cleaning stations where reef manta rays congregate to feed and be cleaned by small reef fish. The site offers a gentle, slow-moving drift dive through healthy coral gardens with frequent manta encounters year-round, though the rainy season (November–April) sees the largest aggregations due to plankton blooms. This is one of the park's most photographed and accessible sites for all diving levels.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Central Komodo, Makassar Strait
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Drift dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 15 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15-30 meters (50-100 feet) dry season; 10-20m wet season
- **Current**: Mild to moderate drift
- **Best Time**: November to April (peak manta season); year-round diving possible

## Marine Life

Reef manta rays are the star attraction, with excellent opportunities to photograph them feeding and interacting with cleaner fish. Additional marine life includes reef sharks (white-tip and black-tip), sea turtles, barracuda, trevally, napoleon wrasse, jacks, fusiliers, clownfish, and numerous reef fish species.

## Dive Profile

The site is a drift dive in the shallower range (10–15 meters), making it accessible to less experienced divers. The reef runs along a gently sloping sandy plateau. Most of the manta action occurs in the 5–15-meter zone. Divers typically enter upstream and drift with the current while scanning above and below for mantas, then exit via the dive boat at the predetermined pickup point.

## Entry and Exit

Giant stride entry from the dive boat is standard. The dive follows the reef as it drifts in the current. Exit is via pickup by the dive boat; always deploy a surface marker buoy (SMB) well in advance so the crew can position the boat safely.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Book with operators familiar with manta behavior to maximize encounters
- Arrive early in the rainy season (November–April) for the highest manta counts
- Use a wide-angle lens for manta photography—they are large and often close
- Maintain a steady depth to keep mantas in frame as they drift
- Avoid sudden movements; mantas are shy and will flee if startled
- Listen to your guide's specific briefing about current direction and expected drift distance

## Safety Considerations

Currents are generally mild at this site but can vary with tides. Be prepared to adjust depth if current increases. Avoid touching the reef or disturbing the manta rays. Be aware of jellyfish and sea urchins, particularly on the reef. Dive within your certification limits.

## Photography

Excellent wide-angle opportunities when mantas pass close overhead. Their wing-spans and feeding behavior provide dramatic compositions. Early morning dives typically offer the best light for reef and manta photography. Macro opportunities exist with smaller reef fish and invertebrates.
