# Salt Pier

*Bonaire's industrial-reef pier dive at the Cargill salt works — colorful sponge- and coral-encrusted pilings from 5 to 15 meters, with confirmed seahorses, frogfish, barracuda schools, sea turtles, and cubera snapper. Access prohibited when ships are docking.*

## Overview

Salt Pier is one of Bonaire's most distinctive dives: a working industrial pier whose concrete support pilings have been colonized by corals and sponges over decades, creating dense artificial reef habitat in shallow water. The site is managed by Cargill Salt Works; diving is only permitted when no ships are loading salt. The reef slope begins at approximately 10 meters (32 feet) off the pilings and extends to about 15 meters in the deeper sections. The site is an "underwater castle" of encrusted columns with minimal current and easy shore entry — one of Bonaire's most beginner-friendly yet photographically rich dives.

## Site Information

- **Location**: South-central Bonaire, at the Cargill salt pier (operated by Cargill Salt Works)
- **Entry Type**: Shore entry (sandy beach, north or south side of pier)
- **Site Type**: Pier/artificial reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 15 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–30 meters
- **Current**: Minimal
- **Best Time**: Morning or afternoon (check ship schedule first)

## Marine Life

Multiple sources confirm the following species at Salt Pier specifically: seahorses, frogfish, octopuses, moray eels, and territorial damselfish nests on and around the pilings. Larger visitors include schools of barracuda, sea turtles, grunts, snappers, cubera snapper, and spotted eagle rays. Colorful blennies and gobies inhabit the encrusted surfaces. The reef slope features low-density hard corals, sponges, and gorgonians. The pillars themselves create a sheltered microhabitat distinct from the open reef.

## Dive Profile

Enter from the sandy beach on either the north or south side of the pier. Swim out to the closest pilings and descend alongside the columns. The reef slope begins at 10 meters off the base of the pilings. Most marine life is found at 5–15 meters depth among the encrusted columns. The shallow depth permits extended bottom time. Both sides of the pier offer slightly different viewing angles on the pilings.

## Entry and Exit

Easy sandy beach entry on either the north or south side. The southern side is preferable in rougher wave conditions. Exit at the same point. **Do not dive when ships are loading** — this is both prohibited and dangerous. Check the pier from shore before gearing up. A $40 USD annual nature fee (STINAPA Bonaire National Marine Park) is required.

## Tips

- Always check from the road whether a ship is at the pier before gearing up
- Morning and afternoon both work — the light on the pilings changes throughout the day
- Both sides of the pier are worth exploring; the marine life distribution differs
- A macro lens captures the frogfish, seahorses, and blennies living on the encrusted surfaces

## Safety

**Never dive when ships are present** — the restriction is strictly enforced and the risk from propeller wash and anchor chain movement is real. The pilings may have sharp edges; avoid contact. Use normal buoyancy care around the encrusted surfaces.

## Photography

The illuminated pilings in morning and afternoon light create dramatic architectural compositions. Frogfish and seahorses are the macro signature shots. Barracuda schooling between the columns frame well in wide-angle. A strobe or video light recovers the reds and oranges of the sponge growth.
