# Karpata

*North Bonaire wall dive in the national marine park — concrete staircase entry, outstanding hard coral formations, and a reef slope with sand chutes dropping to 45 meters. Known for turtles and chromis schools.*

## Overview

Karpata is located on the northwest coast of Bonaire, just above 1000 Steps and within the Bonaire National Marine Park. The site features a fringing reef with pronounced buttresses and sand channels running from the shallows down to 45 meters — the reef architecture creates distinct corridors and channels rather than a continuous wall face. The entry is via a concrete staircase from a designated boarding point. Sources describe the coral formations as among the most intact on this section of the northwest coast. Visibility ranges 20–30 meters; water temperature approximately 26°C.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Northwest Bonaire, within Bonaire National Marine Park, just north of 1000 Steps
- **Entry Type**: Shore entry via concrete staircase
- **Site Type**: Wall dive (reef buttresses and sand chutes)
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 40+ meters (sand chutes; most recreational diving at 10–25 meters)
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–30 meters
- **Current**: Generally manageable; can be strong occasionally
- **Best Time**: Early morning

## Marine Life

Documented species at Karpata include hawksbill turtles (regular sightings), large chromis schools, parrotfish, triggerfish (doctor fish), and hard and soft coral assemblages. Eagle rays are reported. The reef supports broad reef fish diversity typical of the northern protected coast. No comprehensive site-specific survey was found; the species list reflects what multiple local sources report for this specific site.

## Dive Profile

Descend the concrete staircase to the entry point. The reef slope begins immediately, with sand chutes and buttresses creating channels that funnel down to deeper water. The most interesting structure and marine life concentration is in the 10–25 meter range, where the coral formations are at their most developed. The sand chutes provide navigation landmarks for return to the entry point.

## Entry and Exit

Concrete staircase entry from the road. The staircase descends to the water; exercise standard caution on wet concrete steps. Bonaire Nature Tag required. Best visited early morning for calmer conditions and fewer divers.

## Tips

- Early morning for best light and least company
- The sand chutes serve as reliable landmarks for orientation
- Look for turtles on the reef slope, particularly feeding on sponges
- Worth combining with 1000 Steps in a two-dive day on the northern coast

## Safety

Currents can pick up at this exposed north-coast location — check conditions before entering. The sand chutes drop deep; maintain depth awareness when following them.

## Photography

The reef buttresses and sand chutes create dramatic perspective shots. Chromis schools provide soft-light wide-angle compositions. Hawksbill turtles are reliably encountered and usually tolerant of patient photographers. Morning light enters the reef face favorably.
