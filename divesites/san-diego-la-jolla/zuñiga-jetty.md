## Zuñiga Jetty

Zuñiga Jetty is a shallow artificial reef dive at the mouth of San Diego Bay — a rocky granite structure extending 7,500 feet from Coronado's northern tip toward Point Loma, attracting abundant reef fish, California sea lions, and seasonal spiny lobster.

## Overview

Zuñiga Jetty (also spelled Zuniga) marks the southern boundary of the San Diego Bay entrance channel. The jetty was constructed from large granite boulders, and its east (sheltered) face is the primary dive zone. The rock-to-sand interface at the base of the jetty structure draws in a classic Southern California reef fish assemblage in relatively shallow, calm water — typically 3–9 metres (10–30 feet). This is a fish-watching and lobster site rather than a dramatic wall or wreck dive; its appeal lies in accessible marine life and sea lion encounters.

**Important access restriction**: Diving inside San Diego Bay (between Ballast Point and Zuñiga Point) is illegal and divers have been ticketed. Access the ocean-facing side of the jetty's tip only. Due to constant Naval and commercial vessel traffic at San Diego Bay's entrance, a dive flag and surface tender are mandatory.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Northern tip of Coronado Island, at the mouth of San Diego Bay
- **Entry Type**: Shore entry
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 10 meters
- **Water Temperature**: 15–20°C (59–68°F); 5–7mm wetsuit

## Marine Life

Sand bass (barred sand bass), calico bass (kelp bass), sheephead, and opaleye are abundant along the rock-to-sand seam. Halibut lie on the sandy bottom on both sides of the jetty. California spiny lobster congregate under rocks in season (October–March open season). Swell sharks and horn sharks rest in crevices. Garibaldi appear near shallower rocky sections. Señoritas and other wrasse-family fish are constant. California sea lions haul out on the outer jetty rocks and enter the water regularly — they can be territorial; do not approach or corner them. Harbor seals also visit occasionally.

## Dive Profile

The jetty slope is gradual. The most productive zone is the rock-to-sand seam in 3–9 metres, working parallel to the jetty structure. Dive on a slack-to-incoming tide for the clearest water — outgoing tide flushes murky bay water offshore. Carry a torch for examining lobster cavities and shark crevices.

## Safety Considerations

This site sits at the entrance to one of the US Navy's busiest ports. Boat, submarine, and Naval vessel traffic is constant and unpredictable. Fly a dive flag and have a dedicated surface tender at all times. Tidal currents at the bay entrance can be significant — stick to the slack window (±1 hour around slack tide). Sea lions can be aggressive if cornered — give them wide berth. Do not dive inside the bay.
