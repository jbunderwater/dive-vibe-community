## HMCS Yukon (DDE 263)

HMCS Yukon is a massive 366-foot Royal Canadian Navy destroyer escort intentionally sunk in July 2000 at Wreck Alley off Mission Beach — San Diego's most famous and most demanding wreck dive.

## Overview

The HMCS Yukon (pennant DDE 263) was a Mackenzie-class destroyer escort commissioned by the Royal Canadian Navy on 25 May 1963. Decommissioned in 1993, she was sold to the San Diego Oceans Foundation and deliberately sunk at Sunken Harbor off Mission Beach on 14 July 2000 to create an artificial reef. The sinking did not go as planned: the night before the ceremony, the vessel flooded in rough weather and rolled onto her port side — leaving her masthead at approximately 18 metres (60 feet) instead of the planned 9 metres (30 feet). The ship now rests on her port side in 30 metres (100 feet) of water.

Before sinking, the ship was prepared with extensive diver-safety modifications: dozens of "Swiss-cheese" access holes were cut into the hull and interior bulkheads were removed to ensure divers always have multiple exit routes. The vessel is 366 feet (111.6m) long with a 42-foot (12.8m) beam and contains six decks and over 100 compartments.

**Safety warning:** At least five divers have died on this wreck. Conditions inside are cold, dark, disorienting, and can be surgy. The ship lying on its side means every internal compartment is rotated 90 degrees — floors become walls. This is an Advanced-only site; wreck penetration requires specialist training and certification.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Wreck Alley, approximately 2 miles west of Mission Beach, San Diego
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive (charter from Mission Bay)
- **Site Type**: Wreck
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 metres (100 feet); top of wreck at approximately 18 metres (60 feet)
- **Water Temperature**: 15–19°C (59–66°F); hood and gloves required

## Key Features

The intact superstructure includes the bridge, deck gear, and twin three-inch gun turrets (forward and aft). Notable external features include dolphin cutouts on the bow and the mortar well near the stern. The Burma Road passageway on Deck 3 is a famous interior feature. The engine room, visible through cut access panels, contains machinery in remarkable condition. Collapsed superstructure sections add navigation complexity that demands experience and a guide for first-time visitors.

## Marine Life

The wreck has been heavily colonized since 2000. Lingcod, rockfish, cabezon, wolf eels, garibaldi, sheephead, kelp bass, and señorita are resident year-round. Strawberry anemones (Corynactis californica) and white metridium anemones coat the outer hull and interior surfaces. California spiny lobster shelter in hull cavities. Sea lions from nearby haul-out points occasionally visit. Best visibility is July through October, when upwelling relaxes and water clarity improves.

## Dive Profile

Descend the mooring line to the deck (approximately 18–23 metres) and begin exploration of one section — the ship is too large to cover in a single dive. Most divers focus on the forward gun, bridge, or stern on separate dives. The three-dimensional nature of the wreck, combined with its sideways orientation, can cause rapid inadvertent depth gain. Monitor depth and gas supply constantly. Plan a safety stop and a conservative ascent — surface boat traffic in summer requires careful ascent procedure with an SMB.

## Safety Considerations

Advanced Open Water certification is the minimum; Wreck Diver certification strongly recommended before any penetration. Carry redundant lights for interior sections. Cold thermoclines (water can drop to 13°C at depth) reduce thermal margin. Never dive solo. Book with an experienced local charter operator who knows the wreck.
