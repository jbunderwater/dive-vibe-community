## La Jolla Cove

La Jolla Cove is San Diego's most iconic shore dive — a protected rocky reef and kelp forest inside the Matlahuayl State Marine Reserve, famous for year-round sea lions and summer aggregations of leopard sharks.

## Overview

La Jolla Cove sits within the Matlahuayl State Marine Reserve, giving it legal protection that has allowed marine life to thrive for decades. The cove features a rocky reef and kelp forest beginning just offshore, with multiple distinct habitats accessible to beginners: shallow seagrass beds, rocky reef slopes, towering kelp forest, and the famous "Seven Caves" — sea caves carved into the sandstone cliffs at the water's edge. Maximum depth in the immediate cove is approximately 12–15 metres; some divers venture farther outside the cove to 15–18 metres in kelp beds.

Calm morning conditions — before sea breezes pick up — offer the best visibility and the easiest entry. The sandy entry beach slopes gently into the water.

## Site Information

- **Location**: La Jolla Cove, San Diego (32°51'01.9"N 117°16'21.4"W) — at the foot of Coast Blvd, La Jolla
- **Entry Type**: Shore entry
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 15 meters
- **Water Temperature**: 15–19°C (59–66°F) year-round; 5–7mm wetsuit recommended

## Marine Life

**Leopard Sharks**: Summer aggregations (June–December, peak August–September) bring leopard sharks (Triakis semifasciata) into the warm shallow waters of the cove and adjacent La Jolla Shores, where they congregate on sandy patches at 2–8 metres to thermoregulate and mate. Harmless toward divers.

**California Sea Lions**: Year-round resident colony. Juveniles are playful and approach divers — maintain a respectful distance and avoid cornering them. Sea lions can be startlingly fast and curious.

**Garibaldi**: California's state marine fish is abundant throughout the rocky reef. Bright orange adults are territorial and will actively approach divers.

**Fish and Invertebrates**: Opaleye, halfmoon, sheephead, kelp bass, and various rockfish shelter in reef crevices. Bat rays and horn sharks hunt the sandy patches. California moray eels and octopus inhabit rocky ledges. Nudibranchs, anemones, and sea urchins colonize every hard surface.

## Site Features

The Seven Caves are sandstone sea caves accessible by kayak from above and by divers from below. Buoyancy control is important inside the caves — divers should have basic skills before attempting the cave passages. The main reef slope descends from about 3 metres at the cove edges to 12–15 metres. Kelp forest begins just outside the cove mouth and extends northward toward La Jolla Shores.

## Dive Profile

Enter from the small sandy beach at the base of the stairs. Descend along the reef slope in 3–6 metres before working deeper. Early morning dives (before 9am) typically offer the best conditions: calmer water, better visibility, and more sea lion activity. Plan dives for 60+ minutes in the 5–15 metre range. Surge is minimal on calm days; during swell, conditions can be challenging even for intermediate divers.
