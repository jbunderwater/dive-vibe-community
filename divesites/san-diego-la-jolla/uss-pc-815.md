## USS PC-815

USS PC-815 is a challenging deep wreck dive featuring the remains of a World War II naval patrol craft that sank in a dramatic collision.

## Overview

The USS PC-815 was a sub-chaser commissioned in April 1943 with an unfortunate history of accidents—earning it the nickname the "jinxed sub-chaser." On September 11, 1945, the vessel collided with the destroyer USS Laffey in dense fog off San Diego Bay. The Laffey struck the PC-815 just aft of the bridge on the port side, causing the patrol craft to catch fire and sink within five minutes with one sailor lost. Navy demolition teams later demolished the wreck in November 1945, reducing it to scattered remains on the seafloor. This is an advanced-only site requiring excellent air management and deep diving experience.

## Site Information

- **Location**: 32°37'54"N, 117°14'12"W — off San Diego Bay, approximately 2 miles southwest of Point Loma

## Wreck History

The USS PC-815 was a PC-461-class submarine chaser built at Albina Engine and Machine Works in Portland, Oregon, commissioned April 20, 1943. She displaced 295 tons and could reach 20 knots. L. Ron Hubbard — later founder of Scientology — held command of the vessel for approximately 80 days in 1943. His tenure was marked by two major controversies: he reported sinking Japanese submarines off Cape Lookout, Oregon, a claim investigated by Vice Admiral Frank Jack Fletcher and dismissed ("there was no submarine in the area"), and he was relieved of command after conducting unauthorized gunnery exercises in Mexican territorial waters near the Coronado Islands in June 1943.

After Hubbard's departure, PC-815 continued as a shore patrol vessel off San Diego. On September 11, 1945, the vessel collided with the destroyer USS *Laffey* in dense fog off San Diego Bay. The collision started a fire; PC-815 sank within five minutes with one crew member lost. Navy demolition teams demolished the wreck in November 1945 to remove the navigation hazard from the busy shipping channel. The scattered remains now rest in 27 metres of water.

## Marine Life

Despite being a debris field rather than an intact wreck, the metal structure supports typical San Diego bottom species. Calico bass, kelp bass, sheephead, and opaleye have been reported around the debris. California spiny lobster shelter under hull sections. On excellent visibility days, garibaldi and various reef fish are visible. The sandy bottom between debris pieces hosts leopard sharks (seasonal) and halibut.

## Dive Profile

The site consists primarily of a scattered debris field — Navy demolition in 1945, followed by a 1983 detonation equivalent to a 2.8 magnitude earthquake, has flattened most structures with air pockets. Divers explore engine parts, hull plating, and scattered metal pieces across the sandy bottom at 27 metres. Visibility is highly variable — typically 5–10 metres, but has been reported at 40+ feet (12+ m) on excellent days. The debris field nature means exploration is relatively open rather than penetration-style.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access via charter from Mission Bay or Point Loma area. Marissa Charters and other San Diego operators run trips to this site. The wreck is not marked with a buoy — navigation to the GPS coordinates and use of a descent line are required.

## Tips and Recommendations

This is an advanced dive primarily of historical interest rather than structural spectacle. Experienced wreck divers who want to visit a WWII "jinxed sub-chaser" will find a flat debris field rather than photogenic wreck architecture. Bring a torch for examining debris. Best visited on flat-calm days for optimum visibility.

## Safety Considerations

Advanced divers only. 27 metres requires careful gas management and controlled ascent rate with safety stop. The site sits in a busy shipping lane approach — maintain surface marker buoy discipline. Unexploded ordnance may be present: do not handle any metal objects found on the site. Moderate current possible; check pre-dive.
