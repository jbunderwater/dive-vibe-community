## Indra (Wrack)

Indra (Wrack) is a historic wreck dive in North Carolina, North America.

## Overview

The USS Indra (ARL-37) was a 330-foot landing craft repair ship that served in the Vietnam War before being transferred to North Carolina and sunk as an artificial reef on 4 August 1992, deliberately placed in just 18 metres of water only 10 miles from Beaufort Inlet to be accessible to new wreck divers. Large holes were cut in her sides before sinking and Hurricane Florence in 2018 bisected the hull, creating easy swim-through access throughout the now-open interior, which is home to barracuda, amberjack, sea bass, flounder, toadfish, and occasional sand tiger sharks. Since the 1990s the Indra has likely hosted more diver visits than any other wreck off the Outer Banks, earning affectionate nicknames like the "Indra Maru" among regulars.

## Marine Life

The Indra hosts a rich fish community across all levels of the wreck. Schools of Atlantic spadefish and amberjack hover above the superstructure, and sand tiger sharks are reported patrolling the hull sections. Black sea bass and sheepshead occupy the lower hull and interior spaces, and moray eels are frequently encountered in the hold areas. Discovery Diving reports the top deck starting at 30 ft, with the bottom at 60–70 ft, and visibility typically ranging 30–50 ft.

## Wreck History

USS Indra (ARL-37) was an Achelous-class landing craft repair ship built for the US Navy during World War II. She served in the Vietnam War era, providing repair services to riverine and coastal craft. In January 1992 she was transferred to the State of North Carolina, and on 4 August 1992 was deliberately sunk as part of the state's artificial reef program, placed in approximately 60–70 ft of water only 10 miles from Beaufort Inlet to provide an accessible dive site close to shore. Two large access holes were cut in the hull before sinking to facilitate recreational diving. Since the early 1990s the Indra has been one of the most-visited wrecks in North Carolina, used extensively for training dives at all certification levels.

## Dive Profile

The Indra sits upright on the sandy bottom with the top deck at approximately 30 ft (stern) and the bottom at 60–70 ft. The wreck is 330 ft long and largely intact, providing an extensive multi-level dive. The cut access holes and open deck areas allow easy swim-throughs without overhead-environment certification requirements. Discovery Diving lists visibility as typically 30–50 ft, with water temperature 75–78°F in summer. Average visibility is reported at 26–30 ft, with silt on the bottom that can be stirred by fin kicks.

## Entry and Exit

Charter access from Beaufort or Morehead City. The Indra is only 10 miles from Beaufort Inlet, making it a shorter boat ride than the offshore sites — typically under 30 minutes. Discovery Diving and Olympus Dive Center both serve the Indra regularly. The short transit and accessible depth make this a frequent site for training dives and dive certification checkouts.

## Tips and Recommendations

The Indra is the ideal first wreck dive in North Carolina — close inshore, shallow, upright, and extensively penetrable. Buoyancy control near the silty bottom is important to preserve visibility. The large cut holes in the hull make swim-throughs approachable for newer divers, but maintain awareness of exit routes. The shorter transit also makes this a better option for divers prone to seasickness.

## Safety Considerations

At 60–70 ft this is a relatively forgiving depth, though divers should maintain good buoyancy to avoid silting out visibility. The wreck's accessible location and shallower depth make it suitable for Open Water and Advanced Open Water divers with proper supervision. Although the wreck is described as intact and penetrable, divers without overhead environment training should stay within daylight zones and maintain clear exit routes at all times.
