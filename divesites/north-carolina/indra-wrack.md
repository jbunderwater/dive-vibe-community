## Indra (Wrack)

Indra (Wrack) is a historic wreck dive in North Carolina, North America.

## Overview

The USS Indra (ARL-37) was a 320-foot landing craft repair ship that served in Vietnam before being sunk as an artificial reef on 4 August 1992, approximately 10 miles from Beaufort Inlet. The maximum depth to the sandy bottom is 60–70 ft (approximately 18–21 m), with the top deck starting at about 30 ft. Two large access holes were cut in her sides before sinking, and the wreck sits upright, providing an accessible multi-level dive for all certification levels. The Indra is among the most-visited wrecks in North Carolina for its proximity to shore, accessible depth, and reliable marine life.

## Marine Life

The Indra hosts a rich fish community. Schools of Atlantic spadefish and amberjack hover above the superstructure. Black sea bass and sheepshead occupy lower hull sections. Discovery Diving and DiveBuddy reports note the top deck at 30 ft with the bottom at 60–70 ft, visibility typically 26–50 ft. Sand tiger sharks are occasionally reported but are not a regular feature at this shallow inshore site as they are at the deeper offshore wrecks. No site-specific scientific marine life survey was found; species accounts are sourced from dive operator and diver reports.

## Wreck History

USS Indra (ARL-37) was an Achelous-class landing craft repair ship, laid down 12 February 1945 as LST-1147 and launched 21 May 1945. She earned two battle stars for Vietnam War service. Decommissioned May 1970, struck from the Naval Vessel Register in 1984, transferred to the State of North Carolina in January 1992, and deliberately sunk on 4 August 1992 as part of the NC Artificial Reef Program, placed approximately 10 miles from Beaufort Inlet in 60–70 ft of water. Two large access holes were cut in the hull before sinking to facilitate recreational diving. Since the early 1990s the Indra has been one of the most-visited wrecks off the Outer Banks, used extensively for training dives at all certification levels.

## Dive Profile

The Indra sits upright on the sandy bottom with the top deck at approximately 30 ft (stern) and the bottom at 60–70 ft. The wreck is 330 ft long and largely intact, providing an extensive multi-level dive. The cut access holes and open deck areas allow easy swim-throughs without overhead-environment certification requirements. Discovery Diving lists visibility as typically 30–50 ft, with water temperature 75–78°F in summer. Average visibility is reported at 26–30 ft, with silt on the bottom that can be stirred by fin kicks.

## Entry and Exit

Charter access from Beaufort or Morehead City. The Indra is only 10 miles from Beaufort Inlet, making it a shorter boat ride than the offshore sites — typically under 30 minutes. Discovery Diving and Olympus Dive Center both serve the Indra regularly. The short transit and accessible depth make this a frequent site for training dives and dive certification checkouts.

## Tips and Recommendations

The Indra is the ideal first wreck dive in North Carolina — close inshore, shallow, upright, and extensively penetrable. Buoyancy control near the silty bottom is important to preserve visibility. The large cut holes in the hull make swim-throughs approachable for newer divers, but maintain awareness of exit routes. The shorter transit also makes this a better option for divers prone to seasickness.

## Safety Considerations

At 60–70 ft this is a relatively forgiving depth, though divers should maintain good buoyancy to avoid silting out visibility. The wreck's accessible location and shallower depth make it suitable for Open Water and Advanced Open Water divers with proper supervision. Although the wreck is described as intact and penetrable, divers without overhead environment training should stay within daylight zones and maintain clear exit routes at all times.
