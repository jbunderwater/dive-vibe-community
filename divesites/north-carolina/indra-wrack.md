## Indra (Wrack)

Indra (Wrack) is a historic wreck dive in North Carolina, North America.

## Overview

The USS Indra (ARL-37) was a 328-foot Achelous-class landing craft repair ship that served in the Vietnam War before being transferred to North Carolina and sunk as an artificial reef on 4 August 1992, deliberately placed in approximately 18 metres of water only about 6 miles off Atlantic Beach to be accessible to new wreck divers. Large holes were cut in her sides before sinking and Hurricane Florence in 2018 bisected the hull, creating easy swim-through access throughout the now-open interior, which is home to barracuda, amberjack, sea bass, flounder, toadfish, and occasional sand tiger sharks. Since the 1990s the Indra has likely hosted more diver visits than any other wreck off the Outer Banks, earning affectionate nicknames like the "Indra Maru" among regulars.

## Marine Life

The Indra provides structure that supports a variety of inshore marine life. Discovery Diving — the primary charter operator — notes occasional stingrays or sharks, eels and octopi hiding in the wreckage, and tropical fish such as yellow tang and damselfish in late summer. No published dive survey documents the full species community at this wreck; claims of amberjack, spadefish, barracuda, and sheepshead commonly attributed to the Indra are typical of NC inshore wrecks generally but are not confirmed site-specifically at this location. Discovery Diving reports visibility typically ranging 30–50 ft, with the top deck starting at approximately 35 ft and the bottom at 60–70 ft.

## Wreck History

USS Indra (ARL-37) was an Achelous-class landing craft repair ship built for the US Navy during World War II (commissioned October 1945). She deployed to Vietnam in 1968, participating in two campaigns with the Mobile Riverine Force and earning two battle stars. In January 1992 she was transferred to the North Carolina Division of Marine Fisheries, and on 4 August 1992 was deliberately sunk as part of the state's artificial reef program, placed in approximately 60–70 ft of water approximately 6 miles off Atlantic Beach to provide an accessible dive site close to shore. Two large access holes were cut in the hull before sinking to facilitate recreational diving. Since the early 1990s the Indra has been a frequently dived North Carolina wreck, used extensively for training dives at all certification levels; it is nicknamed both "Indra Maru" and "Indra Doria" by regulars.

## Dive Profile

The Indra sits upright on the sandy bottom with the top deck at approximately 35 ft and the bottom at 60–70 ft. The wreck is 328 ft long and largely intact, providing an extensive multi-level dive. The cut access holes and open deck areas allow easy swim-throughs without overhead-environment certification requirements. Discovery Diving lists visibility as typically 30–50 ft, with water temperature 75–78°F in summer. The silt bottom can be stirred by fin kicks, reducing visibility.

## Entry and Exit

Charter access from Beaufort or Morehead City. The Indra is approximately 6 miles off Atlantic Beach, making it a shorter boat ride than the offshore sites — typically under 30 minutes. Discovery Diving and Olympus Dive Center both serve the Indra regularly. The short transit and accessible depth make this a frequent site for training dives and dive certification checkouts.

## Tips and Recommendations

The Indra is the ideal first wreck dive in North Carolina — close inshore, shallow, upright, and extensively penetrable. Buoyancy control near the silty bottom is important to preserve visibility. The large cut holes in the hull make swim-throughs approachable for newer divers, but maintain awareness of exit routes. The shorter transit also makes this a better option for divers prone to seasickness.

## Safety Considerations

At 60–70 ft this is a relatively forgiving depth, though divers should maintain good buoyancy to avoid silting out visibility. The wreck's accessible location and shallower depth make it suitable for Open Water and Advanced Open Water divers with proper supervision. Although the wreck is described as intact and penetrable, divers without overhead environment training should stay within daylight zones and maintain clear exit routes at all times.
