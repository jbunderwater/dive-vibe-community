## Queen Anne's Revenge Shipwreck

Queen Anne's Revenge Shipwreck is a historic wreck dive in North Carolina, North America.

## Overview

Queen Anne's Revenge was Blackbeard's flagship — the vessel he captured as the French slaver Concorde in 1717 and used to blockade Charleston harbour before running her aground at Beaufort Inlet on 10 June 1718. The wreck lies in approximately 28 ft (8.5 m) of water about a mile offshore from Fort Macon State Park. Visibility is extremely limited (typically 2–10 feet due to the tidal inlet environment). More than 300,000 artifacts have been recovered from the site — including 24 of the estimated 30 cannons, a large anchor raised in 2011, a bronze bell dated 1705, and surgical instruments — many displayed at the North Carolina Maritime Museum in Beaufort. Recreational access is tightly controlled: the NC Underwater Archaeology Branch permits only 36–48 recreational divers per year, exclusively through the structured "Dive Down" educational programme.

## Marine Life

The tidal inlet environment with 2–10 ft visibility limits marine life observation. Sheepshead and black sea bass are commonly found around the cannon and ironwork. The shifting sand periodically exposes and covers different sections of the wreck.

## Wreck History

Queen Anne's Revenge began her life as La Concorde, a French merchant and privateer vessel built circa 1710. On 28 November 1717, the pirate Edward Teach (Blackbeard) captured La Concorde near Martinique and converted her to his flagship, reportedly arming her with approximately 40 cannons. She served as his flagship for less than a year, during which Blackbeard captured numerous prizes and blockaded the harbour of Charleston, South Carolina.

On 10 June 1718, Queen Anne's Revenge ran aground entering Beaufort Inlet (then called Topsail Inlet), in present-day Carteret County, North Carolina. Crew and supplies were transferred to smaller vessels afterward. The grounding may have been deliberate — historians debate whether Blackbeard intentionally wrecked the ship to reduce his crew before seeking a pardon.

The wreck was discovered on 21 November 1996 by Intersal Inc., a private research firm, in 28 ft (8.5 m) of water approximately one mile offshore of Fort Macon State Park. Archaeological recovery has yielded more than 300,000 artifacts (QAR Project 2024), including 24 of the estimated 30 cannons, a large anchor raised in 2011, a bronze bell dated 1705, surgical instruments, and trade goods. The wreck was listed on the National Register of Historic Places in 2004. Post-2015 archaeological work has been complicated by ongoing litigation between North Carolina, Intersal Inc., and Nautilus Productions over artifact rights.

## Dive Profile

The site lies in approximately 28 ft (8.5 m) of water about one mile offshore from Fort Macon State Park. Visibility is typically only 2–10 ft due to the tidal inlet environment. This is not a traditional recreational dive — access is restricted to the structured "Dive Down" educational programme run by the NC Underwater Archaeology Branch, accepting only 36–48 recreational divers per year. The shifting sand means the wreck's exposed features change with tide and storm activity. The anchor raised in 2011 and most recoverable artifacts have been removed to preservation facilities; remaining features on the seafloor may include cannon remnants and concretions.

## Entry and Exit

The wreck is one mile offshore from Fort Macon State Park, accessible by boat only. Dive access has historically been limited to structured educational programmes through the North Carolina Maritime Museum in Beaufort (where over 300,000 recovered artifacts are displayed). Casual recreational diving is not routine — contact the NC Underwater Archaeology Branch for current access information.

## Tips and Recommendations

Visit the North Carolina Maritime Museum in Beaufort to view the remarkable artifact collection before any dive. The museum's Blackbeard exhibit provides essential context for understanding what you will (and will not) be able to see underwater given the visibility constraints. If dive access is available through a structured programme, guideline navigation is essential given the very limited visibility.

## Safety Considerations

The tidal inlet environment creates strong currents that vary significantly with tidal stage — diving is only safe at or near slack tide. Visibility of 2–10 ft means buddy separation risk is high; maintain contact throughout. As an active archaeological site, disturbing or collecting any material is prohibited by state and federal law.
