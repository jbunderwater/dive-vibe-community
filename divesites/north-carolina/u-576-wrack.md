## U-576 (Wrack)

U-576 (Wrack) is a historic wreck dive in North Carolina, North America.

## Overview

U-576 was lost on 15 July 1942 in a rare convoy battle off Cape Hatteras: after torpedoing several ships in a 19-vessel convoy, the damaged submarine surfaced in the middle of the convoy and was simultaneously attacked by two US Navy Kingfisher aircraft dropping depth charges and a merchant ship firing its deck gun, sinking her with all 45 crew within minutes. NOAA discovered both U-576 and the freighter SS Bluefields she had sunk in 2014, lying about 240 yards apart in approximately 700 feet of water — making this the only known US site where archaeologically preserved convoy battle remains from both sides rest so close together. The depth places it well beyond recreational diving and it is protected as an international war grave, but it is a significant managed site within the Monitor National Marine Sanctuary's broader protection zone.

## Wreck History

U-576 was a German Type VIIC submarine (67.1 m long, 769 tonnes surfaced). On 15 July 1942, U-576 attacked Convoy KS-520 — a group of 19 merchant ships en route from Norfolk, Virginia to Key West, Florida — approximately 30 miles off Cape Hatteras. The submarine fired four torpedoes, sinking the Nicaraguan cargo ship Bluefields and damaging the American Chilore and Panamanian tanker J. A. Mowinckel. U-576 then unintentionally surfaced amid the convoy. She was immediately attacked by gunfire from the merchant vessel Unicoi and depth charges dropped by two US Navy Vought OS2U Kingfisher aircraft. All 45 crew perished with no survivors.

In August 2014, NOAA's Office of National Marine Sanctuaries located U-576 and the Bluefields 35 miles off Cape Hatteras at 721 ft (220 m) depth, approximately 240 yards apart. First visual observations using remotely operated vehicles were conducted on 24 August 2016. NOAA describes this as "the only known location in US waters that contains archaeologically preserved remains of a convoy battle where both sides rest so close together."

## Access Note

At 721 ft (220 m) depth, this site is far beyond recreational and even most technical diving limits. It is protected as an international war grave within the Monitor National Marine Sanctuary and is accessible only to NOAA research missions. This site is included for historical reference only — **no recreational diving is possible.**
