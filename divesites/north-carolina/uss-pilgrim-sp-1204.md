## USS Pilgrim (SP-1204) (Wreck)

USS Pilgrim (SP-1204) began life as a private sailing yacht before being requisitioned by the U.S. Navy and commissioned as a patrol vessel in 1917, assigned to patrol the North Carolina coast during World War I. After decommissioning, she was sold and eventually scuttled as a breakwater in Beaufort harbor in 1935. The remains now lie in very shallow water in the Beaufort harbor area, with portions of the hull visible at low tide.

## Overview

The USS Pilgrim is a shallow, historically interesting wreck located in sheltered Beaufort harbor rather than the open Atlantic. At approximately 6 meters (20 feet) maximum depth and with the hull showing above water at low tide, this is a calm-water dive with no offshore conditions to contend with. The wreck is in fragmented condition after nearly 90 years of submersion and storm damage, but recognizable hull timbers and structural elements remain. This is a harbor/protected-water dive suited for beginning divers or for those interested in the site's history.

## Historical Background

The original vessel was a privately owned yacht purchased and converted by the U.S. Navy into Patrol Vessel USS Pilgrim (SP-1204) in 1917. After WWI she was decommissioned, sold back to private owners, and eventually scuttled to serve as a breakwater in Beaufort's harbor in 1935. While not a dramatic dive, the site connects to both WWI naval history and Beaufort's long maritime heritage.

## Marine Life

Harbor marine life typical of North Carolina's protected estuarine waters. Sheepshead, spadefish, and various juvenile reef fish shelter around the hull structure. Blue crabs, hermit crabs, and small invertebrates inhabit the hull timbers. Sea nettles (jellyfish) are seasonally present in summer months.

## Dive Profile

Entry from shore at the Beaufort harbor area. The dive is a simple exploration of the scattered hull remains at 3–6 meters. Key features are the hull timber sections and any remaining structural elements. The wreck is in deteriorated condition — manage buoyancy carefully to avoid stirring up silt. Maximum depth is 6 meters, making this an appropriate dive for newly certified or student divers.

## Entry and Exit

Shore entry from the Beaufort waterfront or adjacent access points. Calm harbor conditions mean this can typically be dived in weather that would cancel offshore trips. Check with local operators (Discovery Diving Company) for the best current access point. A dive flag is required in harbor waters.

## Tips and Recommendations

- A good alternative when offshore weather cancels wreck trips
- Suits as a checkout or training dive location for local dive shops
- Combine with a visit to the North Carolina Maritime Museum in Beaufort, which documents the local maritime history
- Harbor traffic: always dive with a visible dive flag and be aware of passing boats

## Safety Considerations

Minimal hazards at this site — shallow, calm, protected harbor water. Primary considerations are boat traffic in the harbor and seasonal jellyfish. Standard buddy diving protocols apply.

## Photography

Low ambient light and harbor turbidity make photography challenging. A video light or strobe is recommended even at 6 meters depth. Close-up shots of encrusted hull timbers and resident invertebrates are the main subjects.
