## HMT Bedfordshire (Wreck)

HMT Bedfordshire was a British Royal Navy armed trawler assigned to anti-submarine patrol duty off North Carolina when she was torpedoed and sunk by U-558 on May 11, 1942. All 37 crew members were lost. She now rests in 105 feet (32 meters) of water off Cape Lookout — protected as a war grave and listed on the U.S. National Register of Historic Places since 2015. The Bedfordshire is one of only two Royal Navy vessels on American soil commemorated with a formal British ceremony each spring.

## Overview

The Bedfordshire lies in three separate sections roughly 145 feet in combined length, with the two main pieces about 75 feet apart and a third section some 200 feet away. The wreck reflects considerable violence — the torpedo strike broke the vessel apart, and the scattered debris field includes I-beams, deck plates, pipes, and six depth charges that were never detonated. The Gulf Stream's proximity to this Cape Lookout site delivers good visibility of 12–20 meters and warm summer conditions. The site was listed on the National Register of Historic Places in 2015 and is administered jointly by NOAA and the National Park Service.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Off Cape Lookout, ~22 miles south of Beaufort Inlet, North Carolina
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 32 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 12–20 meters (40–70 feet)
- **Current**: Moderate — Gulf Stream influence; can increase with eddies
- **Water Temperature**: 22–27°C (72–81°F) summer; cooler in spring/fall
- **Best Season**: May to October

## Marine Life

Large schools of amberjack and spadefish are reliably present around the wreck sections. Grouper, black sea bass, and sheepshead inhabit the debris field. Six depth charges still rest on or near the wreck — they are visible but must not be disturbed. The encrusted deck plates and machinery support sponges, corals, and associated invertebrates. Sand tiger sharks are occasionally sighted, along with barracuda and southern stingrays on the sandy bottom.

## Dive Profile

The wreck is in three sections — the main bow-to-stern section is the primary dive target. The separated pieces provide two distinct dive areas. The scattered debris field between sections holds much of the marine life and historical artifacts. Note the six depth charges visible at the site — approach with caution and do not touch or disturb unexploded ordnance. The wreck is a war grave; removing artifacts is a federal offense.

## Entry and Exit

Charter boats depart from Morehead City and Beaufort. Discovery Diving Company and Olympus Dive Center run regular trips to the Bedfordshire. Permanent moorings are set at the main section. Carry an SMB for the safety stop — current can be present.

## Tips and Recommendations

- This is a war grave: do not remove any artifacts and treat the site with respect
- Six depth charges are visible at the site — do not touch or disturb unexploded ordnance
- The site is also known as "HMT Bedfordshire" in some references vs. "HMS Bedfordshire" — HMT (His Majesty's Trawler) is correct
- Bring a dive light for the debris field and darker sections under the hull plating
- A 5mm wetsuit is recommended — temperatures drop at depth even in summer
- An annual memorial ceremony at Ocracoke Island honors the lost crew each spring

## Safety Considerations

Depth, current, and unexploded ordnance are the primary considerations at this site. Never touch or attempt to move the depth charges. Dive within your certification limits — 32 meters is at the upper end of recreational diving. Duke Dive Medicine in Morehead City provides recompression services.

## Photography

The scattered debris field with identifiable machinery, depth charges, and hull sections makes compelling documentary-style photography. Wide-angle shots of the main hull section with ambient light filtering down are dramatic. The debris field macro subjects — encrusted pipes, winches, and deck fittings — reward careful close-up work.
