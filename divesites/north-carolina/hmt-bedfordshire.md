## HMT Bedfordshire

HMT Bedfordshire is a historic wreck dive in North Carolina, North America.

## Overview

The HMT Bedfordshire was a Royal Navy armed trawler operating out of Morehead City on antisubmarine patrol when U-558 fired two torpedoes at her in the early morning hours of 11 May 1942, sinking her instantly with all 37 crew — her only survivor was a stoker detained ashore the night before. The 162-foot wreck rests in 32 metres about 25 miles southeast of Beaufort Inlet, broken into three sections spread across the sand, with six depth charges still visible and Gulf Stream visibility commonly reaching 40–70 feet in summer. Designated a WWII War Memorial by Great Britain and listed on the National Register of Historic Places, the site carries a solemn character — the four crew members who washed ashore on Ocracoke are still honoured in an annual US Coast Guard ceremony at the British Cemetery there.

## Marine Life

Large schools of amberjack are a reported regular feature over the scattered hull sections. Sheepshead and black sea bass occupy the encrusted I-beams and deck plates. Discovery Diving notes visibility typically 40–70 ft with Gulf Stream-influenced water in summer. The site is colonised by encrusting reef communities across the debris field.

## Wreck History

HMT Bedfordshire was a 162.3-ft (49.5 m), 443 GRT trawler built in July 1935 by Smith's Dock Company, North Yorkshire, for the Bedfordshire Fishing Company of Grimsby. The Royal Navy requisitioned her in August 1939, converting her for anti-submarine patrol with a 4-inch gun, machine guns, depth charges, and ASDIC sonar. She was assigned to the US East Coast to assist with the surge in German U-boat activity off North Carolina.

At 05:40 on 11 May 1942, German submarine U-558 (Kapitänleutnant Günther Krech) fired two torpedoes at Bedfordshire off Ocracoke Island — the first missed, the second struck directly, sinking her instantly with no survivors from the 37-man crew. A single crew member, Stoker Sam Nutt, survived only because he had been detained ashore the previous night. Four bodies that washed ashore on Ocracoke are buried in the British Cemetery there; another sailor is buried at Hatteras.

The wreck was discovered in 1980 and surveyed by NOAA in 2009. It was listed on the National Register of Historic Places on 31 July 2015. A UK-US Memorandum of Understanding signed in January 2016 confirms the vessel remains British sovereign property and a designated military war grave. An annual commemorative ceremony is held on 11 May at Ocracoke.

**Note:** Unexploded depth charges remain near the stern and must not be disturbed.

## Dive Profile

The wreck lies in two sections approximately 80 ft apart in 105 ft (32 m) of water. Maximum relief is about 6 ft — the single boiler is the highest structure. The dive involves swimming over a spread of I-beams, hull plates, pipes, and machinery at approximately 100–105 ft. Discovery Diving lists this as an intermediate dive with 40–70 ft typical visibility and 75–80°F water in summer.

## Entry and Exit

Charter access from Beaufort or Morehead City. Discovery Diving (414 Orange St, Beaufort) and Olympus Dive Center (Morehead City) serve this site on offshore charters. The wreck is approximately 25 miles southeast of Beaufort Inlet. No shore access is possible.

## Tips and Recommendations

Allow time to find and observe the six depth charges visible near the stern — a dramatic and historically significant detail of the site. The low profile rewards slow, careful exploration. Combine a dive trip to the Bedfordshire with a visit to the British Cemetery on Ocracoke Island for full historical context.

## Safety Considerations

Unexploded depth charges are present — approach with extreme caution and do not touch or disturb. This is a legally protected British war grave; artifact removal is prohibited under both UK and US law. The 105-ft depth is at the deep end of recreational diving; nitrox is recommended. Offshore conditions off the Outer Banks can change quickly — always follow charter operator guidance.
