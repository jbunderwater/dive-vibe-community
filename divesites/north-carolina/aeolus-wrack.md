## Aeolus (Wrack)

Aeolus (Wrack) is a historic wreck dive in North Carolina, North America.

## Overview

The Aeolus was a US Navy cable repair ship (approximately 426–438 ft) deliberately sunk as artificial reef AR-305 on 29 July 1988 off Morehead City; Hurricane Fran in 1996 split her into three sections, creating what locals call "Club Aeolus" — a reliably shark-dense wreck lying in 33 metres of Gulf Stream-influenced water. Sand tiger sharks adopted the cable storage compartments as a lounge, and during the 2012 season a dozen or more were resident at once, swimming at arm's length from divers amid a backdrop of frenzied jacks, barracudas, and dense grouper populations working the wreck's exterior. The three-hour boat ride from Morehead City is well worth it: the clear offshore water and reliable shark activity make the Aeolus one of North Carolina's benchmark wreck dives.

## Marine Life

Sand tiger sharks are the defining feature — Discovery Diving confirms "sand tiger sharks that can often be found inside the cable storage compartment." During July 2012, underwater photographer Mike Gerken (Evolution Underwater) documented approximately a dozen sand tiger sharks resident simultaneously — a first-hand account of the shark density. Jacks, barracudas, and tunnies (little tunny / Atlantic bonito) are confirmed by local dive photographers and charter operators; grouper were not confirmed at Aeolus specifically. Discovery Diving lists visibility typically ranging 40–70 ft with water temperatures 75–80°F in summer.

## Wreck History

The Aeolus (ARC-3) was a US Navy cable repair ship measuring approximately 426–438 ft — sources vary slightly — originally commissioned 18 June 1945 as the attack cargo ship USS Turandot (AKA-47). After serving in the Pacific theater's final months transporting troops and cargo under Operation Magic Carpet, she was decommissioned 21 March 1946. Reacquired in 1954, converted, and recommissioned as USS Aeolus (ARC-3) on 14 May 1955, she served until transfer to Military Sealift Command in 1973 as USNS Aeolus (T-ARC-3), then deactivated in 1985. She was deliberately sunk on 29 July 1988 as artificial reef AR-305, approximately 22 miles south of Beaufort Inlet in 110 ft (33.5 m) of water. Hurricane Fran in 1996 broke the hull into three major sections, partially righting some portions and creating the multi-level configuration divers encounter today.

## Dive Profile

The Aeolus lies in three sections in 110 ft of water, with the highest deck structures at approximately 85–90 ft. The three-piece layout creates a multi-level dive covering a large footprint. The cable storage compartments amidships are the primary shark congregation area. Discovery Diving notes the navigation can be challenging in lower visibility given the scattered wreck sections, and that limited penetration is possible. Bottom time at 110 ft limits dives to 20–30 minutes on air.

## Entry and Exit

Access is exclusively by charter boat. Olympus Dive Center (Morehead City) and Discovery Diving (Beaufort) both run regular full-day charters that visit the Aeolus, with the transit from Beaufort Inlet taking approximately 1.5 hours. The site is typically a second dive on a full-day charter pairing. No shore access is possible.

## Tips and Recommendations

The Aeolus earns its "Club Aeolus" nickname for reliable shark encounters throughout the summer season. For the best shark activity, plan visits June through September. Navigation among the three wreck sections benefits from a dive slate or wreck map — ask the charter operator for a briefing before descent. Buoyancy control in the cable compartments is especially important given the confined space.

## Safety Considerations

At 110 ft this is at the recreational depth limit; Nitrox is widely recommended by local operators. The site is 22 miles offshore — sea conditions on the open Atlantic off North Carolina can change rapidly, and charters may be scrubbed on short notice. Water temperature ranges from the low 60s°F (16°C) in winter to over 80°F (27°C) at the surface in summer. Thermoclines can cause sudden temperature drops at depth. Penetration of wreck sections requires appropriate training and equipment — do not enter confined spaces without overhead environment certification.
