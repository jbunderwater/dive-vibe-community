## Aeolus (Wreck)

The Aeolus is a 400-foot U.S. Navy cable repair ship deliberately sunk in July 1988 as part of North Carolina's artificial reef program, approximately 20 miles south of Beaufort Inlet. Originally commissioned as the attack cargo ship USS Turandot in 1945, she was later converted and recommissioned as USS Aeolus (ARC-3) in 1954. She now lies in 110 feet (33 meters) of water and is among North Carolina's most popular and marine-life-rich artificial reef dives.

## Overview

The Aeolus was intentionally prepared and sunk to create a productive artificial reef, and after decades of colonization she delivers on that promise spectacularly. A powerful hurricane in the 1990s broke the wreck into two main sections, creating additional habitat and entry points. The site sits within reach of Gulf Stream influence, giving it better-than-average visibility of 12–20 meters and warm summer water temperatures. During the 2012 dive season, approximately a dozen sand tiger sharks took up permanent residence inside the wreck — a phenomenon local divers dubbed "Club Aeolus" — and sharks have remained a key feature of the site ever since.

## Site Information

- **Location**: ~20 miles south of Beaufort Inlet, North Carolina
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 33 meters
- **Top of Wreck**: ~24 meters (80 feet)
- **Typical Visibility**: 12–20 meters (40–70 feet)
- **Current**: Moderate — Gulf Stream proximity creates variable current
- **Water Temperature**: 24–28°C (75–82°F) in summer
- **Best Season**: May to October

## Marine Life

The Aeolus is North Carolina's premier shark encounter dive. Sand tiger sharks — sometimes a dozen or more — use the interior sections of the wreck as a resting area, hovering nearly motionless in the calm interior spaces. Large schools of amberjack, spadefish, and Atlantic spadefish orbit the exterior. Grouper, sea bass, and sheepshead are resident throughout the hull. Barracuda are common. The wreck surface is encrusted with soft corals, sponges, and hydroids that attract a variety of nudibranchs and invertebrates. In late summer, tropical fish including damselfish and angelfish appear along with the warm Gulf Stream water.

## Dive Profile

The wreck lies in two main sections split by the hurricane damage. The forward section is shallower, while the stern is slightly deeper. The sand tiger sharks are typically found in the more sheltered interior spaces — approach slowly and maintain neutral buoyancy at a respectful distance. The large openings in the hull created by the reef preparation and storm damage allow safe swim-throughs for divers with proper buoyancy skills. A nearby wreck, the USCG Spar, lies only a few hundred feet away and can be included on the same dive if air allows.

## Entry and Exit

Charter boats operate from Morehead City and Beaufort. Discovery Diving Company and Olympus Dive Center regularly run trips to the Aeolus. Permanent moorings are in place. Always carry an SMB for the safety stop — current can be present at this offshore location.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Sand tiger sharks are non-aggressive; observe them calmly without chasing or cornering them
- Bring a dive light for the interior sections where sharks rest
- The adjacent USCG Spar wreck is only a few hundred feet away — ask your operator about doing both
- A 3mm or 5mm wetsuit is appropriate for summer; conditions cool off by October
- Visibility is often better on incoming tide; local operators will know optimal timing
- Nitrox is beneficial at this depth to extend bottom time within recreational limits

## Safety Considerations

Depth and moderate current are the primary considerations. The site is 20 miles offshore. Duke Dive Medicine in Morehead City provides recompression if needed. Dive within your certification limits — the maximum depth of 33 meters is at the edge of recreational limits.

## Photography

Sand tigers hovering in the blue interior light of the wreck are the signature shot. A wide-angle lens with dual strobes works best for full-wreck context shots. The encrusted hull exterior provides excellent macro subjects. The split wreck sections photographed from outside with sharks visible through the openings make dramatic compositions.
