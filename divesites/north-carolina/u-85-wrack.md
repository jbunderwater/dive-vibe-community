## U-85 (Wreck)

The U-85 was the first German U-boat sunk off the U.S. coast during World War II, destroyed on April 14, 1942, by the USS Roper using depth charges and deck gun fire. She now lies 14 miles east of Oregon Inlet in approximately 110 feet (34 meters) of water, on her starboard side with an 80-degree list — one of the most historically significant and challenging wreck dives on the Outer Banks.

## Overview

The U-85 rests around the corner of the Outer Banks in an exposed position where the cold, green Labrador Current dominates, producing persistently strong currents, limited visibility, and cold summer water temperatures of 13–16°C (55–60°F). This is a stark contrast to the warmer, clearer Gulf Stream-influenced sites further south. The wreck's exposure to current and the challenging water conditions make this an Advanced-rated dive. However, the historical significance, visible battle damage, and intact torpedo tubes make it a bucket-list site for wreck divers.

## Site Information

- **Location**: 14 miles east of Oregon Inlet, Outer Banks, North Carolina
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 34 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 5–10 meters (15–30 feet) — Labrador Current dominates this area
- **Current**: Strong and persistent — always present at this exposed location
- **Water Temperature**: 13–18°C (55–64°F) in summer; cold year-round
- **Best Season**: June to September (weather window); conditions rarely ideal

## Marine Life

The cold, nutrient-rich Labrador Current supports abundant marine life despite the limited visibility. Large schools of bluefish and mackerel patrol the wreck. Black sea bass and tautog are plentiful around the hull structure. Spiny lobster and various crab species shelter within the wreck. Sandbar sharks and occasional sand tiger sharks are sighted. The hull is encrusted with cold-water corals, anemones, and bryozoans that thrive in these cooler conditions.

## Dive Profile

The wreck lies on her starboard side with the bow pointing into the predominant current. At the bow, the outer hull is largely gone, exposing the pressure hull and four forward torpedo tubes — two of which reportedly still contain visible torpedoes. Aft of the conning tower, distinctive battle damage from Roper's 3-inch deck gun is clearly visible. The stern section shows propeller shaft damage. Given the strong current, descend quickly and work close to the hull. Plan a conservative bottom time of 15–20 minutes maximum at this depth and in these conditions.

## Entry and Exit

Charter boats operate out of Oregon Inlet fishing center and Manteo. The site is exposed and weather windows are limited — operators will cancel if conditions are unsafe. Always dive this site with experienced local guides. Strong surface current makes SMB deployment essential for boat pickup.

## Tips and Recommendations

- This is an Advanced dive — experience with strong current and cold water is essential
- Wear a 7mm wetsuit or drysuit; 13–16°C water drains heat rapidly
- Maintain a strong grip on the descent/ascent line in current
- Limited visibility means staying close to the wreck; bring a powerful dive light
- Always carry an SMB and be prepared for a significant drift during ascent
- Roanoke Island Outfitters in Manteo and local Hatteras operators run this trip

## Safety Considerations

Strong current, cold water, and limited visibility combine to make this the most challenging recreational wreck dive on the Outer Banks. Only experienced Advanced-certified divers should attempt it. The site is 14 miles offshore. Duke Dive Medicine in Morehead City has a recompression chamber. Never dive this site without an experienced local guide.

## Photography

Limited visibility makes wide-angle photography difficult — short focal lengths and powerful strobes are essential. The exposed torpedo tubes at the bow are the primary macro and close-up target. Battle damage details on the conning tower and aft section reward close-focus work. Silhouette shots of divers working along the hull in green ambient light capture the dramatic feel of this site.
