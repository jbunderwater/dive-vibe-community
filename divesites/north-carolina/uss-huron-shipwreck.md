## USS Huron Shipwreck

The USS Huron was a U.S. Navy gunboat that sank during a violent storm on November 24, 1877, with the loss of 98 of her 132 crew. The wreck now lies approximately 250 yards from the beach at Nags Head, between mileposts 11 and 12, in 26–30 feet (8–9 meters) of water. In 1991, NC DNCR designated the site as North Carolina's first Historic Shipwreck Preserve — now called a Heritage Dive Site. It is one of the few wreck dives in North Carolina accessible directly from the beach.

## Overview

The Huron is a shallow, accessible wreck dive and snorkel site located just off the beach at Nags Head on the Outer Banks. The ship's remains — including boilers, cannonball storage racks, the large propeller, and the rudder — are spread across a sandy bottom at 26–30 feet. While visibility averages only 5–10 feet due to the surf zone and stirred sand, the historical significance and ease of access make this a popular destination for local divers, beginning divers, and history enthusiasts. During summer months, buoys mark the bow and stern of the wreck. The site is clearly visible at low tide when portions of the boiler and machinery emerge.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Nags Head, Outer Banks, NC — ~250 yards from the beach at mileposts 11–12
- **Entry Type**: Shore entry
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 9 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 1.5–3 meters (5–10 feet) — surf zone turbidity
- **Current**: Variable surf zone surge; can be challenging in rough weather
- **Water Temperature**: 22–27°C (72–81°F) in summer; much colder in winter
- **Best Season**: May to September; calmer mornings preferred

## Historical Significance

The Huron disaster became a catalyst for major improvements in the U.S. Life-Saving Service (predecessor to the Coast Guard) after investigations revealed systemic failures in the rescue response. The wreck still holds the original ship's structure, boilers, and mechanical components. The site was designated North Carolina's first Historic Shipwreck Preserve in 1991 in recognition of its historical importance.

## Marine Life

Atlantic spadefish, sheepshead, and sea bass are common around the metal structure. Smooth dogfish sharks are occasionally sighted. The boiler and machinery provide habitat for juvenile reef fish, small crabs, and various invertebrates. Blue crabs are frequently seen on the sandy bottom adjacent to the wreck.

## Dive Profile

Enter the water at the designated beach access point and navigate to the wreck using natural landmarks or compass bearing — the buoys visible from the beach help orient the approach in summer. The wreck sits at 26–30 feet with most features spread flat on the sand. Key highlights include the large boiler (often visible above water at low tide), the propeller and rudder assembly at the stern, and the cannonball storage racks amidships. Plan for short, exploratory dives given the limited visibility.

## Entry and Exit

Shore entry directly from the Nags Head beach. Public beach access is at the designated parking area near MP 11–12. Enter at a point perpendicular to the wreck and swim out approximately 250 yards. Surf conditions dictate dive safety — do not enter in heavy surf. Exit at the same beach access point. The wreck can also be reached by kayak from the beach.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Dive in calm conditions: mornings are typically calmer before afternoon sea breezes develop
- Bring a compass — surface navigation to the buoys is straightforward in summer, but a compass helps in choppier conditions
- Low tide reveals parts of the wreck above water; arrive at low tide for the best surface reference
- A dive flag is required — the beach is near boat traffic
- Free diving and snorkeling are also viable given the shallow depth
- The nearby Outer Banks Visitor Center has historical information about the wreck

## Safety Considerations

The primary hazard is surf and surf-zone surge, which can disorient divers and reduce visibility to near zero in rough conditions. Check surf forecasts (NOAA buoy data or surfline.com) before diving. Do not dive in breaking surf over 2 feet. The shallow depth eliminates decompression concerns. A lifeguard-staffed beach is nearby in summer months.

## Photography

Limited visibility makes wide-angle difficult — bring a short-focal-length macro lens or a wide-angle dome port with a powerful strobe. The boiler machinery is the best photo subject. Surface snorkel shots of the boiler emerging from the water at low tide make striking topside photographs.
