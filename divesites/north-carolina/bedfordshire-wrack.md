## Bedfordshire (Wrack)

Bedfordshire (Wrack) is a historic wreck dive in North Carolina, North America.

## Overview

The HMT Bedfordshire was a British armed trawler torpedoed by U-558 on 11 May 1942 with the loss of all 37 crew, and its remains lie in three sections in 32 metres of water about 25 miles southeast of Beaufort Inlet — a genuine WWII war grave that has been listed on the National Register of Historic Places and formally declared a British War Memorial. The hull is low and scattered — the highest point barely four feet off the sand — with I-beams, deck plates, machinery, and six depth charges still visible in the sediment, and the site typically offers visibility of 40–70 feet thanks to its offshore Gulf Stream position. The four crew members who washed ashore on Ocracoke Island are buried in the British Cemetery there, maintained to this day by the US Coast Guard, giving the dive an unusual weight of history and commemoration.

## Marine Life

Discovery Diving reports large schools of amberjack as a regular feature over the Bedfordshire wreck. The low-profile hull sections, I-beams, and exposed machinery host sheepshead and black sea bass, while the offshore Gulf Stream-influenced position brings clear water in summer. NOAA's 2009 survey documented the site's ongoing colonisation by reef communities over the decades since 1942.

## Wreck History

HMT Bedfordshire was a 162.3-ft (49.5 m) British deep-sea trawler built in July 1935 by Smith's Dock Company, North Yorkshire, for the Bedfordshire Fishing Company of Grimsby. The Royal Navy requisitioned her in August 1939 for anti-submarine duties, arming her with a 4-inch gun, machine guns, depth charges, and ASDIC detection equipment. She was transferred to the US East Coast to assist with anti-submarine patrols.

On 11 May 1942, at 05:40, German submarine U-558 (commanded by Kapitänleutnant Günther Krech) attacked the Bedfordshire off Ocracoke Island, North Carolina, firing two torpedoes — the first missed, the second scored a direct hit, sinking her immediately with all 37 crew. The wreck was discovered in 1980 and surveyed by NOAA in 2009. It was listed on the National Register of Historic Places on 31 July 2015 and formally designated a British war memorial; the UK has not relinquished sovereign title.

Four crew members' bodies recovered at Ocracoke are buried in the British Cemetery there, maintained by the US Coast Guard. A second sailor is buried at Hatteras. An annual commemoration ceremony is held each 11 May.

**Note:** Unexploded depth charges remain near the stern. Divers are warned by operators not to disturb this area.

## Dive Profile

The wreck lies in two main sections approximately 80 ft (25 m) apart in 105 ft (32 m) of water, with very little relief — the highest point, the single boiler, stands only about 6 ft off the seabed. The low profile means divers swim across a scattered debris field of I-beams, hull plates, pipes, and machinery. Discovery Diving notes visibility typically 40–70 ft and water temperature 75–80°F in summer. This is an intermediate-level dive given the depth and offshore location.

## Entry and Exit

Access is by charter boat from Beaufort or Morehead City. Discovery Diving (Beaufort) and Olympus Dive Center (Morehead City) both serve this site. The wreck lies approximately 25 miles southeast of Beaufort Inlet. No shore access is possible.

## Tips and Recommendations

Because the wreck is low-relief, good trim and buoyancy control help avoid silting up the visibility over the scattered hull sections. The site rewards a slow, methodical exploration rather than a hurried swim — allow time to spot the six visible depth charges at the stern and appreciate the historical weight of the site. The British Cemetery on Ocracoke Island is worth a visit before or after diving.

## Safety Considerations

Unexploded depth charges remain at the stern — do not touch or disturb any ordnance. The site is protected as a British war grave; artifact removal is illegal. At 105 ft this is a deep recreational dive; nitrox is recommended. Offshore North Carolina conditions can deteriorate rapidly; always defer to the charter operator's weather judgment. This is a protected war grave under both UK and US law.
