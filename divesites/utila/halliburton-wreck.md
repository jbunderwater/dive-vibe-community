# Halliburton Wreck

*A large wreck off Utila's south side named after an oil services company connection, one of the Bay Islands' deeper recreational wreck dives.*

## Overview

The Halliburton Wreck is a substantial vessel lying on a sandy slope off the south coast of Utila at depths of 18–30 meters. The wreck is named for a connection to the Halliburton oil services industry, reflecting the industrial history of the Western Caribbean. The vessel's size provides multiple dive environments — the shallow superstructure, the main hull, and the deeper stern section — giving divers several visits worth of exploration. After years on the seabed, the wreck is well-encrusted and serves as a productive artificial reef. This is an advanced-level dive due to the maximum depth of 30 meters and the open-ocean-influenced southern exposure.

## Site Information

- **Location**: South coast of Utila, Bay Islands, Honduras
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–28 meters (65–90 feet)
- **Current**: Moderate; the south-facing exposure creates variable conditions
- **Best Time**: Year-round; calmer April to September on the south side

## Marine Life

The Halliburton's substantial hull provides extensive habitat for the Caribbean wreck community. Large cubera snappers and Nassau groupers hold station near the bow. Schools of horse-eye jacks circle the superstructure. Moray eels — multiple species — inhabit the hull from stem to stern. Nurse sharks rest in the hold areas and sheltered sections. Caribbean reef sharks patrol the sandy plain around the wreck. The hull encrustation includes large sponge formations — barrel sponges, orange tube sponges, and encrusting purple sponges. Wire corals and black coral emerge at the deepest sections of the hull.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the shallower superstructure sections at 18–20 meters and begin a methodical exterior exploration. Work forward to the bow and aft to the stern, dropping to the maximum 30-meter depth at the keel sections. Interior penetration of accessible areas is possible but requires wreck training and a torch. Plan the dive around the air supply — begin deep and work shallower as gas depletes. Perform the safety stop at 5 meters above the wreck top before surfacing. Nitrox extends bottom time and is recommended.

## Entry and Exit

Served by Utila Town operators. South coast crossing of 15–20 minutes. Entry via giant stride from the dive boat. Deploy an SMB for the open-water ascent.

## Tips

- Nitrox 32% significantly extends the time available to explore the full wreck
- The deepest sections near the keel are only productive if you budget air carefully for the shallower portions
- Bring two dive torches for wreck diving — a primary and a backup
- Interior penetration of the engine room and holds requires a wreck certification and proper buddy protocols
- Combining this wreck with another south-coast reef or wall dive makes an excellent advanced two-tank day

## Safety

The 30-meter maximum depth requires careful gas management. Narcosis can be a factor — know your personal limits. Do not penetrate enclosed spaces without proper wreck training, dive torches, and a buddy team equipped for penetration. The hull has deteriorated sections with sharp edges. Always perform a 3–5 minute safety stop at 5 meters. Carry an SMB and deploy before ascending from depth.

## Photography

Large wreck photography at depth rewards a strobe system for color restoration. Wide-angle shots of the hull section from the sandy seabed capture the scale of the vessel. Look for the juxtaposition of the orange sponge encrustation against the hull steel for color contrast shots. Grouper and snapper portraits near the bow are excellent subjects. Video lights significantly enhance video footage at this depth.
