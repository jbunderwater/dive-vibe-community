# Los Arcos Marine Park

Iconic granite arch formations with tunnels, swim-throughs, and some of Banderas Bay's best macro life — Puerto Vallarta's most-visited dive site

## Overview

Los Arcos is a cluster of granite islets rising dramatically from the sea just south of Mismaloya, forming the centerpiece of Banderas Bay's diving scene. The largest formation measures roughly 160 meters wide and towers 50 meters above the waterline, but the real spectacle is underwater: a network of arches, tunnels, and caves that divers can swim through at recreational depths. The site is protected as a national marine park, which means no fishing or removal of marine life — a policy that has produced some of the most abundant reef communities on Mexico's Pacific coast.

The western side of the main islet holds two more challenging sites, El Cañón and La Quijada del Diablo (The Devil's Jaw), where the rock face forms a near-vertical wall descending beyond 25 meters. The central and eastern areas, including the submerged peak known as El Bajo del Cristo and the sandy-bottom section called Las Lajas, suit a wider range of experience levels.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Near Mismaloya, 12 km south of Puerto Vallarta
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Pinnacle with tunnels and arches
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 18 meters (60 feet) for main areas; 35 meters on El Cañón
- **Typical Visibility**: 10-20 meters
- **Water Temperature**: 22-28°C (72-82°F)
- **Current**: Usually mild; can strengthen on western wall
- **Best Time**: May to November

## Marine Life

Los Arcos is particularly renowned for its nudibranchs — brilliantly colored sea slugs tucked into every rocky crevice. Moray eels are ubiquitous, with green and tiger morays occupying holes throughout the arches. Octopus are common on the rocky slopes, often spotted hunting at dusk. Sea turtles visit regularly, resting on the bottom or cruising along the reef top. Schools of snapper and parrotfish move through the arches in large numbers, and eagle rays are a frequent sight along the base of the formations. Look for lobster sheltering under ledges and watch the sandy areas at Las Lajas for garden eels and jawfish. In winter months, humpback whales can sometimes be heard (and occasionally seen) in the deeper blue water beyond the site.

## Dive Profile

Most dives begin on the calmer eastern side, descending to El Bajo del Cristo at 12-18 meters, then working through the arches and tunnels. Swim-throughs range from short caverns you can navigate with a single kick to a notable 20-meter passage accessible from the base of the main arch. Beginners typically stay between 5-12 meters in the sheltered central area. Intermediate and advanced divers can cross to the western wall, where El Cañón opens as a narrow canyon starting at 20 meters and dropping to 30+ meters with dramatically eroded walls. Allow extra bottom time for exploring the tunnel systems.

## Entry and Exit

All diving is by boat — Los Arcos is a 10-15 minute ride from Mismaloya Beach, or roughly 30 minutes from the Puerto Vallarta marina. Operators moor or drift the formations; entry is giant stride off the back of the boat. Carry an SMB for ascent, as boat traffic around the islets can be heavy, especially midday.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Dive early morning for the best visibility and fewest other groups on the water
- A dive torch brings out the nudibranchs and reveals creatures inside the tunnels
- El Cañón and La Quijada del Diablo are for experienced divers — notify the divemaster if you want to access the western wall
- The site is also popular with snorkelers, so maintain awareness on ascent
- Night dives here are excellent: octopus, crustaceans, and sleeping turtles are all more visible after dark
