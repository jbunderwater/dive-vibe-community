# Los Arcos Marine Park

Iconic granite rock arches and tunnels south of Mismaloya — Puerto Vallarta's most-visited dive site and a protected marine park teeming with nudibranchs, moray eels, and Pacific reef species

## Overview

Los Arcos (officially Los Arcos de Mismaloya) is a cluster of granite islets located about 7 miles south of Puerto Vallarta, between Mismaloya Beach and Las Gemelas. The formations rise dramatically above the waterline and are protected as a national marine park — no fishing or removal of marine life is permitted, which has produced notably dense marine communities by Mexican Pacific standards.

Underwater, the site divides into several named areas. El Acuario (The Aquarium) is the shallowest section, staying under 12 meters, and is used specifically for beginner and introductory dives thanks to its calm conditions and extraordinary fish density. El Bajo del Cristo covers a submerged peak ranging from 9 to 24 meters, with volcanic rock formations housing snappers, parrotfish, moray eels, and nudibranchs. El Cañón del Diablo (Devil's Canyon) is the most demanding area: an approximately 800-foot vertical wall that begins around 18 meters and drops toward 36 meters, featuring dramatically eroded canyon walls and stronger current exposure. The surrounding waters are among the deepest in Banderas Bay.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Near Mismaloya, 12 km south of Puerto Vallarta
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef with arches and tunnels
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner (main areas); Intermediate to Advanced (El Cañón)
- **Maximum Depth**: 13 meters (El Acuario); 36 meters (El Cañón del Diablo)
- **Typical Visibility**: 10-20 meters
- **Water Temperature**: 22-28°C (72-82°F)
- **Current**: Mild in central areas; can strengthen on western wall and in El Cañón
- **Best Time**: May to November

## Marine Life

Los Arcos is best known for nudibranchs — brilliantly colored sea slugs found in every rocky crevice and on tunnel walls throughout the site. Moray eels are ubiquitous in the arches. Octopus are common on the rocky slopes, especially active at dusk. Sea turtles visit regularly, resting on the substrate or cruising the reef top. Eagle rays patrol the base of the formations. Large schools of snapper and parrotfish move through the arches. Lobster shelter under ledges throughout the site. Garden eels and jawfish inhabit the sandy patches at depth. Pufferfish, butterflyfish, and angelfish are staples of the shallow areas. During winter months, humpback whales can sometimes be heard and occasionally spotted in the open water beyond the islets.

## Dive Profile

Most dives begin on the calmer eastern side, descending into El Acuario or El Bajo del Cristo between 9-24 meters, then working through the arches and tunnels. Swim-throughs range from short caverns navigable in a single kick to longer passages that reward exploration with a dive light. Beginners stay in the 5-12 meter zone in the sheltered central section. Intermediate and advanced divers can work toward El Cañón del Diablo on the western side, where the canyon walls drop beyond recreational limits. Allow extra bottom time for the tunnel systems.

## Entry and Exit

All diving at Los Arcos is by boat. The site is a 10-15 minute ride from Mismaloya Beach, or about 30 minutes from the Puerto Vallarta marina. Operators moor or drift the formations depending on conditions; entry is giant stride from the back of the boat. Deploy an SMB before ascending — boat traffic around the islets is heavy during midday hours.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Dive early morning for the best visibility and to avoid the afternoon snorkeling crowds
- Bring a dive torch — nudibranchs and creatures inside the tunnels are far more visible with artificial light
- El Cañón del Diablo is for experienced divers only; notify your divemaster if you want to access the western wall
- Night dives are excellent here: octopus, lobster, and sleeping turtles are far more approachable after dark
- The park protection means marine life is noticeably bolder and more abundant than at unprotected sites in the bay
