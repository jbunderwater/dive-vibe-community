# Las Marietas Islands

UNESCO Biosphere Reserve with volcanic tunnels, caves, and walls — sea turtles, manta rays, moray eels, and nudibranchs in protected marine waters 70 minutes from Puerto Vallarta

## Overview

Las Marietas Islands (Islas Marietas) are a pair of small volcanic islands located approximately 70 minutes by boat northwest of Puerto Vallarta in the open mouth of Banderas Bay. The islands were designated a Marine Protected Area in 2005 and recognized as a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve, protecting one of the most ecologically significant marine environments on Mexico's Pacific coast. Fishing, anchor damage, and removal of marine life are prohibited, and all visitors pay a protected area fee.

The islands were originally made famous internationally by oceanographer Jacques Cousteau in the 1970s. Underwater, the volcanic origins of the islands are evident everywhere: the formations are riddled with passages, tunnels, caverns, and caves created by lava flow erosion over millennia. Two main dive sites serve most trips: La Montana (The Mountain), where instructors assess diver skill levels on a broad underwater slope with volcanic formations, and La Pared (The Wall), where passages, openings, and tunnels penetrate the volcanic rock from multiple angles. Hard coral coverage is significant, and gorgonians, sea fans, sponges, nudibranchs, and echinoderms cover the walls.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Northwestern Banderas Bay; 70 minutes from Puerto Vallarta
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Cave/cavern
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 35 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 10-12 meters average; up to 20 meters in good conditions
- **Water Temperature**: 17-30°C (seasonal variation; thermoclines possible)
- **Current**: Moderate; can be strong on exposed sides
- **Best Time**: November to May (calmer seas, better visibility)

## Marine Life

Sea turtles are one of the Marietas' most reliable encounters — both green and hawksbill turtles inhabit the protected waters, and they are accustomed to diver presence. Manta rays pass through the islands, particularly when current is running. Moray eels occupy the volcanic passages throughout the site. The hard coral and gorgonian growth supports nudibranchs, mollusks, and echinoderms in good density. Tropical fish species include angelfish, butterflyfish, parrotfish, snapper, and damselfish across the reef areas. Dolphins are sometimes encountered near the islands on the surface transit. The protected status means marine life is notably more abundant and less wary than at unprotected sites of similar depth range.

## Dive Profile

La Montana is the shallower, more exploratory site — divers work across a broad volcanic slope and over rock formations, suitable for a range of experience levels. La Pared is more technical, with passages and tunnels requiring good buoyancy control to navigate without disturbing the walls. Depths at La Pared reach 35 meters on the deeper passages. A torch is essential for the tunnel sections. Both sites can be covered in a two-tank day trip. The protected area rules mean dive groups are accompanied by certified guides familiar with the access restrictions.

## Entry and Exit

Boat departure from Nuevo Vallarta (approximately 8:30 AM) or Marina Vallarta (approximately 9:00 AM), with the crossing taking about 70 minutes. A protected area fee is payable at the islands. Entry is giant stride; dive boats anchor or moor in designated areas. Full-day trips typically run 4-6 hours including two dives and surface interval.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Book in advance — the protected area has visitor limits and popular trip dates fill quickly
- Bring a dive torch; the volcanic tunnels at La Pared are dark and the encrusting life on the walls is far more visible with light
- Water temperature can be significantly cooler than Puerto Vallarta — bring an appropriate wetsuit (5mm recommended outside summer)
- Sea turtles are relaxed here but give them space, especially when surfacing to breathe
- The northern exposed side of the islands (El Morro) experiences stronger current and is suited for more experienced divers only
