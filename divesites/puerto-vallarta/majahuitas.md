# Majahuitas

Protected cove where the Sierra Madre mountains plunge into the sea — five underwater trails, a large Cortez garden eel colony, spotted eagle rays, sea turtles, and occasional giant manta rays to 33 meters

## Overview

Majahuitas is an isolated cove on the south shore of Banderas Bay where the Sierra Madre Occidental mountains drop directly into the Pacific. No road reaches the village of Majahuitas — all access is by water — and the result is a marine environment that sees relatively little disturbance compared to sites closer to Puerto Vallarta. The underwater landscape reflects this: fish populations are healthy, marine life is relaxed, and the reef structure is intact.

The site offers five possible underwater trails through the rocky reef and sandy channel areas, which means repeat visits explore different sections without repetition. The trails work through a combination of rock formations, sandy patches, and coral-encrusted reef at depths ranging from 8 to 33 meters. Majahuitas is best known for its large Cortez garden eel colony — one of the most significant concentrations in the bay — and for regular encounters with spotted eagle rays that patrol the deeper sandy sections.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Southern Banderas Bay, ~45 min from Puerto Vallarta; boat access only
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 33 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 10-25 meters
- **Water Temperature**: 22-28°C (72-82°F)
- **Current**: Mild, with occasional light surge
- **Best Time**: May to November

## Marine Life

Cortez garden eels are the signature species at Majahuitas, emerging from sandy bottom sections in large numbers across a broader area than at most other bay sites. The colony's size makes observation easier without fully spooking the eels. Spotted eagle rays patrol the reef edges and deeper sandy zones regularly. Sea turtles — including hawksbill and olive ridley — visit the site and are unhurried around divers. Giant manta rays appear occasionally, particularly during the warmer months when current increases nutrient flow through the bay. Reef fish include parrotfish, angelfish, damselfish, and trumpetfish. Octopus are present on the rocky sections throughout the site.

## Dive Profile

Dives at Majahuitas follow one of five underwater trails through rock and sandy terrain, typically starting in the shallower zone (8-15 meters) and working deeper based on experience level and trail selection. The most popular trails lead to the garden eel colony on the sandy flats and the deeper reef sections where eagle rays are most commonly spotted. The mild current and relatively sheltered cove environment make buoyancy and navigation comfortable for intermediate divers. Advanced divers can extend to 30-33 meters to access the deeper reef structure.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access from Puerto Vallarta, approximately 45 minutes each way. Giant stride entry; exit back to the boat. Conditions inside the cove are consistently calmer than the open bay, though a light surge may be present on the rocky sections.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Approach garden eel colonies from a distance, descending slowly rather than swimming directly toward them — they retract completely when startled and may take several minutes to re-emerge
- Eagle rays are most frequently spotted on the sandy transitions between reef sections at 15-25 meters
- Five distinct trails means repeat dives here are genuinely different — ask the divemaster to take you on a different route on each visit
- Sea turtles here are accustomed to low-traffic diving; maintain distance when they surface to breathe
- Morning light illuminates the shallower reef sections well for natural-light photography
