## Shark Point

The northwest tip of Gili Trawangan — a current-prone drift site with blacktip and whitetip reef sharks, canyons, and the Glenn Nusa tugboat wreck at 28 meters.

## Overview

Shark Point is on the northwest side of Gili Trawangan, where sandy bottom transitions to progressively deeper canyons. The site is known for reef sharks and is subject to strong current, making it Advanced-level diving. The Glenn Nusa, a tugboat deliberately sunk in February/March 2016 as an artificial reef, lies on a flat sandy bottom at approximately 28 meters between the Halik and Shark Point dive sites. The wreck measures approximately 22–29 meters long and was intentionally cleaned and sunk as a collaborative project between Gili Islands dive centers and the Gili Eco Trust. The wreck requires Advanced Open Water certification due to its depth, and is only accessible when current conditions permit.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Northwest Gili Trawangan, accessible from Lombok
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Drift dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 28 meters
- **Current**: Strong; wreck accessible only in moderate current
- **Best Time**: May to October

## Marine Life

Whitetip and blacktip reef sharks at the canyon systems (confirmed by multiple operators). The Glenn Nusa wreck hosts oriental sweetlips, snappers, unicornfish, and — per Dive Central Gili — batfish, blue-spotted stingrays, pipefish, leaf scorpionfish, and nudibranchs. Turtles and lobsters noted at the site by Gili Divers.

## Safety Considerations

Strong current is the primary hazard — the wreck is inaccessible in strong current and divers dive Shark Point instead. Advanced Open Water certification required. Always carry an SMB and coordinate the pickup point with the boat before descent.

## Notes on the Glenn Nusa Wreck

The Glenn Nusa was a tugboat purchased, stripped, cleaned, and intentionally sunk in February/March 2016 as an artificial reef. The project was a collaboration between all Gili Islands dive centers and the Gili Eco Trust. The wreck lies upright on a sandy bottom at ~28 meters and was designed with large openings for safe penetration. This claim is confirmed by three independent operator sources.
