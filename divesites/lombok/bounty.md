## Bounty

The Gili Islands' most accessible wreck dive — a former construction pontoon on Gili Meno's slope, fully colonised by soft corals and sheltering pygmy seahorses, frogfish, and angelfish.

## Overview

The Bounty Wreck (also called Meno Wreck) is a sunken pontoon lying off the coast of Gili Meno. The structure originally served as a floating pier used during the construction of the Gili Meno jetty. Sources disagree on exactly when it sank: Dive Central Gili and Trawangan Dive state it sank during a storm in 2001; PADI describes it as sinking "around 2003." The pontoon was left where it came to rest and later incorporated into the Gili Eco Trust's Biorock reef restoration network.

The wreck is approximately 30 m long and 15 m wide. Sources also differ on the depth of its shallowest point: some state 6 m, others 8–9 m; the deepest section reaches 17–18 m. The accessible depth range makes it suitable for Open Water divers throughout.

Over the years the structure has been completely colonised by soft and hard corals, including large gorgonian sea fans, and now functions as a thriving artificial reef. Pygmy seahorses are found in the soft coral growth, and the Bounty is widely described by local operators as one of the best sites in the Gili Islands for this species.

**Note on location:** OSM coordinate data places this site on the northwest side of Gili Meno. Multiple dive operator websites describe it as "south-west" of Gili Meno. The discrepancy has not been resolved across sources.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Off the coast of Gili Meno (location described as south-west by most operators; OSM coordinate indicates northwest — discrepancy unresolved)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive (pontoon/artificial reef)
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate (accessible to Open Water divers throughout the wreck)
- **Maximum Depth**: 18 meters
- **Shallowest Point**: Sources report 6–9 m (sources disagree; confirmed to be very shallow)
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–25 m
- **Current**: Mild to moderate
- **Best Time**: May to October

## Marine Life

Pygmy seahorses in the soft coral growth of the wreck — confirmed by multiple local operators as the best site in the Gilis for this species. Frogfish in various colour morphs on the encrusted hull. Scorpionfish camouflaged against the hull surface. Ornate ghost pipefish in the wreck structure. Angelfish species (confirmed: emperor, blue-girdled, masked varieties reported). Schooling fusiliers and butterflyfish around the wreck. Sweetlips in resident groups. Drummerfish around the hull. Turtles cruise the shallow sections.

*All species listed are confirmed at this specific site by local dive operators. Sea moths also occasionally reported.*

## Dive Profile

Descend to the deepest section at 17–18 m and work up toward the shallowest point at 6–9 m. Search soft coral growth on the hull methodically for pygmy seahorses — your guide will know likely positions. The entire wreck can be circled multiple times at this depth. Complete the safety stop above the shallow section.

## Entry and Exit

Boat from Gili Meno or Gili Trawangan operators. Mooring on or near the wreck. Entry via back roll. Minimal current on most days. Ascent from the wreck structure for the safety stop.

## Tips and Recommendations

- This is the premier Gili Islands site for pygmy seahorse searching — ask your guide for current locations before descending
- Frogfish are cryptic; look for irregular shapes on the encrusted surfaces rather than colour
- A torch reveals full colour on the soft corals
- Combine with Meno Wall for a Gili Meno diving day
- The wreck depth range makes it suitable for Advanced Open Water deep adventure dives

## Safety Considerations

The wreck structure has sharp metal edges — do not touch. Do not attempt interior penetration without wreck diving training and appropriate equipment. Scorpionfish are extremely well-camouflaged on the hull — never rest hands or knees on the wreck surface. Always carry an SMB.

## Photography

Pygmy seahorses on soft coral require a dedicated macro lens and patience. Frogfish in their cryptic positions suit macro. Wide-angle captures the full pontoon structure with accompanying fish. The coral-covered hull makes a strong close-focus wide-angle subject.
