## Thomas T. Tucker

Thomas T. Tucker is a US Liberty ship wrecked on its maiden voyage at Olifantsbos Point, Cape Point, on 27 November 1942, now lying in 5–10 metres of cold Atlantic water within Table Mountain National Park.

## Overview

The SS Thomas T. Tucker (Hull Number 269) was a Liberty ship built by the Houston Shipbuilding Corporation, laid down 16 June 1942, launched 31 August 1942, and completed 21 September 1942. On her maiden voyage from New Orleans to Suez carrying cargo for British forces in North Africa — including 25 Sherman tanks, 16 tank cars, and 200 motor vehicles — she ran aground at Olifantsbos Point on 27 November 1942. The captain had misjudged the ship's position, in part because the large quantity of iron in the cargo caused a compass deviation of approximately 37 degrees. He believed the ship was near Robben Island; she had in fact missed Cape Town by some 23 nautical miles. There were no casualties, and a rescue operation over approximately five months removed crew and some equipment.

The wreck is now split into three main sections strewn across a rocky reef in 5–10 metres of water, with the boiler positioned higher on the beach. The hull plates, machinery, and scattered metal are thoroughly colonised by Cape kelp and cold-water invertebrates. Visibility at the exposed, surf-washed site is generally poor. Access is on foot from the Olifantsbos parking area within Table Mountain National Park — this is a shore-entry dive, not a boat dive. A TMNP permit is required.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Olifantsbos Point, Cape Point, Table Mountain National Park (MPA), Cape Town
- **Entry Type**: Shore entry (on foot from Olifantsbos parking area)
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive (scattered sections)
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 10 meters (surf zone)
- **Water Temperature**: 8–14°C — thick wetsuit or drysuit required
- **Visibility**: Generally poor due to surge and particle load
- **Access**: TMNP permit required; site diveable only on exceptionally calm days

## Marine Life

The wreck is heavily colonised by Cape kelp (Ecklonia maxima) and cold-water invertebrates typical of the Atlantic seaboard. No site-specific marine life species reports were found for this site beyond general kelp and encrusting invertebrate communities.

## Tips

- Check surf forecast rigorously — the site is exposed to full Atlantic swell and rarely calm
- The most rewarding approach is the Olifantsbos Shipwreck Trail on foot, combining Thomas T. Tucker with the nearby Nolloth wreck (~3km)
- At low tide, wreck sections are partially exposed on the beach and can be inspected without diving
