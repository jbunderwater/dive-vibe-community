# Castle Rock

*Rocky reef dive in Simon's Town, False Bay, with dense kelp, endemic species, and regular Cape fur seal interactions*

## Overview

Castle Rock is a well-known shore dive in Simon's Town on the western shore of False Bay. The reef structure here consists of large granite and sandstone boulders draped in dense Cape kelp (Ecklonia maxima), creating a layered habitat that Cape Town's cold-water species depend on. The site takes its name from the prominent rocky outcrop visible from the shoreline. Water temperatures range from 8°C in winter to around 14°C in summer, driven by the Atlantic's Benguela Current, making this a cold-water destination requiring a 5mm or 7mm wetsuit. Visibility is highly variable — from 3 metres in surge conditions to 15 metres on calm summer days. The best diving typically occurs in the calmer summer months (December to April), with a southeast wind abating before mid-morning.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Simon's Town, False Bay, Cape Town, South Africa
- **Entry Type**: Shore entry
- **Site Type**: Coral reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 18 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 3-15 meters (10-50 feet)
- **Water Temperature**: 8-14°C (46-57°F) — wetsuit 5-7mm essential
- **Current**: Variable, surge in swell
- **Best Time**: November to April (calmer conditions)

## Marine Life

Cape fur seals (Arctocephalus pusillus) are the signature sighting — playful and inquisitive, they often approach divers closely in the kelp. Broadnose sevengill sharks (Notorynchus cepedianus) patrol the sandy margins between reef patches, especially in summer. Other typical species include spotted gully sharks, pyjama sharks, kob, steenbras, Cape knifejaws, Roman sea bream, snoek, klipvis (klipfish), various nudibranchs, sea cucumbers, red bait (ascidians), and dense brittle star populations covering the boulders. Octopus are common in crevices and overhangs.

## Dive Profile

Entry is over the rocky shoreline — check the swell and surge before committing. Descend directly off the rocks to the kelp fringe, then work your way deeper along the reef structure. The shallowest kelp begins at around 2–3 metres and the rocky substrate extends to 18 metres before giving way to sand. The most productive zones are the kelp forest margins at 5–12 metres where seals hunt and sharks rest. Plan your dive to end at 50 bar or more reserve given the shore exit over rocks.

## Entry and Exit

Enter from the rocky shore at the base of Castle Rock. A good grip of the rocks and wave awareness are essential — wait for a lull in the surge before stepping in. Exit at the same point or slightly upcurrent. Booties and gloves are recommended for the entry scramble. The shore entry demands comfortable experience with kelp navigation.

## Tips

- Dive in the morning before sea breezes pick up — southeast wind creates uncomfortable surface chop by afternoon
- Wearing a hood significantly reduces heat loss in 8–10°C water; full 5mm or 7mm wetsuit or drysuit is appropriate
- Sevengill sharks are more common during summer months; dawn dives improve chances of sighting
- Never chase or corner seals — let them approach on their own terms
- Carry a surface marker buoy (SMB) for any open-water safety stop

## Safety

Cold water induces rapid heat loss — plan shorter dives if wearing a wetsuit in winter conditions. The rocky shore entry and exit is the primary hazard; abort the dive if swell exceeds your confidence level. Sevengill sharks are generally docile but should not be baited or cornered. Always dive with a buddy in the kelp forest, where orientation can be disorienting for new visitors.

## Photography

Castle Rock is excellent for wide-angle photography of seal interactions in the kelp canopy, and for macro work on the boulder surfaces where nudibranchs, sea slugs, and brittle stars are abundant. The kelp fronds backlit by surface light make strong atmospheric shots. Strobes or video lights are needed in deeper sections where ambient light is filtered by the dense canopy.
