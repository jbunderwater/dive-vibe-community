# Castle Rock

*Shore dive in Simon's Town with kelp forest, Cape fur seals, and a diverse granite boulder reef to 18 metres*

## Overview

Castle Rock is a well-known shore dive in Simon's Town on the western shore of False Bay, sitting at the southern edge of the Castle Rocks restricted zone within the Table Mountain National Park Marine Protected Area. The site takes its name from the prominent rocky outcrop visible from the shoreline. The reef consists of large granite and sandstone boulders draped in dense Cape kelp (Ecklonia maxima), creating layered habitat that Cape Town's cold-water species depend on. Massive pinnacles rise from 14 metres up to 5 metres, providing an average dive depth of around 10 metres.

Water temperatures range from 8°C in winter to around 14°C in summer, driven by the Benguela Current, making this a cold-water destination requiring a 5mm or 7mm wetsuit. Visibility is highly variable — from 3 metres in surge conditions to 15 metres on calm summer days. The best diving typically occurs in calmer summer months (November to April), before sea breezes build in the afternoon.

*Note: broadnose sevengill sharks were historically a signature species here ("Millers Point Shark Alley"), but a local Cape Town dive operator (Dive Team Cape Town) reports they have been elusive since 2016. Sevengill sharks have been removed as a reliable current sighting. Great white sharks are absent from False Bay since approximately 2017–2018.*

## Site Information

- **Location**: Simon's Town, False Bay, Cape Town, South Africa
- **Entry Type**: Shore entry
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 18 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 3–15 meters
- **Water Temperature**: 8–14°C — 5–7mm wetsuit or drysuit essential
- **Current**: Variable; surge in swell
- **Best Time**: November to April (calmer conditions)

## Marine Life

Cape fur seals are the signature sighting — playful and inquisitive, regularly encountered in the kelp. Pyjama sharks, spotted gully sharks, and shy sharks rest in the reef crevices. Other documented species include Cape knifejaw, Roman sea bream, snoek, klipvis (klipfish), nudibranchs, sea cucumbers, red bait (ascidians), brittle stars, and octopus.

*Broadnose sevengill sharks, formerly a signature species at Castle Rock ("Millers Point Shark Alley"), have been reported as elusive since 2016 by local dive operators. They cannot be listed as a reliable sighting.*

## Dive Profile

Descend directly off the rocks to the kelp fringe, then work your way deeper along the reef structure. The shallowest kelp begins at around 2–3 metres and the rocky substrate extends to 18 metres before giving way to sand. The most productive zones are the kelp forest margins at 5–12 metres where seals hunt. Plan your dive to end at 50 bar or more reserve given the shore exit over rocks.

## Entry and Exit

Enter from the rocky shore at the base of Castle Rock. Good grip on the rocks and wave awareness are essential — wait for a lull in surge before stepping in. Exit at the same point or slightly upcurrent. Booties and gloves are recommended for the entry scramble.

## Tips

- Dive in the morning before sea breezes pick up — SE wind creates uncomfortable surface chop by afternoon
- A hood significantly reduces heat loss in 8–10°C water
- Observe seals passively — sit still on the sand and they often approach out of curiosity
- Carry a surface marker buoy (SMB) for any open-water safety stop

## Safety

Cold water induces rapid heat loss — plan shorter dives if wearing a wetsuit in winter. The rocky shore entry and exit is the primary hazard; abort the dive if swell exceeds your comfort level. Always dive with a buddy in the kelp forest, where orientation can be difficult for new visitors.

## Photography

Castle Rock is excellent for wide-angle photography of seal interactions in the kelp canopy, and for macro work on the boulder surfaces where nudibranchs, sea slugs, and brittle stars are abundant. The kelp fronds backlit by surface light make strong atmospheric shots.
