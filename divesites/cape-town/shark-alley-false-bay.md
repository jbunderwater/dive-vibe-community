## Shark Alley (False Bay)

Shark Alley (False Bay) is a shark-encounter boat dive site adjacent to Seal Island in False Bay, historically famous for great white shark breaching — though great whites have been largely absent from False Bay since approximately 2017–2018.

## Overview

Shark Alley is a shallow channel near Seal Island in False Bay, where the Cape fur seal colony historically attracted large numbers of great white sharks hunting from below — a behaviour well-documented by researchers and filmed for wildlife documentaries. The breaching season ran from approximately June to September. However, peer-reviewed research published in 2025 (Frontiers in Marine Science) confirmed that great white sharks disappeared from False Bay between 2015 and 2019, with the population effectively absent since mid-2018. The primary driver appears to be predation pressure from two orcas, nicknamed Port and Starboard, which specifically target shark livers; lethal shark net operations have also been implicated.

Cage diving operations at Seal Island continue, but the sharks most commonly encountered in False Bay today are bronze whaler sharks (copper sharks), which have increased in abundance as the great white population declined. Occasional great white sightings have been reported from 2023 onward — potentially signalling a partial return — but sightings remain sporadic and cannot be relied upon. Divers visiting primarily for great white encounters should consider Gansbaai (Shark Alley between Dyer Island and Geyser Rock) as a more reliable current alternative.

## Site Information

- **Location**: False Bay, near Seal Island, Cape Town, South Africa
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 10 meters
- **Water Temperature**: 14–18°C — wetsuit essential
- **Best Season**: June–September historically for great whites; currently uncertain

## Marine Life

Cape fur seals are abundant at Seal Island (one of Africa's largest seal colonies). Bronze whaler sharks are the primary shark species currently sighted in cage diving operations. Broadnose sevengill sharks have also increased in the area following the great white decline. Great white sharks were absent from False Bay from approximately 2017–2018; sporadic sightings have occurred from late 2023, but consistent presence is not confirmed.

*No diving qualifications are required for shark cage experiences at this site. Open-water diving adjacent to the cage is not a standard offering.*
