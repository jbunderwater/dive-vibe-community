## SAS Transvaal Wreck

SAS Transvaal Wreck is the northernmost of five scuttled vessels in Smitswinkel Bay, False Bay — a Loch-class frigate lying upright at 34 metres that forms the centrepiece of one of South Africa's most celebrated artificial reef wreck complexes.

## Overview

The SAS Transvaal (HMSAS Transvaal F602) was a Loch-class frigate of 2,000 tonnes displacement and 93.6 metres length, originally laid down on 20 January 1944 at Harland and Wolff in Belfast as HMS Loch Ard, and launched 2 August 1944. She was transferred to the South African Naval Defence Force during fitting-out and completed as HMSAS Transvaal on 21 May 1945. The vessel served in the South African Navy before being sold for scrap and scuttled in Smitswinkel Bay on 3 August 1978 to form an artificial reef.

The wreck lies upright on a flat sandy bottom with its bow pointing northeast at 34 metres, making it one of the deepest of the five Smitswinkel wrecks. The main deck lies at approximately 29 metres. The wreck is deteriorating: the bow section has broken from the hull just aft of the foredeck winch and fallen over to starboard, and upper deck structures have collapsed, though the quarterdeck remains in relatively good condition with intact rudder and shaft brackets. Internal compartments in the central section are accessible via vertical shafts but are structurally unstable — penetration carries significant risk and should not be attempted without proper training and equipment.

Invertebrate colonisation is exceptional: the wreck hosts encrusting communities of sponges, soft corals, sea fans, walking anemones, and mauve sea cucumbers. Scorpionfish are well-camouflaged against the encrusted plating and present a sting hazard. Visibility typically ranges from 6–8 metres, with occasional days exceeding 20 metres in winter when the bay settles after SE winds.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Smitswinkel Bay, False Bay (northernmost of five wrecks), Cape Town
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive (from Miller's Point, ~4km)
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive (upright frigate)
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 34 meters (sand); main deck at ~29m
- **Water Temperature**: 10–16°C — drysuit or 7mm wetsuit recommended
- **Visibility**: 6–8m typical; up to 20m+ on good winter days
- **Best Time**: Winter (April–September)
- **Permit**: TMNP area — check current access requirements

## Marine Life

Sponges, soft corals, sea fans, walking anemones, mauve sea cucumbers, and barred fingerfish are documented at this wreck by authoritative sources. Scorpionfish are common and well-camouflaged. Fish density is high throughout the wreck structure. The site hosts invertebrates rarely documented elsewhere in the region.

*Wreck penetration requires appropriate training and equipment — interior access carries risks of disorientation, silt-out, and structural collapse. This should not be characterized as routine.*

## Tips

- Bring a torch — invertebrate colours are dramatic but require light at depth
- Fine silt inside the wreck becomes suspended instantly and reduces visibility to near zero; avoid fin kicks near the sediment
- Surge from southerly swells can make the anchor descent uncomfortable; dive in settled conditions
- Scorpionfish are easy to brush against; examine surfaces before touching
