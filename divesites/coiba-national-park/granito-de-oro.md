## Granito de Oro

Granito de Oro (Little Golden Grain) is a small volcanic islet off Isla Coiba's northeastern coast, encircled by a healthy coral reef that multiple operators and dive guides rate among Coiba's best sites for beginners and intermediate divers.

## Overview

The islet rises steeply from the water on a strip of white sand with a few palm trees, and beneath the surface the volcanic rock is wrapped in a ring of hard coral reef. The site is notably protected from the currents that sweep the more exposed western sites, giving it consistently calmer conditions and often better visibility than elsewhere in the park. Depths slope gradually from 5 meters near shore down to around 18 meters at the reef base. Most diving takes place between 5 and 15 meters, where coral cover and fish concentrations are highest.

Granito de Oro is accessible on day trips from Santa Catalina (roughly 1.5 hours by boat) as well as on liveaboards, and is frequently used as a first or last dive of the day to bookend more demanding dives at El Faro or Montana Rusa. Difficulty is Intermediate rather than Advanced: while Coiba's remote location always demands respect, the site itself has mild currents and shallow depths suitable for newly certified divers comfortable with boat diving.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Northeastern Coiba Island, Panama
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 18 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–25 meters
- **Current**: Mild (protected site)
- **Water Temperature**: 26–30°C year-round
- **Best Time**: Year-round; December–April for calmest seas

## Marine Life

Confirmed sightings at this site include hawksbill and green sea turtles, whitetip reef sharks (primarily in the deeper rocky sections), king angelfish, razor surgeonfish, bluebarred parrotfish, white-spotted puffer, spot-fin porcupinefish, moray eels, and large schools of grunts and snapper. The snorkeling-report.com survey also lists wrasses and triggerfish. Macro life is abundant in crevices along the volcanic rock faces. The site is widely described as having an "aquarium-like" quality given the density of species in a small area.

No site-specific reports of bull sharks or pelagic megafauna were found for Granito de Oro; those encounters are associated with the more exposed sites such as El Faro and Montana Rusa.

## Dive Profile

Enter in shallow water (5–8 meters) and follow the reef slope down to around 15–18 meters where rocky substrate and deeper coral sections begin. Most divers spend the majority of the dive in the 5–12 meter zone where light is best and fish density highest. The site is small enough that a single tank allows a full circumnavigation at a relaxed pace. Ascend along the coral slope for a final safety stop in the shallows.

## Entry and Exit

Boat-only access. Anchoring is typically done in the shallow sand zone near the islet. Giant-stride or back-roll entry. Exit by ascending to the anchor area or signaling the boat with an SMB for pickup if drift has occurred.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Excellent first or last dive of the day — low air consumption allows extended time in the shallows
- Macro photography rewarding: look for puffers and porcupinefish in crevices
- Turtles are commonly encountered year-round; approach slowly and avoid chasing
- Snorkeling is also excellent for non-divers in the group

## Safety Considerations

Mild conditions for Coiba, but standard boat-dive safety applies: deploy SMB on ascent, maintain buddy contact, follow the briefing. Sun exposure is high on shallow profiles.

## Seasonal Notes

Diveable year-round. Dry season (December–April) provides calmest surface conditions and warmest water (29–30°C). Rainy season (May–November) maintains excellent underwater visibility with slightly cooler surface temperatures (26–28°C).
