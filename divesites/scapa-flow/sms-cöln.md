## SMS Cöln

The most intact of the seven German warships on the seabed of Scapa Flow, SMS Cöln retains all of her main structures in recognisable form — giving divers the clearest sense of what these WWI light cruisers actually looked like, and earning a reputation as one of the finest intermediate wreck dives in northern Europe.

## Overview

SMS Cöln was a light cruiser of the Cöln class, the lead ship of a design of which only two were completed — her sister ship SMS Dresden also lies at Scapa Flow. Built by Blohm & Voss in Hamburg (construction started 1915, launched 5 October 1916), she displaced 5,620 tons and measured 115 metres in length. She was commissioned into service in 1918 — just ten months before the Armistice — and saw little action beyond mining and patrolling in the German Bight. She interned at Scapa Flow on 21 November 1918 and was scuttled on 21 June 1919.

The SMS Cöln lies on her starboard side in 34–36 metres, rising to approximately 22 metres at the shallowest point. Of the four German cruisers at Scapa Flow, the Cöln was the least affected by salvage: only the engine room was stripped, and even there the hull form is preserved. The result is a wreck that still looks like a warship — bridge, armoured control tower, rangefinder, gun positions, torpedo tube, davits, anchor capstans, and even a distinctive raised gun all survive in recognisable condition.

## Marine Life

The Cöln's intact hull surfaces support a rich community of cold-water sessile life. Dead man's fingers in multiple colours encrust the hull, interspersed with plumose anemones and encrusting sponges. Pollock and saithe school above the wreck in summer. Ling and conger eels occupy the deeper hull sections. Lobsters and edible crabs are abundant. Coal chunks spilled from the coal bunkers are a characteristic feature of the seabed around the wreck.

## Dive Profile

The shotline is typically tied to the lifeboat davit at around 22 metres, making descent and orientation easy. From there, work aft and downward toward the bridge section, which is immediately striking — the armoured control tower, with its solid cylindrical form and viewing slits, dominates the midships area. Behind the bridge, a 5.9-inch wedge gun is found under the deck with its barrel pointing upward — a mysterious position no one has been able to fully explain. Anchor chains run from the hawse pipes to the capstans, as clearly as on any model. Moving further aft, a torpedo tube protrudes vertically from the seabed alongside a distinctive high-elevation gun still sitting proudly on deck. Two stern 5.9-inch guns are in their expected positions. The ship's davits hang down over the deck, and the kedge anchor remains in place at the stern. The engine room shows the salvage damage, but the hull shape reforms at the forward bulkhead, and the wreck recovers its full character from there to the bow.

## Entry and Exit

Day charter boats and liveaboards depart from Stromness and St Margaret's Hope. The Cöln has very little current and can be dived at most tidal states — the shotline at the davit at 22m makes for a relaxed descent. Deploy an SMB at 5–6 metres on ascent.

## Tips and Recommendations

- A drysuit is mandatory — water temperature 6–12°C year-round
- The Cöln is arguably the best single wreck dive in Scapa Flow for divers wanting to understand what a German cruiser looked like — plan your first dive here as context for the other sites
- The shotline at 22m keeps the whole dive comfortably within sport diving range
- Torch recommended for the armoured control tower viewing slits and for the coal bunker area
- Look for the unexplained breech-facing gun under the deck — it is a well-known talking point
- Best months: May to September for best conditions and longest days

## Safety Considerations

Water temperature is 6–12°C year-round — a drysuit and appropriate undersuit are essential. The maximum depth of 36m is at the boundary of sport diving limits; narcosis is unlikely to be a serious issue but monitor your condition. The wreck is a protected scheduled monument; artefact removal is illegal under Scottish law. Deploy an SMB on ascent and listen for boat traffic.
