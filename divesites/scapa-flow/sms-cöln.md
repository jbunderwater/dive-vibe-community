## SMS Cöln

SMS Cöln is a historic wreck dive in Scapa Flow, Orkney, Scotland.

## Overview

SMS Cöln was the lead ship of the Cöln-class light cruisers of the Imperial German High Seas Fleet, and one of only two ships of her class to be completed. Built by Blohm & Voss in Hamburg, she was launched on 5 October 1916 and commissioned on 17 January 1918, just ten months before the end of World War I. She measured 155.5 metres in length and displaced 5,620 tonnes normally (7,486 tonnes at full combat load). Cöln saw limited wartime service — mining operations and patrol duties in the German Bight — before the fleet mutiny of October 1918 cancelled a planned final sortie against British forces.

She arrived at Scapa Flow for internment on 22 November 1918. On 21 June 1919, Rear Admiral Ludwig von Reuter ordered the fleet scuttled rather than surrender under the terms of the Treaty of Versailles; Cöln sank at 13:50. She was never raised for salvage.

Today, SMS Cöln is considered the most intact of the four light cruisers remaining on the seabed of Scapa Flow. She rests on her starboard side at 34–36 metres, with the shallowest point — the lifeboat davit — at approximately 22 metres, making her accessible to competent sport divers. The descent line is typically secured to the davit, providing an easy entry. Divers can identify the armoured control tower, the intact bridge structure, anchor chains through the hawse pipes, two aft 5.9-inch guns, a kedge anchor, and a large rudder. The midships section shows salvage damage. Small shoaling fish cover the wreck; pollock, cod, ling, conger, lobster, crab, scallops, squat lobster, starfish, and anemones inhabit the hull and surrounding seabed; seal pups are occasionally encountered.

## Wreck History

- **Built**: Blohm & Voss, Hamburg; launched 5 October 1916, commissioned 17 January 1918
- **Class**: Cöln-class light cruiser (lead ship)
- **Displacement**: 5,620 tonnes standard; 7,486 tonnes full combat load
- **Length**: 155.5 metres
- **Service**: Limited — mining, patrols in German Bight; no major fleet actions
- **Interned at Scapa Flow**: 22 November 1918
- **Scuttled**: 21 June 1919 at 13:50 by order of Rear Admiral von Reuter
- **Current orientation**: On starboard side; shallowest point 22 m, seabed 34–36 m

## Dive Profile

- **Entry**: Boat; descent line to lifeboat davit at 22 m
- **Maximum depth**: 34–36 m
- **Visibility**: Excellent (rated 10/10 by local operators)
- **Current**: Low; rated 6/10 (moderate awareness required)
- **Bottom time**: 30–45 minutes; decompression planning required at deeper sections
- **Penetration**: Not recommended; wreck orientation restricts safe entry

## Marine Life

Small shoaling fish are present throughout the hull. Pollock, cod, and ling school around the superstructure. Conger eels inhabit crevices. Lobster, edible crab, velvet swimming crab, scallops, squat lobster, starfish, and anemones colonise the wreck surfaces. Occasional seal pups visit divers.

## Photography

Cöln's intact profile and relatively shallow access point make it one of the most photogenic wrecks in Scapa Flow. The armoured control tower and gun emplacements provide strong compositional subjects.
