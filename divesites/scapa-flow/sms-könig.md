## SMS König

SMS König is the lead ship of the König-class dreadnought battleships and the most extensively salvage-damaged of the three unsalvaged battleships at Scapa Flow — the only wreck in the Fleet where divers can enter and view internal machinery through salvage access points.

## Overview

SMS König was the lead ship of the König-class dreadnought battleships of the Imperial German High Seas Fleet. She was laid down at Kaiserliche Werft Wilhelmshaven, launched on 1 March 1913, and commissioned on 9 August 1914. Wikipedia gives her length as 175.4 metres and displacement as 25,796 tonnes standard (28,600 tonnes full load); the scapa-flow.co.uk dive operator site gives 146 metres length and 25,388 tonnes — sources disagree, with 175.4 m and 25,796 t being the class-level Wikipedia figure. She was armed with ten 30.5 cm guns in five twin turrets, crewed by 41 officers and 1,095 enlisted men.

König served as vanguard of the III Battle Squadron at the Battle of Jutland on 31 May–1 June 1916, sustaining ten large-calibre shell hits that killed 45 men and wounded 27. She arrived at Scapa Flow for internment on 6 December 1918. On 21 June 1919, Rear Admiral Ludwig von Reuter ordered the fleet scuttled to prevent capture; König sank at 14:00 that day. She was never raised for salvage and was designated a maritime scheduled ancient monument in 2001.

Today, König lies completely inverted on a sandy floor, with the hull rising to approximately 22–24 metres and the seabed at 40 metres. Salvage operations by Metal Industries used dynamite to create access holes, making this the only wreck in the High Seas Fleet where divers can view internal machinery. Despite the extensive salvage damage, the exposed engine room retains four of its six turbines, seven casemate guns, boilers, a 12-inch armoured citadel at the stern, and propeller shafts. She is described as the least-visited of the three surviving battleships.

## Dive Profile

- **Entry**: Boat; descent line to hull at 22–24 m
- **Maximum depth**: 40 m (seabed)
- **Visibility**: Rated 8/10 by local operators; best in summer
- **Current**: Moderate (6/10); check tides
- **Bottom time**: Typically 30–45 minutes at advanced depths
- **Penetration**: Possible via salvage access holes to engine room; requires appropriate wreck penetration training — not appropriate for divers without specific overhead environment experience

## Marine Life

Schools of pollack are commonly seen at the descent line. Cod, ling, and conger inhabit sheltered areas of the hull. Lobster, edible crab, velvet swimming crab, scallops, squat lobster, starfish, and anemones encrust the wreck structure. As the least-visited of the three battleships, marine life is described as abundant relative to the König and Markgraf.

## Safety

Diving SMS König requires Advanced certification minimum. Depth reaches 40 m, and penetration of the hull's interior via salvage access holes carries significant overhead environment risk — wreck penetration training is required before entering any internal section. Tidal conditions dictate dive windows; book with an established Scapa Flow operator. This is a protected scheduled ancient monument — no artefact contact or removal under any circumstances.
