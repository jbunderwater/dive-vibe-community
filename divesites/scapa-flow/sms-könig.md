## SMS König

The most heavily salvaged of the three German battleships at Scapa Flow, SMS König lies completely inverted at 42 metres — and uniquely among the fleet, the extensive salvage blasting means divers can actually see inside the internal workings of a dreadnought-class battleship, making this a historically unrivalled site.

## Overview

SMS König was a König-class battleship laid down in 1911, launched in 1913, and commissioned in 1914. She led the German fleet at the Battle of Jutland in May 1916, absorbing the most punishment of any German ship that day — struck by 10 heavy and six medium-calibre hits, she listed to port and suffered 45 killed and 27 wounded. She interned at Scapa Flow in November 1918 and was scuttled on 21 June 1919.

Today she lies completely upside-down on the seabed with her bows pointing north. Salvage by Nundy's Metal Industries was extensive: the hull was blasted open at multiple points to extract non-ferrous metals. While this makes the König the most structurally damaged of the battleships, it also makes her unique — the only wreck in Scapa Flow where sport divers can see the interior workings of a battleship without penetration. The bow barbette of A turret, the underside of the anchor capstan, seven casemate guns lying upside-down on the seabed along the starboard side, and the two surviving rudders are the headline features.

## Marine Life

The König hosts the same temperate cold-water community found across Scapa Flow's German fleet. Pollock and saithe school around the hull in summer. Ling and conger eels shelter in the blasted sections. Lobsters, edible crabs, velvet swimming crabs, and squat lobsters occupy every crack and crevice. Dead man's fingers — in white, cream, orange, and pink — encrust the hull. Sea urchins and multiple starfish species dot the seabed around the wreck. Plumose anemones create a colourful fringe along the hull edges.

## Dive Profile

The König lies inverted at 42–46 metres at its deepest points (the seabed), with the hull rising to around 24–25 metres at the keel. The bow is the starting point for most dives — the barbette of A turret and anchor capstan undercarriage are clearly visible due to the collapse from salvage blasting. Swimming along the starboard flank reveals seven casemate guns lying on the seabed, their gun barrels pointing outward. At the stern, the propellers have been removed but the port shaft can still be seen, and two rudders remain — one flat, one in situ. The blasted sections in the midships area allow views into internal spaces not accessible on the other battleships.

Given the depth (42m+), bottom time is limited to around 15–20 minutes at maximum depth on air. Many divers dive nitrox or plan a multi-level profile working from the deeper seabed-level features up to the shallower keel section.

## Entry and Exit

Day charter boats and liveaboards depart from Stromness and St Margaret's Hope. The König can be dived at most tidal states. Descent is on the shot line. Deploy an SMB at the 6-metre safety stop and allow adequate ascent time from depth. A liveaboard allows for two dives on this wreck — highly recommended to do it justice.

## Tips and Recommendations

- A drysuit is mandatory — water temperature 6–12°C year-round
- Nitrox is strongly recommended to extend bottom time at 40m+ depths
- Advanced open-water or AOWD certification with deep dive experience is the minimum recommended level for this site
- Plan your dive carefully: the deepest features (seabed, rudders) compete with the shallower keel, so decide your priority before entry
- A torch is invaluable for illuminating the interior sections exposed by salvage blasting
- Best months: June to August for longest daylight and clearest water in the Flow
- Two dives are needed to see the wreck properly — consider a liveaboard for this reason

## Safety Considerations

Narcosis risk is real at 40m+ on air; diver reaction varies. If you feel impaired, ascend immediately. The depth means decompression obligation accumulates quickly — watch your computer and begin ascent with adequate gas reserve. Cold water (6–12°C) increases gas consumption and fatigue. Never exceed the depth limit appropriate to your certification. Deploy an SMB on ascent to alert surface traffic.
