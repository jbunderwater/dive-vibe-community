## SMS König

SMS König is a historic wreck dive in Scapa Flow, Orkney, Scotland.

## Overview

SMS König was the lead ship of the König-class dreadnought battleships of the Imperial German High Seas Fleet. Laid down at Kaiserliche Werft Wilhelmshaven, she was launched on 1 March 1913 and commissioned on 9 August 1914. She measured 175.4 metres in length and displaced 25,796 tonnes. Armed with ten 30.5 cm guns in five twin turrets, she was crewed by 41 officers and 1,095 enlisted men.

König served as vanguard of the III Battle Squadron at the Battle of Jutland on 31 May–1 June 1916, sustaining ten large-calibre shell hits that killed 45 men and wounded 27. She arrived at Scapa Flow for internment on 6 December 1918. On 21 June 1919, Rear Admiral Ludwig von Reuter ordered the fleet scuttled to prevent capture; König sank at 14:00 that day. She was never raised for salvage and was designated a maritime scheduled ancient monument in 2001.

Today, König lies completely upside down on a sandy floor at 40 metres, with the hull rising to approximately 20–24 metres. Salvage operations by Nundy's (Metal Industries) created access holes through dynamite, making this the only wreck in the High Seas Fleet where divers can view the internal workings. Despite the damage, the exposed engine room retains many intact components, seven casemate guns, turbines, boilers, a 12-inch armoured citadel, and propeller shafts remain identifiable. Being the least dived of the three remaining battleships, marine life is abundant: schools of pollock greet divers at the descent line, and cod, ling, conger, lobster, various crabs, scallops, starfish, and anemones colonise the upturned hull.

## Wreck History

- **Built**: Kaiserliche Werft Wilhelmshaven; launched 1 March 1913, commissioned 9 August 1914
- **Class**: König-class dreadnought battleship (lead ship)
- **Displacement**: 25,796 tonnes standard; 28,600 tonnes full load
- **Length**: 175.4 metres
- **Battle of Jutland**: Served as fleet vanguard, sustained 10 heavy hits, 45 killed
- **Interned at Scapa Flow**: 6 December 1918
- **Scuttled**: 21 June 1919 at 14:00 by order of Rear Admiral von Reuter
- **Current orientation**: Upside down, hull at 20–24 m, seabed at 40 m

## Dive Profile

- **Entry**: Boat; descent line to hull at 20–24 m
- **Maximum depth**: 40 m (seabed)
- **Visibility**: Good (rated 8/10 by local operators); best in summer
- **Current**: Can be present; check tides
- **Bottom time**: Typically 30–45 minutes at advanced depths
- **Penetration**: Possible via salvage access holes to engine room; advanced/technical only

## Marine Life

Schools of pollock are commonly seen at the descent line. Cod, ling, and conger inhabit sheltered areas of the hull. Lobster, edible crab, velvet swimming crab, scallops, squat lobster, starfish, and anemones encrust the wreck structure.

## Safety

Diving SMS König requires Advanced certification minimum. Depth reaches 40 m, and penetration of the hull's interior carries significant overhead environment risk — technical or wreck penetration training required. Tidal conditions dictate dive windows; book with an established Scapa Flow operator.
