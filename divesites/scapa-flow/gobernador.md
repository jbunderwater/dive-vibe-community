## Gobernador

A Scapa Flow blockship in the tidal narrows of Burra Sound, encrusted with cold-water invertebrates and divable only during the brief slack tide window.

## Overview

The Gobernador is one of Scapa Flow's blockships — vessels deliberately sunk to obstruct the narrow sounds around the anchorage, preventing enemy submarine access to the Fleet's mooring ground. The blockships were sunk in the restricted tidal waterways including Burra Sound, where swift currents make diving possible only around the brief slack tide period. The Gobernador lies in 16 meters of water in the powerful Burra Sound narrows, and like the other blockships, has developed an exceptional cold-water invertebrate community as a result of the tidal nutrient delivery. The strong tidal flow that restricts diving also feeds the anemones, hydroids, and soft corals that encrust every surface of the wreck, creating some of the most spectacular cold-water invertebrate photography in the United Kingdom.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Burra Sound, Scapa Flow, Orkney, Scotland
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck (blockship)
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 16 meters (52 feet)
- **Typical Visibility**: 8–20 meters (26–66 feet) — variable with tidal phase
- **Current**: Very strong — slack tide only
- **Water Temperature**: 6–14°C (43–57°F)
- **Best Time**: May–October (slack tide timing critical)

## Marine Life

The strong tidal currents that make Gobernador challenging to dive are the same currents that create its exceptional marine life density. Plumose anemones in white and orange colonies cover every available surface. Dead man's fingers corals (Alcyonium digitatum) are prolific. Conger eels, edible crabs, and velvet swimming crabs occupy the wreck interior. Large schools of saithe move through the sound at slack. Nudibranchs are present on encrusting surfaces. The strong tidal flow maintains excellent visibility compared to more sheltered parts of the Flow.

## Dive Profile

Burra Sound blockship dives require a negative entry — descend immediately upon entering the water to reach the wreck before the current resumes. Bottom time at the wreck during slack is limited — 20–30 minutes is typical. Work systematically along the most accessible sections, staying close to the wreck structure. The shot line provides the ascent route — do not attempt free ascent in tidal waters.

## Entry and Exit

Expert local skipper mandatory for safe Burra Sound diving. A negative entry to reach the wreck quickly as slack tide begins. The dive window is short — experienced Scapa Flow divers plan their dive profile to maximize wreck time within the slack period. Shot line ascent back to the boat. Do not surface in the current — always ascend the line.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Only dive the Gobernador with an experienced Scapa Flow skipper who knows the Burra Sound slack tide windows precisely
- Practice the negative entry technique before the dive — hesitation wastes precious slack time
- Keep cylinders fully open before entry — the rapid descent requires immediately functioning equipment
- The tidal current creates excellent visibility compared to the main Flow — the trade-off of time pressure is worth it
- Bring a primary and backup light source — the wreck interior spaces are dark

## Safety Considerations

Burra Sound diving is the most technically demanding diving in Scapa Flow due to the extreme tidal currents. Diving outside the slack window is extremely dangerous. A missed shot line on ascent in the current can result in a rapidly drifting diver unable to return to the boat. Experienced guide, negative entry, and absolute current awareness are mandatory. This is not a site for Scapa Flow beginners — complete the German fleet dives first.

## Photography

The Gobernador's exceptional invertebrate coverage — denser than most of the German fleet wrecks due to the stronger tidal feeding — creates outstanding cold-water photography opportunities. Plumose anemone colonies in the clear tidal water photograph brilliantly with a video light or strobe. The time pressure of the slack window demands pre-focused camera settings and efficient underwater movement.
