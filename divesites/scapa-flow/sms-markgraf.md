## SMS Markgraf

Scapa Flow's most technically demanding and most dramatic battleship — almost completely upturned on her starboard side at depths reaching 45 metres, with an iconic bow rising from the seabed and twin rudders standing 3.5 metres tall at the stern.

## Overview

SMS Markgraf is the deepest and widely regarded as the most impressive of the three remaining König-class battleships in Scapa Flow. She was the third of the four König-class ships, built by A.G. Weser in Bremen, launched on 4 June 1913, and commissioned on 1 October 1914. She measured 175.4 metres in length and displaced 25,796 tonnes normally (28,600 tonnes at full load). Her armament included ten 30.5 cm (12-inch) guns in five twin turrets. She was scuttled at 16:45 on 21 June 1919 — the last of the three surviving battleships to sink that day.

The Markgraf now lies almost completely upturned on her starboard side, with the superstructure having prevented her from rolling entirely inverted. She is open on the starboard side, and it is along this edge — the compressed interface between the overturned superstructure and the seabed — that much of the diving interest lies. The maximum depth is approximately 45 metres (sources report depths between 45 and 47 metres depending on the section measured), with the shallowest accessible point at 24 metres. Two shot lines are typically rigged: one to the anchor chain overlapping the hull at the bow, and one at the stern. The enormous bow rising sheer from depth is one of the defining wreck images in the world. At the stern, the twin rudders stand approximately 3.5 metres tall and remain intact having survived both the 1919 scuttling and all subsequent salvage. Seven 5.9-inch gun casemates, a 6-inch armour belt, and various rangefinders are accessible for experienced divers.

## Marine Life

The Markgraf's depth limits biological activity compared to shallower wrecks, but the cold-water community is still significant. Plumose anemones are established throughout the accessible hull surfaces. Large conger eels are resident in enclosed hull sections. Spider crabs and edible crabs are found across the wreck. Pollack and saithe use the dramatic bow section as a vertical reference point. The wreck rates 9/10 for marine life abundance according to local operators.

## Dive Profile

Two shot lines allow targeted dives to either the bow or the stern. The bow profile descends the sheer face of the hull to approximately 45 metres — the classic dreadnought stem ascending directly from the seabed, with the massive anchor hawse pipe and the start of the armour belt visible. The stern dive encounters the twin rudders at depth and the portholes of the cruiser stern pressed into the seabed. The seven 5.9-inch gun casemates and the 6-inch armour belt are accessible on the starboard edge of the wreck. Nitrox is strongly recommended to extend no-decompression time at these depths.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive only. Advanced Open Water certification is the minimum; many operators also require Deep Diver and Wreck Diver specialties. Some operators require BSAC Dive Leader or equivalent for sections below 40 metres. Shot line descent and ascent exclusively. Dry suit, cold-water regulators, redundant signalling, and a dive computer calibrated for nitrox are standard kit. A diving permit from Orkney Islands Council Harbours Department is required.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Save the Markgraf for the final days of a Scapa liveaboard week — after completing the Kronprinz Wilhelm and König
- Plan separate dives for the bow and the stern — one dive cannot do justice to both objectives
- The twin stern rudders standing 3.5 metres tall are Scapa Flow's most iconic recognisable stern feature — plan to find them
- At 45 metres, bottom time without nitrox is severely limited; use Nitrox 32 or better
- The two shot lines (bow and stern) reflect the wreck's scale — use the correct one for your planned dive profile
- April–May offers the clearest water before the plankton bloom, ideal for wide-angle bow photography

## Safety Considerations

This is a protected scheduled monument under the Protection of Wrecks Act 1973. The approximately 45-metre maximum depth is beyond what many recreational divers are prepared for in cold water with elevated gas consumption. Gas turns must be planned conservatively. The Markgraf requires honest self-assessment — if 40-metre cold-water diving is not comfortable, this wreck is not appropriate. Penetration of any interior section requires extensive technical wreck training. No artefact contact or removal. A diving permit from Orkney Islands Council Harbours Department is required.

## Photography

The sheer bow rising from the seabed is one of the most photographed images in wreck diving worldwide. A wide-angle lens and powerful strobes are required to approach the scale of the bow. The twin stern rudders are slightly more manageable compositionally but equally impressive. These subjects reward a dedicated photography dive with no competing objectives. Powerful artificial lighting is mandatory — natural light at 45 metres in Scottish waters is negligible.
