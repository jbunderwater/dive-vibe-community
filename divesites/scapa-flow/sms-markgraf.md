## SMS Markgraf

Widely regarded as the crown jewel of Scapa Flow, the SMS Markgraf is the deepest of the German fleet wrecks — a 146-metre König-class battleship lying almost completely upturned at 47 metres, in remarkable condition and offering what many divers consider one of the greatest wreck dives in the world.

## Overview

SMS Markgraf was a König-class battleship displacing 25,797 tons, measuring 146 metres long and 28 metres wide. She saw extensive action in WWI, participating in most of the High Seas Fleet engagements including the Battle of Jutland. After Germany's defeat, she interned at Scapa Flow in November 1918 and was scuttled on 21 June 1919.

She lies almost completely upturned — rolled onto her starboard side but prevented from going fully inverted by her superstructure, which props her up off the seabed. The result is that the starboard side faces divers, and the casemate guns, armour belt, salvage-blasted torpedo room, and the iconic twin rudders are all accessible. Lying at 44–47 metres, the Markgraf was too deep to salvage efficiently; this depth has preserved it in better condition than the other battleships. It was designated a protected scheduled monument in recognition of its historical importance.

## Marine Life

The Markgraf hosts a rich cold-water community typical of the German fleet wrecks. The deeper hull sections are colonised by dead man's fingers, plumose anemones, and encrusting sponges, with the brighter colours concentrated in the 20–30m range where light penetration is better. Pollock and saithe school above the wreck. Ling and conger eels inhabit the darker recesses near the seabed. Lobsters, edible crabs, and velvet crabs occupy the hull plating.

## Dive Profile

The bow rises impressively from the seabed at 47 metres — sitting at the bow looking up towards the lighter surface water is described as one of the defining images of Scapa Flow. The six-inch armour belt is particularly prominent here, held in place by heavy bolts cast into the plates. Along the starboard side are casemate gun positions — 14 smaller 5.9-inch guns used for defence; several remain in place. Salvage teams blasted a deep V-shaped opening in the hull to access the torpedo rooms, exposing gears, shafts, and anchor capstan mechanisms — a striking view into the ship's inner workings. The twin rudders at the stern, rising 3.5 metres straight up from the hull, are another iconic feature. Propeller A-frames have been broken by salvagers, but the shaped hull form that enclosed the propellers is clearly visible.

The top of the wreck (shallowest point) sits at around 24 metres, allowing a multi-level dive: explore the deepest features first, then ascend along the hull for photography and marine life observation before a safety stop.

## Entry and Exit

Liveaboards (based at Stromness) and day charter boats both serve this site. Descent is on the shot line. Given the 47m maximum depth, most divers plan nitrox or careful air profiles. An adequate gas reserve for a multi-level ascent and 5–6 minute safety stop at 6 metres is essential. A liveaboard strongly recommended — two or more dives are needed to see the Markgraf properly.

## Tips and Recommendations

- A drysuit is mandatory — water temperature 6–12°C year-round
- Nitrox 32% is widely used on the Markgraf to extend bottom time at depth
- Advanced certification with documented deep dive experience is recommended; technical divers may use trimix to mitigate narcosis
- Begin at the bow (47m) and work shallower — bow, armour belt, casemate guns, rudders, then ascend along the hull
- A good torch is essential for the blasted torpedo room area and to bring out colours on the deep sections
- Allow at least two dives to appreciate the scale — the wreck is 146 metres long
- Best visibility: June to August; the Flow can have excellent vis (10–20m) in summer

## Safety Considerations

At 47m, narcosis is a significant risk on air — impairment at this depth is well-documented. Know your personal response to nitrogen narcosis and be prepared to abort the dive and ascend if you feel confused or euphoric. Decompression obligation accumulates rapidly below 40m; monitor your dive computer from the moment of descent. Cold water (6–12°C) accelerates air consumption; check your gas at regular intervals. The wreck is a protected scheduled monument — do not touch, move, or remove any artefacts. Deploy an SMB on ascent.
