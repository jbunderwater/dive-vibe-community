## SMS Karlsruhe

The shallowest and most broken-up of the four German cruisers at Scapa Flow, the SMS Karlsruhe rewards patient exploration — its extensively blasted hull offers unparalleled access to the internal engineering of a WWI-era warship, and it is widely regarded as the most life-covered wreck in the German fleet.

## Overview

SMS Karlsruhe was a light cruiser of the Königsberg class, displacing 5,440 tons and measuring 112 metres in length. Built at the Kaiserliche Werft in Kiel in 1916, she carried 8 × 15cm guns, 3 × 8.8cm AA guns, four 50cm torpedo tubes, and up to 200 mines — a formidable mine-laying cruiser. When she led the line of interned cruisers surrendering to the Royal Navy in November 1918, she was the most recently commissioned of the group. She was scuttled off the island of Cava on 21 June 1919.

Salvage operations in 1963–1965 were particularly damaging, blasting much of the midships open to reach non-ferrous metals. Yet for divers, this destruction is part of the site's appeal: nowhere else in Scapa Flow can you see the boiler rooms and engine room machinery so clearly. The bow and stern sections are in far better condition, with recognisable cruiser lines and intact fittings.

## Marine Life

The Karlsruhe consistently impresses divers with the density of its marine life. The broken hull surfaces — greater in area due to the salvage damage — are colonised by dead man's fingers in a range of colours, plumose anemones, encrusting sponges, and soft corals. Conger eels inhabit the darker recesses. Lobsters, edible crabs, and velvet swimming crabs are abundant. Large pollock shoals drift above the wreck in summer. Two 5.9-inch gun turrets remain on the wreck, their open backs revealing breech and elevation mechanisms that make an attractive backdrop for fish photography.

## Dive Profile

The Karlsruhe lies on her starboard side at about 45 degrees, with the seabed at around 27 metres and the shallowest hull rising to approximately 16 metres. The boat mooring is typically placed at the stern. Work forward from the lovely cruiser stern with its intact teak decking, passing the point where the hull opens dramatically — here you can peer into the boiler rooms and observe the complexity of the machinery at close quarters. Moving further forward, the armoured control tower (with 4-inch steel walls) has fallen clear of the wreck; the bridge structure itself was constructed of brass to avoid compass interference. Massive anchors lie on the seabed at the bow, their chains running through the hawse pipes. Two gun turrets with their backs open are among the most photographed features on the wreck.

## Entry and Exit

Day charter boats depart from Stromness and St Margaret's Hope. The Karlsruhe can be dived at any state of the tide, giving it broader windows than the Burra Sound blockships. Entry by giant stride or back roll; ascend on the shot line deploying an SMB at the safety stop.

## Tips and Recommendations

- A drysuit is mandatory — water temperature 6–12°C year-round
- The shallow depth (max 27m, hull at 16m) allows generous bottom times compared to the battleships
- The Karlsruhe is an excellent introduction to the German fleet for divers without deep experience
- Explore the open midships section slowly and with good buoyancy control — silting can reduce visibility inside the hull quickly
- A torch helps illuminate the dark recesses between boilers and machinery
- Visit the two gun turrets near the bow — the open backs are a unique feature not found on the better-preserved cruisers
- Best months: May to September

## Safety Considerations

Water temperature is 6–12°C year-round; drysuit and appropriate undersuit are essential. The silty interior of the blasted midships section can reduce visibility rapidly if buoyancy is poor — maintain trim and avoid fin-kicks near the seabed inside the hull. Narcosis is not a concern at these depths. Deploy an SMB on ascent and listen for boat traffic above.
