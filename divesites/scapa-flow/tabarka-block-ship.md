## Tabarka (block ship)

A tidal Scapa Flow blockship lying upside-down in 15 metres in Burra Sound's powerful current — divable only at slack tide, with its intact propeller as the defining feature and a two-stage sinking history spanning Kirk Sound and Burra Sound.

## Overview

The Tabarka began life as SS Pollux, built in 1909 by Wrf. voorh. Rijkee & Co. in Rotterdam for N.V. Koninkl. Nederland. Stoomboat Mij. (KNSM, the Royal Netherlands Steamship Company) in Amsterdam. She displaced 2,886 tonnes and measured approximately 326.8 feet (approximately 99.6 metres) in length. The vessel was operated by the U.S. Shipping Board from 1918–1919, then acquired by French owners and renamed SS Tabarka in 1931. Subsequent ownership changes occurred in 1937 and 1940 under British Ministry of Shipping management.

In July 1940 she was requisitioned at Falmouth. She was sunk as a blockship in Kirk Sound on 23 March 1941. In 1944, she was raised and resunk as a blockship in Burra Sound on 27 July 1944 — her current location. The Tabarka is described as a "big big favourite" by local dive operators and is regularly requested by name by repeat visitors to Scapa Flow. Her intact propeller remains her most admired feature. Like all Burra Sound diving, the Tabarka can only be dived during the brief slack tide window — precise timing is essential.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Burra Sound, Scapa Flow, Orkney, Scotland
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 15 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 8–25 metres — excellent during tidal flow, limited at full slack
- **Current**: Extreme — slack tide only, negative entry required; peak flow 4–5 knots
- **Water Temperature**: 6–14°C (43–57°F)
- **Best Time**: May–October

## Wreck History

- **Original name**: SS Pollux
- **Built**: 1909, Wrf. voorh. Rijkee & Co., Rotterdam, for KNSM Amsterdam
- **Gross tonnage**: 2,886 tonnes
- **Length**: Approximately 99.6 metres (326.8 feet)
- **Renamed**: SS Tabarka, 1931, under French ownership (Union Maritime Auxiliaire S.A., Rouen)
- **Requisitioned**: July 1940 at Falmouth
- **First sinking**: Kirk Sound, 23 March 1941 (as blockship)
- **Raised and resunk**: Burra Sound, 27 July 1944 (current wreck location)
- **Current orientation**: Upside-down; resting at approximately 15 metres

## Marine Life

The Burra Sound tidal environment delivers exceptional marine life density. The Tabarka's upside-down hull carries prolific plumose anemones on its underside. Dead man's fingers corals are established throughout accessible surfaces. The intact propeller is encrusted with sessile organisms and surrounded by resident fish. Conger eels and edible crabs occupy hull interior spaces accessible through openings in the bow and stern. Schools of saithe pass through the sound on the tidal flow.

## Dive Profile

A negative entry is mandatory — submerge immediately upon entering the water and descend rapidly to the wreck before the current resumes. The slack window is typically 20–30 minutes for active diving. Navigate toward the intact propeller at the stern as the primary objective, then explore the hull underside and accessible sections. The bow and stern remain the most intact sections; the mid-hull is more broken. Ascend on the shot line — free ascent in Burra Sound is not safe.

## Entry and Exit

Expert local skipper with precise knowledge of Burra Sound slack timing is non-negotiable. The negative entry technique must be practised before arrival at the site. The shot line provides the sole ascent route. All cylinders must be fully open and regulators tested before rolling into the water.

## Tips and Recommendations

- This is among the most sought-after blockship dives in Scapa Flow — experienced divers plan their liveaboard week specifically to include it
- Negative entry proficiency is essential — practise it before you need it here
- Verify all equipment is operational before entry — there is no time to sort problems on the wreck
- The intact propeller is the signature feature — navigate to it efficiently within the slack window
- The tidal current creates better visibility here than in the main Flow — the operational complexity is worthwhile

## Safety Considerations

The Tabarka is the most operationally demanding Scapa Flow dive site due to extreme tidal current. The consequences of entering outside slack, losing the shot line, or experiencing equipment failure in Burra Sound are severe. Only experienced divers with proper dry suit training and competent buddies should attempt this dive. The skipper's timing judgement is absolute — if conditions are not right, the dive does not happen.

## Photography

The intact propeller in the clear tidal water is one of Scapa Flow's most photographed features outside the German fleet. The upside-down hull orientation creates unusual perspectives. Pre-set camera exposure and strobe positioning before the dive — there is no time to adjust settings during the limited slack window. Tidal current visibility (often better than the main Flow) rewards the operational complexity with excellent image quality.
