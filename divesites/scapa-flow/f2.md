## F2

A WWII German escort boat paired with the YC-21 salvage barge — two wrecks within 100 metres of each other at 15–16 metres, regularly rated one of the best non-fleet wreck diving sites in Scapa Flow.

## Overview

The F2 was built by Germaniawerft at Kiel in 1935 as a Geleiteboot (escort boat), the second of a class of ten. She displaced 756 tonnes and measured approximately 75 metres in length, with oil-fired steam turbines giving her a speed of 26 knots. Her experimental high-pressure boilers proved problematic in service, making her a poor sea boat; as a result she was lengthened by four metres and converted to a torpedo recovery vessel at Wilhelmshaven in 1938–39.

Following the end of WWII, the F2 came into British hands and was moored at Lyness on Hoy near the Scapa Flow Visitor Centre. She sank at her moorings in a storm on 30 December 1946. The wreck was not considered worthy of salvage until 1967–68, when she was sold for scrap to Metrec Engineering. The company used a 500-tonne wooden barge, YC-21, in the salvage operation; that barge foundered in a storm on 15 November 1968 while holding salvaged components from the F2, including a 20mm anti-aircraft gun, and now lies approximately 100 metres southeast of the F2 wreck in 15–16 metres of water.

The F2 herself lies on her port side (forward section) with the stern upright, with the hull broken in half aft of the bridge where salvage operations damaged the structure. Forward identifiable features include the gun, mast, and starboard anchor cable. The site is consistently cited by local operators as one of the best wreck diving sites in the Flow and serves as an ideal acclimatization dive before the deeper German fleet wrecks.

## Wreck History

- **Built**: Germaniawerft, Kiel, 1935
- **Type**: Geleiteboot (escort boat), second of class of ten
- **Displacement**: 756 tonnes
- **Length**: Approximately 75 metres (lengthened by 4 metres in 1938–39)
- **Conversion**: Lengthened and converted to torpedo recovery vessel, Wilhelmshaven, 1938–39
- **Fate**: Sank at moorings in storm, 30 December 1946, Lyness, Hoy
- **YC-21 barge**: Wooden salvage barge, 500 tonnes; sank 15 November 1968 during salvage of F2 components; lies ~100 m southeast of F2

## Marine Life

Scapa Flow's cold, nutrient-rich water supports dense cold-water invertebrate communities on all wreck surfaces. The F2 carries plumose anemones, dead man's fingers, encrusting sponges, and various sea star species. Conger eels, crabs, and lobsters occupy sheltered spaces in the wreck structure. Schools of saithe and pollack are common in the water column above. The YC-21 barge provides additional habitat for similar species.

## Dive Profile

The 15–16 metre depth provides a comfortable Intermediate dive with extended no-decompression time. Begin on the F2, exploring the forward section and its identifiable features, before swimming the approximately 100 metres southeast to the YC-21. The YC-21 is upright and holds salvaged F2 components including a 20mm anti-aircraft gun. A shot line or reel is recommended for the transit between wrecks. Ascend on the shot line and conduct a safety stop.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive from Stromness-based operators. Most Scapa Flow liveaboards and day charter operations include the F2 as part of their rotation, particularly as a first or last dive of the day. Dry suit essential. Descend directly on a shot line.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Use the F2 as a practice dive to verify dry suit buoyancy management before tackling the deeper fleet wrecks
- Camera settings, strobe placement, and buoyancy should all be tested here before the German fleet dives
- The 20mm anti-aircraft gun on the YC-21 barge is a compelling photographic subject
- Ask your skipper whether the connecting line between wrecks is in place — some operators maintain a guide line for the transit
- The F2 is worthwhile in its own right for marine life, not merely as a warm-up dive

## Safety Considerations

Dry suit proficiency is essential in Scapa Flow waters. Visibility can be poor in certain seasons — brief on navigation and buddy protocols before descending. If swimming the transit between the two wrecks, maintain buddy contact and carry a compass bearing. Hypothermia risk is real in these waters without proper thermal protection.

## Photography

The F2's shallowness and rich invertebrate coverage make it an excellent photography site for testing setups before the main fleet dives. Plumose anemone clusters and dead man's fingers photograph well with artificial light. The 20mm anti-aircraft gun on the YC-21 is a distinctive subject. The shallow depth allows more natural light than the deeper battleships on summer dives.
