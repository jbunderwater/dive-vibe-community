## SMS Kronprinz Wilhelm

The most accessible of Scapa Flow's three German battleships, SMS Kronprinz Wilhelm lies inverted with her main 12-inch gun turrets exposed and explorable — offering a once-in-a-lifetime encounter with the main armament of a WWI dreadnought in just 38 metres of water.

## Overview

SMS Kronprinz Wilhelm was a König-class battleship displacing 25,388 tons, measuring 175 metres in length with armour plating over 12 inches thick around turrets and the fire-control tower. She participated in the Battle of Jutland as part of the vanguard fleet, suffering no damage or loss of life. In March 1917 she was rammed by her sister ship Grosser Kurfürst, flooding tonnes of water into the hull and requiring dry-dock repairs at Wilhelmshaven. She interned at Scapa Flow in November 1918 and quietly settled upright to the seabed at 11:15pm on 21 June 1919 before rolling inverted.

She lies upturned with her port side uppermost, rising to just 12 metres at the shallowest point and descending to 38 metres at the seabed. The deck rests in only 5 metres above the seabed at the stern, where D and E Turrets prop up the hull — creating an accessible underspace where divers can view the 12-inch gun barrels at close range. Her main armament is the defining feature: the Kronprinz Wilhelm has retained more accessible main-calibre gun positions than almost any other divable battleship wreck in the world.

## Marine Life

The shallow keel section at 12 metres receives good light penetration and supports a diverse community. Pollock and cod school above the hull. Ling and conger eels inhabit the deeper sections. Lobsters, edible crabs, king and queen scallops, squat lobsters, and sea urchins are abundant around the wreck. Starfish in multiple species dot the seabed. Dead man's fingers in cream, white, orange, and pink colours cover the hull surfaces throughout.

## Dive Profile

The dive starts at the hull, which rises to just 12 metres — an immediate sense of the battleship's scale. Working aft toward the stern, the deck rises approximately 5 metres from the seabed as the hull is propped up by D and E Turrets. With good buoyancy control, divers can move under the inverted deck to see the barrel of D Turret lying half-buried in silt, and E Turret's two barrels forced into the deck beyond. Swimming forward along the starboard side reveals five casemate guns lying on the seabed (some partially obscured by wreckage but all recognisable), plus two additional aft casemate guns overhead under the overhanging deck — easy to miss but obvious once pointed out. The spotting top — a crow's nest originally housing observers feeding range corrections to the gun crews — sits at the ship's highest inverted point. At the bow end, armour plates stripped by salvagers have swung down and now sit vertically alongside the hull, dramatically exposing the deck edge.

## Entry and Exit

Day charter boats and liveaboards depart from Stromness and St Margaret's Hope. The Kronprinz Wilhelm can be dived at any state of the tide. The shallower keel makes descent easier than on the Markgraf; the full wreck rewards multiple dives given its 175-metre length. Ascend on the shot line, deploying an SMB at 5–6 metres.

## Tips and Recommendations

- A drysuit is mandatory — water temperature 6–12°C year-round
- The stern area with D and E Turrets is the priority — plan to visit this first while bottom time is maximal
- Nitrox is recommended to extend bottom time at the deeper stern sections (35–38m)
- Allow multiple dives to see the full wreck; its 175-metre length makes one dive an incomplete experience
- Torch essential for viewing inside the turret underspace and for illuminating casemate gun detail
- The Kronprinz Wilhelm is less technical than the Markgraf but still benefits from deep dive experience
- Best months: June to August for clearest water and longest days

## Safety Considerations

Maximum depth of 38m brings narcosis risk on air — nitrox helps, but monitor your condition. Cold water (6–12°C) increases gas consumption noticeably compared to warm-water destinations; plan your gas reserves conservatively. The space beneath the inverted deck near the turrets involves partial overhead environment — only enter with appropriate buoyancy control and a torch. The wreck is a protected scheduled monument. Deploy an SMB on ascent.
