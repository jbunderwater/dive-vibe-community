## SMS Dresden

A Cöln-class light cruiser lying on her port side in 38 metres of water, the SMS Dresden is often used as the first dive of a Scapa Flow week — its shallower bow section allowing conservative shakedown profiles while the deeper stern rewards more experienced divers.

## Overview

SMS Dresden was a Cöln-class light cruiser, one of only two of this class to be completed — her sister ship SMS Cöln also rests on the seabed of Scapa Flow. Built by Howaldtswerke in Kiel and launched in April 1917, she displaced 5,620 tons and measured 115 metres in length. She was the replacement for the earlier SMS Dresden, which had been scuttled off the coast of Chile after the Battle of the Falklands in 1915. The second Dresden was commissioned just ten months before the Armistice and saw virtually no action. Following turbine failure, she was unable to steam to Scapa Flow with the main fleet in November 1918, arriving separately on 6 December 1918 and already leaking badly. She was scuttled on 21 June 1919.

She lies on her port side with the bow section at around 23 metres and the stern descending to 38 metres. Salvage operations were surprisingly limited compared to her sister ships; the Dresden has retained her shape well and many original features remain in place.

## Marine Life

Crabs and lobsters inhabit the nooks of the machinery sections. Pollock shoal above the wreck during summer. Ling and conger eels occupy sheltered sections of the lower hull. Dead man's fingers, plumose anemones, and encrusting sponges cover the hull surfaces throughout. A solitary bathtub lies in the sand near the officer's accommodation area — a poignant reminder that these were working warships with living quarters.

## Dive Profile

The inclined profile — bow at 23m, stern at 38m — makes multi-level diving natural and rewarding. Many dive operators favour starting at the stern (38m), descending on the shot line to the starboard side. Two 5.9-inch aft guns are pressed hard into the seabed and clearly visible. Swimming forward along the deck, the aft capstan comes into view, then the kedge anchor still in its original position at the stern. The small-calibre 3.4-inch AA guns appear amidships. A scattering of white fire bricks from the boiler room lining marks the boiler room section. The armoured control tower — a cylindrical structure protected by four inches of steel — is a centrepiece; viewing slits on the front allow a torch to illuminate the sealed interior. Three holes on the roof mark where optics were once mounted. Two forward 5.9-inch guns complete the bow section. The fore deck has peeled away and now lies almost flat on the seabed; nearby, a lone bathtub rests in the sand.

## Entry and Exit

Day charter boats and liveaboards depart from Stromness and St Margaret's Hope. The Dresden can be dived at most tidal states. Descent on the shot line. The graduated depth profile allows an easy transition from stern to shallower bow section as the dive progresses, naturally managing ascent time. Deploy an SMB at 5–6 metres on ascent.

## Tips and Recommendations

- A drysuit is mandatory — water temperature 6–12°C year-round
- The Dresden is frequently scheduled as the first dive of a trip, using the shallower bow as a gentle introduction to Scapa Flow conditions
- Torch essential for the armoured control tower viewing slits and for illuminating the gun breach mechanisms
- Two dives needed to see the whole wreck properly — stern/midships on one dive, midships/bow on the second
- Look for the bathtub near the officer's quarters — a well-known curiosity
- Best months: May to September

## Safety Considerations

The stern section at 38m puts this firmly in deep-dive territory. Narcosis risk on air; nitrox is commonly used. Cold water (6–12°C) increases gas consumption — monitor your cylinder pressure more frequently than at warmer destinations. The wreck is a protected scheduled monument. Deploy an SMB on ascent.
