# Dean's Blue Hole

The world's second-deepest known marine blue hole at 202 meters — a circular underwater sinkhole off Long Island that is the world record venue for freediving and a profound advanced scuba experience.

## Overview

Dean's Blue Hole is a spectacular natural geological formation off the western coast of Long Island, Bahamas. The sinkhole is approximately 35 meters in diameter at the surface, suddenly widening to roughly 100 meters diameter at a depth of around 20 meters, then plunging to a maximum depth of 202 meters. The hole is formed in limestone karst terrain and connects to the broader cave system of the Bahamas' complex underwater geology. It is world-famous as the venue for competitive freediving world records — William Trubridge has set multiple records here — and the hole's pure water, complete protection from current, and extraordinary visibility (easily 30–40+ meters in the sinkhole itself) make it one of the most visually dramatic dive experiences in the world. Recreational scuba divers are limited to the upper 40 meters, which still represents an extraordinary dive.

## Marine Life

The upper section of the blue hole (0–30 meters) supports typical Caribbean reef fish including parrotfish, blue tang, and sergeant majors that swim in from the adjacent reef. The transition zone from the entrance shelf to the main chamber at 20 meters is the most biologically productive section. Below 30 meters the sinkhole is essentially devoid of marine life — the extreme depth and lack of light penetration create a biologically poor but visually stunning environment.

## Dive Profile

Shore entry via the small beach adjacent to the hole. Descend through the narrow entrance zone to the wider chamber below 20 meters. Recreational divers typically hover at the chamber entrance at 20–25 meters to appreciate the scale before ascending. Advanced divers can explore the chamber walls to 30–35 meters — this is where the blue hole's distinctive appearance, with the pale-white limestone walls disappearing into infinite blue below, is most striking. Maximum recreational depth: 40 meters (appropriate equipment and certification required).

## Entry and Exit

Shore entry from the small beach at the blue hole's edge. Entry by walking in or shallow dive entry from the rock ledge. The hole has no current, making entry and exit straightforward. The calm, sheltered nature of the sinkhole makes it suitable for multiple dives per day.

## Tips and Recommendations

Freediving or snorkelling in the upper sections (0–20 m) is remarkable and requires no certification. Scuba diving to 30–40 meters requires appropriate experience and certification. The world freediving community regularly trains at Dean's Blue Hole — visitors should be aware that training sessions may be in progress and should not interfere with freedivers on the surface. Visiting at low sun angle (morning or evening) creates dramatic light shafts into the sinkhole from above.

## Safety Considerations

The extreme depth of this sinkhole creates narcosis risk for scuba divers who exceed 30–35 meters. Recreational divers must strictly observe their depth limits — the visually compelling experience of looking down into 202 meters can create a compelling urge to descend further. Always carry a freediving float/DSMB for surface visibility. The hole's calm nature is deceptive — proper training and planning are still required for deep sections.

## Photography

Dean's Blue Hole is one of the most photographed natural formations in the Bahamas. The classic image is looking upward from the chamber at 25–30 meters toward the circular opening above, with natural light streaming down and a freediver descending in silhouette. Wide-angle to fisheye captures the full dramatic scale. The transition from the entrance shelf to the wider chamber is best captured at the junction depth.
