## Nahoon Wreck

The Nahoon is one of Martinique's finest wreck dives — a three-masted vessel with an eventful 82-year history, deliberately sunk in 1993 off Les Anses d'Arlet and now blanketed in sponges and corals at 35 metres, with its steering wheel still mobile and its masts still recognisable on descent.

## Overview

The vessel that became the Nahoon was built in 1911 in Royan, France, as *Quinette de Richemond*, a buoy and beacon maintenance ship that spent its early career servicing navigation marks in the North Sea. It later operated around the Gironde estuary and in Madagascar before reaching the Caribbean. The ship was purchased in 1967 by Henry Wakelan, who repaired and renamed it *Nahoon*, sailing it as a training vessel around the Caribbean for nearly 20 years. By 1984, the ship was abandoned in Fort-de-France Bay.

In 1993, several local dive centres and the Regional Tourism Committee arranged for the Nahoon to be cleaned of environmental contaminants and towed in a ceremonial procession towards Cap Salomon, where it was deliberately sunk on a white sandy bottom off Anse Dufour at 35 metres depth to serve as an artificial reef.

**Historical note**: Some sources have described the Nahoon as a "schooner," but multiple operator records confirm it was a former buoy-tender converted to a training/charter vessel — not originally a schooner. The three masts remained visible on the wreck after sinking. One source cites a sinking date of 1992; the majority of sources and the Alpha Plongée operator page confirm 1993.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Off Anse Dufour / Cap Salomon, Les Anses d'Arlet, southwest Martinique
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 35 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–25 meters
- **Current**: Light to moderate
- **Best Time**: November to May

## Marine Life

The wreck is colonised with yellow, orange, red, and purple sponges and coral species across the hull. Barracuda and silvery jacks school between the masts above the wreck. A large green moray eel regularly patrols the hull. Snappers, spiders, and lobsters are documented residents. Stingrays are seen on the sandy bottom around the keel. Spanish mackerel (thazards) and stonefish have been confirmed at the site. The main deck, forward cabin, and dock are all accessible. The steering wheel remains preserved and reportedly still mobile.

## Dive Profile

- **Depth range**: 35 metres on the sandy bottom; superstructure visible from ~20 metres on descent
- **Entry**: Boat dive from Les Anses d'Arlet operators
- **Typical dive**: Descend to the main deck; visit the engine room and holds; examine the steering wheel; explore masts from above; ascend via the hull structure
- **Certification**: CMAS 2 / Advanced Open Water minimum

## Safety

At 35 metres, this is a deep recreational dive with significant narcosis risk for some divers. Bottom time is limited; agree on turnaround depths before descending. The masts and rigging have been damaged over the years by storms, fishing lines, and diver impact — one source notes only one mast remained visible in recent years. Carry a torch for interior spaces.
