## Martinique

Diamond Rock's cathedral-sized tunnel dive, twelve wrecks from the catastrophic 1902 Mount Pelee eruption at St. Pierre, and French Caribbean coral gardens along the leeward coast.

## Description

Martinique offers diverse dive sites ranging from 2 to 60 meters depth, spanning the volcanic coast from St. Pierre in the north to Diamond Rock in the south. The 1902 eruption of Mount Pelee destroyed the town of St. Pierre and sank over seven vessels in the harbor — including the Roraima, considered one of the Caribbean's premier wreck dives. At the southern tip, Diamond Rock (Rocher du Diamant) rises 175 meters from the sea with a triangular tunnel large enough to swim through, connecting both sides of the volcanic islet. Between these landmarks, the leeward coast offers accessible reef diving and shore entries.

### Diving Opportunities

- **Boat Diving**: Boat-accessible sites including Diamond Rock, St. Pierre wrecks, and offshore reef systems
- **Shore Diving**: Shore-accessible sites including Anse Dufour and Anse Noire — known for turtle encounters in shallow water
- **Wreck Diving**: Documented wreck sites concentrated at St. Pierre, including remnants from steamers, schooners, and barges sunk during the 1902 eruption. The Roraima (a Canadian steamship) is the signature wreck dive.
- **Wall Diving**: Wall sites along the volcanic coast with dramatic drop-offs
- **Cave Diving**: Cave site with volcanic formations
- **Reef Diving**: Reef sites along the calm Caribbean leeward coast with healthy hard and soft coral formations

### Accessibility

- **Getting There**: Aime Cesaire International Airport (FDF) at Le Lamentin receives direct flights from Paris (8.5 hours), Montreal, Miami, and inter-Caribbean connections from Guadeloupe, St. Lucia, and Dominica. Express des Iles ferry connects to Guadeloupe, Dominica, and St. Lucia.
- **Dive Operators**: Multiple operators including Abaca Dive (catamaran and speedboat departures), Canopee Bleue (electro-solar boat, beginner-friendly), Apapadlo (St. Pierre specialist), Immersion Caraibes, and Alpha Plongee (Diamond Rock specialist). Most operators run two departures daily.
- **Accommodation**: St. Pierre has small hotels convenient for wreck diving. Les Anses d'Arlet and Sainte-Luce on the south coast offer beachfront hotels near reef sites. Fort-de-France provides urban hotels with easy access to all regions.
- **Transportation**: Rental cars recommended for accessing dive sites across the island. Taxis available from the airport. Some operators offer hotel pickup. Roads are well-maintained.
- **Facilities**: Full-service dive centers with equipment rental, PADI and French federation (FFESSM) certification. Nitrox available at several operators. Diamond Rock trips require intermediate to advanced certification due to currents and depth.

### Marine Life & Environment

- **Water Conditions**: Water temperature 26-29°C year-round, visibility up to 30 meters during December-May dry season, currents can be strong at Diamond Rock and exposed sites
- **Marine Biodiversity**: Sea turtles (hawksbill and green, regularly seen at Anse Dufour), barracudas, moray eels, lobsters, seahorses, angelfish, parrotfish, and colorful sponge gardens on the volcanic substrate. The St. Pierre wrecks host encrusting corals and resident fish communities after over 120 years of colonization.
- **Conservation**: Martinique's marine areas are under French environmental protection. Diamond Rock is a classified historic monument (HMS Diamond Rock, 1804). St. Pierre wreck sites are protected as underwater heritage.

## Additional Information

- **Best Time to Visit**: December to May (dry season) for the best visibility and calmest seas. Diving is available year-round. Hurricane season runs June to November, though Martinique is south of the main hurricane belt.
- **Currency**: Euro (EUR)
- **Language**: French (official), Creole widely spoken
- **Safety**: Recompression chamber available at Fort-de-France hospital. Diamond Rock requires intermediate to advanced certification due to depth (up to 60m) and currents. St. Pierre wreck penetration requires proper training. Shore entries at Anse Dufour and Anse Noire are beginner-friendly.
