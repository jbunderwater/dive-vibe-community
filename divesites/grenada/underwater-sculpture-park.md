# Underwater Sculpture Park

Grenada's Underwater Sculpture Park is one of the most celebrated dive destinations in the Caribbean — an internationally recognised public art installation by British sculptor Jason deCaires Taylor, where 75 original sculptures (plus 27 additional works added in 2023) rest on the seafloor of Molinere Bay, slowly being colonised by coral and becoming part of the living reef.

## Overview

Created in 2006, the sculpture park has become Grenada's most iconic dive attraction, drawing visitors from around the world. The original 75 sculptures cover an area of 800 square metres in 3–8 metres of water in the Molinere Beauséjour Marine Protected Area, north of St. George's. In 2023, Jason deCaires Taylor added 27 new artworks including the Coral Carnival series, expanding the park to over 100 works. Taylor's installation includes groups of human figures — most famously "Vicissitudes," a circle of 26 children holding hands and facing outward into the current — as well as individual figures including "The Lost Correspondent," a man at a typewriter desk surrounded by newspaper articles from 1970s Grenada. The sculptures are made of pH-neutral cement and stainless steel designed to allow coral polyps to attach, and the textured surfaces now carry hard and soft coral, sponges, and encrusting organisms. The site is accessible by scuba diving, snorkelling, and glass-bottom boat. Visibility is typically 15–25 metres. The shallow depth makes the site accessible to all levels, including snorkellers and glass-bottom boat visitors.

## Marine Life

The sculptures have become genuine reef habitat over nearly two decades. Hard and soft corals, sponges, and encrusting organisms have colonised every surface, and the reef fish community now treats the sculptures as natural structure. Parrotfish, damselfish, sergeant majors, and wrasse are abundant throughout. ScubaBoard trip reports note seahorses and frogfish among the sculptures. The "Vicissitudes" circle of 26 figures, now encrusted with coral growth, is the park's most recognisable work. Note: specific fish species attributed to this site across various sources are typical Molinere Bay reef fish rather than sculpture-specific populations.

## Dive Profile

The entire park can be explored at 3–8 meters. Descend to the sandy bottom and navigate between sculpture groups — your dive guide or a pre-dive briefing will orient you to the layout. The "Vicissitudes" circle of ring-linked figures and the various individual standing and seated figures are the primary stops. Snorkelers can see much of the park from the surface. The shallow depth allows unlimited bottom time within recreational limits.

## Entry and Exit

The site is accessible by scuba diving, snorkelling, and glass-bottom boat from Grand Anse or True Blue operators — the Sculpture Park is a standard offering on most Grenada dive itineraries. Mooring buoys are available to prevent anchor damage. Giant stride entry for scuba divers.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Visit in the early morning before the snorkel tour boats arrive — the park is more peaceful and less crowded
- The "Vicissitudes" circle is most photogenic approached from outside the ring looking inward, with light filtering through the gap between figures
- The sculptures look entirely different as coral growth changes them over time — revisiting years apart shows remarkable transformation
- Bring the widest-angle lens available — the sculptures demand wide-angle treatment to capture multiple figures in context
- Combine with Flamingo Bay or Happy Hill for a two-tank morning showcasing Grenada's reef variety

## Safety Considerations

Do not touch the sculptures — coral and organisms on the surfaces are fragile. Maintain neutral buoyancy throughout to avoid contact with the seafloor and disturbing visibility. The shallow depth creates no decompression risk, but good buoyancy control protects the site from damage. Snorkel boats from tour operators can be numerous — be aware of surface vessel activity.

## Photography

The Underwater Sculpture Park is one of the Caribbean's premier underwater photography destinations. The human figures draped in coral create images that combine art, nature, and time in a uniquely evocative way. Morning light angled through clear water at 45 degrees creates dramatic lighting through the "Vicissitudes" circle. Macro photography on the sculpture surfaces reveals extraordinary encrustation detail invisible to the naked eye. A fish-eye or ultra-wide-angle lens captures multiple figures in a single frame for the strongest compositional impact.
