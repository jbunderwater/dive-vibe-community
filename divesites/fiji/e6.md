# E6

Named after Ektachrome E6 slide film by the photographers who first shot it — a seamount in Bligh Water so vivid it consumed roll after roll.

## Overview

E6 is a seamount in the Vatu-i-ra channel of Bligh Water, positioned roughly in the center of the narrowest passage between Viti Levu and Vanua Levu. The site was named by early underwater photographers who found that the colors here consumed unusual amounts of Ektachrome E6 slide film — the colors were that saturated. The seamount rises sheer-sided from approximately 900 meters of water to within about a meter of the surface, intercepting the nutrient flow funneled between Fiji's two largest islands. The two current-exposed sides of the pinnacle attract schooling pelagics and hunting predators; the sheltered lee side, where dive boats typically moor, supports delicate soft corals and sea fans that benefit from reduced flow. E6 is a signature site on the NAI'A liveaboard circuit and is regularly included on Bligh Water itineraries.

**Note:** E6 is located in Bligh Water (Vatu-i-ra channel), not in Beqa Lagoon. These are distinct diving areas on opposite sides of Viti Levu.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Vatu-i-ra channel, Bligh Water, Fiji (between Viti Levu and Vanua Levu)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Pinnacle/seamount
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 28 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–35 meters
- **Current**: Strong on current-facing sides; sheltered on lee side
- **Best Time**: May to October

## Marine Life

The current-swept sides of the pinnacle concentrate pelagic life. Schooling barracuda, bigeye trevally, and surgeonfish are reliably present. Occasional hammerhead sharks patrol the deeper water around the seamount base; eagle rays pass through regularly. Reef fish diversity is high throughout the structure: anthias, fusiliers, and leaf scorpionfish populate the lee side soft coral zone. The soft corals and sea fans on the sheltered face provide the colorful backdrop that originally inspired the film-name. NAI'A's logs note that the boat typically moors on the lee side where divers find the most protected, color-rich terrain.

## Dive Profile

Boats moor on the sheltered (lee) side of the pinnacle. Descend along the mooring line and begin exploring the soft coral-encrusted lee face before rounding the pinnacle to the current-exposed sides where pelagic action concentrates. The summit is close enough to the surface to allow ascents with excellent gas remaining. Current on the upstream faces can be strong; use the pinnacle structure as a windbreak and position yourself in the current shadow to observe without fighting the flow. Ascend along the pinnacle structure to complete a safety stop near the summit.

## Entry and Exit

Access is exclusively by liveaboard — E6 sits in open water in the Vatu-i-ra channel and is not reachable as a day trip from any resort. The NAI'A and other Fiji liveaboards stop here on Bligh Water circuits. Entry by giant stride or back roll. The boat holds position on a mooring buoy in the sheltered zone. Strong current on the exposed sides means divers should not drift past the pinnacle without a clear plan to return or signal for the tender.

## Tips and Recommendations

- **Start on the lee side**: The sheltered face of the pinnacle has the best soft coral color and the calmest conditions. Use it to orient to the site before rounding to the current side.
- **Watch the current flanks for pelagics**: Barracuda and trevally schools concentrate on the sides most exposed to current flow. The hammerheads, when present, appear in the deep blue water below these zones.
- **Liveaboard only**: E6 is not a day-trip site. Plan a Fiji liveaboard itinerary that specifically routes through Bligh Water (Vatu-i-ra) to access this site.
- **Photography**: The lee face is a close-focus wide-angle paradise; the current-exposed sides reward patience with wide-angle pelagic shots when the schools are present.

## Safety Considerations

Current in the Vatu-i-ra channel can be extremely strong. Always remain within reach of the pinnacle structure or the mooring line. If swept off the pinnacle, deploy an SMB immediately and signal for the tender rather than attempting to fight back against the current. The seamount's proximity to the surface means overtopping the summit in surge is possible — be aware of your vertical position at shallow depth.
