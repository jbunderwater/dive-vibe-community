## Stetson Bank

Stetson Bank is a pinnacle dive site in Flower Garden Banks, North America.

## Overview

Stetson Bank is the westernmost unit of the Flower Garden Banks National Marine Sanctuary, located approximately 80 miles (129 km) from the Texas coast, and it stands apart from its two coral-reef siblings in fundamental geology. Instead of limestone, its substrate is uplifted siltstone and claystone — softer rock that erodes into a moonscape of parallel ridges and pinnacles colonized by algae, sponges, and fire coral rather than reef-building corals. Unique creatures like sailfin blennies, golden-phase smooth trunkfish, and longlure frogfish make Stetson a standout fish-watching destination. The site covers just 1.4 square miles (3.6 sq km), making it the smallest of the three original sanctuary dive units.

- **Location**: Flower Garden Banks, North America
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive

- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 17 meters (56 feet) at the shallowest reef crest; full depth range 17–61 meters

## Marine Life

NOAA's Stetson Bank page and DAN Alert Diver document a fish community distinct from the coral-dominated East and West Banks. Because the substrate cannot support large reef-building corals, species diversity is different: NOAA notes the site "hosts a denser tropical fish community with more juveniles" than the main banks. Signature species include sailfin blennies, golden-phase smooth trunkfish (a color variant found at few other locations globally), longlure frogfish, spotted scorpionfish, squirrelfish, and morays. Large schools of crimson creolefish hover over the Sierra Madracis formation — a notable concentration of the ten-ray star coral (*Madracis decactis*) at Buoy 3, the largest coral formation on Stetson. Bermuda chub school in the water column above the ridge. Spotted eagle rays and silky sharks are documented visitors; hawksbill sea turtles have been observed. Invertebrate life is rich: spiny lobsters, arrow crabs, moray eels, octopuses, and encrusting sponges and fire coral. Nine coral species have been documented at Stetson — fewer than the East/West Banks, but the site qualifies as a coral community.

## Dive Profile

Stetson Bank covers 1.4 square miles (3.6 sq km; approximately 36 acres in its core), with a crest of eroded siltstone and claystone forming a 1,500-foot-long ridge of pinnacles separated by deep troughs. The reef crest reaches to approximately 56 feet (17 m) below the surface. Five mooring buoys service the site, with depths ranging 73–85 feet (22–26 m) at the buoy anchor points. The softer siltstone substrate hosts algae, sponge encrustation, and fire coral rather than the massive brain and star corals of East/West Banks. Gullies, ridges, and sand flats create complex topography excellent for patient fish-watching. Because Stetson is more exposed to freshwater inputs from the Gulf, NOAA notes that dives commonly begin through a murky green thermocline 15–20 feet deep before giving way to clear water below. Summer visibility exceeds 100 feet; winter visibility can drop to 40–60 feet.

## Entry and Exit

Stetson Bank lies approximately 80 miles (129 km) from the Texas coast — closer to shore than East/West Banks — making occasional day-boat visits more feasible. Access is primarily via liveaboard from Freeport, Texas (M/V *Fling*, Texas Caribbean Charters), though some shorter day-charter trips include Stetson. Five mooring buoys accommodate vessels; anchoring is prohibited throughout the sanctuary.

## Tips and Recommendations

Stetson rewards patient, macro-focused diving. Move slowly over the pinnacles to spot camouflaged frogfish and scorpionfish, and check every crevice for sailfin blennies and arrow crabs. The Sierra Madracis pinnacle is a highlight — position yourself in the water column above it to be surrounded by the creolefish school. The north wall "blue cruiseway" is worth a pass for shark encounters. Stetson is excellent for photographers seeking unusual and photogenic small species rarely seen on conventional coral reefs.

## Safety Considerations

Open Gulf conditions — 70+ miles offshore — require the same self-sufficiency as East and West Banks. Currents are variable; the green thermocline layer on descent can disorient divers unused to it. Carry a surface marker buoy. Stony coral tissue loss disease disinfection protocols are mandatory; follow charter guidelines. Winter visibility can drop significantly, which should be factored into dive planning.

## Photography

Stetson Bank is a macro photographer's paradise in the Gulf of Mexico. Longlure frogfish, sailfin blennies displaying on the siltstone, and golden-phase trunkfish are portfolio subjects rarely available elsewhere in U.S. waters. The sponge-encrusted pinnacles also provide wide-angle opportunities, particularly when the creolefish school is dense above the Sierra Madracis formation. A 60mm or 100mm macro lens rewards close work on the ridge surfaces; wide-angle captures the pinnacle architecture in good summer visibility.
