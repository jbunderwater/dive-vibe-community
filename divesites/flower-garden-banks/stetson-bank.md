## Stetson Bank

Stetson Bank is a pinnacle/ridge dive site in Flower Garden Banks, North America.

## Overview

Stetson Bank is the westernmost unit of the Flower Garden Banks National Marine Sanctuary, located about 80 miles off the Texas coastline. It stands apart from East and West Banks — instead of limestone, its crest of eroded siltstone and claystone creates a moonscape of parallel rocky ridges and open gravelly flats encrusted with sponges, fire coral, and algae. The bank rises to approximately 56 feet (17 m) at its shallowest point, with the reef crest extending down to 130 feet within recreational dive limits. Unique creatures like sailfin blennies, golden-phase smooth trunkfish, longlure frogfish, and spotted scorpionfish make Stetson a standout fish-watching destination. The bank was first identified as having corals by Woods Hole oceanographer Henry C. Stetson in 1953.

## Marine Life

NOAA's Stetson Bank page and DAN Alert Diver document a fish community distinct from the coral-dominated East and West Banks. NOAA confirms: sailfin blennies, frogfish, spotted scorpionfish, southern stingrays, sea urchins, spotted moray eels, sponges, octopus, fire coral, hawksbill turtles, creolefish, and spiny lobsters. DAN Alert Diver additionally documents scrawled cowfish, golden-phase smooth trunkfish, rock hind, and redlip blennies. Large schools of crimson creolefish hover over the Sierra Madracis pinnacle — a distinctive peak of ten-ray star coral (*Madracis decactis*). Silky sharks are documented patrolling the north side of the bank. Spotted eagle rays are sighted regularly. Nine coral species have been documented at Stetson, fewer than at East and West Banks, and the reef crest is dominated by algae, sponges, and fire coral rather than the boulder brain and star corals found on the main banks. Sandbar sharks have been reported by visiting divers in addition to silky sharks.

## Dive Profile

Stetson Bank is the smallest and westernmost of the three main sanctuary dive units — 1.4 square miles (3.6 sq km) total, with a crest of eroded siltstone and claystone forming a 1,500-foot-long ridge of parallel pinnacles separated by deep troughs and open gravelly sand flats. The bank rises to approximately 56 feet (17 m) at its shallowest point; typical dive depth is around 70–90 feet. Five mooring buoys service the site. Unlike the limestone of East and West Banks, Stetson's substrate hosts mostly algae and sponge encrustation with nine documented coral species. Gullies, ridges, and the moonscape topography create excellent conditions for fish-watching. Summer visibility exceeds 100 feet; winter visibility drops to 40–60 feet as cooler water moves through. Dives typically begin through a murky green-water layer about 15–20 feet deep that gives way to clearer water below — a characteristic noted by NOAA for this site.

## Entry and Exit

Stetson Bank lies approximately 30 miles northwest of West Flower Garden Bank, about 80 miles from the Texas coastline — closer to shore than East and West Banks (which are 113–119 miles out). Access is by charter vessel from Freeport, Texas; Texas Caribbean Charters operates the M/V Fling and offers dedicated Stetson Bank and Kraken wreck trips in addition to the standard Flower Gardens runs. The closer-to-shore location makes Stetson more accessible for day-charter visits than the main banks. Five mooring buoys accommodate vessels up to 100 feet. Anchoring is prohibited throughout the sanctuary.

## Tips and Recommendations

Stetson rewards patient, macro-focused diving. Move slowly over the pinnacles to spot camouflaged frogfish and scorpionfish, and check every crevice for sailfin blennies and arrow crabs. The Sierra Madracis pinnacle is a highlight — position yourself in the water column above it to be surrounded by the creolefish school. The north wall "blue cruiseway" is worth a pass for shark encounters. Stetson is excellent for photographers seeking unusual and photogenic small species rarely seen on conventional coral reefs.

## Safety Considerations

Open Gulf conditions — 70+ miles offshore — require the same self-sufficiency as East and West Banks. Currents are variable; the green thermocline layer on descent can disorient divers unused to it. Carry a surface marker buoy. Stony coral tissue loss disease disinfection protocols are mandatory; follow charter guidelines. Winter visibility can drop significantly, which should be factored into dive planning.

## Photography

Stetson Bank is a macro photographer's paradise in the Gulf of Mexico. Longlure frogfish, sailfin blennies displaying on the siltstone, and golden-phase trunkfish are portfolio subjects rarely available elsewhere in U.S. waters. The sponge-encrusted pinnacles also provide wide-angle opportunities, particularly when the creolefish school is dense above the Sierra Madracis formation. A 60mm or 100mm macro lens rewards close work on the ridge surfaces; wide-angle captures the pinnacle architecture in good summer visibility.
