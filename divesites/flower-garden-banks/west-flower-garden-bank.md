## West Flower Garden Bank

West Flower Garden Bank is the westernmost of the two original coral reef units in Flower Garden Banks National Marine Sanctuary, approximately 113 miles south of the Texas/Louisiana border in the Gulf of Mexico.

## Overview

West Flower Garden Bank rises from the Gulf floor on a salt dome, with a coral-dominated reef cap over 52% covered in living coral — as much as five times the coverage of comparable Caribbean reefs. The reef top reaches approximately 55–60 feet (17–18 m), with five mooring buoys set at 68–80 feet (21–24 m). Massive boulder-shaped brain and star corals dominate, rising 15–20 feet above adjacent sand channels. Texas Caribbean Charters typically visits West Bank as the first dive of a liveaboard trip and describes it as "famous for cruising manta rays."

## Marine Life

NOAA and DAN Alert Diver document species confirmed at this site. Fish: great barracuda, horse-eye jacks, blue and brown chromis, bluehead wrasses, queen and French angelfish, damselfish, smooth trunkfish, balloonfish, parrotfish, squirrelfish, and moray eels. Larger animals: oceanic manta rays (*Mobula birostris*) are present year-round across both Flower Garden Banks; over 100 individuals identified by photo-ID. Research published in 2018 confirmed the area surrounding both banks as juvenile manta ray nursery habitat. Spotted eagle rays are reported in winter months. Whale sharks visit from July through September. Scalloped hammerhead sharks are present from January through early April. Loggerhead sea turtles and nurse sharks are regularly sighted. Dominant corals include three threatened *Orbicella* species: mountainous star coral (*O. faveolata*), lobed star coral (*O. annularis*), and ridge-forming star coral (*O. franksi*). Threatened coral species account for approximately 66% of total coral cover at West Bank.

## Dive Profile

Five mooring buoys service West Bank at depths of 68–80 feet (21–24 m). The reef cap runs to mesophotic depths beyond recreational range. Typical recreational dives cover 65–90 feet over the coral boulder landscape. Visibility can exceed 30 metres in summer; winter visibility is reduced. Water temperature ranges from the mid-70s°F in winter to the mid-80s°F in summer. The annual mass coral spawning event occurs 7–10 days after the August full moon, when seven species of brain and star coral simultaneously release gametes in ascending clouds of light-pink bundles.

## Entry and Exit

West Flower Garden Bank is approximately 113 miles from the Texas coast and cannot be reached by day boat. The primary operator is Texas Caribbean Charters aboard the M/V *Fling*, a 100-foot liveaboard departing from Freeport, Texas. Two- and three-day trips run from approximately February through October. West Bank is typically the first site visited on the overnight journey. Reserve well in advance; August coral spawning trips book out months ahead.

## Tips and Recommendations

The August full-moon spawning (7–10 days after, beginning around 8:30–9 p.m.) is the signature event at West Bank. Night dives during spawning reveal the peak of the gamete cloud rising from the reef. Manta rays are most reliably encountered on slow drift passes along the reef edge — do not approach aggressively. January through early April is best for hammerhead schools; July through September for whale sharks. NOAA requires gear disinfection against stony coral tissue loss disease — follow charter protocols exactly.

## Safety Considerations

West Flower Garden Bank shares the same open-ocean risk profile as East Bank — approximately 113 miles offshore with variable currents, rapidly changing Gulf weather, and multi-hour emergency evacuation times. Use the mooring line on descent and ascent when current is running. Carry a surface marker buoy at all times. Not suitable for novice divers. Weather windows during late-summer hurricane season can close with little warning.

## Photography

The boulder coral landscape rewards wide-angle shooting at natural light depths. Texas Caribbean Charters advises divers to "LOOK UP" for manta rays — approaching the surface and shooting upward into a manta overhead is a signature image. During spawning, position just above the reef and shoot into the ascending gamete cloud. Eagle rays and hammerhead schools (when present) provide silhouette opportunities in the blue water column.
