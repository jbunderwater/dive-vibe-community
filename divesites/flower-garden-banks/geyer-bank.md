## Geyer Bank

Geyer Bank is named after Richard A. Geyer, a Texas A&M University geophysicist who headed the university's Department of Oceanography from 1966 to 1980. NOAA describes him as the "grandfather of oceanography" at Texas A&M. A pear-shaped feature with two distinct domes sitting atop an active salt diapir, Geyer covers 11.5 square miles (29.8 sq km) approximately 125 miles (201 km) from land. The reef crest features at least four species of reef-building coral plus the invasive orange cup coral (*Tubastraea coccinea*). Geyer is thought to be the first known spawning aggregation site for the rare marbled grouper (*Dermatolepis inermis*) and is documented for its enormous reef butterflyfish aggregations.

## Overview

Geyer Bank is one of seven sanctuary banks with reef habitat within the 130-foot (40-meter) recreational diving limit, added to the sanctuary in 2021. NOAA confirms the shallowest peak at 105 feet (32 m), with the bank descending to 722 feet (220 m) at depth — the sanctuary's overall deepest point is also at Geyer. The reef crest provides varied diving terrain with coral heads, sand patches, and fish fauna. The site is designated both a Habitat Area of Particular Concern (HAPC) and Essential Fish Habitat. The marbled grouper spawning aggregation finding has major conservation implications, as this rare species is under fishing pressure.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Gulf of Mexico, southern Flower Garden Banks complex (27.72°N, 93.47°W)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 32.0 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15-30 meters (50-100 feet)
- **Water Temperature**: approximately 27-29°C (80-84°F) summer; approximately 15-20°C (59-68°F) winter
- **Current**: Moderate to strong; variable conditions
- **Best Time**: May-October (summer season); February-April (winter season)

## Marine Life

NOAA documents "enormous numbers of reef butterflyfish" concentrating at Geyer Bank at certain times annually — DAN describes them as so dense "you'd think you are on a Pacific reef." The reef community includes at least four species of reef-building coral plus the invasive orange cup coral (*Tubastraea coccinea*). Deeper mesophotic zones host black corals, octocorals, sponges, algae, and invertebrates. No detailed species census comparable to East/West Banks has been published specifically for Geyer Bank. The marbled grouper spawning aggregation is the site's most scientifically documented feature.

## Dive Profile

Descend via moored buoy to the reef crest at approximately 32 meters (105 feet) — NOAA confirms the shallowest peak is at 105 feet. NOAA mooring buoys at Geyer are at depths of 106–256 feet (32–78 m), so buoy anchor depth varies significantly by buoy position. Explore the reef at various depths between 25–32 meters, observing coral formations and watching for the butterflyfish concentrations. Budget adequate air and time for safety stops. This is the shallowest deep reef site among the non-primary sanctuary banks.

## Entry and Exit

Access is exclusively by dive boat with moored buoys. Giant stride entry with full gear is standard. Descend carefully along the moored line to the reef structure. Plan for 15-20 minute extended safety stops at 5 meters, especially given the 40-meter starting depth. Deploy SMB and coordinate with boat crew for ascent timing and pickup.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Book with Flower Garden Banks operators experienced in deep reef diving
- Watch for marbled grouper during spawning season (specific times) if research permits access
- Bring adequate thermal protection; Gulf water can be 15-20°C (59-68°F) in winter at depth
- Bring a camera for butterflyfish aggregation photography
- Plan dives conservatively with substantial air reserves
- Stay close to the moored line for safe ascent
- Monitor bottom time and depth carefully

## Safety Considerations

Geyer Bank presents substantial technical challenges:
- Deep starting depth (40 meters) at recreational maximum
- Extended decompression obligations
- Offshore location with limited emergency response
- Strong currents possible; condition awareness critical
- Cooler water in winter (15-20°C); appropriate thermal protection required year-round

Essential requirements:
- Advanced Open Water or Deep Diver certification minimum
- Recent deep-water diving experience
- Expert buoyancy control and air management
- Extended safety stop discipline (15-20 minutes)
- Strong buddy communication and teamwork
- SMB deployment and signaling skills
- Physical fitness for deep diving in current

Not suitable for recreational divers without substantial deep-water training.

## Photography

Excellent opportunities for wide-angle composition of coral formations and butterflyfish schools. Bring macro lighting for invertebrate subjects. Summer provides better visibility; early morning dives offer optimal lighting. The large butterflyfish aggregations are exceptional for action photography.
