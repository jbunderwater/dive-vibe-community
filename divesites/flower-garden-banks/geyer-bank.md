## Geyer Bank

Geyer Bank is a pear-shaped deep reef in the Flower Garden Banks National Marine Sanctuary, notable for massive seasonal butterflyfish aggregations and what NOAA describes as the first known spawning aggregation of the rare marbled grouper.

## Overview

Geyer Bank lies approximately 125 miles (201 km) from shore on the upper continental slope, positioned atop an active salt diapir. Added to the sanctuary in the 2021 expansion, it covers 11.5 square miles (29.8 sq km) with two distinct domes; the shallowest peak rises to 105 feet (32 meters) — within the recreational dive limit of 130 feet. Named after Richard A. Geyer (1915–2002), a Texas A&M University geophysicist described as the "grandfather of oceanography" in the Gulf, the bank features a coral community of at least four reef-building species plus the invasive orange cup coral (*Tubastraea coccinea*; 46 colonies were removed in 2006). The site is designated a Habitat Area of Particular Concern (HAPC) and is particularly significant for marine research: NOAA identifies Geyer Bank as the first known site for marbled grouper (*Dermatolepis inermis*) spawning aggregations — a rare finding with major conservation implications.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Gulf of Mexico, 125 miles offshore (27.72°N, 93.47°W)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 32.0 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–30 meters (50–100 feet)
- **Water Temperature**: 20–21°C (68–70°F) in winter; 29–30°C (84–86°F) in summer
- **Current**: Moderate to strong; variable offshore conditions
- **Best Time**: May–October

## Marine Life

NOAA documents reef butterflyfish (*Chaetodon sedentarius*) aggregating at Geyer Bank in "enormous numbers" seasonally. Rare marbled grouper (*Dermatolepis inermis*) are documented with spawning aggregations at this site. Black corals and bushy orange gorgonians provide habitat for brittle stars. The broader community includes angelfish, squirrelfish, and sponge invertebrates. Large sargassum blooms have been documented on the reef crest. The invasive orange cup coral (*Tubastraea coccinea*) is present. Deeper mesophotic zones host octocorals, black corals, and additional fish species.

*No comprehensive site-specific species census beyond the above NOAA-documented species was found in the sources reviewed.*

## Dive Profile

The shallowest dome peaks at 32 meters (105 feet); most exploration occurs in the 25–32 meter range. The reef features coral heads interspersed with sand patches. Given the deep starting depth, bottom time is limited for no-decompression recreational profiles — plan conservatively with extended safety stops. Mooring buoys are available at Geyer Bank per NOAA, eliminating the need to anchor.

## Entry and Exit

Access is exclusively by charter vessel. Texas Caribbean Charters (texascaribbean.com) is the primary operator serving the sanctuary from Freeport, Texas. At 125 miles from shore, Geyer is one of the farther offshore sites in the expanded sanctuary. Giant stride entry with full gear is standard. Deploy an SMB for ascent; open Gulf conditions at this distance require self-sufficiency.

## Tips and Recommendations

Time visits to observe the butterflyfish aggregations, which NOAA describes as occurring seasonally in enormous numbers. Exercise caution around marbled grouper if present — this is a conservation-significant species and the only known spawning site of its type. Plan dives conservatively given the 32-meter starting depth and extended distance from shore.

## Safety Considerations

At 32 meters, Geyer Bank pushes the practical limits of no-decompression recreational diving. Air management and careful depth monitoring are essential. The 125-mile offshore position means emergency response is a multi-hour operation. Strong currents are possible. Advanced Open Water or equivalent minimum; recent deep-water diving experience strongly recommended. Comply with all HAPC regulations.

## Photography

Seasonal butterflyfish aggregations offer exceptional wide-angle schooling shots. Orange gorgonians with brittle stars are striking macro subjects. The deeper starting depth reduces available light — supplemental lighting benefits macro work at the reef crest.
