## Épave de Caret

Épave de Caret is a sunken sand-dredging vessel west of Îlet Caret in the Grand Cul-de-Sac Marin, deliberately sunk around 1998–2000 as a fish aggregation device and artificial reef.

## Overview

The wreck is a former sand-dredging vessel that was intentionally sunk within the Grand Cul-de-Sac Marin, a 15,000-hectare protected marine reserve. It was sunk approximately 25–28 years ago (reported timelines suggest approximately 1998–2000) to create substrate for marine growth and to serve as a fish concentration device. The wreck is identified on excursion routes from Sainte-Rose to Îlet Caret.

The hull is mostly shallow — the shallowest sections are at 2–3 metres and visible from the surface in calm conditions. The deepest recorded dive point is approximately 18 metres. GwadaPlans describes it as a "ship's hold grounded in the shallows that serves as a nursery for fish."

Penetration of the interior is not permitted — the hull is structurally unstable and fire corals are present on interior surfaces.

## Site Information

- **Location**: West of Îlet Caret, Grand Cul-de-Sac Marin, northern Guadeloupe
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive (accessible on Sainte-Rose excursions)
- **Site Type**: Wreck
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 18 metres (shallowest sections 2–3 m)

## Marine Life

GwadaPlans and Nomad Diver describe the wreck as a fish nursery habitat. The surrounding Grand Cul-de-Sac Marin reef system supports diverse reef life, but no specific species have been named in published sources at this particular wreck. Regional marine life applies; no site-specific species reports found.

## Safety

No penetration — hull is unstable and fire corals are present inside. The wreck is shallow and visible from the surface; most visitors approach as a snorkel site on excursions.
