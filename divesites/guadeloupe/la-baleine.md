## La Baleine

La Baleine ("The Whale") is a wall dive in the Passe de la Baleine, the tidal passage between Terre-de-Haut and Terre-de-Bas in Les Saintes, where a confirmed 19th-century French Navy wreck lies buried at the passage floor.

## Overview

The primary dive feature is the wall along the Passe de la Baleine (Whale Passage), a tidal channel generating drift conditions and strong fish life. Maximum depth reaches 25 metres. The site is also known for the Épave de la Passe de la Baleine — the remains of the Anémone, a French Navy schooner.

**The Anémone wreck:** The vessel was built in Bayonne, France in 1823 and served as a patrol and customs ship in Guadeloupe to combat the illicit slave trade. It sank during the hurricane of 7–8 September 1824. The entire crew of 29 men, including Captain Guillotin, died in the sinking. The wreck was looted during the 1990s before archaeological investigation could take place. Archival research in a 2013 PhD thesis confirmed the vessel's identity. Between 2015 and 2019, a formal archaeological excavation involving approximately 50 people catalogued 554 artefact inventory numbers across 101 field days and 922 total diving hours. These findings are documented in five comprehensive reports and two scientific articles.

After 200 years on the seabed, the Anémone's remains are substantially buried in sediment and encrusted with marine growth. The wreck fragments at 18–22 metres in the passage floor should not be disturbed — this is an active archaeological site.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Passe de la Baleine, between Terre-de-Haut and Terre-de-Bas, Les Saintes, Guadeloupe
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 25 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–30 meters in the passage
- **Current**: Moderate to strong tidal current — dive must be timed with favourable tidal flow
- **Best Time**: Year-round; time dives to the tidal schedule

## Marine Life

Dive operators and divers report seeing sea fans extended in the tidal current, schooling reef fish, and hawksbill turtles navigating the passage. Barracuda are present in the mid-water column. The passage connects the Caribbean and Atlantic sides, which historically attracts larger mobile species. No site-specific species list beyond these reported sightings has been found in published sources; generic regional species should not be attributed to this specific site without confirmation.

## Dive Profile

The tidal current creates a drift dive format. Entry is up-current from the passage; divers drift through the wall section and the passage floor before exiting down-current for boat pickup. Deploy an SMB before surfacing — the boat follows divers through the passage. Visit the wall face before descending to the passage floor to inspect the historic wreck remains. Do not collect or disturb any artefacts or structures — the site is under archaeological protection.

## Entry and Exit

Les Saintes-based dive operators schedule this dive according to tidal windows. Drift format — the boat drops divers up-current and picks up at the exit end. Always deploy an SMB on ascent.

## Tips

- This dive must be timed with the tidal schedule; ask your operator specifically about the optimal window for the current visit
- The Anémone wreck fragments are of genuine historical significance — the only confirmed 19th-century French Navy wreck confirmed by formal archaeological excavation in the Caribbean. Do not disturb
- The passage current creates excellent conditions for sea fan photography when extended in the flow
- Combine with Pointe à Cabrit on the same day for contrasting Les Saintes conditions

## Safety

Current in the passage is a fundamental characteristic, not an exception. Only dive with a certified Les Saintes operator who knows the current timing. Deploy an SMB before any ascent. Never swim against the passage current.
