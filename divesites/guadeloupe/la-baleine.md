## La Baleine

La Baleine is a wall dive site in the Passe de la Baleine (Whale Pass) between Terre-de-Haut and Terre-de-Bas in the Les Saintes archipelago, southern Guadeloupe. The site is also the location of the Épave de la passe de la Baleine — a historic shipwreck provisionally identified by archaeologists as the *Anémone*, a French Royal Navy schooner lost here in 1824.

## Overview

The Passe de la Baleine is a tidal passage between the two main inhabited islands of Les Saintes. A wall descends on the Terre-de-Haut side of the passage, and the current-swept conditions concentrate filter feeders and attract predatory fish.

The submerged wreck at this site has been under archaeological investigation since 2015. Researchers from the French Ministry of Culture and associated institutions have provisionally identified it as the *Anémone*, a schooner of the French Royal Navy built in 1823 in Bayonne, which sank on 7–8 September 1824 during a hurricane in which the entire crew perished. The identification is based on recovered artefacts including coins bearing the effigy of Louis XVIII dated 1818 and 1822, copper sheathing typical of naval vessels, two caronades (short-barrelled cannons), lead balls, and hull structural elements. The wreck covers approximately 300 m² of seabed at roughly 25 metres depth in the middle of the Saintes bay. **The identification as *Anémone* remains provisional** — archaeological research has not conclusively confirmed the vessel's identity.

The wreck had been looted in the 1970s and 1990s before formal archaeological investigation began, limiting portable artefact recovery despite reasonable structural preservation.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Passe de la Baleine, between Terre-de-Haut and Terre-de-Bas, Les Saintes, Guadeloupe
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive with historic wreck component
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 25 metres
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–30 metres
- **Current**: Moderate to strong tidal current through the passage; timing with tidal schedule required
- **Best Time**: Year-round; time dives with favourable tidal flow

## Marine Life

The tidal current through the passage concentrates filter feeders — sea fans and sea whips are dense and extended in the flow. Fish schools station in the current. The wreck remains attract groupers and lobsters. Hawksbill turtles navigate the passage.

*Note: No site-specific marine life survey has been published for La Baleine. Species listed above are consistent with the documented fauna of Les Saintes wall passages but are not confirmed by a survey specific to this site.*

## Dive Profile

Enter up-current and drift through the passage. The wall face provides a depth reference. Visit the wreck section on the passage floor (18–22 metres) before continuing the drift. Exit at the down-current end and signal the boat. Drift dives in tidal passages require a chase boat and SMB deployment.

## Entry and Exit

Operated by Les Saintes-based dive operators who know the tidal schedule. Entry is timed to the current. Drift dive format — boat drops divers up-current and picks up at the exit end. Deploy SMB on ascent.

## Tips

- This dive must be timed with the tidal schedule — ask your operator specifically when the passage is running at the right speed
- The site is a designated archaeological protection zone — do not touch, move, or remove any artefacts from the wreck
- Combine with Pointe du Gouvernail for two contrasting Les Saintes dives

## Safety

Current in the passage is a fundamental feature of the site. Only dive when the operator confirms current speed is appropriate. Never attempt to swim against the passage current. Deploy SMB early for the drift boat to track your position.
