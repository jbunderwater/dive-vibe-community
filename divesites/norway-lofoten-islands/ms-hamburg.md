## MS Hamburg

MS Hamburg is a historic wreck dive in Svolvær harbour, Lofoten Islands, Norway.

## Overview

The MS Hamburg was a Norwegian factory ship capable of processing 175 tonnes of round fish per day. She was sunk in Svolvær harbour on 4 March 1941 during Operation Claymore, a British and Norwegian commando raid targeting German fish-oil production facilities in the Lofoten Islands. The British forces considered towing the technologically advanced vessel to England but deemed it too dangerous, and the ship was fired upon and sunk.

The wreck sits in the harbour in approximately 18 metres of water and has been a dive site for over 50 years. The wreck is clearly marked by a well-visible buoy. The hull is colonized by the anemone and soft coral growth characteristic of Norwegian cold-water wrecks. Sources report depth ranges from 6 metres to 25 metres along the wreck, with the shallowest accessible portions near the deck structures. Water clarity reportedly decreases below 18 metres at this site. The sheltered harbour location and accessible depth make this one of the more frequently dived wrecks in the Lofoten archipelago.

Note: The wreck's exact length has not been independently verified to two sources; one source reports approximately 120 metres, but this has not been cross-referenced. Tonnage figures vary between sources and are not included here pending verification.
