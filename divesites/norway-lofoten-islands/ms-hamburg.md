## MS Hamburg

D/S Hamburg (listed in OSM as MS Hamburg) is a historically significant WWII-era wreck dive lying on her starboard side in Svolvær harbour, Lofoten Islands, Norway.

## Overview

Originally built in 1911 at the Bremer Vulcan shipyard in Germany as D/S Emir for Deutsche Ost-Afrika Linie, the vessel passed through several owners before being converted in 1939 into a fish-processing freezer ship and renamed D/S Hamburg — one of the most technically advanced vessels of her type at the time, capable of processing around 175 tonnes of fish per day.

On 4 March 1941, the Hamburg was caught in Svolvær harbour during Operation Claymore, the first British-Norwegian commando raid on the Lofoten Islands. According to Dykkepedia, the German crew opened the sea cocks to scuttle the vessel rather than allow capture; Norwegian newspaper NRK and Museum Nord note British forces also fired upon the ship after deciding they could not tow it. Several German crew members were lost. The wreck has been a protected cultural monument under the Norwegian Cultural Heritage Act since it exceeded 100 years of age.

The wreck is 128 metres long (6,136 BRT post-conversion) and lies on her starboard side approximately 100 metres from the Svolvær waterfront. She spans a depth range of roughly 5–27 metres, making shallow sections accessible and deeper sections suitable for intermediate divers. The stern section was destroyed; the remainder is in reasonable condition, with winches still visible on deck and the masts present. Anemones and cold-water marine life typical of Norwegian fjords colonize the hull. NRK (2021) noted the hull is showing signs of increased deterioration.

Powerful dive lights are recommended. Water temperature averages 4–6°C in winter and up to 12°C in summer; drysuit diving is required year-round.
