## MS Hamburg

MS Hamburg is a WWII wreck in Svolvær harbour, sunk during the British commando raid Operation Claymore on 4 March 1941.

## Overview

The Hamburg was a German factory ship — a purpose-built fish-processing vessel — lying on her starboard side in approximately 6–25 metres of water in Svolvær harbour. She is one of the most accessible wrecks in the Lofoten Islands, reached quickly by RIB from Ballstad with Lofoten Diving.

The ship was sunk on 4 March 1941 during Operation Claymore, the first British commando raid on Lofoten. British forces, including troops of No. 3 and No. 4 Commando and HMS Somali, targeted German-held fish-oil and glycerine production facilities on the islands. The Hamburg and seven other merchant vessels were scuttled or sunk, accounting for approximately 18,000 tons of shipping.

The cold, clear water of Svolvær harbour has kept the Hamburg's hull largely intact. The wreck is colonized by dense growth of Dahlia anemones and dead man's fingers typical of Norwegian cold-water wrecks, and large cod, wolffish, and lumpfish have taken up residence in and around the structure. Visibility in the sheltered inner waters near Svolvær averages 10–20 metres. The shallowest point of the wreck begins at approximately 6 metres, with the maximum depth around 25 metres at the bow section.

A drysuit is strongly recommended year-round; water temperatures range from 4–6°C in winter to 12–15°C in summer.
