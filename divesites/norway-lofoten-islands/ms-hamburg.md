## MS Hamburg

MS Hamburg is a WWII wreck dive in the industrial harbour of Svolvær, accessible from shore, and one of the most historically significant wrecks in Lofoten.

## Overview

The MS Hamburg was a German fish factory/processing ship (Fabrikschiff) built in 1911, with a gross register tonnage of approximately 6,136 GRT. Originally a fast fruit carrier, it was converted to a fish factory vessel after a 1938 fire, becoming at the time the most modern fish-processing ship in the world, capable of processing 175 tonnes of round fish per day. In early 1941 it was operating in Svolvær harbour under German occupation.

On 4 March 1941, British and Norwegian commandos raided the Lofoten Islands during Operation Claymore. HMS Tartar engaged the Hamburg with gunfire; British forces considered towing the vessel to England as a prize but determined it was too dangerous. German crew members opened the bottom vents to scuttle the ship, which sank in Svolvær harbour with the loss of several German lives. It was the largest of approximately eight vessels sunk during the raid, which destroyed an estimated 18,000 tonnes of shipping in total.

The wreck lies on its starboard side in the harbour. The seabed depth is approximately 22–25 metres; the upper structure and deck are accessible from as shallow as 5–15 metres, making this an unusually accessible wreck with a wide depth range. The hull is largely intact — winches remain on deck, the masts are present, and cargo holds can be entered by suitably equipped divers. A lifeboat rests on the seabed alongside. Cold-water anemones and soft corals have colonised the metalwork. Wolffish and large cod are reported around the structure. Entry is from shore in Svolvær harbour; local operator guidance is recommended for orientation.

Water temperature is sub-10°C year-round; drysuit is essential.
