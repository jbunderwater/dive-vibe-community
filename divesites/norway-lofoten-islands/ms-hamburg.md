## MS Hamburg

MS Hamburg is a WWII-era fish factory wreck lying in approximately 18 metres of water in Svolvær harbour, Lofoten Islands.

## Overview

The Hamburg was a German fishmeal and fish oil factory ship of approximately 6,136 GRT, built around 1911. During the winter of 1940–1941 she was moored in Svolvær harbour, serving as a floating fish-processing facility — at the time described as among the most modern of its kind.

On 4 March 1941, British and Norwegian commandos executed Operation Claymore, a raid on the Lofoten Islands targeting German fish oil and fishmeal production. British forces considered towing the technologically advanced Hamburg back to England but judged it too risky. HMS Tartar fired upon and sank her. The Hamburg settled in the harbour at approximately 18 metres depth, where she has remained ever since.

One tonnage conflict exists across sources: some accounts cite approximately 6,000 GRT for the Hamburg specifically, while other references to Operation Claymore report 9,000 tonnes or 18,000 tonnes as the total shipping tonnage destroyed across all vessels in the raid. The ship's individual tonnage has not been independently reconciled across sources.

The wreck has been a dive site for decades and is substantially intact: deck winches and masts remain in place, and one of the ship's lifeboats rests on the harbour bottom beside the hull. Cold, nutrient-rich water has driven heavy colonisation by anemones, dead man's fingers, and encrusting invertebrates. Cod, lumpfish, and wolffish are commonly reported in and around the wreck structure. Visibility in the sheltered harbour typically ranges 10–20 metres.

The moderate depth (18 m maximum) and protected harbour setting make this suitable for Intermediate drysuit-qualified divers.
