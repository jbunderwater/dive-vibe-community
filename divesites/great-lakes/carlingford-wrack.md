## Carlingford (Wrack)

The Carlingford is a historic Lake Erie wreck lying in approximately 20 metres of water off the Ontario north shore, in the stretch between Port Colborne and Port Dover. Like its neighbours on this well-dived stretch of lakebed, the Carlingford is a product of Lake Erie's demanding sailing history, preserved in cold freshwater conditions.

## Overview

Lake Erie was the busiest commercial shipping route in North America for much of the 19th century, and the hazards of its shallow waters, sudden storms, and unpredictable ice claimed hundreds of vessels. The Carlingford is one of many wooden-hulled schooners from this era that now rest on the lake bottom off Ontario's north shore. The 20-metre depth places this wreck squarely in the recreational diving range, and the cold freshwater has preserved hull structure, hardware, and even cargo remnants from the original sinking. Visibility ranges from 5 to 15 metres depending on weather conditions.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Lake Erie, Great Lakes, Canada
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 5–15 metres
- **Water Temperature**: 4–22°C (seasonal); bottom typically 6–10°C
- **Current**: Variable; wind-driven current possible
- **Best Time**: June to October

## Marine Life

The Carlingford's wreck structure serves as a fish aggregating device on the otherwise featureless sandy bottom of northern Lake Erie. Yellow perch school densely around the hull in summer. Walleye move through the area, particularly at dawn and dusk. Bass establish territories inside the hull openings. Round gobies are abundant on the bottom. Zebra mussels blanket all hard surfaces below the thermocline.

## Dive Profile

The wreck lies on a flat sandy bottom and is generally easy to navigate for intermediate divers. Explore the hull exterior from bow to stern, noting structural details that speak to the vessel's working life. The cargo hold and anchor hardware are primary features. Ascend with adequate reserve gas and perform a standard safety stop at 5 metres.

## Entry and Exit

Charter dive boats from Port Colborne and surrounding area provide access to this and nearby wrecks. This part of Lake Erie is well-served by local dive operators who run regular wreck diving trips. Entry by giant stride or back roll. Deploy SMB on ascent.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Combine this dive with other nearby Lake Erie wrecks on the same charter day
- Drysuit or thick wetsuit recommended — bottom temperatures are cold
- A torch is useful for examining sheltered hull sections
- Photo opportunities improve significantly with additional lighting equipment
- Respect the historical integrity of the wreck — artifacts are protected

## Safety Considerations

Lake Erie conditions can deteriorate rapidly. Monitor weather forecasts and be conservative in dive planning. Cold water and wind-driven current are the primary environmental hazards. Always dive with a buddy and carry an SMB.

## Photography

Northern Lake Erie wreck photography rewards patience — visibility can range dramatically from excellent to poor. Plan for both ambient light shooting and supplemental lighting. The yellow perch schools provide naturalistic framing opportunities around the hull.
