## A. R. Colborn and Crane (Wrack)

The A. R. Colborn and Crane wreck site is located in Lake Huron near the southern end of the lake, off the coast of Ontario, Canada. This site preserves the remains of a 19th-century vessel in the cold, clear freshwater conditions that make Great Lakes wreck diving world-class.

## Overview

Lake Huron's cold freshwater has preserved this wreck remarkably well. The site lies in approximately 20 metres of water on a sandy bottom in the southern reaches of Lake Huron, not far from the shores of Ontario's "Blue Water" region near Sarnia. Freshwater wrecks in this area are known for retaining wooden hull planking, iron hardware, and even cargo remnants that would long since have deteriorated in saltwater. Visibility typically ranges from 5–12 metres, improving in calmer summer conditions.

## Marine Life

The wreck structure attracts a diverse community of freshwater species. Smallmouth bass and yellow perch are the most commonly encountered fish, often hovering around the hull in schools. Lake trout patrol the deeper sections. Zebra and quagga mussels blanket most hard surfaces, providing habitat for amphipods and small invertebrates. Crayfish pick their way through the debris field around the wreck's base. In late summer, large walleye are occasionally spotted near the wreck.

## Dive Profile

Descend the anchor or mooring line to the top of the wreck. Explore the exterior hull, noting the well-preserved wooden construction typical of 19th-century Great Lakes sailing vessels. The site sits on a flat sandy bottom, making orientation easy. Allow adequate bottom time to circle the wreck before beginning your ascent. Perform a 3-minute safety stop at 5 metres.

## Entry and Exit

Access is by charter dive boat from operators in the Sarnia or Point Edward area of Ontario. Entry by giant stride or back roll. Deploy an SMB for your safety stop. Follow the dive boat crew's instructions for pickup.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Wear a drysuit or thick wetsuit — Lake Huron bottom temperatures are cold year-round
- Bring a torch for examining the hull's interior shadows and under-deck areas
- Do not remove or disturb artifacts — wrecks in Ontario waters are protected
- Maintain careful buoyancy to protect the fragile wooden hull sections
- Plan dive profiles conservatively to allow for extended bottom time exploration

## Safety Considerations

Lake Huron can develop significant wave action with northern winds. Always dive with a current weather forecast and be prepared to abort if conditions worsen. Cold water increases the risk of hypothermia, and adequate thermal protection is essential regardless of season. Dive with a buddy and carry an SMB.

## Photography

The preserved wooden construction of this 19th-century wreck provides compelling photographic subjects — rigging fragments, hull planking, and iron fasteners are all visible. Ambient freshwater light gives a characteristic blue-green tone to wide-angle shots. Macro lenses reveal intricate mussel colony structures and the small invertebrates that live among them.
