## Specular (Wrack)

The Specular is a 19th-century wooden schooner wreck in Lake Erie near Leamington or Windsor, Ontario — the southernmost part of Lake Erie in Canadian waters. Lying in approximately 20 metres of Lake Erie's western-central basin, the Specular rests in productive, fish-rich waters with the distinctive character of western Lake Erie wreck diving.

## Overview

The western end of Lake Erie near Windsor, Ontario and the mouth of the Detroit River was one of the busiest waterways in 19th-century North America. The Specular was among the hundreds of commercial vessels that navigated these waters before succumbing to storm or accident. She lies at approximately 20 metres in the western Lake Erie basin, where cold bottom water has preserved the wooden hull far beyond what saltwater would allow. The western basin's visibility is typically the most variable in Lake Erie — ranging from 3 metres in poor conditions to 10 metres in good ones — and summer surface temperatures can reach 22–24°C while the bottom remains cold.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Lake Erie, Great Lakes, Canada (western basin, near Windsor/Leamington area)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 3–10 metres
- **Water Temperature**: 4–24°C (seasonal); warmest summer temperatures in the Great Lakes
- **Current**: Variable; western Lake Erie develops current quickly with wind
- **Best Time**: June to October

## Marine Life

The western Lake Erie fishery is among the most productive in North America. Yellow perch gather in enormous schools around the Specular in summer. Walleye — the species Lake Erie is most famous for — patrol the wreck edges and hunt perch. Smallmouth bass are resident on the hull. Channel catfish cruise the bottom debris field. White bass and lake herring are present seasonally. Zebra mussels blanket all submerged hard surfaces.

## Dive Profile

The Specular lies on a flat bottom in the western Lake Erie basin. Standard bow-to-stern exploration. The thermocline (typically at 10–12 metres in summer) marks the transition from warm surface water to cold bottom water — prepare for this temperature change. Silty conditions in the western basin require careful buoyancy. Safety stop at 5 metres for 3 minutes.

## Entry and Exit

Charter dive boats from Leamington or Windsor, Ontario, or from the US side at Monroe or Toledo, Michigan. Entry by giant stride. Deploy SMB on ascent.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Western Lake Erie's exceptionally warm summer surface water is unusual for Great Lakes diving
- The walleye and perch concentrations are among the most impressive in freshwater diving globally
- Visibility varies considerably — check conditions with operators before booking
- Drysuit recommended for cold bottom water despite warm surface
- Heritage protection laws apply in both Ontario and Ohio waters

## Safety Considerations

Western Lake Erie weather changes rapidly — the most storm-prone section of the Great Lakes. Always check forecasts before heading out. The warm surface contrasts sharply with cold bottom water. Commercial shipping traffic near the Detroit River requires awareness. Dive with a buddy and carry an SMB.

## Photography

Dense fish aggregations — perch and walleye in numbers unusual in freshwater diving — create spectacular wildlife photography. The warm surface light in summer produces attractive ambient conditions in the upper water column.
