## Cedarville (Wrack)

The Cedarville is a 20th-century steel steamer that sank in the Straits of Mackinac, Michigan, in May 1965 following a collision with the Norwegian vessel Topdalsfjord in foggy conditions. The 180-metre-long limestone carrier rests in approximately 32 metres of water in the Straits of Mackinac, making it one of the more accessible large steel wrecks in the Great Lakes system.

## Overview

The Cedarville's sinking was a tragic event — ten crew members were lost when the US Steel vessel went down quickly in the Straits. The vessel now lies on her starboard side on a rocky bottom in the Straits of Mackinac, the narrow passage connecting Lake Huron and Lake Michigan. The wreck is a compelling dive due to its relatively recent vintage (1965), the intact nature of the hull, and the drama of her violent end, which left the vessel listing and partially broken. The Straits of Mackinac experience some of the clearest water in the Great Lakes system, with visibility often exceeding 15 metres. Water temperatures at depth are consistently cold.

## Marine Life

The Straits of Mackinac are known for exceptional freshwater fish populations. Lake trout — among the most prized sport fish in the Great Lakes — are commonly encountered around the Cedarville at depth. Cisco (lake herring) school in open water above the wreck. Large walleye and yellow perch inhabit the shallower portions. The clean, cold Straits water supports less zebra mussel colonisation than Lake Erie but more than Lake Superior, creating a middle-ground ecosystem on the hull.

## Dive Profile

The Cedarville lies on her side, presenting a dramatic profile. Descend to the hull at approximately 20 metres and work deeper to the maximum depth of 32 metres at the keel. The ship's scale — 180 metres long — means a single dive will only explore a section of the vessel. Focus on the bow area (intact and photogenic), midships cargo holds, or stern with its propeller. Multiple dives are needed to fully explore this wreck.

## Entry and Exit

Charter dive boats from Cheboygan and Mackinaw City, Michigan provide access. The Straits area has an active dive charter community due to its numerous wrecks. Current in the Straits must be monitored carefully. Entry by giant stride. Deploy SMB on ascent.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Dive the Cedarville on slack current periods for the best conditions
- The wreck's large scale rewards repeat visits to different sections
- Drysuit essential for bottom temperatures
- The Straits area offers numerous additional wreck dive opportunities
- Lake trout sightings are common and make memorable encounters — have a wide-angle lens ready
- Current in the Straits can exceed 1 knot — plan accordingly

## Safety Considerations

Current in the Straits of Mackinac is the primary safety consideration. Dive timing should be coordinated with slack water periods when possible. The cold water demands proper drysuit diving skills. The wreck's size and complexity make it easy to become disoriented — maintain reference to the anchor or mooring line. Always dive with a buddy.

## Photography

The Cedarville's scale and the excellent Straits visibility combine to create exceptional wide-angle photography opportunities. The 20th-century steel construction retains its shape well, with portholes, machinery, and hull features providing compelling close-up subjects. Lake trout encounters add dramatic wildlife photography potential.
