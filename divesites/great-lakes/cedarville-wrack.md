## Cedarville (Wrack)

The Cedarville is a 20th-century steel freighter that sank in the Straits of Mackinac, Michigan, on May 7, 1965, following a collision with the Norwegian vessel Topdalsfjord in dense fog. The 588-foot (179-metre) limestone carrier rests in approximately 34 metres (110 feet) of water in the Straits of Mackinac, making it one of the more accessible large steel wrecks in the Great Lakes system.

## Overview

The Cedarville was built in 1927 at River Rouge, Michigan, and carried 14,411 tons of limestone when she was struck on her port side by the Topdalsfjord in dense fog on the morning of May 7, 1965. Ten crew members died — eight went down with the ship and two more succumbed to exposure. Twenty-five crew survived. The Cedarville's captain was later charged with and pled guilty to faulty seamanship.

The wreck lies on her starboard side at approximately 45 degrees from inverted, about 3.5 miles southeast of the Mackinac Bridge south tower. The hull is largely intact and lies at depths of 40 to 110 feet (12 to 34 metres), with the highest point of the hull approximately 35 feet (11 metres) below the surface. The "fatal gash" from the collision is visible amidships. The Straits of Mackinac experience some of the clearest water in the Great Lakes system, with visibility often 8–18 metres. Water temperatures at depth are consistently cold.

## Marine Life

The Straits of Mackinac are known for exceptional freshwater fish populations. Lake trout — among the most prized sport fish in the Great Lakes — are commonly encountered around the Cedarville at depth. Cisco (lake herring) school in open water above the wreck. Large walleye and yellow perch inhabit the shallower portions. The clean, cold Straits water supports less zebra mussel colonisation than Lake Erie but more than Lake Superior, creating a middle-ground ecosystem on the hull.

## Dive Profile

The Cedarville lies on her starboard side at approximately 45 degrees from inverted. The highest point of the hull is at about 11 metres, with the maximum depth at the keel reaching 34 metres. The ship's scale — 588 feet (179 metres) long — means a single dive will only cover a section of the vessel. Focus on the bow area (intact and photogenic), midships cargo holds with the visible collision gash, or the stern. Multiple dives are needed to fully explore this wreck.

## Entry and Exit

Charter dive boats from Cheboygan and Mackinaw City, Michigan provide access. The Straits area has an active dive charter community due to its numerous wrecks. Current in the Straits must be monitored carefully. Entry by giant stride. Deploy SMB on ascent.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Dive the Cedarville on slack current periods for the best conditions
- The wreck's large scale rewards repeat visits to different sections
- Drysuit essential for bottom temperatures
- The Straits area offers numerous additional wreck dive opportunities
- Lake trout sightings are common and make memorable encounters — have a wide-angle lens ready
- Current in the Straits can exceed 1 knot — plan accordingly

## Safety Considerations

Current in the Straits of Mackinac is the primary safety consideration. Dive timing should be coordinated with slack water periods when possible. The cold water demands proper drysuit diving skills. The wreck's size and complexity make it easy to become disoriented — maintain reference to the anchor or mooring line. Always dive with a buddy.

## Photography

The Cedarville's scale and the excellent Straits visibility combine to create exceptional wide-angle photography opportunities. The 20th-century steel construction retains its shape well, with portholes, machinery, and hull features providing compelling close-up subjects. Lake trout encounters add dramatic wildlife photography potential.
