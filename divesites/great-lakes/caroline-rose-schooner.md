## Caroline Rose (Schooner)

The Caroline Rose is a deliberately sunk schooner lying in 16.5 metres of water near Tobermory, Ontario. Built in 1940 in Lunenburg, Nova Scotia, she was towed to Driftwood Cove on Georgian Bay by the Tobermory Maritime Association and scuttled in 1990 as a dive attraction. The wreck lies outside the Fathom Five National Marine Park boundaries.

## Overview

The Caroline Rose was purposefully sunk to create an accessible dive site near Tobermory. The wreck lies outside Fathom Five National Marine Park boundaries at Driftwood Cove. Although the vessel was intact when sunk in 1990, it has since been flattened — but substantial artifacts remain: the propeller, chains, pulleys, ropes, and galley sink. Georgian Bay water clarity around the site can be excellent. Bottom temperatures remain cold (around 4°C) year-round, requiring proper thermal protection.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Georgian Bay, Lake Huron, Fathom Five National Marine Park, Ontario, Canada
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 16 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 10–20 metres
- **Water Temperature**: 4–16°C (seasonal); bottom consistently cold
- **Current**: Minimal in protected Georgian Bay waters
- **Best Time**: June to September

## Marine Life

Georgian Bay's cold, clear waters support healthy lake trout and smallmouth bass populations that frequent the Caroline Rose. Yellow perch school around the hull and masts. The shallow sections of the wreck, where sunlight penetrates well, are colonized by algae that attract small forage fish. Lake whitefish are occasionally seen in the water column above the deck. Zebra mussels encrust the hull below 6 metres.

## Dive Profile

The Caroline Rose sits upright on the bottom with the main deck at roughly 12 metres and the keel at 16 metres. Beginners can comfortably explore the full length of the vessel within recreational NDL limits. The intact hull with open deck areas and visible interior spaces through hatchways makes this an engaging dive without requiring wreck penetration. Ascend the anchor line for a standard safety stop at 5 metres.

## Entry and Exit

Charter dive boats from Tobermory Harbour visit the Caroline Rose regularly, often as a second dive after a deeper wreck in the morning. The Tobermory dive charter infrastructure is well-developed with multiple operators, boat tours, and glass-bottom boat viewing options. Entry by giant stride. Deploy SMB on ascent.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Drysuit or 7mm wetsuit required — Georgian Bay bottom water is cold regardless of season
- The wreck is often combined with Tobermory harbour wrecks in a single dive day
- Tobermory is extremely busy in July and August — book charters well in advance
- The wreck lies OUTSIDE Fathom Five National Marine Park — no park pass required for this site specifically
- Despite being flattened, the scattered artifacts reward patient exploration

## Safety Considerations

Although shallow and beginner-friendly, the cold water demands proper thermal protection. Hypothermia can set in faster in cold freshwater than in tropical seas. Ensure your exposure suit provides adequate insulation for the temperature. Always dive with a buddy. Boat traffic is high around Tobermory in summer — deploy an SMB before surfacing.

## Photography

The Caroline Rose is a photographer's favourite at Tobermory. The combination of exceptional clarity, intact hull, and manageable depth allows wide-angle shots of the entire wreck in natural light. The bow and stern profiles are classic subjects, and fish life adds natural animation. Natural light in clear Georgian Bay water has a luminous, turquoise quality distinct from deeper or murkier Great Lakes sites.
