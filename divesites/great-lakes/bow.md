## Bow

The Bow is one of three dive sites marking sections of the Edmund Fitzgerald, the most famous shipwreck in Great Lakes history. The bow section of the massive ore carrier lies on the bottom of Lake Superior at approximately 160 metres depth, making this a technical diving site far beyond recreational limits. This site is referenced primarily as a historical and technical diving landmark.

## Overview

The Edmund Fitzgerald sank on November 10, 1975, during a ferocious November storm on Lake Superior — the most dangerous month for Great Lakes shipping. The 222-metre-long ore carrier went down with all 29 crew members, becoming the most famous freshwater maritime disaster of the 20th century. The Fitzgerald broke into two main sections — bow and stern — which lie approximately 170 metres apart in approximately 160 metres of water in Canadian waters south of Michipicoten Island, Ontario. The bow section, pointing northeast, lies upright and is the more intact of the two pieces. As with all sections of the Edmund Fitzgerald, the wreck serves as an official grave site under both US and Canadian law, and diving to the wreck requires government permits.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Lake Superior, Great Lakes, Ontario, Canada
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 160 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 3–8 metres at depth
- **Water Temperature**: 2–4°C at depth year-round
- **Current**: Minimal near the bottom
- **Best Time**: July to August (most stable surface conditions)

## Marine Life

At 160 metres depth in Lake Superior, marine life is sparse but notable. Deep-water lake trout and sculpin are occasionally encountered around the wreck. The extreme cold and depth limit biological activity significantly compared to shallower Great Lakes sites. The wreck itself has been colonized by freshwater sponges and bacterial mats on some surfaces.

## Dive Profile

This site is strictly for technical divers with appropriate training in deep mixed-gas diving. Depths of 160 metres require trimix breathing gas, extensive decompression planning, and specialized training far beyond recreational scuba certification. Bottom times at this depth are extremely limited — measured in minutes — with decompression obligations exceeding an hour. Surface support from a fully equipped technical diving vessel is mandatory.

## Entry and Exit

Technical diving expeditions to the Edmund Fitzgerald are rare and require permits from the Canadian government (Transport Canada) and the Ontario Ministry of Heritage, Sport, Tourism and Culture Industries. Accessing the site requires a large, capable vessel and full technical diving support infrastructure.

## Tips and Recommendations

- This site is not accessible to recreational divers — it requires technical mixed-gas diving qualifications
- The wreck is an official grave site — treat it with utmost respect
- Permits are required and are not routinely granted; this is a restricted site
- Numerous books, documentaries, and museum exhibits about the Edmund Fitzgerald are available for those who want to understand this historic vessel
- The Great Lakes Shipwreck Museum at Whitefish Point, Michigan displays the Fitzgerald's recovered bell and provides excellent historical context

## Safety Considerations

Lake Superior is the most dangerous of the Great Lakes, with cold water temperatures that are lethal within minutes for an unprotected diver. At 160 metres, the risks associated with technical diving — oxygen toxicity, narcosis, decompression sickness, equipment failure — are compounded by the extreme cold and remoteness of the site. Only highly experienced technical divers with specialized equipment should consider this site.

## Photography

The handful of authorized expeditions to the Edmund Fitzgerald have produced striking documentary footage. The preserved bow section still bears the vessel's name visible on the hull — one of the most iconic images in Great Lakes diving history.
