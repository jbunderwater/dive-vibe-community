## Great Lakes

Freshwater wreck diving with historic ships preserved in cold, clear waters.

## Description

The Great Lakes (Superior, Michigan, Huron, Erie, and Ontario) hold one of the world's largest concentrations of freshwater wrecks — primarily 19th- and 20th-century commercial vessels lost in storms, collisions, and fires. The cold, low-salinity, dark water has preserved wooden hulls, rigging, fittings, and even cargo on many wrecks to a degree unmatched in saltwater.

### Diving Opportunities

- **Wreck Diving**: The primary draw — hundreds of identified wrecks from schooners to lake freighters, ranging from shallow recreational depths to deep technical sites.
- **Boat Diving**: Most wrecks are reached by charter boat from lakeside ports.
- **Shore Diving**: Some wrecks in protected bays and bottomland preserves are shore-accessible.

### Accessibility

- **Getting There**: The Great Lakes are accessible via airports in Toronto, Detroit, Cleveland, Chicago, Milwaukee, Minneapolis, Buffalo, and many regional cities on both sides of the US/Canada border.
- **Dive Operators**: Charter operators run scheduled trips from ports on each lake; many specialize in particular wreck groups (Tobermory/Fathom Five on Lake Huron, Alpena/Thunder Bay on Lake Huron, Kingston on Lake Ontario, etc.).
- **Accommodation**: Range of lakeside lodging in dive-active ports.
- **Transportation**: Personal or rental vehicle is typically required to reach the various dive ports.

### Conditions

- **Water Type**: Freshwater. Bottom temperatures stay cold year-round (typically 4–8 °C); surface temperatures vary seasonally.
- **Visibility**: Variable — often 5–15 meters; some sites in clear bays or deep open water see considerably more.
- **Season**: Generally May–October for recreational diving; many sites are ice-locked or impractical in winter.
- **Marine Life**: Freshwater fish (lake trout, salmonids, smallmouth bass, walleye) and invasive species (zebra and quagga mussels, round goby) are common, but the wrecks themselves — not biology — are the primary attraction.

## Additional Information

- **Currency**: US Dollar / Canadian Dollar (varies by lake/state/province)
- **Language**: English (also French in parts of Ontario and Québec watershed)
- **Safety**: Cold freshwater diving requires drysuit or thick-wetsuit proficiency. Many sites are deep and call for advanced training. Boat traffic on the lakes can be significant. Verify the location of the nearest hyperbaric chamber before diving.
