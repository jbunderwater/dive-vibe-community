## Little Wissahickon (Wrack)

The Little Wissahickon is a 19th-century wooden schooner wreck in Lake Erie near Cleveland, Ohio. One of numerous historic vessels resting on the western Lake Erie bottom, this small schooner offers a compact and accessible wreck dive in the productive waters of the American side of Lake Erie.

## Overview

The Little Wissahickon was a canal schooner — smaller than most Great Lakes traders — that served the Lake Erie trade routes in the 19th century. She rests in approximately 20 metres of western Lake Erie water near the Cleveland, Ohio area. While Lake Erie's western basin is not as famous for wreck diving as the Ontario north shore, it holds a significant collection of historic vessels accessible from Ohio ports. The western basin's warm summer surface temperatures (reaching 22–24°C) contrast with cold bottom water below the thermocline, and visibility can vary from 3 to 12 metres depending on conditions.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Lake Erie, Great Lakes, USA (Ohio waters, western basin)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 3–12 metres
- **Water Temperature**: 4–24°C (seasonal); western basin has warmest summer temperatures in Lake Erie
- **Current**: Variable with wind direction
- **Best Time**: June to October

## Marine Life

Western Lake Erie is one of the most productive fishing grounds in North America. Yellow perch swarm around the Little Wissahickon in summer, creating spectacular fish aggregations. Walleye — the prized game fish of Lake Erie — hunt near the wreck structure, especially at dawn and dusk. Smallmouth bass claim hull openings as territory. Channel catfish are found on the silty bottom around the wreck. Round gobies are omnipresent. Zebra mussels coat all hard surfaces below the thermocline.

## Dive Profile

The wreck lies on a flat, silty bottom. The compact size of the Little Wissahickon makes complete exploration possible within a single dive. Explore the hull length from bow to stern, noting the construction typical of small 19th-century canal vessels. Silty conditions require careful buoyancy. Safety stop at 5 metres.

## Entry and Exit

Charter dive boats from Cleveland, Lorain, or Sandusky, Ohio provide access. Ohio's Lake Erie dive charter community is active and experienced. Entry by giant stride. Deploy SMB on ascent.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Western Lake Erie conditions vary significantly day to day — check forecasts and charter recommendations
- The warm surface temperatures in summer allow wetsuit diving at the surface, but bring thermal protection for bottom temperatures
- The perch and walleye aggregations are often more impressive than the wreck itself
- Ohio heritage protection laws apply to underwater cultural resources

## Safety Considerations

Lake Erie weather changes rapidly and without much warning. The western basin's shallow depth means waves build quickly. Always check weather forecasts before heading out. Cold water below the thermocline demands thermal protection. Always dive with a buddy and carry an SMB.

## Photography

Dense perch schools provide exceptional wildlife photography around the Little Wissahickon. In good visibility, the compact wooden hull fits in wide-angle frames. Walleye portraits require patience but are memorable.
