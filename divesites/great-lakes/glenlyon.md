## Glenlyon

The Glenlyon is a large steel steamer wreck in Lake Superior at Isle Royale National Park, Michigan. This bulk carrier, built in 1893 and lost in November 1924 after running aground on a shoal off Menagerie Island, rests in the cold, clear waters of Isle Royale's Siskiwit Bay — one of the best-preserved steel wrecks in the Great Lakes system.

## Overview

The Glenlyon was a 328-foot (100-metre) steel-hulled package freighter built in 1893 by F.W. Wheeler & Company. On November 1, 1924, after sheltering from severe weather in Isle Royale's Siskiwit Bay, the vessel ran aground on the reef off Menagerie Island in the early morning hours. All crew were rescued. She now rests in 15 to 60 feet (4.6 to 18.3 metres) of water on the shoal — her bow on the bay side, stern sections on the lake side. Lake Superior's cold, clear water has preserved the hull remarkably well: the bow, engine room, drive shaft, propeller, boilers, and engine are still in place. The wreck was placed on the National Register of Historic Places in 1984.

Access to Isle Royale requires a national park boat or seaplane — the park is approximately 72 kilometres from the nearest Michigan shore. This remoteness, combined with cold Lake Superior water and the park's permit requirements, makes this an Advanced dive destination despite the relatively shallow depth.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Isle Royale National Park, Lake Superior, Michigan, USA (Siskiwit Bay, off Menagerie Island)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 18 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 8–20 metres
- **Water Temperature**: 4–12°C (seasonal); Lake Superior bottom water consistently near 4°C
- **Current**: Minimal in Siskiwit Bay
- **Best Time**: July to September (park season)

## Marine Life

Lake Superior has the lowest biodiversity of the Great Lakes but supports impressive fish populations. Lake trout — one of Lake Superior's signature species — frequent the Glenlyon site and are often seen in substantial numbers. Cisco (lake herring) school in open water above the wreck. Rainbow smelt are abundant. The relative absence of invasive zebra mussels (less established in Lake Superior than the lower Great Lakes) means the hull maintains a more natural appearance.

## Dive Profile

The Glenlyon's 328-foot length means multiple dives are needed for full exploration. The wreck is scattered in 15–60 feet of water across the shoal, with bow on the bay side and stern sections on the lake side. The engine room, drive shaft, propeller, engine, and boilers remain intact and in place. The bow section and stern machinery are the most photogenic destinations. Ascend on the anchor line with a standard safety stop at 5 metres.

## Entry and Exit

Isle Royale is accessible only by ferry or seaplane from Houghton, Copper Harbor, or Grand Portage, Minnesota. Once in the park, diving is accessed by charter boat from Rock Harbor or Windigo. Isle Royale National Park requires a park entrance fee. All diving within the park requires registering with park rangers. Entry by giant stride from the dive boat. Deploy SMB on ascent.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Isle Royale is a remote national park — plan accommodation carefully (Rock Harbor Lodge or camping)
- A drysuit is essential — Lake Superior water is cold year-round
- Diving must be registered with Isle Royale National Park rangers
- The wreck is on the National Register of Historic Places — do not disturb any artifacts
- Isle Royale ferries run a limited seasonal schedule; book well in advance
- The park offers several other wreck sites — combine with the America or Kamloops for a full diving expedition

## Safety Considerations

Lake Superior is famously cold and conditions can change rapidly. Even in summer, water temperatures require adequate thermal protection — a drysuit is the standard recommendation. Lake Superior is remote, and emergency services may take longer to reach than on the lower lakes. Always dive with a buddy and carry an SMB.

## Photography

Lake Superior's exceptional clarity makes the Glenlyon a standout photography destination. The large steel hull photographs beautifully in wide-angle, and the relative lack of zebra mussel coverage shows the original construction details. Lake trout encounters are outstanding for wildlife photography.
