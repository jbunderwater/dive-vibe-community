## Lycoming (Wrack)

The Lycoming is a 19th-century Lake Erie wreck resting in approximately 20 metres of water in Lake Erie south of the Ontario shoreline, in the area between Cleveland, Ohio and Port Stanley, Ontario. This wreck represents the numerous commercial sailing vessels that worked the Lake Erie trade routes before steam power dominated.

## Overview

The Lycoming rests in the central section of Lake Erie — relatively close to the American shore based on coordinates, in the deeper central basin of the lake. Lake Erie's central basin reaches maximum depths of 60+ metres, but the Lycoming lies at the shallower accessible depth of 20 metres where recreational diving is optimal. Cold freshwater has preserved the vessel well, and the central Lake Erie basin is known for somewhat better visibility than the turbid western basin. Visibility ranges from 5–12 metres depending on conditions and weather.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Lake Erie, Great Lakes (central basin, near Ontario/Ohio border)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 5–12 metres
- **Water Temperature**: 4–22°C (seasonal); bottom 4–8°C
- **Current**: Variable with wind
- **Best Time**: June to October

## Marine Life

Yellow perch are abundant, as they are throughout Lake Erie. The wreck structure draws large schools that hover above the deck in summer. Walleye patrol the wreck edge. Smallmouth bass are territorial on the hull. Round gobies populate the bottom debris field. White bass are sometimes seen in the open water column above the site. Zebra mussels are densely packed on all submerged hard surfaces.

## Dive Profile

Standard Lake Erie wreck profile — descend to the wreck top and explore bow to stern on the flat sandy bottom. The central Lake Erie basin's cooler bottom water provides good preservation of wooden hull elements. Ascend with adequate reserve gas for safety stop at 5 metres.

## Entry and Exit

Charter dive boats from Ohio ports (Lorain, Vermilion) or Canadian ports (Port Stanley, Port Burwell) on the Ontario north shore provide access. Entry by giant stride. Deploy SMB on ascent.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Central Lake Erie offers calmer summer conditions than the western basin in many situations
- Drysuit recommended for cold bottom temperatures
- Check visibility conditions with the charter operator — central basin varies considerably
- Heritage protection laws apply to all Great Lakes wrecks in both US and Canadian waters

## Safety Considerations

Lake Erie weather can deteriorate in any section of the lake. Central basin storms can be severe. Always dive with weather awareness. Cold bottom water demands thermal protection. Dive with a buddy and carry an SMB.

## Photography

Central Lake Erie wreck photography benefits from better clarity than the western basin. The wooden hull and hardware provide historical subject matter. Yellow perch schools are plentiful photographic subjects.
