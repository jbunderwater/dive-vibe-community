## Robert Gaskin (Wrack)

The Robert Gaskin is a beloved 19th-century wooden schooner wreck in the St. Lawrence River near Brockville, Ontario. Part of the Thousand Islands region's outstanding wreck collection, the Robert Gaskin lies in the clear, flowing freshwater of the St. Lawrence, her hull preserved in remarkable condition in the 140+ years since she sank.

## Overview

The Robert Gaskin was a two-masted wooden schooner that sank in the St. Lawrence River near Brockville in the late 19th century. She now rests in approximately 20 metres of the St. Lawrence's clean, flowing freshwater in the heart of the Thousand Islands region. The constant flow of the river maintains excellent visibility — typically 8–15 metres — and the cold water has kept the wooden hull in outstanding condition. The Robert Gaskin is consistently listed among the best wreck dives in Ontario, notable for the completeness of her hull and the richness of the surrounding Thousand Islands diving environment. Divers who make the trip specifically for the Robert Gaskin frequently describe it as one of the finest freshwater wreck experiences they have had anywhere.

## Site Information

- **Location**: St. Lawrence River, Ontario, Canada (Thousand Islands, near Brockville)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 metres
- **Typical Visibility**: 8–15 metres
- **Water Temperature**: 4–22°C (seasonal)
- **Current**: Moderate; St. Lawrence River current varies seasonally
- **Best Time**: June to September

## Marine Life

The Thousand Islands section of the St. Lawrence is one of the most diverse freshwater aquatic environments in eastern North America. Northern pike are a signature species here — large specimens, often exceeding a metre, are regularly encountered near the wreck. Smallmouth bass inhabit the hull. Yellow perch and walleye are present. Muskellunge (muskie) inhabit the area and are occasionally sighted. The flowing river keeps the hull in better condition than equivalent lake wrecks — less mussel colonization means more of the original vessel's character is visible.

## Dive Profile

Plan the dive to account for river current. Descend to the wreck and use the hull as a current shelter while exploring. The Robert Gaskin's upright profile and intact character make navigation easy even in modest visibility. On ascent, deploy SMB early given the river environment. Coordinate pickup with the dive boat.

## Entry and Exit

Multiple charter dive operators work out of Brockville, offering regular trips to the Robert Gaskin and other Thousand Islands wrecks. This is a well-served dive destination. Entry by giant stride. River current management requires an experienced dive crew.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The Robert Gaskin is one of the finest freshwater wreck dives in Canada — highly recommended for any wreck diving enthusiast
- Plan a multi-day Brockville area trip combining the Robert Gaskin, Lillie Parsons, and Keystorm
- Current experience is valuable — assess conditions before entry
- Drysuit recommended for cold freshwater conditions
- The Thousand Islands National Park scenery above water is spectacular — combine diving with sightseeing

## Safety Considerations

St. Lawrence River current requires active management. Commercial shipping traffic makes a dive flag and SMB mandatory. Cold water demands thermal protection. Always dive with a buddy. The Brockville area has good emergency services response, but standard conservative diving practices apply.

## Photography

The Robert Gaskin is regarded as one of the finest wreck photography subjects in freshwater diving. The complete, upright hull, the clarity of the St. Lawrence River, and the abundant fish life — particularly northern pike — create exceptional images. The 19th-century wooden hull's natural appearance (less mussel-encrusted than lake wrecks) reveals historical construction details vividly.
