## Keystorm (Wreck)

The Keystorm is a steel-hulled cargo steamer that sank in the St. Lawrence River on October 12, 1912, after striking Scow Island shoal in fog near Alexandria Bay, New York. The 256-foot (78m), 2,300-ton freighter, built in England, now lies on her starboard side on a slope — bow at approximately 7.6 metres (25 feet), stern at 35 metres (115 feet) — in the Thousand Islands stretch of the St. Lawrence River. All crew were rescued when she sank.

## Overview

The Keystorm sank in 1912 after striking a shoal in dense fog and now lies on her starboard side in the St. Lawrence River, technically in US waters near the Brockville area. The wreck lies on a slope with the bow at about 7.6 metres (25 feet) and the stern at 35 metres (115 feet), providing a range of depths in a single dive. The wreck is intact — divers on the mooring line reach the port side of the bow with the anchor chain hanging down. The winch, wheelhouse, and deck machinery are all visible. The St. Lawrence's constant flow maintains excellent visibility (8–15 metres typical). The site is in US waters but is commonly accessed by Canadian and US operators from the Brockville/Thousand Islands region.

## Site Information

- **Location**: St. Lawrence River, Ontario, Canada (Thousand Islands)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 35 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 8–15 metres
- **Water Temperature**: 4–22°C (seasonal)
- **Current**: Moderate; varies with season
- **Best Time**: June to September

## Marine Life

The Thousand Islands St. Lawrence supports outstanding freshwater fishing and diving wildlife. Northern pike are frequently encountered around and beneath the Keystorm. Muskellunge (muskie) inhabit the area. Large smallmouth bass are resident near the hull. Yellow perch, walleye, and other species complete the community. The river's current reduces heavy zebra mussel colonization, leaving portions of the steel hull in a more natural state than equivalent lake wrecks.

## Dive Profile

Plan the dive around the current direction. Enter upstream and navigate to the wreck, using the hull as a current break while exploring. The Keystorm's steel construction means machinery, cargo holds, and hull fittings are all accessible exploration targets. On ascent, deploy an SMB early given the boat traffic and current in the St. Lawrence.

## Entry and Exit

Charter dive boats from Brockville are the primary access point. Brockville has multiple dive operators specializing in Thousand Islands wreck diving. The town's dive charter infrastructure also supports access to nearby wrecks including the Robert Gaskin and Henry C. Daryaw. Entry by giant stride from the dive boat.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Current diving experience is valuable before attempting this site
- The Thousand Islands region deserves a multi-day diving trip
- Brockville is an excellent base with dive operators, accommodation, and restaurants
- Combine this dive with a visit to the Brockville Underwater Sculpture Park for variety
- The steel hull's industrial features reward close-up examination with a torch

## Safety Considerations

St. Lawrence River current can be strong and varies with season and upstream water levels. Assess conditions carefully before each dive. The river carries commercial shipping traffic — a dive flag visible from the surface is required, and an SMB on ascent is mandatory. Always dive with a buddy.

## Photography

The Keystorm's steel construction and the river's clear water create excellent photographic conditions. Northern pike are compelling portrait subjects, often posing motionless in the current near the hull. The industrial scale of the vessel provides dramatic wide-angle compositions.
