## Mountain Point

A quiet, less-visited reef on the northwest tip of Virgin Gorda where caves, rock passages, and a well-preserved northern reef section reward patient divers with sleeping nurse sharks, tarpons, and a long list of resident reef species.

## Overview

Mountain Point sits on the northwest side of Virgin Gorda, close to the neighboring Cow's Mouth formation. It is described by bvi-dive-map.com as "a less popular diving site" — which in the BVI means less crowded and often in better condition than the better-known destinations. The underwater topography is varied: sandy patches covered in corals and sea sponges alternate with caves, passages, and rock formations that invite careful exploration. The northern reef section is particularly well preserved. Depth reaches a maximum of around 23 meters (74 ft) but much of the interesting diving is in shallower sections. Swell can be high at this exposed northwest-facing location, but current is typically low. Despite the novice classification, conditions should be checked before diving due to the exposed aspect.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Northwest tip of Virgin Gorda, near Cow's Mouth
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Coral reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 23 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: Good
- **Current**: Low
- **Best Time**: Calmer summer months recommended; swell can be high on exposed northwest face

## Marine Life

Nurse sharks are regularly found resting in the smaller caves and rock shelters — one of the more reliable nurse shark sightings in the Virgin Gorda area. The reef supports a broad mix of species: grunts and parrotfish are abundant; angelfish, squirrelfish, butterflyfish, and lizardfish are present throughout. Tarpons and jacks cruise the reef edge, and barracuda hover in the water column. Permits and garden eels occupy the sandy areas. Lobsters shelter in rock crevices. Stingrays rest or cruise the sandy bottom between formations.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the reef structure and begin exploring the caves and passages — nurse sharks are most likely found in the smaller rock cavities in the shallower sections. Move through the rocky formations methodically, using natural light to look into overhangs before committing to entry. The northern reef section is in excellent condition and worth prioritizing during the dive. Sandy patches between coral formations offer access to garden eels and stingrays. Ascend from the northern reef end for a safety stop before boat pickup.

## Entry and Exit

Access is by dive boat. Entry via giant stride or back roll. The northwest-facing exposure means conditions should be verified before arriving at the site. Deploy an SMB for ascent signaling.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Check conditions before diving — the northwest-facing position can expose the site to swell
- The smaller rock cavities are the best places to look for resting nurse sharks — approach slowly and do not disturb them
- The northern reef section has the best coral coverage — plan to spend time there
- A less-visited site: going mid-week or early morning means likely having the reef to yourself
- Close to Cow's Mouth — operators often pair the two sites in a single trip

## Safety Considerations

Swell can be significant on the exposed northwest face — confirm current conditions with your operator. While current is typically low, the exposed location can create surge near the surface. Use care when entering and exiting the rocky formation areas. Carry an SMB.

## Photography

The cave and passage formations create opportunities for silhouette and ambient-light shots framing divers in rock openings. Nurse sharks resting on the sand in rock cavities are among the best portrait subjects at the site. The dense reef fish populations — grunts, angelfish, parrotfish in schools — work well for mid-range reef shots.
