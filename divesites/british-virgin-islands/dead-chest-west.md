## Dead Chest West

A multi-layered dive on the western face of Dead Chest Island, with overhangs, two distinct caves, arches, and a shallow labyrinth of alleys that rewards divers who take their time.

## Overview

Dead Chest West follows the western shoreline of Dead Chest Island, where the dramatic topside cliff face continues below the waterline into a complex series of formations. The dive unfolds in layers: a spur-and-groove ridge section with an archway near the start, followed by overhangs that shelter fairy basslets and snappers, two separate caves (one with a triangular opening full of glassy sweepers, one set inside a large bowl cut into a massive monolith), and — in the shallows toward the end of the dive — a maze of alleys, arches, and ravines worth exploring on remaining air. A large green moray eel is frequently resident near the second cave. The site is generally calm with low current, and the moderate swell exposure makes it accessible most days.

## Site Information

- **Location**: West face of Dead Chest Island, between Salt Island and Peter Island, British Virgin Islands
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Coral reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 17 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: Good
- **Current**: Low
- **Swell**: Moderate
- **Best Time**: December to April; accessible most of the year given its sheltered west-facing aspect

## Marine Life

Schools of colorful grunts and mahogany snappers are typical throughout the site. Fairy basslets swim just below overhangs, often feeding upside-down along the cave roofs. The two caves shelter glassy sweepers, banded coral shrimp, and spiny urchins. A large green moray eel is a reliable resident near the second cave. Spotted lobsters hide in deeper crevices, and redspotted hawkfish perch on coral heads. Wrasses, gobies, chromis, and Spanish hogfish round out the reef fish community. Encrusting sponges color the overhangs.

## Dive Profile

Begin at the mooring and locate the first ridge with the archway — this marks the start of the upper reef zone at roughly 10–15 meters. Follow the base of the cliff working south, passing through or under overhangs as topography allows. The first cave has a distinctive triangular opening at around 12 meters; peer inside with a torch to see sweepers and shrimp. Continue to the large bowl formation where the second cave is recessed at the back of the monolith — the resident moray is often visible here. As air supply diminishes, work back into the shallows (5 meters and above) where the alleys and arches provide excellent exploration before the safety stop.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access via mooring buoy. Entry is back roll or giant stride. A dive torch is strongly recommended to appreciate the cave interiors and overhang residents. Bring a spare light for the caves. An SMB for ascent is standard practice given the offshore position of Dead Chest Island.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Bring a torch — cave interiors and overhangs look dramatically different with light
- Explore the shallows (5 meters) near the end of the dive; the alleys and arches are often the highlight
- Look for the resident green moray at the back of the large bowl cave
- Slow down: this site rewards patient divers who check every crevice
- The second cave is large enough to peer into but not intended for penetration diving

## Safety Considerations

Cave entries require proper training and equipment; the two caves at Dead Chest West are cavern/ambient-light environments and should not be fully penetrated without cave diving certification. The overhangs and narrow passages make buoyancy control important — avoid disturbing sediment by fin-kicking near the seabed. A torch is helpful but avoid shining it directly at resting animals. Moray eels are harmless unless provoked.

## Photography

The caves and overhangs are excellent subjects — a wide-angle lens with a strobe to illuminate the coral shrimp and sweepers inside the triangular cave opening works well. Fairy basslets swimming upside-down along cave roofs are distinctive BVI portrait subjects. The shallow maze of alleys in the latter part of the dive offers natural-light wide-angle shots through arch openings.
