## Cistern Point

A quieter western Cooper Island reef site with distinctive cistern-like craters in the rock, active cleaning stations, colorful sea fans, and an unusually diverse mix of species from blue tangs and turtles to nurse sharks and tarpon.

## Overview

Cistern Point sits on the western shore of Cooper Island, just a short boat ride from the Cooper Island anchorage. The site takes its name from the large cistern-like holes in the reef structure — natural craters and depressions in the rock that create a distinctive underwater topography of ledges, craters, and rocky outcroppings. Sea fans, gorgonians, and colorful corals coat the rocky surfaces and sandy bottom.

The site is notably less visited than nearby RMS Rhone or Painted Walls, giving it a quieter character that many divers appreciate. It is also known for active cleaning stations, where smaller fish service larger species — a behavior that tends to concentrate fish activity in predictable locations and is rewarding to observe.

Depths here are shallow — topping out at around 12 meters — making it a good second dive after a deeper morning dive.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Western side of Cooper Island
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 12 meters
- **Depth Range**: 6–12 meters (20–40 feet)
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–25 meters
- **Current**: Light to moderate
- **Swell**: Low (sheltered western aspect)
- **Best Time**: Year-round; good afternoon dive option

## Marine Life

Species diversity at Cistern Point is high relative to its modest depth. Blue tangs and queen angelfish patrol the reef actively. Schools of snappers and grunts congregate around the cistern depressions. Chromis and damselfish school above the coral heads. Sergeant majors are abundant. Tarpon — unusual at this depth — occasionally cruise through the site. Southern stingrays rest in sandy areas between rock formations. Turtles visit regularly. Moray eels occupy crevices in the rocky sections. Nurse sharks rest under ledges in the deeper depressions. The cleaning stations attract a rotating cast of larger fish coming for parasite removal.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the reef top and orient using the western-facing cliff as your reference. Work across the rock face, exploring the cistern-like craters and ledges at each depth level. The 6–12-meter depth range rewards horizontal exploration rather than vertical profiling. Look into each crater and depression for resting nurse sharks and moray eels. Identify the cleaning station locations early in the dive and return to observe activity. The sandy bottom areas between rocky sections hold stingrays. Ascend directly up from the reef with SMB when you reach your planned air reserve.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access, short ride from the Cooper Island anchorage. Entry by giant stride or back roll. The western exposure provides some shelter from easterly trade winds and swell, making entry and exit straightforward on most days. Exit via the mooring or with SMB as briefed.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Look for cleaning stations early in the dive and note their locations — they reward a return visit later in the bottom time
- Peer into every cistern-like crater carefully — nurse sharks often rest at the bottom of these depressions
- The site pairs well as an afternoon second dive after a morning dive at RMS Rhone or Painted Walls
- This is one of the BVI's quieter sites; midweek visits will find it largely free of other dive groups
- Tarpon are unusual for this depth range — look along the edges of rocky overhangs for these large silvery fish

## Safety Considerations

The site is relatively benign. Standard precautions apply: avoid contact with fire coral, maintain buoyancy, and be aware of boat traffic in the Cooper Island anchorage. Carry an SMB for ascent. Nurse sharks resting in the craters should be observed from a respectful distance and not disturbed.

## Photography

The cistern crater formations make interesting wide-angle compositions when combined with the sea fans framing them. Cleaning station activity rewards patient shooting. Portrait subjects include queen angelfish, blue tangs, and moray eels. If nurse sharks are resting in the craters, a wide-angle shot looking down into the depression with the shark at the bottom is a distinctive composition.
