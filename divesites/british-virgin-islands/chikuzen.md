## Chikuzen

A 246-foot Korean refrigerator vessel deliberately set ablaze and cast adrift in 1981, now lying on its port side in 23 meters of water northwest of Tortola — one of the BVI's most marine-life-rich wreck dives, home to a legendary resident Goliath grouper.

## Overview

The Chikuzen sits approximately 7.5 miles northwest of Tortola between Tortola and Anegada, resting in open water far from any reef. This isolation is precisely what makes it special: in a featureless sandy expanse, the wreck functions as an oasis that attracts an extraordinary concentration of pelagic and reef species.

The ship was a 246-foot (75 m) refrigerator vessel based in St. Maarten, where it serviced Japanese fishing fleets. Multiple sources consistently describe it as a "Korean refrigerator vessel." One source (H2O Trips) additionally claims it was built in Japan in 1960 under the original name Seujo Maru No. 1 and later renamed by Dutch owners; this specific detail appears in only one source and is not independently confirmed, so should be treated with caution. By early 1981, the aging vessel was beyond repair. When authorities in St. Maarten ordered its relocation ahead of an approaching hurricane, the owners chose a different solution: they set the ship on fire and cast it adrift, hoping it would sink offshore. The burning hulk drifted instead toward Marina Cay in the BVI, prompting local dive operators and boat owners to attempt to hold it off. Tugboats from St. Croix and Antigua eventually towed the smoldering wreck away from populated areas. One towline snapped, severely injuring a crew member who required airlift to hospital. The Chikuzen finally sank roughly 12 kilometers northwest of Tortola.

The ship now lies on its port side in 23 meters (75 feet) of water, with the starboard rail rising to approximately 15 meters (50 feet). The hull is extensively encrusted in corals and sponges. Three large cargo holds remain intact and are accessible through open hatches — divers who enter report "nice surprises in each one." The pilothouse has collapsed, but the rest of the structure is largely preserved.

The irony is often noted: a vessel that spent its working life as a refrigerator for dead fish now shelters an extraordinary abundance of living ones.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Approximately 7.5 miles (12 km) northwest of Tortola, between Tortola and Anegada
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 23 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–30 meters (site sits in open water with no reef silting)
- **Current**: Variable — exposed offshore location
- **Swell**: Weather-dependent; the exposed position means the site is not always divable
- **Best Time**: Calm conditions required; summer often most reliable

## Marine Life

The Chikuzen is one of the BVI's premier pelagic encounter sites. Horse-eyed jacks school in dense formations around the wreck. Large barracuda cruise the upper structure. Cobia are commonly observed. Groupers — including large jewfish (Atlantic goliath grouper) — shelter in and around the holds. Southern stingrays rest on the sandy bottom at the base of the hull. Sea turtles visit regularly. A resident 600-pound Goliath grouper has been documented at the site and is a sought-after encounter. The possibility of pelagic shark and ray encounters is always present; one of the only confirmed whale shark encounters in BVI waters occurred here during a Cuan Law dive.

The coral and sponge encrustation on the hull supports abundant smaller reef fish and invertebrates. Schools of smaller fish shelter inside the cargo holds.

## Dive Profile

Descent to the highest point of the wreck — the starboard rail at approximately 15 meters — for orientation. The ship lies on its port side, so the hull rises on one side and the sandy bottom (at 23 meters) is on the other. Work along the hull from bow to stern, examining the coral encrustation and looking into openings for resident fish. The three cargo holds can be entered through open hatches; each can be explored briefly before moving on. The pilothouse area is collapsed but provides interesting terrain. Watch the open water above and around the wreck for pelagic species — jacks and barracuda are often seen schooling in mid-water. Begin ascent with adequate air for a 5-meter safety stop, which can be productive for open-water species observation.

## Entry and Exit

Due to the offshore location, the Chikuzen is typically accessed by liveaboard vessels, with Cuan Law historically being the primary operator running regular trips. Some long-range day charters also serve the site. Conditions must be assessed by the operator before descent — the exposed position means the site may be scrubbed if swell is running. Entry by giant stride or back roll. Surface pickup requires attention to boat positioning in open water; always deploy an SMB.

## Tips and Recommendations

- This site requires a dedicated trip from Tortola — plan it as the primary dive of the day
- The Goliath grouper is a resident, not a guaranteed sighting; approach without rapid movements or bubble blasts near the holds
- Bring a dive torch for cargo hold exploration — each hold has different residents
- The open water above the wreck is as interesting as the structure; look up regularly for schooling pelagics
- Do not attempt cargo hold penetration without proper wreck penetration training and a guideline
- Conditions are weather-dependent; flexibility in your itinerary helps ensure you can actually dive the site

## Safety Considerations

The offshore, exposed location is the primary hazard. Current conditions must be assessed by the operator. Cargo hold penetration requires proper wreck penetration certification, redundant air, and a guideline — do not free-dive into the holds. The open-water ascent in potential current and swell requires SMB deployment. Dive with an experienced buddy and establish clear air turn-around pressures before descending. This is an Advanced site due to the remote location and open-water conditions, not due to the depth alone.

## Photography

The Chikuzen is one of the BVI's top wide-angle wreck subjects. The hull encrusted in coral against blue open water offers classic wreck compositions. Schooling horse-eyed jacks and barracuda in mid-water reward patience and a wide-angle lens. The Goliath grouper, if encountered near the holds, is the top portrait subject — approach slowly and shoot from a distance. Inside the holds, a torch is essential. The remoteness and isolation of the wreck in open water means ambient light is exceptional on good-visibility days.
