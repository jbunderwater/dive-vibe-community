## Thumb Rock

A massive underwater pinnacle near Wreck Alley that rises from 18 meters to within a few meters of the surface, surrounded by schools of creole wrasse and lobsters and known for exceptional marine biodiversity including seahorses, spotted drums, and French angelfish.

## Overview

Thumb Rock is located near the wreck dives of Wreck Alley (the Marie L and Pat wrecks) south of Cooper Island, making it a natural complement on a multi-dive day in that area. The site takes its name from the boulder's distinctive profile — a large pinnacle that rises vertically from approximately 18 meters to within a few meters of the surface, resembling a thumb pointing upward from the seafloor. Despite its relatively modest maximum depth, Thumb Rock packs in a remarkable diversity of species. Large schools of creole wrasse congregate around the formation, and the ledges beneath the overhangs shelter numerous lobsters. The site's biodiversity is considered superior to many other BVI sites and makes it a favourite for underwater photographers.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Near Wreck Alley, south Cooper Island area, BVI
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Pinnacle/seamount
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 18 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: Good; typical BVI conditions
- **Current**: Light to moderate
- **Best Time**: Year-round

## Marine Life

Schools of creole wrasse surround the pinnacle, making them impossible to miss. Lobsters are abundant beneath the ledges and in crevices throughout the formation. Tarpons circle the upper section of the rock. French angelfish and queen angelfish are regularly sighted among the coral structures on the pinnacle's sides. Spotted drums move along the base of the formation. Seahorses have been recorded here — look carefully in the sponge and coral growth along the rock faces. Barracuda are present in the water column above the pinnacle.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the base of the formation at approximately 18 meters and work upward in a spiral pattern around the pinnacle. The lower section features lobsters in overhangs and crevices along with spotted drums. Mid-pinnacle (8–15 meters), the creole wrasse schools become dense and angelfish are most active. The upper section of the rock, close to the surface, is worth careful examination for seahorses hidden in soft coral and sponge growth. The entire pinnacle can be circumnavigated multiple times during a single dive without repeating yourself. Ascend slowly along the rock face for the safety stop.

## Entry and Exit

Access by boat only. Entry via giant stride or back roll from the dive boat. The proximity to the Wreck Alley sites means this dive can easily be combined with one of the nearby wreck dives on the same day. Deploy an SMB during the safety stop for boat pickup.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Slow down and look carefully in the sponge growth for seahorses — they are easily missed
- The wreck sites at Marie L and Pat are nearby; this site pairs well with a morning wreck dive
- Creole wrasse schools are most impressive in the middle section of the pinnacle; ascend slowly to keep them from dispersing
- Good buoyancy control is important to avoid disturbing the fine sediment around the base

## Safety Considerations

Current is generally mild at this site. Be cautious of boat traffic in the Wreck Alley area. Depth at the base is modest but decompression limits should be managed carefully if combining multiple dives in the area. Always dive with a buddy and carry a surface marker buoy (SMB).

## Photography

Thumb Rock is rated highly for photography across multiple subjects. The creole wrasse schools make excellent wide-angle subjects; position yourself below a school to shoot upward against the water column. Spotted drums, French angelfish, and any seahorse encountered reward macro and close-focus wide-angle approaches. The pinnacle's coral-encrusted surface provides strong compositional elements. Natural light penetrates well to the upper section; strobes are useful for the deeper overhangs.
