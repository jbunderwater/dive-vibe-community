## Paul's Grotto

A lesser-visited cave and coral hill site near Mountain Point and Cow's Mouth on Virgin Gorda, densely populated with arrow crabs, fairy basslets, lionfish, and diverse invertebrate life.

## Overview

Paul's Grotto is located near Mountain Point and Cow's Mouth on the northwest side of Virgin Gorda, sharing a cluster of dive sites in that area. The site is characterized by underwater caves and coral-covered hills, with the cave structures and their overhangs supporting an unusually dense and varied community of reef life. The hills are draped in fire coral, staghorn coral, elkhorn coral, and cup coral, while the cave interiors and overhang roofs shelter the more cryptic species that make this site distinctive. Paul's Grotto is consistently described as less crowded than BVI's headline sites, making it a rewarding option for divers who prefer a quieter experience with attentive wildlife encounters. The depth is variable (the JSON source records no fixed maximum, with 25 meters given as an approximate ceiling), and swell can be high given the site's northwesterly exposure.

## Marine Life

Paul's Grotto is exceptional for invertebrate diversity. Arrow crabs and crack crabs are reliably found in the cave overhangs and under ledges. Shrimps, sea urchins, and banded coral shrimp occupy crevices throughout the site. Fairy basslets — which characteristically feed while swimming upside-down — are present in the cave roofs. Fish diversity is high: graysby, sergeant majors, trunk fish, butterfly fish, damselfish, parrotfish, sea bass, and queen fish are all recorded here. Spotted lobsters and rock lobsters are common. Lionfish are present in the site's sheltered cave areas. Barracuda patrol the water column. Moon jellyfish drift through seasonally. Hawksbill and green turtles visit the site regularly.

## Dive Profile

Descend onto the coral hills and begin by exploring the upper sections where staghorn, elkhorn, and fire coral form the framework of the site. Work down into the cave entrances, using a torch to illuminate the arrow crabs, fairy basslets, and crustaceans inside. The caves at this site are ambient-light caverns rather than full penetration environments; explore from the entrance. Sandy pockets between the coral hills shelter turtles and flatfish. The site's variety — open reef hills, cave interiors, overhang structures — rewards a systematic approach that covers different habitat types within the dive. Maximum practical depth is around 20–25 meters, with most of the interesting biology in the 5–18 meter range.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access only. The northwest-facing exposure means this site can be affected by northerly and trade-wind swell; confirm conditions with the operator before the dive. Dive BVI and Charter Guru BVI Charters are the primary operators serving this site. A torch is essential. Entry via back roll or giant stride. An SMB for ascent is standard.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Bring a torch — the cave interiors and overhangs shelter most of the site's most interesting species
- Move slowly and check cave roofs for fairy basslets feeding upside-down
- Arrow crabs in overhangs are striking macro subjects; approach slowly to avoid startling them
- Less crowded than Mountain Point, making wildlife encounters more relaxed
- Swell can limit access; have an alternative site planned (Cow's Mouth is nearby)

## Safety Considerations

Cave sections at Paul's Grotto are ambient-light caverns — they should be explored from the entrance with a torch and not fully penetrated without proper cave diving certification and equipment. Fire coral is present throughout the coral hills; good buoyancy control is essential to avoid contact. Lionfish are present; they are not aggressive but their spines are venomous and should not be touched. Always dive with a buddy and carry an SMB. The exposed northwest location requires a pre-dive conditions check.

## Photography

The cave interiors are exceptional for close-focus wide-angle photography: arrow crabs, shrimps, and fairy basslets against illuminated cavern walls create striking images. The coral hills above — staghorn and elkhorn — are classic Caribbean reef wide-angle subjects. Turtle portraits in the sandy clearings between hills work well in natural light. Lionfish in overhang settings make dramatic subjects for fish portraiture with a strobe.
