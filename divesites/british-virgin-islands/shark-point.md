## Shark Point

A current-exposed site on the northeast corner of Scrub Island where ledges at 18 meters shelter dense marine life, sharks genuinely frequent the area, and small caves and canyons reward divers on the return to the mooring.

## Overview

Shark Point earns its name directly — sharks are commonly sighted here, and the site was named because of their genuine presence rather than as promotional marketing. Located on the northeast corner of Scrub Island, the site faces open water and experiences significant tidal current flows that carry nutrients and baitfish into the ledge system. The primary feature is a system of ledges at around 18 meters that concentrates sea life: large fish shelter under the overhangs and nurse sharks, rays, and tarpon are regularly encountered either on the ledge structure or in the open water beyond it. Small caves and canyons appear on the route back to the mooring, extending the interesting topography into the shallower section of the dive.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Northeast corner of Scrub Island, BVI
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 21 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: Good; open-water location
- **Current**: High; variable with tidal state
- **Swell**: Moderate
- **Best Time**: Conditions permitting; avoid in inclement weather

## Marine Life

Sharks are the defining species — primarily nurse sharks resting under the ledge overhangs, with reef sharks possible in the open water beyond the structure. Rays are frequently sighted in open water alongside the ledge system. Tarpon cruise through the area in numbers, along with large schools of baitfish attracted by the current flow. The ledge overhangs themselves shelter diverse sea life including lobsters and various reef fish species. Small caves on the shallower section of the dive can hold sleeping fish during the day.

## Dive Profile

From the mooring, head east toward the ledge system. The ledges at approximately 18 meters are the primary dive destination — work along the overhang, looking underneath for nurse sharks and into open water for rays and reef sharks. Monitor the open-water column throughout the dive, as larger animals tend to appear with little warning in current-swept sites. On the return journey to the mooring, the route passes through small caves and canyons in the shallower range (5–10 meters) that extend the dive's interest. Current typically runs away from the boat, which means the outward leg is done against any flow — plan air accordingly and do not let current carry you too far from the mooring before turning.

## Entry and Exit

Access by boat only. Entry via giant stride or back roll. The high-current environment requires careful briefing before the dive. Deploy SMB during safety stop for boat pickup. Due to current direction typically running away from the mooring, an SMB and drifting pickup protocol may be necessary if divers are carried off the line.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Check tidal state and current direction before descent — the dive changes significantly with current strength
- Head east from the mooring immediately upon descent to reach the ledge system with remaining air
- Keep an eye on open water throughout the dive — sharks and rays appear in the column, not just on the reef
- Do not attempt in inclement weather; the exposed position makes conditions deteriorate rapidly

## Safety Considerations

High current is the primary hazard. This dive should not be attempted by divers without experience diving in current. The site's exposure means weather and sea conditions can change without warning. Always dive with a buddy. Carry a surface marker buoy (SMB) and be prepared for a live-boat pickup if current separates you from the mooring line. Dive within your air limits — the return leg against current consumes air faster than the outward leg.

## Photography

The ledge system at 18 meters offers strong compositions with overhanging rock and sheltering marine life. Wide-angle shots of nurse sharks beneath the ledges, or rays in open water with the reef structure in the background, are the signature images at this site. Baitfish schools in the current make good mid-water wide-angle subjects. Natural light is strong on the open reef but diminishes under the ledge overhangs — strobes are recommended for shots into the ledge system.
