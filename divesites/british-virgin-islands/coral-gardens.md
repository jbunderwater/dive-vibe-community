## Coral Gardens

A dual-attraction novice dive at Great Dog Island that pairs dense boulder coral formations with the fuselage of a deliberately sunk Shorts 360 aircraft — the wingless wreck has become a thriving artificial reef centerpiece.

## Overview

Coral Gardens is tucked into the eastern tip of the south side of Great Dog Island in the British Virgin Islands. The site is known for two distinct features: extensive coral formations of boulder star coral, great star coral, brain corals, tube sponges, sea fans, and gorgonians; and the wreck of a Shorts 360 twin-turboprop aircraft that rests on the sandy bottom at around 12–15 meters (40–50 ft). The aircraft belonged to Atlantic Air BVI and made an aborted takeoff in 1993, landing in the water approximately 60 meters from the runway. It was initially raised and stored in a hangar, then used as a prop in a BBC film before being deliberately sunk here as part of the BVI's artificial reef program. The plane has no wings or tail section but has become, as bvi-dive-map.com notes, "the center of sea life, attracting even bigger fish." In December 2019, three additional art-installation aircraft shaped like sharks (bull, hammerhead, and nurse shark) were sunk nearby as part of an environmental recycling project. Mooring balls are available. Low swell and low current make this a comfortable novice-level dive.

## Site Information

- **Location**: South side of Great Dog Island, British Virgin Islands
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 15 meters
- **Depth Range**: 6–15 meters (20–50 feet)

- **Best Time**: Year-round

## Marine Life

Under the overhangs of the main wreck and coral formations: lobsters, spotted drums, grunts, and snappers. On the sand surrounding the wreck: stingrays, goatfish, lizardfish, and sand filefish. Schools of sennet frequent the area. Barracuda and occasional turtles are regularly sighted. The wreck structure itself shelters small reef fish and has accumulated significant coral and sponge encrustation since sinking.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the mooring line and approach the coral garden section first — the boulder and brain coral mounds are dense and worth exploring before moving to the wreck. The aircraft fuselage rests on the sand in the shallower part of the site; swim around the exterior and look into the open cabin for resident fish. Finish with the nearby "Sharkplane" installations in the sandy patches, then ascend gradually through the shallow coral zone for a safety stop before surfacing for boat pickup.

## Entry and Exit

Access is by dive boat. Mooring balls are available at the site. Entry via giant stride or back roll. Deploy an SMB for ascent signaling.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Explore the coral garden before the wreck to start the dive in the richest coral zone
- Bring a dive torch to illuminate the wreck interior and the overhangs where lobsters and spotted drums hide
- The "Sharkplane" art installations are in the sandy areas — look for them on a secondary pass
- Do not enter confined spaces inside the aircraft without proper training and equipment
- Maintain good buoyancy near the wreck to avoid disturbing accumulated silt

## Safety Considerations

Conditions are typically calm. Avoid stirring up silt inside the fuselage. Do not attempt wreck penetration beyond the open-door sections without proper certification and equipment. Always carry an SMB for ascent signaling.

## Photography

The aircraft fuselage makes a striking wide-angle subject, particularly when a diver is silhouetted at the cabin opening. The "Sharkplane" installations add unusual creative opportunities. Macro subjects abound on the sponge and coral-encrusted surfaces. Natural light is good in clear conditions; a torch helps in the overhangs.
