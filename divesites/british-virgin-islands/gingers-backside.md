## Ginger's Backside

The sheltered northern face of uninhabited Ginger Island on the Sir Francis Drake Channel, a calm and colorful reef rich in macro subjects and consistently recommended for underwater photography.

## Overview

Ginger's Backside faces north onto the Sir Francis Drake Channel from Ginger Island, which is entirely uninhabited and part of the national park. The "backside" is the island's northern face — the channel side, as opposed to the ocean-facing south. Being channel-facing, this site is almost always calmer and flatter than the southern exposure, and the low swell and current make it an excellent choice for a relaxed second dive or surface interval site. The reef is characterized by colorful coral formations and extensive nooks and crannies that shelter a remarkable variety of small creatures. Ginger Island's uninhabited status and relatively light dive traffic mean the marine life here is generally undisturbed and approachable.

## Site Information

- **Location**: North side of Ginger Island, Sir Francis Drake Channel, British Virgin Islands
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Coral reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 21 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: Good
- **Current**: Low
- **Swell**: Low (channel-facing, sheltered)
- **Best Time**: Year-round; particularly calm in the mornings

## Marine Life

Ginger's Backside is particularly rich in macro subjects. Crabs, lobsters, and octopuses hide in reef crevices and are more commonly spotted here than at busier sites. Turtles are frequent visitors and comfortable around divers due to limited boat traffic. Barracuda and southern sennet (smaller barracuda) school in the water column. Nurse and reef sharks are reliably sighted, as are tarpons cruising through the site. Moray eels occupy rock formations throughout the reef. Porcupinefish and parrotfish are common at mid-depths. The diverse invertebrate life makes this a strong choice for macro photography.

## Dive Profile

Descend from the mooring onto the reef top and work east or west along the base of the island's northern cliff. Depths range from shallow reef-top at 5 meters down to sandy patches at roughly 21 meters. The productive depth range is 8–18 meters where the reef structure is most complex. Concentrate exploration on reef crevices, under ledges, and in sandy pockets between coral heads — this is where the octopuses, lobsters, and resting nurse sharks are found. The site is generally relaxed enough to allow slow, systematic searching.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access via mooring buoy. Entry is back roll or giant stride. The calm channel conditions make this a comfortable entry even on rougher days elsewhere. An SMB for ascent is standard BVI practice, though traffic here is lighter than at the Norman Island sites.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Bring a macro lens or camera — octopuses, crabs, and lobsters are the highlights of this site
- Look carefully under ledges and in crevices; the macro subjects here require patience to spot
- This site pairs well with a morning dive at Alice's Backside (the south face) or Ginger Steps
- The calm conditions make it suitable for less experienced intermediate divers
- Almost always diveable, making it a good backup when swells close other sites

## Safety Considerations

The calm channel conditions make this one of the BVI's lower-risk sites. Standard precautions apply: dive with a buddy, monitor air supply, and carry an SMB. The reef crevices are not large enough for penetration; explore entrances only. Handling octopuses or other marine life is prohibited in BVI national park waters.

## Photography

Ginger's Backside is among the best macro photography sites in the BVI. Octopuses, lobsters, crabs, and moray eels are well-suited subjects. The calm, low-current conditions allow precise hovering for small subject work. Turtles at this site are relatively relaxed and offer close portrait opportunities. For wide angle, barracuda schools and tarpon in the water column are rewarding subjects.
