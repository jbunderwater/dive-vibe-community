## Dolphin Rocks

An often-overlooked site on the northern side of George Dog featuring distinct eastern canyons and western ledges, best dived in summer when calm conditions allow baitfish to gather and bring in barracuda, bonito, mackerel, and tarpon.

## Overview

Dolphin Rocks sits on the northern side of George Dog, one of the Dog Islands — a cluster of small rocky outcrops north of Tortola that are better known for sites like The Chimney and Bronco Billy. Dolphin Rocks tends to receive less attention than its neighbours, which means the site maintains a quieter character. The site has distinct geological character on each side: the eastern portion is defined by canyons cutting through the rock, while the western area features ledges, smaller rocky formations, and crevices that shelter marine creatures and invertebrates. Because the site faces open water to the north, it is weather-dependent — best conditions occur during the calmer summer months when the wash rocks attract baitfish that in turn draw in the larger pelagic species.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Northern side of George Dog, Dog Islands, BVI
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 15 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: Good in calm conditions
- **Current**: Low
- **Swell**: High; weather-dependent
- **Best Time**: Summer months (June–September) for calmest conditions and best pelagic activity

## Marine Life

During calm summer conditions, baitfish aggregate around the wash rocks, attracting a mix of smaller pelagic species: barracuda, bonitos, mackerels, and tarpons are all regularly reported at this time of year. The ledges and crevices on the western side shelter resident reef fish and invertebrates throughout the year. The canyon systems on the eastern side support their own community of species in the deeper sections of the formation. The site is described as offering interesting, colorful fish and beautiful underwater topography.

## Dive Profile

Dive planning should reflect the site's east-west divide. Start at the eastern section to explore the canyon systems, then work around to the western ledges and rocky formations for the second half of the dive. In summer when baitfish are present, the open water above the rocks provides the best action for pelagic encounters. The ledges on the western side reward detailed exploration — search crevices for invertebrates and sheltering fish. The modest maximum depth (15 meters) means good bottom time is available. This is a good site for a second dive when the first was at a nearby deeper Dog Islands site.

## Entry and Exit

Access by boat only. Being in exposed open water, entry conditions must be assessed before the dive. In significant swell, the site should not be attempted. Entry via giant stride or back roll. Deploy SMB during safety stop for boat pickup.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Plan Dolphin Rocks for summer when conditions are calmer and pelagic activity peaks
- Treat the canyon (east) and ledge (west) sections as two separate dive areas to get full value from the site
- Combine with The Chimney or Bronco Billy for a full Dog Islands day
- The site's low profile among operators means marine life is often less habituated to divers — particularly good for pelagic encounters

## Safety Considerations

The exposed northern position means Dolphin Rocks should only be dived in settled, calm weather. Swell can build rapidly in the Dog Islands area. The site is rated Advanced due to open-water exposure and weather dependency. Always dive with a buddy. Carry a surface marker buoy (SMB) as a precaution against drift.

## Photography

The site's topographic variety — canyons on one side, ledges on the other — provides compositional diversity within a single dive. Pelagic subjects like barracuda and tarpon are best photographed with a wide-angle lens, positioning below the fish against the water column above. The western ledge crevices reward macro work. The site's colourful reef fish against blue-water backgrounds suit the natural light conditions typical of northern BVI sites.
