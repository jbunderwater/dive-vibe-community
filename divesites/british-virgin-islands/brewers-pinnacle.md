## Brewers Pinnacle

Isolated granite pinnacles off the western tip of Brewers Bay on northern Tortola — their steep faces, covered in deep-water gorgonians and sea fans, create a maze of passages from 25 to 110 feet that rewards careful navigation with jacks, eagle rays, and lobsters.

## Overview

Brewers Pinnacle sits off the western tip of Brewers Bay on Tortola's north coast. The site is too distant from shore to be dived from the beach and is accessible only by boat. The defining feature is a cluster of massive granite pinnacles of varying height and bulk that rise steeply from the ocean floor, some coming within 9 meters (30 ft) of the surface. The gaps between the sheer structures form a confusing maze of alleyways, dead ends, and narrow passages — a layout that rewards experienced divers with good navigation skills. Pinnacle walls are encrusted with deep-water gorgonians, sea fans, and fire coral. The site is best dived in summer when there is no north swell; winter conditions can make it unsuitable. Depths range from about 8 to 33 meters (25–110 ft), with baitfish, mackerel, and tarpon drawn to the pinnacle structures. The site receives fewer visitors than many BVI locations, giving it a secluded character.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Off the western tip of Brewers Bay, northern Tortola
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Pinnacle/seamount
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters
- **Depth Range**: 8–33 meters (25–110 feet)
- **Typical Visibility**: Good in calm conditions
- **Current**: Variable; can be strong
- **Best Time**: Summer (when north swell is absent)

## Marine Life

Large jacks patrol the pinnacle crests in open water. Eagle rays and sea turtles are regularly sighted by observant divers. Baitfish and mackerel school around the structures, attracting tarpon. Among the rocky passages and reef: lobsters in crevices, queen angelfish picking at the encrusted surfaces, and whitespotted filefish. Blennies occupy small territories on the pinnacle faces. The gorgonians and deep-water sea fans on the walls support small invertebrates at depth.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the base of the pinnacles and begin navigating the passages between them. The structures rise to varying heights — some crowned near the surface — creating a layered dive with distinct zones at depth, mid-water, and shallow. Work through the alleyways methodically; the confusing layout is part of the site's character but demands attention to compass bearing and air supply. Ascend along the outer face of one pinnacle to finish the dive in the shallower zone before a safety stop at 5 meters. Current can be present; monitor it throughout the dive and time your ascent to avoid drift.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access only — the site is too far from Brewers Bay beach for shore diving. Entry via giant stride or back roll. Deploy an SMB before ascending from depth. Coordinate ascent and boat pickup carefully given potential current.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Best dived in summer when north swell is absent — confirm conditions with your operator before booking
- The pinnacle maze demands solid navigation skills; compass work is essential
- Watch carefully in the water column between pinnacles for eagle rays and large jacks passing through
- Carry an SMB and deploy it before ascending — current and boat traffic can be factors at the surface
- A secluded site with fewer divers than many BVI locations — worth the extra effort to get there

## Safety Considerations

This is an advanced site. Strong current can develop around the pinnacles and in the passages between them. The confusing topography of dead ends and narrow passages requires good navigation skills and disciplined air management. Always carry and deploy an SMB for ascent. Not recommended during north swell conditions.

## Photography

Wide-angle: pinnacle walls draped in gorgonians with divers silhouetted in the alleyways make striking compositions. The open-water pelagic action — jacks, eagle rays — rewards patient wide-angle shooters. Macro subjects include fire coral, blennies, and crevice invertebrates on the pinnacle faces.
