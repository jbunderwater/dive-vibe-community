## Fallen Jerusalem

An uninhabited 48-acre National Park island south of Virgin Gorda, where massive volcanic boulders — similar to those at The Baths — create a dramatic underwater landscape of caves, swim-throughs, and tunnels on the northwest shore.

## Overview

Fallen Jerusalem is an uninhabited island located south of Virgin Gorda, declared a National Park in 1974 and a bird sanctuary in 1959 to protect its population of red-billed tropic birds. The island's name derives from the appearance of the heavy volcanic boulders scattered across its surface — the jumbled rock formations look like ruins, evoking the image of a collapsed ancient city.

Below the waterline, the same enormous boulders that define the island's surface create an equally dramatic underwater environment. On the northwest shore, the boulders form a network of swim-throughs, tunnels, and cave-like passages that recall the famous geological formations at The Baths on Virgin Gorda — some divers describe it as exploring The Baths in 35 feet of water. The passages are open, cathedral-like voids rather than tight technical caves, but the terrain is complex enough to reward careful navigation.

## Site Information

- **Location**: South of Virgin Gorda, between Virgin Gorda and Anegada
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Cave/cavern
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 25 meters (though the main formations are shallower)
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–25 meters
- **Current**: Low
- **Swell**: Low
- **Best Time**: Year-round; National Park status ensures reef protection

## Marine Life

The shaded interior passages and overhangs shelter nocturnal species that linger through the day. Schools of glassy sweepers fill the cave interiors and flash coppery silver in dive torch light. Snappers and squirrelfish occupy the larger swim-throughs and overhangs. Sea turtles have been spotted by snorkelers at the shallower boulder fields, and are a realistic expectation for divers too. The rocky crevices and crannies throughout the site house small reef fish; careful examination of every crack rewards patient observers.

## Dive Profile

Descend and navigate to the northwest shore where the boulder formations are most dramatic. Work through the main swim-throughs and tunnel sections at 6–10 meters, using a dive torch to illuminate the interior surfaces and the glassy sweeper schools that fill the passages. The site's topography rewards unhurried exploration — there is no single route; the maze of boulders offers many paths. For divers who want more depth, the boulder formations continue deeper on the seaward side to around 25 meters. Ascend from the reef with an SMB.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access only. No permanent moorings — anchor or drift depending on operator preference. The northwest shore is the primary dive area. Entry by giant stride or back roll from the boat. The exposed southern BVI location means some swell is possible; check conditions before diving the more exposed deeper sections. North Lee Bay beach on the island's north side provides a sheltered surface interval option if conditions allow going ashore.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Bring a dive torch — glassy sweepers and encrusting life inside passages are best appreciated with light
- Navigate the tunnel system slowly; the complexity of the boulder terrain rewards patience
- This is a less-visited site than The Baths above water — expect quieter conditions
- If you have dived The Baths at Virgin Gorda (as a snorkel or walk), the underwater version here provides interesting context
- Look in every crevice and cranny — the site concentrates small reef fish in the rocky gaps

## Safety Considerations

The cave and swim-through passages at Fallen Jerusalem are daylight-penetration features — all sections described are open to natural light and do not require technical cave training. However, navigation in complex boulder terrain requires good buoyancy and spatial awareness. Do not force passage through tight sections. Carry a torch as a backup navigation aid. Always dive with a buddy and deploy an SMB on ascent. The Advanced rating reflects the site's terrain complexity and potential exposure, not extreme depth.

## Photography

Wide-angle inside the passages to capture the tunnel effect with ambient light streaming in from both ends. A dive torch or strobe helps illuminate glassy sweeper schools and encrusting surfaces inside the darker sections. The contrast between the dim boulder interiors and the blue-lit ocean outside creates strong compositional opportunities. Macro opportunities exist throughout the rocky surface.
