## Monkey Point

A sheltered, aquarium-like reef on the south side of Guana Island with walls, small caves, and remarkably approachable fish — an ideal novice dive backed by a beautiful white sand beach.

## Overview

Monkey Point is located on the southern side of Guana Island, a short boat ride from operators on Tortola and Virgin Gorda. The site offers a combination rarely found in a single location: a sandy white beach that divers can use between dives, and calm, clear waters hosting an exceptionally diverse and approachable marine community. The dive is typically described as aquarium-like in character — the fish are so accustomed to divers that some will allow remarkably close approaches. The reef features small walls and rocky formations with caves to explore, creating varied terrain at a shallow, accessible depth. The site is well suited to newly certified divers, snorkellers, and underwater photographers looking for fish portraiture.

## Site Information

- **Location**: South side of Guana Island, BVI
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 9 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: Good; protected location
- **Current**: Low
- **Swell**: Can be moderate (High swell on some days)
- **Best Time**: Year-round; calmer in summer

## Marine Life

Bluestriped grunts and yellowtail snappers are abundant throughout the reef, moving in schools around the rocky formations. Yellow jacks are commonly seen in the water column. Cuttlefish are a notable sighting here — less commonly encountered at other BVI reef sites. Sea turtles frequent the area and are often relaxed in the presence of divers. The fish at Monkey Point are reported to be unusually tolerant of close approaches, with some individuals reportedly allowing contact — though divers should resist touching marine life to protect both the animals and the reef ecosystem.

## Dive Profile

The dive begins with a descent onto the shallow reef adjacent to Guana Island's south shore. Follow the natural reef contour, investigating small walls and rocky formations that drop a few meters before levelling onto sandy patches. Small caves and overhangs appear along the base of the larger formations — peer inside for sleeping fish or lobsters sheltering during the day. The cuttlefish are most often seen hovering near the reef edge. Yellow jacks move in loose schools above the reef crest. At 9 meters maximum, air consumption is low and bottom time can be extended. Finish the dive near the beach for an easy exit and surface interval on the sand.

## Entry and Exit

Access by boat. The site has a sandy beach for surface intervals. Entry and exit via boat or, in calm conditions, potentially from shore near the beach. Conditions are generally calm thanks to the sheltered aspect under Guana Island.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Move slowly and let the fish come to you — the resident population is notably unhurried
- The white sand beach makes this a good site for a relaxed surface interval between dives
- Cuttlefish are more likely to be seen hovering near the reef edge than in the open water
- Excellent for underwater portrait photography given the fish's approachability

## Safety Considerations

Monkey Point is one of the BVI's most accessible dive sites. The shallow depth and calm conditions make it suitable for newly certified divers. Be aware that swell can occasionally be moderate. Always dive with a buddy. Carry a surface marker buoy (SMB) even at shallow sites.

## Photography

Monkey Point rewards photographers at every level. The approachable fish — particularly grunts, snappers, and cuttlefish — allow for frame-filling portraits without a long lens. Wide-angle shots of the small caves and schooling fish capture the aquarium character of the site. Sea turtle encounters provide strong mid-water compositions. Shallow depth means good natural light for most of the dive.
