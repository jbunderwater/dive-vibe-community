## The Invisibles

A pair of submerged pinnacles north of Virgin Gorda that rise to within four feet of the surface with no marker buoy — a navigation hazard for sailors and an exceptional dive for advanced divers seeking current-swept coral, large fish aggregations, and open-ocean encounters.

## Overview

The Invisibles takes its name from a genuine navigational danger: two side-by-side pinnacles north of Virgin Gorda that reach to within approximately 1.2 meters (4 feet) of the surface, yet carry no marker buoy. What makes them hazardous to passing boats makes them outstanding for diving. Strong currents sweep across the pinnacles, carrying nutrients that sustain dense coral and sponge growth, and attracting large volumes of fish. The rock surfaces are heavily honeycombed with ledges and overhangs, and the pinnacles themselves are beautifully decorated with colorful corals and sponges in shades of violet, blue, yellow, pink, and orange. The sandy bottom around the bases sits at approximately 20 meters depth.

## Site Information

- **Location**: North of Virgin Gorda, BVI
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Pinnacle/seamount
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: Good; reduced in strong current conditions
- **Current**: Moderate to strong; variable
- **Swell**: High
- **Best Time**: Calm weather only; unsuitable in rough conditions

## Marine Life

The current-swept pinnacles aggregate large numbers of fish: big schools of horse-eye jacks patrol the complex reef, while dog snappers and permit are regularly encountered in open water around the structure. The ledges and overhangs shelter blackbar soldierfish, glasseye snapper, and fairy basslets. Damselfish, angelfish, and wrasses populate the coral-covered rock faces. The pinnacles' exposure to open-ocean current means larger pelagic visitors are possible.

## Dive Profile

Approach from a mooring or live-boat with the current assessed before descent. The pinnacles are best dived on a slack or moderate current — strong current passes can make the dive extremely challenging. Descend to the top of the pinnacles, which crown at just a few meters depth. Explore the honeycombed ledges and overhangs working down to the base at approximately 20 meters. Horse-eye jacks often circle the pinnacles in large schools mid-water. Watch open water beyond the rock for permit and larger fish. The pinnacles' top is shallow enough that divers can ascend for a safety stop on the structure itself before surfacing. Do not attempt this site in rough or windy weather — conditions deteriorate rapidly with swell.

## Entry and Exit

Access by boat only. The site has no permanent mooring, requiring careful positioning by the boat. In calm conditions, a drift dive approach can work well. Deploy a surface marker buoy (SMB) for safety stop and surface pickup. The lack of a buoy means boats must remain attentive to diver positions.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Only attempt in calm, clear weather — the site is exposed and conditions can change rapidly
- A live-boat with an attentive crew is strongly recommended
- Check current direction before descent; a moderate flow enhances the dive while a strong flow makes it dangerous
- Bring a compass — navigation back from a drift can be challenging

## Safety Considerations

This is one of the more challenging dives in the BVI due to the exposed location, strong currents, and absence of a marker buoy. The site should only be dived by Advanced certified divers with current-diving experience. Always carry a surface marker buoy (SMB) and a whistle or surface signaling device. Dive with a buddy. A live-boat is strongly recommended over a tied-off mooring given the current variability.

## Photography

The combination of colorful pinnacle surfaces, schooling horse-eye jacks, and fairy basslets in the overhangs provides outstanding wide-angle and mid-range photography. The vibrant coral pigmentation — violet, blue, yellow, pink, and orange — makes for striking reef images. Natural light is best in the morning before the sun angle changes. Strobes or video lights improve colour in the deeper overhangs.
