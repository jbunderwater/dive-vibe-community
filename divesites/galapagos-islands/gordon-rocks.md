# Gordon Rocks

The "washing machine" of the Galapagos -- a submerged volcanic crater delivering the most reliable hammerhead encounters in the central islands.

## Overview

Gordon Rocks (locally "La Lavadora") is the eroded remnant of a volcanic crater, now submerged, leaving two pinnacle formations that funnel strong currents and eddies through the gap between them. Widely regarded as the most challenging day-dive in the Galapagos, it earned the nickname "the washing machine" for its intense surge and unpredictable downcurrents. The site is the single best location in the central islands for schooling scalloped hammerhead sharks, which aggregate in the channel and along the outer crater wall. Divers with at least 25 logged dives and prior current-diving experience are recommended before attempting this site. The approach from Puerto Ayora takes approximately 45–60 minutes by day boat.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Approximately 60 minutes northeast of Puerto Ayora, Santa Cruz, Galapagos Islands
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Pinnacle/seamount
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 10–25 meters (seasonal; best in cooler months)
- **Water Temperature**: 20–26°C January–May; 16–20°C June–December
- **Current**: Strong to very strong, with unpredictable eddies and downcurrents
- **Best Time**: June to November for hammerhead schools; year-round diving

## Marine Life

Schools of scalloped hammerhead sharks are the headline attraction, frequently seen cruising through the channel between the pinnacles or patrolling the outer crater wall. Schools of 50 or more are documented during peak months. Scuba Iguana and Academy Bay Diving both confirm hammerhead cleaning stations supported by butterflyfish and bannerfish. Spotted eagle rays glide through in formation. Galapagos sharks and whitetip reef sharks patrol the site. Green sea turtles rest on ledges. Galapagos sea lions weave among divers in the shallows. Large schools of amberjacks and pompano are reported. The rocky substrate hosts king angelfish, hogfish, and moray eels tucked into crevices.

## Dive Profile

Negative entry and rapid descent into the channel between the two rock formations, typically reaching the sandy crater bottom at 30 meters where hammerheads congregate. Divers tuck into rocks on the crater wall and hold position against the current while scanning for shark activity. The outer walls extend considerably deeper -- stay within recreational limits (the spectacle is at the crater level, not in the abyss). The ascent requires timing against surge cycles; safety stops can be challenging due to current and swell near the surface.

## Entry and Exit

Giant stride from a day boat operating out of Puerto Ayora or the Itabaca Channel. Negative entry is standard -- descend immediately on entry with no surface swim. The boat repositions tracking divers' bubbles. Exit by inflating an SMB during safety stop for panga pickup. Surface conditions can be rough with significant swell.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Prior current-diving experience is essential; 25+ logged dives recommended before this site
- Carry a reef hook to hold position on the crater wall in strong current
- Morning departures from Puerto Ayora offer the calmest surface conditions and best hammerhead activity
- Wear a 7mm wetsuit or semi-dry -- thermoclines can drop temperatures to 16–18°C without warning
- Stay close to your guide; separation in these currents is a real risk

## Safety Considerations

This is the most demanding day-dive in the Galapagos and is not suitable for inexperienced divers. Strong and erratic currents create washing-machine conditions that can push divers toward the crater wall or into open water. Cold thermoclines appear suddenly. Surface conditions are frequently rough. Equalize early and often during the rapid negative descent. Downcurrents have been reported in the channel between the pinnacles -- be prepared to kick away from the wall and inflate your BCD. Always carry an SMB, whistle, and mirror. Dive operators may cancel on short notice due to conditions.

## Photography

Wide-angle is essential -- bring the largest dome port available for hammerhead schools against blue water. The channel between the pinnacles creates dramatic silhouette opportunities. Sea lions in the shallows offer close-up wide-angle subjects. Macro is impractical due to surge and currents. Use fast shutter speeds (1/200+) to freeze motion. Morning light penetrating the channel provides the best natural illumination.
