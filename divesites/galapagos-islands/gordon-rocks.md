# Gordon Rocks

The "washing machine" of the Galapagos -- a submerged volcanic crater with four underwater pinnacles delivering the most reliable hammerhead encounters in the central islands.

## Overview

Gordon Rocks is the remains of an eroded tuff cone, with three rocks visible at the surface and four underwater pinnacles between 8 and 30 metres depth. The crater's sandy bottom sits at approximately 32 metres, and the outer walls drop to around 65 metres. The site earns its nickname "the washing machine" for intense, unpredictable currents that funnel between the pinnacles and along the outer wall. This is the single best site in central Galapagos for schooling scalloped hammerhead sharks, which aggregate in the crater and along the outer walls. Advanced Open Water certification and at least 25 logged dives are required by most operators.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Off the northeast coast of Santa Cruz Island, Galapagos Islands
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Pinnacle/seamount
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 10-20 meters (30-65 feet)
- **Current**: Strong to very strong, with unpredictable surge; this is the only Galapagos day-trip site classified as requiring current experience
- **Best Time**: Year-round; hammerheads and cold-season species most reliably June to November

## Marine Life

Schools of scalloped hammerhead sharks are the headline attraction, sometimes numbering in the dozens and occasionally hundreds. Spotted eagle rays glide through in formation. Galapagos sharks patrol the deeper sections. Manta rays have been confirmed at this site. Sunfish (Mola mola) are encountered occasionally, particularly in the cold season. Green sea turtles rest on ledges. Galapagos sea lions weave among divers in the shallows. Golden rays, marble rays, stingrays, whitetip reef sharks, blacktip reef sharks, tuna, and large schools of barracuda round out the action. Reef fish including parrotfish, moray eels, and triggerfish inhabit the pinnacle structures.

## Dive Profile

The dive begins with a negative entry and rapid descent into the crater, typically to 20-30 metres where hammerheads congregate. Divers tuck into the rock pinnacles and hold position against the current while scanning the blue for shark activity. The guide evaluates current direction and visibility upon arrival to determine which pinnacles to use. The ascent requires careful timing, and safety stops can be challenging due to current and swell near the surface.

## Entry and Exit

Giant stride entry from a day boat operating out of Puerto Ayora, Santa Cruz (approximately 25 minutes transit). Negative entry is standard -- full gear before the stride, immediate descent with no surface swim. The boat repositions during the dive to follow divers' bubbles. Exit is by inflating an SMB during the safety stop for panga retrieval. Conditions at the surface can be rough with significant swell.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Master negative entries and have solid current-diving experience before attempting this site
- Carry a reef hook -- holding position on the crater wall in strong current is far easier with one
- Go early in the morning for the calmest surface conditions and best hammerhead activity
- Wear a 7mm wetsuit or semi-dry -- thermoclines can drop temperatures to 16-18 degrees C without warning
- Stay close to your guide and group; separation in these currents is dangerous

## Safety Considerations

This is the most demanding dive available as a day trip from Santa Cruz. Strong and erratic currents create washing-machine conditions. Cold thermoclines can appear suddenly. Surface conditions are frequently rough. Equalize early and often during descent. Always carry an SMB, whistle, and mirror. Dive operators may cancel this site on short notice due to conditions.

## Photography

Wide-angle is essential -- bring the biggest dome port you have for hammerhead schools against the blue water backdrop. The channel between the pinnacles creates dramatic silhouette opportunities with sharks overhead. Sea lions in the shallows offer engaging close-up wide-angle subjects. Macro is impractical due to surge and currents. Shoot on high shutter speed (1/200+) to freeze motion in the turbulent water.
