# Punta Vicente Roca

A cold-water wall dive at Isabela's northwest tip where the Cromwell Current brings mola mola, seahorses, red-lipped batfish, and species found nowhere else in the Galapagos.

## Overview

Punta Vicente Roca occupies the dramatic northwest tip of Isabela Island, where the deep Cromwell Current wells up against the volcanic coastline, bringing cold, nutrient-rich water from the deep Pacific. The site features two coves formed by an eroded volcanic cone, with wall diving from 3-50 metres. This upwelling creates an ecosystem distinct from the rest of the Galapagos, supporting species rarely or never seen at other sites -- including mola mola (ocean sunfish), Pacific seahorses, red-lipped batfish, and frogfish. Multiple ScubaBoard trip reports from 2021-2025 confirm mola mola as abundant and reliably seen here, often in groups, being cleaned by harlequin wrasse and Mexican hogfish. Water temperatures regularly drop to 15-16 degrees C.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Northwest tip of Isabela Island, Galapagos Islands
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: Variable; 6-25 metres (nutrient-rich upwelling reduces clarity in upper water column)
- **Current**: Mild to moderate (calmer than most Galapagos sites)
- **Best Time**: July to October for mola mola; seahorses, batfish, and bullhead sharks year-round

## Marine Life

Mola mola (ocean sunfish) are the star attraction at this site, reliably seen during season at 24-33 metres depth at cleaning stations, where harlequin wrasse and Mexican hogfish service them. Multiple individuals are often present simultaneously. Pacific seahorses cling to rocky outcrops and gorgonians. Red-lipped batfish walk along the sandy bottom on modified pectoral fins. Frogfish hide among the rocky substrate. Green sea turtles are abundant, often congregating on ledges. Galapagos penguins may be seen swimming underwater near the surface. Galapagos fur seals hunt in the area. Bullhead sharks have been confirmed on the reef by divers. Large schools of salema and other baitfish cloud the wall. Barracuda, eagle rays, marble rays, torpedo rays, and stingrays are present.

## Dive Profile

The dive follows the volcanic wall along the base of towering cliffs, typically entering on the sheltered cove side and descending to 15-30 metres. The wall features caves, overhangs, and rocky shelves that harbor seahorses, batfish, and frogfish -- a slow, observant pace rewards careful searching. For mola mola, divers go deeper (24-33 m) to reach the cleaning stations. Unlike the current-swept sites in central Galapagos, Punta Vicente Roca is often relatively calm underwater. The safety stop in the shallows provides opportunities for penguin and fur seal encounters.

## Entry and Exit

Panga from the liveaboard to the entry point along the volcanic cliffs. Giant stride entry into relatively sheltered water on the lee side of the point. The dive drifts along the wall with the mild current. Exit via SMB deployment for panga pickup. Surface conditions are generally calmer than at other Isabela sites due to the sheltered geography of the coves.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Wear the warmest exposure suit you have -- 7mm with hood minimum; many divers use drysuits or 9mm with doubled gloves as water regularly drops to 15-16 degrees C
- Slow down and look carefully at the rocky substrate; seahorses, frogfish, and batfish are cryptic and easy to miss
- Ask your guide specifically about mola mola sighting conditions -- they are most reliable July through October at 24-33 m depth
- Bring both macro and wide-angle setups if possible; this is one of the few Galapagos sites where macro subjects are abundant
- Look in the shallows during your safety stop for penguins -- some of the best encounters happen above 5 metres

## Safety Considerations

Cold water is the primary challenge. Hypothermia risk is real, especially on longer dives or second dives. Water temperatures of 15-16 degrees C demand proper thermal protection. Visibility is often lower than other Galapagos sites due to the nutrient-rich upwelling. The volcanic cliffs above create occasional rockfall -- stay aware of the wall and avoid sheltering directly under unstable overhangs. Carry standard safety equipment including SMB and signaling devices.

## Photography

This is the Galapagos' best macro and critter site. Red-lipped batfish on sandy bottom make extraordinary subjects -- use a macro lens and get low for eye-level portraits. Seahorses on gorgonians require patience and a steady hand. Frogfish reward those who search carefully. For wide-angle, mola mola at cleaning stations in the green-tinted water column are bucket-list shots. Sea turtles stacked on ledges create compelling compositions. Penguins swimming through the shallows offer close-focus wide-angle opportunities. Strobes are essential in the low-visibility, nutrient-rich water to restore colour.
