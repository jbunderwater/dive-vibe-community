## Punta Vicente Roca

Punta Vicente Roca is located on the northwestern tip of Isabela Island — the nose of the seahorse formed by Isabela's distinctive shape. Accessible only by liveaboard, this remote site is internationally recognized as one of the most reliable places in the world to dive with ocean sunfish (Mola mola). The strong cold Cromwell Current that strikes this exposed headland keeps water temperatures among the coldest in the Galápagos.

## Overview

The dive profile follows a sloping volcanic wall punctuated by small sea caves, descending to around 27 meters. A sharp thermocline typically sits at 5–10 meters: the upper water is murky and around 19°C, while below the thermocline temperatures drop to 14°C or lower. Visibility is poor in the shallower turbid zone but improves to 10–20 meters below 12 meters. The cold, nutrient-rich water below the thermocline is where the Mola mola are found, typically at 20–30 meters depth, hovering near the wall while reef fish pick parasites from their skin. This is one of very few places on earth where sunfish can be reliably observed by recreational divers.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Northwest Isabela Island, Galápagos Islands — liveaboard only
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 27 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 3–10 meters in the thermocline; 10–20 meters below 12 meters
- **Water Temperature**: 14–19°C; one of the coldest dive sites in the Galápagos
- **Current**: Some of the strongest in the Galápagos; SMB mandatory
- **Best Time**: July–December for Mola mola; penguins present year-round

## Marine Life

The headline attraction is the Mola mola — specifically Mola ramsayi (southern sunfish), which uses the site as a cleaning station. Groups of 1–10 individuals have been observed at 20–30 meters, assuming a head-up position while king angelfish and Mexican hogfish clean parasites from their skin. Orcas are occasionally sighted here, attracted by the Mola mola. Galápagos penguins dive from rocks near the surface and regularly swim past divers. Sea lions are present and playful. Large shoals of black-striped salemas fill the water column. Additional marine life includes red-lipped batfish, seahorses, frogfish, eagle rays, stingrays, marble rays, barracuda, and sea turtles. Bullhead (horn) sharks rest in crevices. Manta rays and orcas are possible sightings.

## Dive Profile

Descend quickly through the turbid upper layer to reach clear cold water below the thermocline. Plan the dive to spend maximum time at 20–27 meters where Mola mola are encountered. Divers can hover near the wall and wait for sunfish to appear from the deeper blue. Cold water and strong current make thermal protection essential — a thick 7 mm wetsuit or semi-dry is the minimum. An SMB is mandatory due to surface boat traffic from snorkel groups and dinghy activity around the site caves. Ascend through the murky thermocline slowly for a 3-minute safety stop.

## Safety Considerations

This is one of the most challenging dive sites in the Galápagos in terms of cold and current. Water temperature below the thermocline can be 14°C — hypothermia risk is real on repeat dives without adequate thermal protection. Current can be very strong and unpredictable; do not separate from the group. Orca sightings, while spectacular, are rare enough that they should not alter dive behavior. Always deploy SMB during ascent.

## Photography

Punta Vicente Roca is a premier underwater photography destination. Mola mola at cleaning stations offer extraordinary wide-angle and medium-range subjects — approach slowly from below to avoid startling them. Penguins entering from the surface make fast-moving silhouette shots. Red-lipped batfish are superb macro subjects on the cave floors. The wall itself, draped in invertebrates, provides colorful reef compositions.
