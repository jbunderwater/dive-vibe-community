# Mosquera

A sandy islet between North Seymour and Baltra where hundreds of garden eels carpet the sandy platform and a large sea lion colony turns every dive into playful chaos.

## Overview

Mosquera is a low, flat islet formed by volcanic uplift, sitting in the channel between North Seymour and Baltra. The dive site features a rocky eastern shoreline dropping to a sand platform at 12 meters, continuing down to around 25 meters. Scuba Iguana describes a blacktip shark cleaning station on the reef, where sharks circle while butterfly fish tend to them. The site is one of the most accessible dives in the central Galapagos — described as "well-suited for beginners" despite moderate-to-strong currents — and is commonly paired with North Seymour as a full-day excursion from Santa Cruz.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Islet between North Seymour and Baltra Islands, central Galapagos
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 25 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 10-20 meters
- **Current**: Moderate to strong
- **Best Time**: Year-round; sea lions always present

## Marine Life

Hundreds of garden eels occupy the sandy platform, waving from their burrows as far as visibility allows — one of the densest garden eel colonies in the Galapagos. A blacktip shark cleaning station is the standout feature, where blacktip sharks are serviced by butterfly fish at a fixed point on the reef. Galapagos sharks, whitetip reef sharks, and hammerhead sharks have all been confirmed at this site. Eagle rays and golden rays cruise the sand flats; mobula rays appear seasonally. Sea lions from the resident colony are playful and engage divers throughout the dive. Green sea turtles, bonito tuna, parrotfish, groupers, snappers, surgeonfish, and barracuda round out a diverse reef community.

## Dive Profile

The rocky topography on the eastern edge descends to 12 meters where it transitions to a broad sand platform. The garden eel colonies spread across this sandy zone. Deeper sand continues to around 25 meters on the seaward side. The cleaning station is a fixed point where guides know to look for shark activity. Most divers spend the majority of their time on the sandy platform between 10-18 meters. Sea lion interactions are most frequent in the shallower zones near the islet's rocky edges.

## Entry and Exit

Panga from a day boat or liveaboard. Giant stride entry. Current can run moderate to strong, requiring some drift-diving positioning. Exit via panga pickup.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Approach garden eel colonies slowly and low to avoid collapsing the colony — they retract instantly at sudden movement
- Spend time at the cleaning station waiting for shark activity
- Sea lions are most energetic in the early morning; afternoon dives often find them hauled out resting
- Carry full safety gear including SMB; current can carry divers from the entry point

## Safety Considerations

Currents are moderate to strong and can intensify during tidal changes. Standard Galapagos equipment and thermal protection (5-7mm wetsuit) apply. Bull sea lions can be territorial during breeding season (June-September) — maintain distance from large males.

## Photography

The garden eel colony requires patience: approach slowly, settle to the sand level, and allow the eels to re-emerge. Wide-angle captures the colony's scale; macro captures individual portraits. The cleaning station provides predictable shark activity with a fixed focal point. Sea lion interactions reward wide-angle fisheye setups. Good natural light on the sandy platform reduces the need for strobes at mid-day.
