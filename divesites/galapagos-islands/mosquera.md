# Mosquera

A sandy channel between North Seymour and Baltra where a large sea lion colony turns every dive into an underwater circus of playful interactions.

## Overview

Mosquera is a sand and rock islet in the channel between North Seymour and Baltra Islands, home to one of the largest sea lion colonies in the central Galapagos. The dive site features a sandy bottom with scattered rock formations and a blacktip shark cleaning station along the reef profile, creating a relatively accessible environment compared to the Galapagos' more demanding current-swept sites. The main attraction is the sea lion colony -- dozens of animals that treat divers as entertainment, swooping, spinning, and blowing bubbles at close range. The sandy substrate also supports garden eels (including the endemic Galapagos garden eel), rays, and reef sharks.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Channel between North Seymour and Baltra Islands, Galapagos Islands
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 27 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 10-20 meters (30-65 feet)
- **Current**: Moderate to strong (variable)
- **Best Time**: Year-round; sea lions are always present

## Marine Life

Galapagos sea lions dominate this site, with juveniles and adults alike engaging with divers in extended, playful encounters. Galapagos garden eels -- an endemic species -- sway from the sandy bottom in large colonies, disappearing into their burrows as divers approach. Hammerhead sharks, Galapagos sharks, and blacktip reef sharks are regularly encountered; there is a blacktip cleaning station along the reef. Eagle rays and golden rays cruise over the sand flats. Whitetip reef sharks rest under rock ledges. Schools of reef fish including parrotfish, surgeonfish, barracuda, and snappers congregate around the scattered rock formations. Green sea turtles graze on algae. Stingrays and mobulas pass through the channel.

## Dive Profile

The dive is relatively straightforward by Galapagos standards. Entry leads to a sandy slope descending to 20-27 metres, with the sandy platform and rock formations providing structure and marine life concentration points. The endemic Galapagos garden eel colonies are found on the sandy flats between rock formations. Most of the sea lion interaction happens in the 5-12 metre range. Safety stop is easily performed in the calm shallows.

## Entry and Exit

Short panga ride from a day boat or liveaboard anchored in the channel. Giant stride entry into relatively calm water. The sheltered channel location means surface conditions are usually manageable. Exit via direct panga pickup, or SMB deployment if current has carried divers from the entry point.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Spend the majority of your dive time in the shallows (5-12 metres) where sea lion interactions are most frequent and prolonged
- Approach garden eel colonies very slowly and low to the bottom -- they retract quickly if startled
- Despite the calmer conditions, this is still the Galapagos -- carry full safety equipment including SMB and signaling devices
- Morning dives tend to have the most active sea lions; by afternoon they often haul out to rest on the islet
- Resist the urge to touch or chase the sea lions; staying still and letting them approach produces the best encounters

## Safety Considerations

While this is one of the gentler Galapagos dive sites, currents in the channel can be moderate to strong and can pick up unexpectedly, especially during tidal changes. The Advanced rating reflects Galapagos conditions overall -- water temperatures of 18-24 degrees C, distance from medical facilities, and the potential for conditions to shift mid-dive. Bull sea lions can be territorial, especially during breeding season (June-September); maintain distance from large males and follow your guide's direction. Standard thermal protection (5-7mm) is required.

## Photography

Sea lion portraits and interaction shots are the main photographic draw. Use a wide-angle or fisheye lens and get close -- the sea lions will oblige by approaching to within centimetres. Eye-level shots produce the most engaging portraits. Galapagos garden eel colonies make excellent macro or mid-range subjects, but require patience and a slow, low approach. The sandy bottom provides a clean, uncluttered background for ray and shark shots.
