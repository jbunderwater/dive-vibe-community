## Mosquera

Mosquera is a small flat islet formed by a volcanic uprising between North Seymour and Baltra Islands. It hosts one of the largest Galápagos sea lion colonies in the archipelago. The dive site divides into two areas — North Mosquera and South Mosquera — with notably different topographies on each side of the islet.

## Overview

North Mosquera features a sandy bottom at around 10 meters colonized by garden eels, with a rock reef zone connecting to the Seymour Channel where whitetip reef sharks rest. South Mosquera offers a big reef at 5 meters leading to an inclining ravine at 18 meters, with black coral and molluscs, continuing into a submarine canyon with pelagic species at 21–27 meters. The outer reef and sandy platform attract large aggregations of eagle rays and, on occasion, schooling hammerheads. Currents are generally moderate at this site relative to exposed Galápagos locations, making Mosquera a popular choice for intermediate divers and as a warm-up dive.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Between North Seymour and Baltra Island, Galápagos
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Coral reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 27 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 7–15 meters; drops in the Humboldt season
- **Water Temperature**: 18–26°C depending on season; 65–80°F
- **Current**: Moderate to strong; stronger in the Seymour Channel zone
- **Best Time**: December–May for visibility; June–November for pelagic encounters

## Marine Life

Sea lions are the defining feature of any Mosquera dive — a large colony resides on the islet beach, and individuals regularly join divers underwater, performing barrel rolls and mock charges before darting away. The garden eel field on the North Mosquera sandy bottom is a distinctive feature. The reef zone hosts whitetip reef sharks, blacktip reef sharks, and Galápagos sharks at cleaning stations. Eagle rays school over the sandy platform. With luck, hammerheads pass through from the direction of Gordon Rocks. Stingrays, spotted eagle rays, golden cownose rays, and mobula rays are all recorded. Green sea turtles, barracuda, Galápagos eels, and diverse reef fish complete the picture. Occasional orca sightings have been reported near Mosquera, attracted by the sea lion colony.

## Dive Profile

Typically divided into North or South Mosquera based on conditions. North: descend to the sandy bottom at 10 meters, observe the garden eel field, then move along the rock reef into the Seymour Channel zone where currents concentrate fish. South: begin on the 5-meter reef platform, descend along the ravine to 18–21 meters, and follow the canyon margin for pelagic encounters. Sea lions accompany most of the dive regardless of which section is chosen.

## Safety Considerations

Currents at Mosquera are generally manageable but can intensify in the Seymour Channel section without warning. The submarine canyon at 21–27 meters requires careful depth monitoring. Despite being one of the more accessible Galápagos sites, divers should be comfortable with current. Advanced certification is required — currents can intensify in the Seymour Channel zone without warning. Orca presence in the area is occasionally reported; this is no cause for alarm.

## Photography

Sea lion interactions are the primary draw for photographers — their curiosity and speed make for unpredictable but rewarding wide-angle images. The garden eel colony on the sandy flat is a classic shot with patience. Eagle ray formations over the sandy platform offer wide-angle opportunities. Reef cleaning station scenes near the whitetip shark resting areas reward patient composition.
