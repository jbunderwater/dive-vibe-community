# Mosquera

A sandy channel between North Seymour and Baltra where a massive sea lion colony and thousands of garden eels share a drift-and-wall dive over volcanic rock and sand.

## Overview

Mosquera is a flat lava islet created by volcanic uplift, sitting in the channel between North Seymour and Baltra. The dive descends over rocky topography to a sandy platform at 15–18 meters, then meets a wall dropping into deeper water. Two distinct dive sites are typically worked on a day trip: a north site (Mosquera North) and a south site. The sandy bottom hosts what Scuba Iguana describes as a "cleaning station of blacktip sharks" -- dozens of whitetip and blacktip reef sharks congregate here reliably. The east side of the islet has one of the larger sea lion colonies in the central Galapagos, with animals regularly joining divers in the water. Currents are medium to strong, making this a more demanding dive than its proximity to Baltra might suggest.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Channel between North Seymour and Baltra Islands, Galapagos Islands
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 25 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 12–15 meters (40–50 feet)
- **Current**: Moderate to strong
- **Best Time**: Year-round; sea lions always present; eagle rays and golden rays year-round

## Marine Life

Whitetip and blacktip reef sharks are the most reliable large-animal encounter, using the sandy platform as a cleaning station. Garden eels emerge in large colonies from the sandy bottom between rock formations. Golden cownose rays have been observed in schools of 15 or more. Spotted eagle rays and stingrays cruise over the sand. Galapagos sea lions enter the water from the eastern colony and interact with divers. Green sea turtles, barracuda, king angelfish, blue and gold snappers, razor surgeonfish, and a variety of groupers and parrotfish populate the rocky sections. Mobula rays and Galapagos eels round out the site's cast.

## Dive Profile

Entry from the panga leads to a descent over rocky topography to the sand platform at 15–18 meters. The wall section drops below from the platform edge. Garden eel colonies occupy the sandy flats between rock formations; approach slowly from a low angle or they retract. The richest sea lion encounters occur in the shallows near the islet, typically on the ascent. Currents can be moderate to strong -- this is a drift-and-wall dive rather than a mellow sandy-bottom soak.

## Entry and Exit

Short panga ride from a day boat (approximately 25 minutes from the Itabaca Channel on Santa Cruz's north side) or liveaboard anchored in the channel. Giant stride entry into the current. Medium to strong currents mean the panga should track divers' bubbles. Exit via SMB deployment or direct panga pickup.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Spend time on the ascent in the shallows (5–12 meters) for the best sea lion interactions
- Approach garden eel colonies very slowly and low -- they retract quickly if startled
- Look specifically at the sandy platform for the shark cleaning station -- reef sharks often rest or circle in numbers here
- Morning dives tend to have the most active sea lions; by afternoon many haul out on the islet
- Carry full Galapagos safety kit (SMB, whistle, mirror) -- currents can carry you from the entry point

## Safety Considerations

Currents in the channel can pick up unexpectedly, especially during tidal changes. The Advanced rating reflects the current exposure, Galapagos water temperatures (18–24°C), and distance from medical facilities. Bull sea lions can be territorial during breeding season (June–September); follow your guide's direction and maintain distance from large males. Standard thermal protection (5–7mm wetsuit) is required.

## Photography

Reef shark aggregations at the cleaning station are the stand-out photographic subject -- wide-angle from a low position on the sandy bottom. Garden eel colonies require patience and a slow approach; macro or mid-range setups work well. Golden ray schools make outstanding wide-angle subjects. Sea lion portraits and interaction shots reward a fisheye lens and close approach. The sandy bottom provides a clean, uncluttered background for ray and shark shots.
