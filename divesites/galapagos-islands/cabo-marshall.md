# Cabo Marshall

The Galapagos' premier manta ray encounter -- a volcanic wall and plateau on Isabela's northeast coast where giant mantas and mobula rays converge on a cleaning station at 12 meters.

## Overview

Cabo Marshall sits on the remote northeast coast of Isabela Island, accessible only by liveaboard. A shallow underwater plateau at approximately 12 meters serves as a cleaning station for manta rays and mobula rays, with cleaner wrasse attending to passing pelagics. LiveAboard.com and Starfish.ch both confirm this plateau as the functional cleaning station, and the wall below it drops to 40 meters and beyond. The Portuguese Traveler describes the prevailing current as flowing from south to north with highly variable strength -- it is this plankton-laden current that draws the mantas and sharks to the headland. No land visits are permitted. Dive duration typically runs 50–60 minutes depending on depth.

Note: The earlier description of marine iguanas grazing in the shallows has been removed -- no source consulted for this specific site confirms marine iguana activity at Cabo Marshall's dive site. Marine iguana diving is best documented at Cabo Douglas (Fernandina) and Daphne Minor is not confirmed either.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Northeast coast of Isabela Island, Galapagos Islands
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 40 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 10–20 meters
- **Current**: Variable, often strong; prevailing south-to-north flow
- **Best Time**: Year-round; manta encounters most reliable October–January and June–November

## Marine Life

Giant oceanic manta rays are the star attraction, visiting the plateau cleaning station where cleaner wrasse remove parasites. Multiple mantas may be present simultaneously. Mobula rays travel in schools and are regular visitors. Scalloped hammerhead sharks school in deeper water off the wall. Galapagos sharks cruise the wall edge. Spotted eagle rays and golden rays pass through in formation. Sea turtles are present. Whitetip reef sharks, barracuda, yellowfin tuna, and large schools of endemic black-striped salemas populate the wall. Sea lions are present.

## Dive Profile

Panga delivers divers to the entry point based on current reading. The descent leads to the shallow plateau at approximately 12 meters -- the primary zone for manta ray encounters. When mantas are at the cleaning station, the technique is to settle low on the plateau or rock face, remain still, and let the rays circle without disturbance. The wall drops to 40 meters and beyond; recreational divers should stay in the 12–22 meter range where the action concentrates. Exit via SMB deployment for panga pickup.

## Entry and Exit

Accessible exclusively by liveaboard; no permitted land visits. Panga drop-off positioned upstream. Giant stride or back roll entry. Negative entry may be required in stronger current. The panga tracks bubbles. Surface conditions can be rough on Isabela's exposed northeast coast.

## Tips and Recommendations

- When mantas are at the cleaning station, stay low and still -- they circle back repeatedly if undisturbed; any movement or chasing will drive them away
- Never reach toward or touch mantas; let them set the terms of the encounter
- Carry a reef hook to secure yourself to the wall while watching the station in current
- Wear a 7mm wetsuit -- Isabela's northeast coast receives cold upwelling currents
- Conditions at this exposed headland can prevent diving -- be flexible with itinerary planning

## Safety Considerations

Strong and variable currents are the primary hazard. Open-ocean swell and wind make surface conditions unpredictable. Downcurrents along the wall can pull divers deeper -- monitor depth and be prepared to ascend away from the wall. The remote liveaboard-only location means long evacuation times. Always carry an SMB, whistle, and mirror.

## Photography

Mantas at cleaning stations offer world-class wide-angle photography. Position below and to the side of the station with a fisheye or ultra-wide lens, shooting upward to silhouette the rays against surface light. Strobes fill the manta's white belly markings. Mobula ray schools in mid-water are outstanding subjects when numbers are high. Hammerhead schools off the deeper wall require fast shutter speeds (1/200+).
