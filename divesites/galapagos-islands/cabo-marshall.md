# Cabo Marshall

The Galapagos' premier manta ray encounter -- a volcanic wall on Isabela's northeast coast where giant mantas and mobula rays circle a shallow cleaning station plateau above a wall that drops beyond 40 metres.

## Overview

Cabo Marshall sits on the remote northeast coast of Isabela Island, accessible only by liveaboard. This volcanic wall dive is widely considered one of the best sites in the Galapagos for manta ray encounters, with giant manta rays and schools of mobula rays regularly visiting cleaning stations at a shallow plateau around 12 metres. The nutrient-rich current -- flowing generally from south to north -- brings large concentrations of plankton and sustains the cleaning station ecosystem. Below the plateau the wall drops to beyond 40 metres into deep blue water.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Northeast coast of Isabela Island, Galapagos Islands
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 40 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 6-20 metres (variable; nutrient-rich water can reduce visibility to 3-6 m)
- **Current**: Moderate to strong; direction variable but predominantly south to north
- **Best Time**: Year-round; October commonly cited for best conditions

## Marine Life

Giant manta rays and mobula rays are the headline attraction, visiting the cleaning station plateau at around 12 metres. Schools of mobulas have been described as numbering 50 or more individuals. Hammerhead sharks are a common sighting. Galapagos sharks and whitetip sharks cruise the wall. Eagle rays and golden rays pass through. Barracuda form large schools. Sea lions join dives opportunistically. Sunfish (mola mola) have been reported at this site. Sea turtles are present. Large schools of endemic salema sweep past the wall in dense formations.

## Dive Profile

The dive is conducted as a drift along the northeast Isabela coastline. The primary target is the cleaning station plateau at approximately 12 metres, where divers settle low against the wall and allow the rays to circle overhead undisturbed. Below the plateau the wall descends to 40 metres and beyond -- operators recommend staying at or above the plateau level to maximise time at the cleaning station and manage depth. The dive ends with an ascent along the wall and an open-water safety stop.

## Entry and Exit

Panga from the liveaboard delivers divers upstream of the wall entry point. Giant stride or back roll entry. Negative entry may be required depending on current strength. The panga follows bubbles during the drift. Exit via SMB deployment for panga pickup at the downstream end. Surface conditions can be rough due to the exposed northeast location and high swell.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Stay on the plateau at 12 metres rather than descending the deep wall -- all the manta activity is in this zone
- When mantas or mobulas are at the cleaning station, stay low against the wall and remain still; they will circle back repeatedly if undisturbed
- Never chase or reach toward mantas; quiet hovering on the wall produces the closest encounters
- Carry a reef hook to hold position against the current while watching the cleaning station
- Wear a 7mm wetsuit; cold water (approximately 16 degrees C) is common at this exposed northeast location
- Visibility can be poor in nutrient-rich conditions -- adjust expectations but know that this is the same upwelling that brings the rays

## Safety Considerations

Strong and variable currents are the primary hazard. The wall drops well beyond recreational limits -- depth awareness and constant monitoring are essential. High swell on the surface makes pickup conditions challenging. Cold thermoclines are common. The remote liveaboard-only location means medical facilities are a long way off. Carry an SMB, whistle, and mirror. Dive guides may cancel this site on short notice if swell or current make conditions unsafe.

## Photography

Mantas and mobulas at the cleaning station plateau offer world-class wide-angle photography. Position yourself below and to the side of the station with a fisheye or ultra-wide lens, shooting upward to silhouette the rays against the surface light. Strobes help fill the white belly markings. Schools of mobulas swirling around divers create exceptional mid-water compositions. Large barracuda schools above the wall make strong silhouette subjects. The variable visibility can actually work in favour of atmospheric wide-angle shots.
