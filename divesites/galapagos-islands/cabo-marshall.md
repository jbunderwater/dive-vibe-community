## Cabo Marshall

Cabo Marshall (Cape Marshall) is on the northeast coast of Isabela Island, in the shadow of Wolf Volcano — which erupted as recently as 2015. There are no land visits permitted at this remote headland; it is accessible only from a liveaboard dive boat. The dive follows the edge of a lava stream from Wolf Volcano that stopped abruptly at the ocean, forming a near-vertical wall of volcanic rock.

## Overview

The underwater terrain is a steep volcanic slope with boulders and lava ridges, sparsely covered with black coral. A plateau at approximately 12 meters serves as an important cleaning station for manta rays and other large rays. The cold Cromwell Current strikes Isabela's west and north coasts here, bringing water temperatures down to 10–18°C — challenging conditions that require a full 7 mm wetsuit, but also the nutrient upwelling that draws manta rays, hammerheads, and mola mola to this isolated site. Mobula rays are frequently seen breaching from the surface while the liveaboard is underway near the headland.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Northeast Isabela Island, Galápagos — liveaboard only, no day boats
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 22 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 3–15 meters; highly variable
- **Water Temperature**: 10–18°C; one of the coldest sites in the Galápagos
- **Current**: Strong and variable; dive direction changes depending on prevailing current
- **Best Time**: December–May for manta rays; June–November for hammerheads and whale sharks

## Marine Life

The plateau cleaning station at 12 meters attracts manta rays, which hover in the current to be serviced by attending fish. Scalloped hammerhead sharks school in the blue water beyond the wall. Galápagos sharks and whitetip reef sharks patrol the wall face. Mola mola (sunfish) are possible visitors. Large endemic schools of black-striped salema swirl around the boulders. King angelfish, yellow-tailed surgeonfish, jacks, and Galápagos grunt inhabit the rocky terrain. Marine iguanas — the largest individuals found anywhere in the Galápagos — feed on green algae in the shallows at the water's edge and are sometimes seen diving. Sea lions, Galápagos penguins, and flightless cormorants are present above the waterline. Seahorses have been found in rock crevices in the shallows. In June–November, whale sharks have been recorded passing the site.

## Dive Profile

The dive direction along the wall is determined by the prevailing current on arrival — operators assess conditions and brief accordingly. Typically a drift dive north-to-south or south-to-north along the wall. Spend time on the cleaning-station plateau waiting for mantas, then follow the wall deeper. The extreme cold demands a pre-planned dive time and conservative air management — cold significantly increases consumption rates. Reef hook recommended for holding station on the plateau in current.

## Safety Considerations

Water as cold as 10°C has been recorded at Cabo Marshall — hypothermia on back-to-back dives is a genuine risk. A 7 mm wetsuit, hood, and gloves are essential. Currents are strong and can reverse direction. Visibility can drop to 3 meters, reducing the effectiveness of buddy contact — stay close. This site requires an experienced Advanced diver; it is not suitable for newly certified divers or those uncomfortable with cold and surge.

## Photography

Cabo Marshall offers dramatic wide-angle subjects: manta rays cruising the plateau, schooling hammerheads in the blue, and the stark volcanic wall itself. Marine iguanas in the shallows and at the surface edge provide topside and underwater portrait opportunities. Cold water reduces working time — prioritize compositions during the briefing and arrive at depth with a clear shooting plan.
