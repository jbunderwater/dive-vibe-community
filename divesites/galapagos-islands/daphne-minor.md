## Daphne Minor

Daphne Minor is one of the Daphne islets located approximately 30 minutes from the Itabaca Channel, north of Santa Cruz. The islet is a collapsed tuff cone and the dive site combines vertical wall sections with pinnacle formations and a small cave — making it one of the more varied topographies among the central island sites.

## Overview

The dive begins at a small cave where whitetip sharks rest, then moves along the outer wall and over a rocky bottom with benthic organisms covering the surface in a multi-colored layer. A prominent pinnacle draws a diversity of rays, Galápagos sharks, turtles, and reef fish. The wall and pinnacle sections are draped in black coral. Currents are typically moderate, making this an accessible site relative to more exposed Galápagos locations, while still producing encounters with sharks and rays from the blue water. An average depth of 15 meters suits intermediate and advanced divers.

## Site Information

- **Location**: The Daphne islets, north of Santa Cruz Island, Galápagos
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 7–12 meters; better in the warm season
- **Water Temperature**: 18–26°C depending on season
- **Current**: Moderate; can strengthen on the open wall section
- **Best Time**: June–December for manta rays and mola mola; year-round for sharks and turtles

## Marine Life

The cave at the start of the dive shelters resting whitetip reef sharks. Playful Galápagos sea lions regularly interact with divers throughout the dive. Giant Pacific seahorses are found around black coral branches on the wall. The pinnacle hosts Galápagos sharks, spotted eagle rays, green sea turtles, barracuda, and schools of salema. During the cold season (June–December), giant manta rays pass through and mola mola (ocean sunfish) are possible sightings. Colorful benthic invertebrates — sponges, tunicates, and black coral — cover the wall face.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the cave entry at 5–8 meters and observe the resting whitetip sharks before moving along the wall. Follow the wall and pinnacle topography from 8 to 20 meters, monitoring depth to avoid unintentional drift into deeper water. Return along shallower sections and ascend on the sheltered side of the islet for a comfortable safety stop.

## Safety Considerations

Wall diving here can lead to gradual depth creep — monitor depth gauges regularly. Moderate current can intensify around the pinnacle and open wall sections. Carry an SMB. The site requires Advanced certification — currents can intensify on the open wall and pinnacle sections, and Galápagos conditions demand solid buoyancy control and current-diving experience.

## Photography

The cave entry provides a classic silhouette opportunity — sharks framed against the lighter water beyond. Seahorses on black coral are a popular macro subject requiring patience and a steady approach. The multi-colored benthic crust on the wall rewards close wide-angle shooting. Sea lion interactions throughout the dive provide fast-moving action opportunities.
