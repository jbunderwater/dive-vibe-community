# Daphne Minor

An "unjustly little-known" tuff cone wall dive northwest of Santa Cruz, featuring a small shark cave, abundant black coral, and seasonal manta ray encounters.

## Overview

Daphne Minor is a tuff cone (compressed volcanic ash) island sitting off the north coast of Santa Cruz. Its underwater flanks drop in a mixed topography of platforms, small caves, gorgonians, and deep walls covered in black coral. The site is described by Scuba Iguana — whose guides know it well — as a little-known gem that groups typically have entirely to themselves. The main seasonal attractions are giant manta rays in winter (June-December) and hammerhead schools in summer (January-May). A small cave on one side of the island is a resting spot for whitetip sharks.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Off the north coast of Santa Cruz Island, northwest of Baltra, Galapagos
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 10-18 meters (plankton often present, reducing visibility but attracting rays)
- **Current**: Moderate to strong
- **Best Time**: June-December for giant manta rays; January-May for hammerhead schools

## Marine Life

Giant manta rays are the headline encounter from June to December, drawn by the plankton-rich water around the island. Schools of hammerhead sharks and schooling rays are more common January to May. Whitetip reef sharks rest in the small cave on the island's flank. Juvenile Galapagos sharks patrol the wall and platform areas. Stingrays, mobulas, and eagle rays cruise past. Sea lions from the Daphne colony are playful and interactive. Green sea turtles rest on ledges. Barracuda, trumpet fish, parrotfish, bonito tuna, and a variety of reef fish populate the wall. Black coral trees and gorgonians encrust the deep wall sections. Seahorses have been confirmed at the site. No site-specific sources confirm marine iguana feeding activity at Daphne Minor; that behavior is characteristic of Fernandina and other western islands.

## Dive Profile

Two distinct faces: one side features even platforms with small caves; the other drops as a deep wall. The platform side is shallower and ideal for observing ray aggregations and sea lion interactions. The wall face has abundant black corals and gorgonians at depth. The dive typically begins on the platform side and transitions to the wall depending on current direction. Maximum practical depth is 20 meters.

## Entry and Exit

Panga from day boat or liveaboard. Entry on the lee side of the island. The dive follows the island's contour with the current. Exit via SMB deployment for panga pickup.

## Tips and Recommendations

- This site is rarely crowded — one of the few central Galapagos sites where you are likely to have the dive to yourself
- Check which season you are diving in and ask your guide whether to prioritize the platform (mantas, rays) or the wall (black coral, cave)
- Look carefully in the cave for whitetip sharks — they can be easy to miss in low visibility
- Plankton in the water is normal and expected; it brings the rays

## Safety Considerations

Moderate to strong currents. The wall drops further than the 20-meter recreational limit — maintain depth awareness. Cold water (18-22°C) requires proper thermal protection. Carry standard Galapagos safety equipment including SMB and signaling devices.

## Photography

The deep wall covered in black coral creates dramatic wide-angle scenes even in reduced visibility. Manta rays approaching out of the plankton-rich water create ethereal photographic opportunities — shoot slightly upward to silhouette them. Sea lions on the platform make reliable wide-angle subjects. Seahorses on the wall require careful searching and macro technique. Use strobes to restore color against the dark volcanic rock.
