# Daphne Minor

A volcanic tuff cone wall dive north of Santa Cruz, encircled by a dramatic black-coral-draped wall and populated by whitetip sharks, sea lions, eagle rays, and rich macro life.

## Overview

Daphne Minor is a barren volcanic tuff cone rising about 120 metres above the sea, located roughly 40 minutes northwest of the Itabaca Channel. Its steep flanks drop underwater into two distinct dive environments: one side features even platforms with small caves where whitetip reef sharks rest, while the other drops into a deep wall draped in black coral and gorgonians. Moderate to strong currents sweep around the island, concentrating plankton and attracting a variety of reef and pelagic species. The site is dived as a day trip or liveaboard stop.

## Site Information

- **Location**: North of Santa Cruz Island, Galapagos Islands (approximately 40 minutes northwest of Itabaca Channel)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 10-18 meters (30-60 feet)
- **Current**: Moderate to strong
- **Best Time**: Year-round

## Marine Life

Galapagos sea lions patrol and play around the island. Whitetip reef sharks rest in the small cave at the start of the dive and in wall crevices. Stingrays and mobula rays pass through. Spotted eagle rays cruise along the wall edge. Green sea turtles are present. Schools of bonito, parrotfish, trumpetfish, and barracuda populate the water column. Macro subjects include Pacific seahorses and Galapagos blennies in the gorgonians and rocky substrate. Groupers, snappers, frogfish, and moray eels round out the site's biodiversity. The wall face on the deeper side is decorated with black coral trees and a variety of sponges.

*Note: Marine iguanas are not confirmed at this dive site. Iguana diving is associated with Cabo Douglas (Fernandina) and is not reported at Daphne Minor by local operators.*

## Dive Profile

The dive begins with entry on the lee side, descending past a small cave where whitetip sharks commonly rest. The platform side of the island features steps and ledges with dense benthic life. The wall side drops deeper, with black coral beginning around 15 metres. Divers typically drift around the island with the current, covering both topographies on a single dive. The shallow reef top provides a good safety stop location. Maximum depth is around 20 metres on the platform side; the wall continues deeper but staying at 20 metres covers the main attractions.

## Entry and Exit

Panga from liveaboard or day boat to the lee side entry point. Giant stride entry near the wall. The dive follows the current around the island. Exit via SMB deployment for panga pickup on the downstream side. Moderate-to-strong currents require the panga to track divers' bubbles throughout.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Look inside the small cave at the start of the dive for resting whitetip sharks -- this is one of the site's signature features
- The black coral wall offers excellent wide-angle reef photography; a strobe helps restore colour in the dark water
- Macro subjects including seahorses and blennies reward slow, careful searching along the gorgonians
- Currents can accelerate at the corners of the island -- stay close to the wall if conditions pick up
- Ask your guide which side has better visibility on the day; conditions can differ between the two faces

## Safety Considerations

Moderate to strong currents are the primary consideration. The volcanic rock is sharp and can damage equipment -- maintain buoyancy control near the wall. Cold water (18-22°C) accelerates air consumption and requires proper thermal protection (5-7mm wetsuit). Standard Galapagos remoteness considerations apply. Carry an SMB, whistle, and mirror. Currents can accelerate at the island's corners; stay close to the wall if conditions intensify.

## Photography

The black coral wall makes an exceptional wide-angle backdrop -- use a strobe to restore the deep-water colour of the coral and gorgonians. Sea lions circling the island provide dynamic wide-angle subjects. Macro subjects including seahorses and Galapagos blennies in the gorgonians reward patience and a dedicated macro lens. The cave at the start of the dive, with resting whitetip sharks, is a striking environmental portrait subject. Shoot in the shallows during the safety stop for best ambient light.
