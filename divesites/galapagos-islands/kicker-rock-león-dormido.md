# Kicker Rock (León Dormido)

Kicker Rock (León Dormido — "Sleeping Lion") is one of the Galápagos Islands' most iconic dive sites, located approximately 1.5 hours by boat northeast of Puerto Baquerizo Moreno on San Cristóbal Island. The formation is the eroded remnant of a vertical tuff cone rising 153 metres above the surface, split by a narrow channel that forms the primary dive corridor.

## Overview

The twin rock pillars and the channel between them create a dramatic underwater environment where pelagic species converge, drawn by the nutrient-rich currents that sweep through the passage. The walls plunge vertically from the surface to depths of 30 metres and beyond at the base of the outer walls. The channel between the two faces averages around 18 metres and is the most sheltered section. Diving here is suited to intermediate and advanced divers due to variable currents, particularly in the open water beyond the channel. Marine life abundance peaks from May to November when cooler, nutrient-dense water creates more active feeding conditions.

## Site Information

- **Location**: San Cristóbal Island, Galápagos, Ecuador — approximately 17 km northeast of Puerto Baquerizo Moreno
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 10-25 meters
- **Current**: Variable; calm in the channel, strong in open water beyond the pillars
- **Best Time**: May to November for peak marine life activity

## Marine Life

Whitetip reef sharks and Galápagos sharks are commonly encountered throughout the dive. Scalloped hammerhead sharks occasionally pass through, especially in deeper water on the outside of the formation. Spotted eagle rays and golden rays are regularly sighted. Green sea turtles are present year-round. Sea lions often join divers near the surface. Barracuda, moray eels, octopus, parrotfish, yellowtail surgeonfish, Mexican hogfish, sergeant major fish, pufferfish, hawkfish, and rainbow wrasse inhabit the wall formations. Marine iguanas have been reported in the area. Above water, blue-footed boobies, masked boobies, and frigatebirds nest on the cliff ledges.

## Dive Profile

Day trips typically conduct two dives covering different sections of the formation. The channel dive takes divers through the narrow passage between the two pillars, averaging 18 metres, in the most sheltered conditions. Wall dives on the exterior face reach 25-30 metres where shark encounters are more frequent. Guides assess current direction before entry and select the approach accordingly.

## Entry and Exit

All dives are by guided day trip from Puerto Baquerizo Moreno. Tours include two dives. Divers enter directly from the tour boat; there is no shore access. Currents in the channel can be strong and direction-reversing, making dive timing critical — guides assess conditions before entry.

## Safety Considerations

Currents are variable and require experience. The channel is generally calmer than the exposed outer walls, but flow can intensify or reverse with the tide. Strong surface conditions are common from June to November. Stay close to the wall and be prepared for surge. Advanced certification or at minimum solid Intermediate experience is expected. Always deploy an SMB for exit.

## Photography

The vertical tuff walls create dramatic silhouette opportunities, particularly looking up through the channel toward the surface. Wide-angle is the primary choice. Eagle rays against the blue water and sharks along the wall are the headline subjects. The sheltered channel allows more stable shooting conditions than the exposed outer walls. Morning light in the channel produces excellent natural illumination.
