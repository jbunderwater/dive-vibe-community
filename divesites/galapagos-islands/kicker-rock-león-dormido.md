# Kicker Rock (León Dormido)

A towering split-rock formation rising 140 meters from the sea, with a dramatic channel dive between sheer volcanic walls patrolled by Galapagos sharks and hammerheads.

## Overview

Kicker Rock, known locally as León Dormido ("Sleeping Lion"), is a pair of massive volcanic tuff formations rising 140 meters above the ocean surface off the western coast of San Cristobal Island. A narrow channel separates the two rock faces, creating one of the most dramatic wall dives in the Galapagos. The site is the most popular dive from San Cristobal and delivers consistent encounters with Galapagos sharks, hammerheads, sea turtles, and eagle rays gliding through the channel between the towering vertical walls.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Offshore from San Cristobal Island, Galapagos Islands
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 25 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 10-20 meters (30-65 feet)
- **Current**: Moderate to strong
- **Best Time**: June to November for best visibility and shark encounters

## Marine Life

Galapagos sharks are the signature species, regularly cruising through the channel in groups of two to six. Scalloped hammerhead sharks appear along the outer walls, especially during the cooler season. Large green sea turtles are nearly guaranteed, often resting on ledges or drifting through the channel. Spotted eagle rays pass through in small groups. Galapagos sea lions hunt in the shallows and frequently interact with divers. White-tip reef sharks rest on the sandy bottom below the channel. Schools of black-striped salema and king angelfish pack the walls, and starfish and anemones cling to the rock faces.

## Dive Profile

The dive typically begins with entry on one side of the rock formation, descending along the outer wall to depth before entering the channel between the two monoliths. The channel floor sits at approximately 20-25 meters with sandy patches. Divers transit through the channel, looking up at the towering walls on either side while scanning for sharks and rays. The dive continues along the outer wall on the opposite side, gradually ascending. The safety stop is performed along the wall or in open water depending on current direction.

## Entry and Exit

Boat departs from Puerto Baquerizo Moreno on San Cristobal, with a 45-minute to one-hour ride to the site. Giant stride entry on the lee side of the rock formation, with the boat captain reading current direction to determine the entry point. The panga follows divers through the dive. Exit via SMB deployment for panga pickup, or direct ladder climb back to the main boat in calmer conditions.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Request the early morning departure for the best light penetrating the channel and most active shark behavior
- Swim slowly through the channel and look up frequently -- sharks and rays often pass overhead as silhouettes
- Wear dark-colored exposure suits to avoid startling the Galapagos sharks, which are curious but not aggressive
- Bring a 5-7mm wetsuit; water temperature can drop to 18°C in the channel due to thermocline effects
- If conditions allow, ask about a second dive on the outer wall for a different perspective on the formation

## Safety Considerations

Current can funnel through the channel unpredictably, sometimes creating a washing effect between the walls. Surge is common near the surface, especially on the exposed side of the rock. The channel can create a downcurrent effect that pushes divers deeper than planned -- monitor depth carefully. Galapagos sharks are bold and may approach closely; maintain calm and do not reach toward them. Always carry an SMB and signaling devices, as currents can carry divers away from the rock formation during ascent.

## Photography

The channel creates extraordinary wide-angle opportunities -- vertical walls on either side with blue water above and sharks cruising through make for iconic compositions. Shoot upward through the channel for dramatic silhouettes of sharks and rays against the light. The walls themselves are photogenic, covered in marine growth with excellent texture. Sea turtles resting on ledges are cooperative portrait subjects. A fisheye or ultra-wide rectilinear lens is ideal. Strobes help fill shadows on the wall but are not essential for the blue-water shark shots.
