# Isla Bartolome

Dive beneath the iconic Pinnacle Rock for one of the world's few opportunities to share the water with Galapagos penguins darting through volcanic rock formations.

## Overview

Isla Bartolome sits near Santiago Island, famous above water for Pinnacle Rock -- a jagged volcanic spire that is one of the most photographed landmarks in the Galapagos. Below the surface, the site offers terraced volcanic steps, overhangs draped in black coral, and rocky channels over dramatic lava formations. The signature experience here is encountering Galapagos penguins underwater, as they rocket through the shallows hunting fish -- one of the very few places in the world where divers can share the water with penguins. Pacific seahorses are confirmed in the black coral on the terraced steps. Sea lions, reef sharks, and rays add to an accessible and memorable dive.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Bartolome Island, near Santiago Island, Galapagos Islands
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 27 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 10-18 meters (30-60 feet)
- **Current**: Mild to moderate
- **Best Time**: Year-round for penguins; June to November for best visibility

## Marine Life

Galapagos penguins are the star attraction, swimming underwater with astonishing speed and agility as they hunt small fish near the surface. These are the only penguins found north of the equator. Galapagos sea lions are ever-present and playful. Whitetip reef sharks rest on sandy patches between rocks and in overhangs. Spotted eagle rays and golden rays cruise over the volcanic bottom. Green sea turtles graze on algae-covered rocks. Pacific seahorses are confirmed among the black coral on the terraced steps. Schools of reef fish, including king angelfish and barracuda, fill the rocky formations. Moray eels inhabit the crevices. Octopus hide among the volcanic rocks.

## Dive Profile

The dive explores the terraced volcanic steps and channels near Pinnacle Rock, descending from the shallows over a landscape of lava formations, boulders, and sandy patches. Maximum depth is 27 metres, but much of the interesting marine life is in the shallower zone of 5-15 metres. Penguins are encountered near the surface, typically in the top 5-10 metres, so allow plenty of time in the shallows rather than focusing on depth. The terraced steps with black coral are found at 12-18 metres. The dive gradually works back to the shallows for an extended safety stop where penguin and sea lion encounters are most likely.

## Entry and Exit

Panga from liveaboard or day boat to entry point near Pinnacle Rock. Giant stride entry into relatively sheltered water. The dive is typically a gentle swim over the volcanic bottom rather than a drift dive. Current is usually mild. Exit via panga pickup. Surface conditions are generally calm in the lee of the island.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Spend the majority of your bottom time in the shallows (3-10 metres) where penguin encounters happen -- do not waste time at depth searching for them
- Penguins are fast and unpredictable; keep your camera ready and track them visually rather than trying to chase
- Morning dives offer the best chance of penguin activity, as they tend to hunt early
- Check the terraced steps and overhangs for black coral and seahorses at 12-18 metres
- Look in holes and under ledges in the volcanic rock for octopus and resting whitetip sharks

## Safety Considerations

This is one of the calmer Galapagos dive sites, but the Advanced rating reflects the remote location, cold water (18-23 degrees C), and distance from medical facilities. Currents can pick up during tidal changes. The volcanic rock formations include sharp edges -- maintain buoyancy control to avoid contact. Penguins are wild animals and should not be chased; they will approach on their own terms. Standard Galapagos equipment and thermal protection (5-7mm wetsuit) apply.

## Photography

Penguins underwater are the bucket-list shot. Use a wide-angle lens and shoot on high shutter speed (1/320 or faster) -- penguins move with explosive speed and blur easily. Pre-focus and shoot in bursts. The shallow depth means excellent natural light. Pinnacle Rock above water makes a dramatic background for split-level shots if surface conditions allow. Sea lions are reliable and cooperative wide-angle subjects. The volcanic rock formations create interesting textures and backgrounds.
