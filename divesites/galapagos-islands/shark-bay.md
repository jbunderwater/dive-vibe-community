# Shark Bay

*A partially sheltered bay on Wolf Island where hammerhead schools, silky sharks, and Galápagos sharks aggregate in high numbers around a sheer wall.*

## Overview

Shark Bay (Bahía Tiburón) occupies a protected bay on Wolf Island, named for the volume of sharks that concentrate there. It offers a different character from the exposed boulder slopes at nearby Landslide — the bay provides partial shelter from open-ocean conditions, though currents remain strong and can produce unexpected drift dives. Scalloped hammerheads, Galápagos sharks, and silky sharks are the main draw. Nearby on Wolf Island, the North Islet site features caverns and tunnels in a sheer wall, and Fondeadero — a separate anchorage site — is one of the very few places in the entire Galápagos archipelago where night diving is permitted.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Wolf Island (Isla Wolf), sheltered bay, Galápagos Islands
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 27 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 10–20 meters
- **Current**: Strong, variable direction; unexpected drift dives occur
- **Water Temperature**: 23–24°C
- **Best Time**: June to November

## Marine Life

Scalloped hammerhead sharks are the headliner, typically encountered in schools at 15–20 meters depth toward the outer bay. Galápagos sharks and silky sharks are regular visitors. Whale sharks appear seasonally. Sea lions are playful and abundant in the shallower bay sections. Eagle rays, golden rays, green sea turtles, and dolphins are confirmed across multiple sources. Moray eels inhabit the wall crevices. Schools of snappers and jacks patrol the wall face.

## Dive Profile

The dive begins in the shallower inner bay at approximately 10 meters where sea lions are most interactive, then moves toward the deeper outer wall dropping to 27 meters where shark concentrations are greatest. The technique — consistent across Wolf Island sites — is to find a sheltered position on the wall, wedge in, and hold position while the sharks approach. Active swimming against the current is counterproductive and disturbs the animals.

## Entry and Exit

Access by panga from the liveaboard vessel. Negative entries are standard. The bay's partial shelter makes entries somewhat calmer than at Landslide, but swell can be heavy near the wall. Deploy an SMB for safety stop panga pickup.

## Tips

- The shallower bay section with sea lions makes a good start before moving to the deeper outer wall
- Hold position against the wall and let the sharks come to you — staying still produces the best encounters
- Check your liveaboard's itinerary for Fondeadero, a separate site in the Wolf Island area where night diving is one of the few permitted in the Galápagos
- Bring a dive light for the wall crevices

## Safety

Despite the bay's relative shelter, currents are strong and unpredictable. Unexpected drift dives occur regularly. Swell can be heavy close to shore and near the wall. Carry an SMB and signaling device. Advanced open water certification with a minimum of 50 logged dives is typically required by operators for Wolf Island.
