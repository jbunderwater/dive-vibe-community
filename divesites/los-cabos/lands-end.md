## Land's End

Where the Pacific Ocean meets the Sea of Cortez at the granite formations of El Arco (The Arch). A resident colony of California sea lions regularly approaches and interacts with divers, making this one of the most entertaining dives in Cabo. The buried remains of the cargo ship *Harry Lundeberg* rest near the pinnacle base — partially concealed by shifting sand, the wreck is visible depending on sea conditions. Schools of cownose rays, baitfish, and snappers patrol the rocky bottom. Depth ranges from 10 to 22 meters across the site.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Cabo San Lucas Marine Park, at the tip of the Baja Peninsula
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Pinnacle with wreck
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 22 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 10-25 meters
- **Current**: Can be strong — this is the meeting point of two bodies of water
- **Best Time**: July to October for warmest water and calmest conditions

## Marine Life

California sea lions are the star attraction and frequently swim directly up to divers. Schools of cownose rays, snappers, and dense baitfish balls are common. Moray eels and pufferfish hide in the rocky crevices. Mobula rays pass through seasonally.

## The Harry Lundeberg Wreck

The *Harry Lundeberg* was a Canadian-built cargo vessel launched in 1943 in North Vancouver as the *Kitsilano Park*, part of Canada's wartime Park Ship program. It underwent several name changes before becoming the *Harry Lundeberg* in 1950. On February 8, 1954, while transporting gypsum from Isla San Marcos, the ship struck a rock shelf near Land's End and was declared a total loss. The wreck measures approximately 134 meters in length. It lay buried under shifting sand for decades until Hurricane Odile partially uncovered sections in 2014. Visibility of the wreck depends on sea conditions — it may be partially or fully concealed.

## Dive Profile

The site surrounds the granite rock formations at the very tip of the peninsula. Surge and current are common due to the convergence of the Pacific and the Sea of Cortez. Start on the sheltered (bay) side and work toward the point as conditions allow. If visible, the wreck sections lie at roughly 12 meters and are worth exploring on the way out. Strong surface current can develop quickly — carry an SMB.

## Safety Considerations

Currents and surge can intensify without warning, particularly on the Pacific side. Not recommended for inexperienced divers when conditions are rough. Boat traffic is heavy in the area — deploy an SMB before surfacing.
