## Sand Falls

One of the most unusual underwater features in the world — a cascade of sand flowing over the edge of the San Lucas submarine canyon. The sand accumulates on a sloping shelf, then flows over the canyon lip under its own weight, creating the appearance of an underwater waterfall. The canyon rim where the falls originate sits at around 27 meters, with the main viewing depth between 30 and 40 meters. The canyon itself descends to extreme depths. The site was first scientifically described in 1959 by Scripps Institution of Oceanography researcher Francis P. Shepard and has been referenced by underwater explorers ever since.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Cabo San Lucas Marine Park, canyon edge near Pelican Rock
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 40 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15-30 meters (variable — recent swells can reduce visibility at the falls)
- **Current**: Variable — can be strong at the canyon edge
- **Best Time**: July to October for best visibility

## Marine Life

The sandy canyon edge hosts garden eels and moray eels in the rocky outcrops. Angelfish, snappers, and schools of Mexican goatfish inhabit the reef above the sand chutes. Larger species cruise the blue water off the wall. The rocky areas above the sand chutes support sea fans and groupers. Octopus and seahorses have been reported in the shallower sections.

## Dive Profile

This is a deep dive requiring Advanced Open Water certification and disciplined air management. Descend along the Pelican Rock reef slope to the canyon edge at approximately 27 meters — the sand rivers are visible flowing over the rim into the abyss. The most dramatic viewing is at 30-40 meters. Bottom time at this depth is limited. Plan conservatively and begin ascent with ample reserve. Note: the activity of the sand falls varies with sea conditions; significant recent swells can temporarily suppress the flow. Nitrox is recommended.

## Safety Considerations

Depth is the primary hazard. The canyon drops to extreme depths below the recreational limit, and narcosis can impair judgment. Strong currents can develop along the canyon edge. Not suitable for Open Water-only certified divers. Carry an SMB and dive computer, and agree on maximum depth and turn pressure before descending.
