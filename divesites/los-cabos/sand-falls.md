## Sand Falls

One of the most unusual underwater features in the world — rivers of sand cascade down a granite chute and over the edge of the San Lucas submarine canyon, which drops to approximately 365 meters (1,200 feet). The phenomenon was first documented in 1959 by oceanographer Francis P. Shepard of the Scripps Institution of Oceanography, and was later brought to international attention by Jacques Cousteau's television productions. The sand flows begin around 27 meters and the most dramatic viewing is between 30 and 40 meters at the canyon rim. The site is adjacent to Pelican Rock, reached in about 10 minutes from the marina.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Cabo San Lucas Marine Park, canyon edge near Pelican Rock
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive (submarine canyon rim)
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 40 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–30 meters (can drop to 10–15 meters after swell)
- **Current**: Variable — can be strong at the canyon edge
- **Best Time**: September to November for best visibility

## Marine Life

The sandy canyon edge attracts garden eels and jawfish in the shallows above the falls. Larger species cruise the blue water off the wall, including spotted eagle rays and occasional mobula rays. The rocky areas above the sand chutes host moray eels, Mexican goatfish, snappers, and groupers.

## Dive Profile

This is a deep dive requiring Advanced certification and strong air management. Descend along the reef slope from Pelican Rock to the canyon edge at approximately 27 meters. The sand rivers are visible here, cascading over the lip into the canyon below. The visual effect — sand flowing in sustained streams over a vertical drop — is unlike anything else in Mexican waters. Bottom time at 35–40 meters is limited; plan conservatively and begin ascent with ample reserve. Nitrox is recommended.

## Safety Considerations

Depth is the primary hazard. The canyon drops far beyond recreational limits and narcosis can impair judgment. Strong currents can develop along the canyon edge, and surge can reduce visibility temporarily after swell. This site is not suitable for inexperienced divers. Carry an SMB and dive computer; agree on maximum depth and turn pressure before descending.
