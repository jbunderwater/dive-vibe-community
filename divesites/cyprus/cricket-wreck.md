# Cricket wreck

A WWI-era British river gunboat used as RAF target practice — now resting upside down at 33 metres with a sandy gap beneath the hull.

## Overview

HMS Cricket was a British river gunboat, 35 metres long, built in 1915. She served in both WWI and WWII before being declared a loss in 1942, stripped for spare parts, and her hull towed to Cyprus where she was anchored in Larnaca Bay and used as target practice by the RAF. She sank in 1947 due to bad weather.

The wreck now rests upside down at 33 metres, creating a sandy gap beneath the hull that divers can swim through. Divers can also enter through escape hatches. Very silty conditions require careful buoyancy control — fin kicks near the wreck can dramatically reduce visibility.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Off Ayia Napa / Larnaca, Cyprus
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 33 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–25 meters
- **Current**: Light
- **Best Time**: March to November

## Marine Life

Bream, groupers, lionfish, moray eels, nudibranchs, octopus, sea slugs, starfish, and tube worms. The inverted hull creates a sheltered environment underneath.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Very silty — excellent buoyancy control essential
- The sandy gap under the inverted hull is a unique swim-through
- Served in both World Wars before being used as RAF target practice
- Escape hatches provide additional entry points
