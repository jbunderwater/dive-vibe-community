# Cricket wreck

An Insect-class British river gunboat that saw service in both World Wars — now lying upside down at 33 metres with a sandy gap divers can swim beneath.

## Overview

HMS Cricket was an Insect-class river gunboat, launched on 17 December 1915 by Barclay Curle & Co. in Glasgow. The vessel was approximately 72 metres (237 feet) long with a displacement of 645 tons. She served in WWI on the Mesopotamian front and during the Russian Civil War on the Dvina River, and was stationed in China during the inter-war period before being recalled to the Mediterranean at the start of WWII.

How HMS Cricket ended up on the seabed near Larnaca is a matter of some conflict in the historical record. Naval records at uboat.net state she was "Sunk as target off Cyprus in August 1944" following severe damage from an air attack off Mersa Matruh, Egypt in June 1941. By contrast, Visit Cyprus and several diving operators state the hull was towed to Cyprus after being stripped for spare parts, anchored in Larnaka Bay, used as RAF target practice, and sank in 1947 due to a severe storm. These two accounts cannot both be correct. Divers and researchers should note this unresolved discrepancy; the 1944 date comes from a dedicated naval history database, while the 1947 account is consistent across multiple Cyprus diving sources.

The wreck rests upside down on the seabed at 33 metres depth. The inverted position creates a distinctive sandy gap beneath the hull where divers can swim. Divers can also access the interior through escape hatches. The site is silty — careful buoyancy control is essential to avoid disturbing visibility.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Larnaka Bay, approximately 1.5 km offshore, east Cyprus
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 33 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–25 meters (silt-sensitive)
- **Current**: Light
- **Best Time**: March to November

## Marine Life

Bream, groupers, lionfish, moray eels, nudibranchs, octopus, sea slugs, starfish, and tube worms have been reported at the site. The inverted hull creates a sheltered environment beneath.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Buoyancy control is critical — the site is silty and fin kicks near the wreck will reduce visibility
- The sandy gap beneath the inverted hull is the signature swim-through feature
- Escape hatches allow interior access — dive within your training limits
- Historical note: sources conflict on the exact date and manner of sinking; the 1944 and 1947 accounts are both reported in published sources
