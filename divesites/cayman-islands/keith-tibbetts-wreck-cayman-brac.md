## Keith Tibbetts Wreck Cayman Brac

A 330-foot former Soviet-built Cuban Navy frigate deliberately sunk in 1996, now a spectacular two-section artificial reef off Cayman Brac's North Shore.

## Overview

The MV Captain Keith Tibbetts — named after a Cayman Islands politician — is one of the most impressive artificial reefs in the Caribbean. This 330-foot vessel was originally built in 1984 in the Soviet Union for the Cuban Navy. Following Cuba's financial difficulties after the Soviet Union's collapse, the ship was acquired and donated to the Cayman Islands government for scuttling as a dive attraction. She was deliberately sunk off Cayman Brac's North Shore in September 1996. A 2004 storm broke her in two sections, which now rest at different depths and angles on the sandy bottom. Her bow rises to within 6 meters of the surface, while the stern section reaches 50 meters. She is the only decommissioned Soviet warship accessible for recreational wreck diving in the Western Hemisphere. Bow guns are visible and the ship's military character is still strongly evident.

## Marine Life

The Keith Tibbetts has become an artificial reef of remarkable productivity in the decades since sinking. Hawksbill and green turtles regularly rest on the hull and swim around the structure. Spotted eagle rays cruise along the wreck flanks. Large green moray eels occupy enclosed spaces within the hull. Schools of horse-eye jacks circle the stern section. Colorful sponges — orange, purple, and yellow — now cover significant portions of the hull surface. Scorpionfish and other cryptic species have colonized the corners and ledges. Grouper occupy the deeper stern sections.

## Dive Profile

The bow section is the standard dive, beginning at the 6-meter bow that almost breaks the surface and working along the deck to the break point where the 2004 storm separated the two sections. Experienced divers can then explore the stern section descending to 30–40 meters, keeping 50 meters as the absolute sport diving limit. Multiple dives are needed to cover the full wreck comprehensively.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive from Cayman Brac operators — the island has several dedicated dive operations. The North Shore is well-sheltered. Giant stride entry. The wreck is buoyed for easy location. SMB deployment on ascent.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The bow section is accessible to recreational divers; the stern descent to 50 meters is for highly experienced divers only
- The bow guns are intact and photograph well — approach from above for the best angle
- Turtle and eagle ray encounters are most common in the 10–25 meter zone around the mid-ship section
- Allow at least 2–3 days on Cayman Brac to dive the wreck multiple times from different angles
- Cayman Brac has its own excellent wall diving that complements the wreck diving

## Safety Considerations

The 50-meter stern depth exceeds recreational sport diving limits — stick to 40 meters maximum on recreational equipment. The storm break creates penetration zones that should only be entered by wreck-certified divers. Ascent requires careful decompression management given the working depths. Ensure your dive computer handles the depth profile before descending into the deeper sections.

## Photography

The Keith Tibbetts is one of the Caribbean's most photogenic wrecks. The military vessel character — bow guns, deck rails, bridge structure — gives it a visual narrative that purely commercial wrecks lack. The bright sponge growth against the grey hull and the magnificent turtle and eagle ray encounters make compelling compositions. Wide-angle is the primary tool here. Natural light penetration on the shallow bow section is excellent.
