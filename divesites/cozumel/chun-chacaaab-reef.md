# Chun Chacaaab Reef

A pristine southern reef near Punta Sur, offering an immaculate coral garden with exceptional wildlife density in one of Cozumel's least-trafficked dive zones.

## Overview

Chun Chacaaab is a Mayan place name and one of the more southerly sites in Cozumel's dive circuit, positioned close to the island's southern tip near the Punta Sur ecological reserve. The site's remoteness from the main tourist concentration means it receives far fewer dive boats than the central corridor sites — and the reef condition reflects this. The coral gardens here are dense and healthy, with large formations of brain coral, star coral, and sea fans in excellent condition. The 18-meter maximum depth makes this accessible for intermediate divers while the southern clarity provides visibility regularly exceeding 30 meters. The site is a favourite among experienced local divers who appreciate unspoiled conditions over convenience.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Deep southern leeward coast, Cozumel, near Punta Sur, Quintana Roo, Mexico
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Coral reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 18 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 25–40 meters (80–130 feet)
- **Current**: Moderate southward drift near the tip
- **Best Time**: November to April; approach to the southern tip can be rougher in summer

## Marine Life

The southern zone's pristine conditions support exceptional wildlife density. Hawksbill and green turtles are virtually guaranteed here — the abundance of sponge growth provides a constant food source, and the low diver traffic means less disturbance. Spotted eagle rays regularly appear in groups, gliding through the coral garden's sandy channels. Large groupers — Nassau, black, and tiger species — hold territories on the deeper reef structures. The splendid toadfish, Cozumel's endemic jewel, is reliably found here; search carefully under coral ledges at 10–15 meters. Queen angelfish and grey angelfish are present throughout. The sponge community — barrel, azure vase, and branching tube sponges — is among the most impressive at this accessible depth range.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the sandy reef floor at 12–18 meters and orient to the spur-and-groove structure. The southern current carries divers along the reef. Spend time investigating the larger coral heads where groupers reside and toadfish hide. Ascend to the upper reef at 5–10 meters for the final portion, where the coral density is highest and the light most vivid. Safety stop in the bright shallow zone before SMB deployment.

## Entry and Exit

One of the longer boat rides in the Cozumel circuit — 35–50 minutes from San Miguel. Operators offering southern circuit trips typically combine Chun Chacaaab with one or two other southern sites. The investment in travel time is justified by the quality of the dive experience. Full drift protocol with SMB deployment applies.

## Tips

- Dive the south in the morning when conditions are typically calmest and light is optimal
- The reduced boat traffic means wildlife is significantly less habituated — move slowly and deliberately
- Budget extra time at each coral head investigating crevices for the splendid toadfish
- A full southern day combining Chun Chacaaab with El Islote or Punta Sur makes excellent use of the travel time
- Carry an extra tank's worth of planning conservatism — the distance from recompression facilities warrants it

## Safety

Remote southern position extends emergency response time significantly. All standard drift diving precautions apply with particular emphasis on SMB deployment. Current near the southern tip can increase unpredictably in summer months. Verify dive insurance and the nearest recompression chamber location (San Miguel) before departing. The potential for sea state changes during a long day in the south makes communication with the boat captain throughout the day important.

## Photography

The pristine, less-trafficked reefs of the far south offer some of Cozumel's cleanest photographic compositions — no other divers in the frame, undisturbed wildlife, and exceptional clarity. Wide-angle captures the scale of the sponge formations. The splendid toadfish hiding in its crevice is one of the most sought-after endemic subjects for visiting underwater photographers. Turtle portraits in undisturbed natural behaviour are achievable here that would be more difficult at busier sites.
