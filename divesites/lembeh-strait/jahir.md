## Jahir

Jahir is named after the local dive guide who first explored and mapped the site, and it has since become one of the most celebrated muck dive spots in the Lembeh Strait. Located on the Bitung side of the northern strait, Jahir is pure black sand muck diving with coral patches beginning at 5 meters and a sand slope descending to 28 meters. It is one of the top night dive sites in the strait alongside Nudi Falls, and its consistent presence of flamboyant cuttlefish and blue-ringed octopus makes it a signature Lembeh experience.

## Overview

Jahir features a gentle black sand slope with scattered rubble, purple heart urchins, and the occasional algae clump. The absence of significant current means the site can be dived all day and into the night without tidal restrictions. The natural and relatively undisturbed ecosystem supports a high density of rare critters. Flamboyant cuttlefish are a near-constant presence, and the site is one of the more reliable locations in Lembeh for blue-ringed octopus encounters. Night dives reveal an entirely different cast of creatures, including bobtail squid and the starry night octopus.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Northern Lembeh Strait, Bitung side, North Sulawesi, Indonesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Muck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 28 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 5–15 meters
- **Water Temperature**: 26–29°C
- **Current**: Minimal; can be dived all day
- **Best Time**: Year-round; flamboyant cuttlefish most active August–January; blue-ringed octopus most common August–December

## Marine Life

Jahir is best known for its cephalopod diversity and the reliability of rare critter encounters:

- **Flamboyant cuttlefish** (*Metasepia pfefferi*) — walks along the bottom on arm tips, flashing rapid color patterns of purple, red, orange, and white; toxic flesh makes it one of the few genuinely venomous cephalopods
- **Blue-ringed octopus** (*Hapalochlaena* sp.) — two species present in Lembeh; characterized by brilliant iridescent blue rings that appear when the animal is agitated; extremely venomous, observed only from a respectful distance
- **Mimic octopus** (*Thaumoctopus mimicus*) — actively hunts across the sand slope
- **Ghost pipefish** — ornate and robust ghost pipefish in algae and rubble patches
- **Hairy frogfish** and **painted frogfish** — on sand and rubble patches
- **Seahorses** — common and thorny seahorses in sponge areas
- **Ambon scorpionfish** — resting on sandy patches, expertly camouflaged
- **Zebra crab** on purple heart urchins (a classic Lembeh pairing)
- **Various eels** — ribbon eels and moray eels in rubble crevices

Night dives add:
- **Bobtail squid** — emerging from the sand after dark
- **Starry night octopus** (*Callistoctopus luteus*) — a nocturnal species that hunts actively at night
- **Orange-bodied tropical bobtail squid**

## Dive Profile

Descend onto the sand at 5 meters and work the slope slowly, pausing to observe flamboyant cuttlefish feeding behavior along the way. The mid-slope zone (10–18 m) holds the highest density of critter activity. Rubble patches and algae clumps warrant thorough inspection for ghost pipefish. The slope continues to 28 meters but the most productive zone is within the 5–20 m range. Night dives require torches and a conservative plan — conditions remain calm after dark.

## Entry and Exit

Access by dive boat. Giant stride or back roll entry. No current management required. Night diving is highly recommended and available through local operators. SMB deployment during ascent is standard. Multiple dives per day on Jahir are common.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Do not touch or attempt to handle the blue-ringed octopus under any circumstances — its venom is lethal and there is no antidote
- Flamboyant cuttlefish are best photographed with patience — follow one slowly and it will continue feeding, providing extended photo opportunities
- Request a night dive at Jahir — it is considered one of the strait's two best after-dark sites
- Zebra crabs on urchins are a reliable sighting; scan the spines of purple heart urchins carefully
- The site is current-free, making it suitable for photographers who want extended time on a single subject

## Safety Considerations

Currents are minimal at Jahir. The most significant hazard is the venomous nature of the blue-ringed octopus — its bite is painless but delivers a potent neurotoxin; observe without any contact. Flamboyant cuttlefish are also toxic if ingested. Stonefish and scorpionfish are present throughout. Night diving requires proper lighting and buddy discipline. Visibility can drop after heavy rainfall.

## Photography

Jahir is a top-tier behavioral photography site. Flamboyant cuttlefish color patterns are among the most visually spectacular subjects in macro diving — dual strobes allow the full range of hues to render faithfully. Blue-ringed octopus portraits with iridescent rings lit against dark sand are an iconic Lembeh image. Use a 100mm macro lens for most subjects; video is equally rewarding here. Bobtail squid are best captured with a focus light and careful approach on night dives.
