## Nudi Falls

Nudi Falls is one of the three most-dived sites in the Lembeh Strait and is routinely described as the strait's most nudibranch-rich location. The name comes from divers' bubble streams causing nudibranchs to tumble off the shallow wall above. The site is exceptional for its terrain diversity — a shallow wall, rocky reef section, sandy slope, and deep soft coral field are all contained within a single dive, making it unlike any other site in the strait.

## Overview

Nudi Falls lies on the mainland side of the strait, roughly midway along its length, just north of the village of Tandu Rusa. The topography is unique for Lembeh: a sheer wall in the shallows (1–8 m) gives way to a rocky reef section, then a sandy slope, and finally a rubble patch with a soft coral forest beginning around 20 meters. Late-morning sunlight filters through overhanging coastal vegetation, creating dramatic lighting on the shallow wall. This site is dived multiple times per day by resident divers and is one of the two most popular night dive sites in the strait (alongside Jahir).

## Site Information

- **Location**: Mainland side, central Lembeh Strait, North Sulawesi, Indonesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Muck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 25 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 5–15 meters
- **Water Temperature**: 26–29°C
- **Current**: Generally mild; can increase in the main channel section
- **Best Time**: Year-round; morning dives benefit from excellent wall lighting

## Marine Life

Nudi Falls is Lembeh's premier nudibranch site, with exceptional species diversity across the wall, reef, and sand sections. Regular sightings include:

- **Nudibranchs** — the highest species density in the strait; Nembrotha, Glossodoris, Chromodoris, Flabellina, Thecacera, and many others across all depth zones
- **Pygmy seahorses** — Bargibanti pygmy seahorses in gorgonian sea fans on the rocky reef section
- **Frogfish** — multiple species hidden in soft coral patches on the deeper section
- **Rhinopias** — occasionally resting at the base of the slope; a prized rarity
- **Blue-ringed octopus** — found in the rubble zone
- **Hairy octopus** and **wunderpus**
- **Harlequin shrimp**, **tiger shrimp**, and **hairy shrimp** in crevices and rubble
- **Garden eels** — colonies visible in the sandy channel section
- **Flasher wrasses** and **anthias** schools on the reef section
- **Gobies** — various species on the coral and rubble
- **Comet longfin** fish

Night dives at Nudi Falls reveal a transformation — the site becomes even more colorful, with nudibranchs feeding actively, mandarin fish courting at dusk, and ghost pipefish more visible in torchlight.

## Dive Profile

Begin on the shallow wall (1–5 m) where large, colorful nudibranchs are visible in excellent natural light. Work along the wall, then transition to the rocky reef section where pygmy seahorses inhabit the gorgonians. Continue down the sandy slope, scanning for rhinopias and blue-ringed octopus in the rubble. The soft coral field at 20–25 m holds frogfish. Plan the ascent to revisit the wall — buoyancy is straightforward as the depth is primarily in the shallow-to-mid range. Air consumption permits extended time at 5–15 m where the diversity is highest.

## Entry and Exit

Access by dive boat. Giant stride entry. Night dives are among Lembeh's most popular and should be booked through local operators. An SMB is required for all dives. Avoid overrunning your air budget in the deeper section — the shallow zone deserves the most time.

## Tips and Recommendations

- This is the best site in Lembeh for nudibranch variety — bring a nudibranch identification card and expect to find species you won't recognize
- Morning light on the shallow wall is exceptional; request the first boat of the day for optimal photography conditions
- The transition from sandy slope to soft coral field at 20 m is where frogfish hide — look at every soft coral branch
- Night dives here are a must — the reef transforms entirely after dark
- Dive it multiple times in a stay; the critter composition shifts and different sections reward slower exploration

## Safety Considerations

Currents are generally mild but can pick up in the main channel section. The site is shallower than most in the strait, making air management straightforward. Stonefish and scorpionfish are present throughout. Night diving requires a primary and backup torch plus SMB. Visibility can drop after heavy rainfall.

## Photography

Nudi Falls is among Lembeh's best all-around photography sites. The shallow wall provides excellent natural light for nudibranch shots without strobes. The soft coral field suits wide-angle with nudibranch foreground subjects. A 100mm macro lens covers most subjects; super-macro (extension tubes) reveals incredible detail on small chromodoris and minute hairy shrimp. Night sessions with dual strobes are outstanding for nudibranch portraits.
