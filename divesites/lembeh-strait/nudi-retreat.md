## Nudi Retreat

Nudi Retreat is named for the large numbers of nudibranchs that historically concentrated near the shallow coral garden at the end of the dive. Situated alongside Nudi Falls in the central strait, Nudi Retreat is one of the three most-dived sites in the Lembeh Strait and is prized for its habitat variety — sandy slopes transition into coral patches studded with sea fans, creating a site that rewards both muck hunters and reef photographers within a single dive.

## Overview

Nudi Retreat features a mix of terrain types uncommon for Lembeh: sandy muck slopes lead into coral bommies and patch reef, with small drop-offs studded with gorgonian sea fans. This diversity creates a site where the species composition shifts dramatically as you move through the dive — frogfish on the open sand, pygmy seahorses in the sea fans, and rays occasionally passing over the sandy sections. The transition from muck to coral and back rewards experienced divers who know what to look for in each habitat. It is one of the more diverse sites in the strait and works well for groups with mixed experience levels.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Central Lembeh Strait, mainland side, North Sulawesi, Indonesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Muck dive (with coral garden elements)
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 5–15 meters
- **Water Temperature**: 26–29°C
- **Current**: Generally mild
- **Best Time**: Year-round

## Marine Life

Nudi Retreat hosts two overlapping assemblages — muck sand species and coral-associated species:

- **Nudibranchs** — the site's namesake; high species diversity across sand, rubble, and coral sections; Bargibanti and other species found in sea fans
- **Pygmy seahorses** (*Hippocampus bargibanti*) — in gorgonian sea fans near the small drop-offs; guides routinely find multiple individuals per dive
- **Frogfish** — various species on sand patches and sponge outcrops
- **Bargibanti pygmy seahorses** — in the coral bommies and sea fans at shallower depths
- **Harlequin shrimp** — in rubble and coral crevices; spectacular coloration
- **Electric clams** (*Ctenoides ales*) — produce rapid light pulses visible in crevices; a striking and unusual Lembeh sight
- **Seahorses** — common and thorny species in the muck and coral transition zone
- **Reef fish** — the coral sections attract anthias, damsels, and wrasse not found at pure muck sites
- **Rays** — stingrays occasionally on the sandy sections
- **Decorator crabs** and various nudibranchs in the coral rubble

## Dive Profile

Begin in the muck section over black sand, searching for frogfish and nudibranchs. Work toward the coral garden and small drop-off, slowing down to search sea fans for pygmy seahorses. Scan crevices for harlequin shrimp and electric clams. The dive ends in the shallow coral garden where additional nudibranch species are found among the corals. The terrain shift mid-dive keeps the experience fresh throughout. Total depth range is accessible to intermediate recreational divers.

## Entry and Exit

Access by dive boat. Giant stride entry. Current is generally mild. SMB for ascent and boat pickup. The site pairs naturally with a dive on adjacent Nudi Falls for a half-day covering both sites.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Ask your guide to search the sea fans carefully for pygmy seahorses — they are tiny and well camouflaged against the fan polyps
- Electric clams in crevices are best found by shining a torch into dark gaps — their light pulses are unmistakable
- The transition between muck and coral mid-dive is where the most surprising sightings occur — don't rush this zone
- Nudi Retreat and Nudi Falls together make an excellent morning program; both are within close range of each other
- Harlequin shrimp are found year-round here — their red and white spotted coloration makes them one of Lembeh's most photographed invertebrates

## Safety Considerations

Mild site conditions. Current is generally not a factor but can increase occasionally. Stonefish and scorpionfish are present on the sandy sections. Sea urchins in the coral rubble require care when maneuvering. Maintain buoyancy throughout to protect the coral. SMB recommended for ascent.

## Photography

Nudi Retreat is exceptional for nudibranch portraiture across multiple habitat types. The sea fan sections allow close-focus wide-angle compositions with pygmy seahorses and fan polyps. Electric clam light pulses are challenging to capture but produce striking images with a fast shutter speed and focus light. Bring a 100mm macro for nudibranchs and pygmy seahorses; consider a zoom for the reef sections. Natural light works well in the shallow coral garden.
