## TK2

TK2 is the middle site in the Teluk Kembahu (Kembahu Bay) complex on the mainland side of the Lembeh Strait, sandwiched between TK1 and TK3. Like its neighbors, TK2 is a dedicated muck site over black volcanic sand, with a focus on cephalopod diversity. The bay was where the mimic octopus was first scientifically documented, and TK2 continues to offer reliable encounters with multiple octopus species, flamboyant cuttlefish, and a range of other rare critters.

## Overview

TK2 shares the same sheltered bay environment as TK1 and TK3, with a black sand slope beginning in the shallows and extending to approximately 20 meters. Rope sponge patches and scattered rubble clusters break up the sand and concentrate marine life. The calm conditions and accessible depth range make TK2 a popular choice for divers of all experience levels, and it is frequently dived as part of a full day working through all three TK sites.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Teluk Kembahu, mainland side, Lembeh Strait, North Sulawesi, Indonesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Muck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 5–12 meters
- **Water Temperature**: 26–29°C
- **Current**: Minimal; very sheltered bay
- **Best Time**: Year-round

## Marine Life

TK2 is particularly productive for cephalopods. Divers regularly encounter:

- **Mimic octopus** (*Thaumoctopus mimicus*) — TK Bay is historically the discovery site; mimics are among the most sought-after critters in Lembeh
- **Coconut octopus** (*Amphioctopus marginatus*) — using shells and debris as portable dens
- **Wunderpus octopus** (*Wunderpus photogenicus*) — often confused with the mimic; identifiable by longer eye stalks and more reddish-brown coloration
- **Flamboyant cuttlefish** (*Metasepia pfefferi*) — walking along the bottom using arm tips, flashing hypnotic color waves
- **Ghost pipefish** — including ornate ghost pipefish hiding in algae clumps
- **Seahorses** — thorny and common species on sponge patches
- Various **nudibranchs** and **shrimp species**

## Dive Profile

Descend onto the black sand flat in the shallows, then work slowly along the slope to maximum depth. The mid-slope zone (8–15 m) typically holds the highest density of octopus activity. Move slowly and scan the open sand surface — mimic octopus move actively and can cover ground quickly. Allow time to watch behavioral sequences; the mimics are highly photogenic when displaying their shape-shifting behavior.

## Entry and Exit

Access by dive boat. Entry via giant stride. The calm bay conditions make this accessible for newer divers. An SMB is recommended for ascent. Multiple dives per day on the TK complex are common.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Distinguish the mimic octopus (white edging on all arms, flatter mantle) from the wunderpus (no continuous white edge, longer eye stalks) — guides can help with identification
- Flamboyant cuttlefish walk on the bottom rather than swimming — move slowly and you can follow one for several minutes
- Check sandy areas near algae patches and around debris piles for hidden ghost pipefish
- TK2 pairs well with TK1 and TK3 as a half-day trip covering the entire bay
- Keep your distance from cephalopods — they are easily stressed and will disappear if crowded

## Safety Considerations

Very mild conditions. Main hazard is the presence of stonefish and scorpionfish resting on the sandy bottom — never place hands on the substrate. Maintain buoyancy control to avoid silt disturbance. Visibility can reduce significantly after heavy rainfall.

## Photography

TK2 is a macro photographer's paradise. The mimic octopus performing its mimicry behavior (flounder, lionfish, banded sea snake) makes for outstanding behavioral images. Flamboyant cuttlefish color patterns are best captured with strobes at close range. Use a 100mm macro lens for octopus portraits; video is equally rewarding here for capturing behavioral sequences.
