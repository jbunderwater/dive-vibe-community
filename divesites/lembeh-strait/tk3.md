## TK3

TK3 is the third and northernmost dive site in the Teluk Kembahu (Kembahu Bay) complex and is widely considered the best of the three TK sites for cephalopod diversity. Lembeh Resort rates TK3 as one of the top octopus sites in the entire strait, with five octopus species regularly recorded. The black sand slope, rope sponge patches, and small coral bommies create an ideal habitat for the rare and unusual. The TK bay was the original discovery site for the mimic octopus, and TK3 continues that legacy.

## Overview

TK3 features a sandy slope beginning at 5 meters and descending to approximately 22 meters, broken up by patches of rope sponges and small coral heads. The sponge patches concentrate marine life and serve as territorial anchors for octopus species. Small bommies in the shallows add texture and host juvenile fish. The site is sheltered and current-free, making it accessible to beginners, yet the density and rarity of the critters make it compelling for experienced photographers.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Teluk Kembahu, mainland side, Lembeh Strait, North Sulawesi, Indonesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Muck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 22 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 5–12 meters
- **Water Temperature**: 26–29°C
- **Current**: Minimal; very sheltered bay
- **Best Time**: Year-round

## Marine Life

TK3 is one of Lembeh's premier octopus sites. Five species are regularly documented:

- **Mimic octopus** (*Thaumoctopus mimicus*) — TK Bay is the species' discovery site; mimics hunt actively in the open sand
- **Coconut octopus** (*Amphioctopus marginatus*) — found sheltering in buried shells and coconut husk den sites
- **Long-arm octopus** (*Octopus* sp.) — occasionally spotted moving across open sand
- **Wunderpus octopus** (*Wunderpus photogenicus*) — identified by its reddish-brown coloring and long eye stalks
- **Mototi octopus** (*Amphioctopus mototi*) — a rare species that displays blue rings when threatened, similar to the blue-ringed octopus

Additionally, TK3 holds:
- **Ambon scorpionfish** (*Pteroidichthys amboinensis*) — one of Lembeh's most sought-after rarities, resting on rope sponges
- **Black saddle snake eel** in the sand, often attended by a commensal shrimp perched on its head
- **Ornate ghost pipefish** and **velvet ghost pipefish**
- **Cockatoo waspfish** (*Ablabys taenianotus*) — leaf-mimicking ambush predators
- **Harlequin shrimp**, **brittle star snapping shrimp**, and **tiger shrimp** under ropes and encrusted debris
- **Cockatoo flounder**

## Dive Profile

Descend to the sand at 5 meters and work methodically along the slope, pausing at each sponge patch to search thoroughly. The rope sponge areas between 8 and 15 meters tend to be the most productive for octopus and Ambon scorpionfish. The deeper section (15–22 m) is worth visiting for the snake eel and deeper rubble areas. Ascend slowly through the bommies in the shallows.

## Entry and Exit

Access by dive boat. Entry via giant stride. No current to manage. SMB recommended for the ascent. The site works well as a third dive in a TK1–TK2–TK3 day sequence.

## Tips and Recommendations

- TK3 is the best of the TK sites for Ambon scorpionfish — ask your guide to search rope sponge patches carefully
- The mototi octopus is rarely seen; if spotted, do not attempt to touch it as it is genuinely venomous like a blue-ringed octopus
- Snake eels in their burrows are often attended by commensal shrimp — look for the shrimp to find the eel
- Cockatoo waspfish rock from side to side to mimic dead leaves — motion can give them away
- This is an excellent site for underwater videographers capturing octopus behavior

## Safety Considerations

Very calm conditions. Stonefish and scorpionfish are present on the substrate — maintain buoyancy and never touch the bottom with hands. The Mototi octopus, while small, is venomous — observe without contact. Run-off from the village can reduce visibility after rain.

## Photography

TK3 rewards macro photographers with exceptional octopus subjects. The dark sand background creates striking contrast for the white-and-brown patterns of the mimic octopus and the reddish mottle of the wunderpus. Ambon scorpionfish are excellent close-focus wide-angle subjects. The snake eel-with-shrimp combination is a classic Lembeh image. Bring a 100mm macro; video is highly recommended for behavioral sequences.
