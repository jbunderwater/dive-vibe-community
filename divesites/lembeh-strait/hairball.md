## Hairball

Hairball is one of the most celebrated muck diving sites in Lembeh Strait, North Sulawesi, Indonesia — described by Lembeh Resort as "probably the single most-photographed muck site on earth." The site's name has two claimed origins: a diver once lost their hair band mid-dive, and separately, the algae covering the substrate is said to resemble a cat's hairball. The hairy frogfish found here in reliable numbers have since reinforced the association, though they did not originate the name. The site's real distinction is its extraordinary documented species richness — over 200 species have been recorded on the slope.

## Overview

Hairball runs along a gently sloping black volcanic sand bottom scattered with sponges, discarded shells, coconut husks, and small rubble patches — exactly the kind of habitat that Lembeh's most prized residents call home. The site sits "on a point" where currents deliver enhanced nutrients, which is thought to contribute to the site's exceptional species density. Visibility averages 8–15 metres, typical for nutrient-rich Lembeh waters, and currents are usually mild. Water temperature holds at 26–29°C year-round. The maximum depth is 28 metres; the most productive critter zones are in the 10–22 metre range.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Lembeh Strait, North Sulawesi, Indonesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Muck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 28 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 8–15 metres (25–50 feet)
- **Current**: Usually mild; occasionally light drift due to the site's exposed point position
- **Best Time**: Year-round; October to May for calmest conditions

## Marine Life

Hairball delivers one of the densest concentrations of rare critters in the strait. Lembeh Resort documents five frogfish species at the site: Warty, Randall's, Painted, Hispid, and Hairy varieties. Mimic octopus and wonderpus are regular sightings, emerging from sandy burrows to hunt in the open. Flamboyant cuttlefish patrol the shallower areas of the slope. Blue-ringed octopus are spotted with surprising regularity. Coconut octopus, robust ghost pipefish, seahorses, pipefish, various nudibranchs, and a rotating cast of scorpionfish and moray eels complete the site's documented list. Night dives at Hairball are described as "dreamlike" with Spanish dancers and stargazers active after dark. No site-specific marine life is attributed beyond what operators have documented.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the sandy slope and work methodically at depths between 10 and 22 metres, where the greatest variety of critters congregates. The bottom slopes gradually, giving divers time to slow down and search carefully without burning through air. The shallowest reaches of the site can be explored during the safety stop, where additional frogfish and juvenile critters are often found under rubble. Maximum depth of 28 metres is rarely needed — the best action happens mid-slope. The site is approximately 16 minutes by boat from Lembeh Resort.

## Entry and Exit

Access is by banca boat from resort operators along the Lembeh Strait. Entry is via giant stride or back roll into calm water. Surface with a deployed SMB to signal the boat crew; pickup is straightforward in the protected strait.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Move slowly — muck diving rewards patience far more than distance covered
- Carry a dive pointer or stick to indicate critters to your guide without touching anything
- A macro or supermacro lens setup is essential; a wide-angle lens will mostly go unused
- Night dives at Hairball are frequently cited as exceptional — many critters are more active after dark
- Brief your guide before the dive on your target species; frogfish and flamboyant cuttlefish are the top requests
- Buoyancy control is critical — silt kicked up from the black sand can cloud visibility for everyone behind you

## Safety Considerations

Hairball is a straightforward muck dive with no significant hazards beyond standard Lembeh precautions. The black sand substrate can be disorienting in reduced visibility — maintain contact with your guide. Blue-ringed octopus are present; never handle any octopus here. Avoid contact with the sandy bottom to prevent silt-out. Always carry an SMB for surface signalling in the busy strait.

## Photography

Hairball is a supermacro photographer's dream. The five documented frogfish species offer portrait opportunities across a range of colour phases. Flamboyant cuttlefish reward patience with extraordinary colour-change sequences. Wonderpus and mimic octopus offer dynamic movement shots as they hunt. Shoot with a 100mm macro or wet dioptre for the smallest subjects. The site's exceptional species richness means revisiting the same area multiple times on a single dive often reveals additional subjects.
