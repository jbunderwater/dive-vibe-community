## Hairball

Hairball is one of the most celebrated muck diving sites in Lembeh Strait, North Sulawesi, Indonesia — and arguably the site that best encapsulates everything Lembeh is famous for. The name has two origins: a diver once lost their hair band mid-dive, leaving hair flowing freely in the water, and the algae covering the slope is said to resemble a cat's hairball. The site's real claim to fame is the extraordinary concentration of rare and bizarre critters packed into a single sandy slope, with hairy frogfish among its most photographed residents.

## Overview

Hairball runs along a gently sloping black volcanic sand bottom scattered with sponges, discarded shells, coconut husks, and small rubble patches — exactly the kind of habitat that Lembeh's most prized residents call home. Visibility averages 8–15 meters, typical for nutrient-rich Lembeh waters, and currents are usually mild. Water temperature holds at 26–29°C year-round. The site dives well at any time of year, though October through May tends to offer the calmest conditions and best critter activity.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Lembeh Strait, North Sulawesi, Indonesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Muck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 28 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 8–15 meters (25–50 feet)
- **Current**: Usually mild; occasionally light drift
- **Best Time**: Year-round; October to May for calmest conditions

## Marine Life

Hairball delivers one of the densest concentrations of rare critters in the Strait. Hairy frogfish — the namesake species — are found here in multiple colour phases, often resting motionless among algae-covered rubble. Mimic octopus and wonderpus are regular sightings, emerging from sandy burrows to hunt in the open. Flamboyant cuttlefish patrol the shallower areas of the slope, flashing their extraordinary colour patterns as they stalk prey across the sand. Blue-ringed octopus, one of the most venomous animals in the ocean, are spotted with surprising regularity. Coconut octopus, robust ghost pipefish, and a rotating cast of nudibranchs complete the critter list. Expert local guides ("spotters") know every centimetre of this site and are essential for finding the smallest subjects.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the sandy slope and work methodically at depths between 10 and 25 meters, where the greatest variety of critters congregates. The bottom slopes gradually, giving divers time to slow down and search carefully without burning through air. The shallowest reaches of the site can be explored during the safety stop, where additional frogfish and juvenile critters are often found under rubble. Maximum depth of 29 meters is rarely needed — the best action happens mid-slope.

## Entry and Exit

Access is by banca boat (traditional outrigger) from resort operators along the Lembeh Strait. Entry is via giant stride or back roll into calm water. Descent is down the anchor line or a shot line. Surface with a deployed SMB to signal the boat crew; pickup is straightforward in the protected strait.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Move slowly — muck diving rewards patience far more than distance covered
- Carry a dive pointer or stick to indicate critters to your guide without touching anything
- A macro or supermacro lens setup is essential; a wide-angle lens will mostly go unused
- Night dives at Hairball can be exceptional — many critters are more active after dark
- Brief your guide before the dive on your target species; hairy frogfish and flamboyant cuttlefish are the top requests
- Buoyancy control is critical — silt kicked up from the black sand can cloud visibility for everyone behind you

## Safety Considerations

Hairball is a straightforward muck dive with no significant hazards beyond standard Lembeh precautions. The black sand substrate can be disorienting in reduced visibility — maintain contact with your guide. Blue-ringed octopus are present; never handle any octopus here. Avoid contact with the sandy bottom to prevent silt-out. Always carry an SMB for surface signalling in the busy strait.

## Photography

Hairball is a supermacro photographer's dream. Hairy frogfish are ideal for portrait shots — find one, frame carefully, and take your time. Flamboyant cuttlefish reward patience with extraordinary colour-change sequences. Wonderpus and mimic octopus offer dynamic movement shots as they hunt. Shoot with a 100mm macro or wet dioptre for the smallest subjects. Early morning dives often have the best light penetration and the most active critters.
