## Makawide

Makawide is consistently ranked among the most species-diverse muck dive sites in the Lembeh Strait, North Sulawesi, Indonesia — a significant claim in a location already regarded as the global capital of muck diving. The site is characterised by a greater variety of substrate types than many purely sand-slope sites in the strait, with a mix of volcanic sand, rubble, and scattered coral growth that supports an unusually broad range of critters within a single dive.

## Overview

The Makawide slope descends gradually to 24 meters across a substrate that transitions between open black sand, rubble zones, and small coral patch areas. This habitat variety is the key to the site's exceptional species diversity — different critters prefer different microhabitats, and Makawide packs multiple environments into a compact dive area. Visibility averages 8–15 meters. Currents are typically light. Water temperature holds at 26–29°C year-round.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Lembeh Strait, North Sulawesi, Indonesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Muck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 24 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 8–15 meters (25–50 feet)
- **Current**: Light to moderate
- **Best Time**: Year-round; October to May for calmest conditions

## Marine Life

Makawide's diversity stands out even by Lembeh standards. Pygmy seahorses (Hippocampus bargibanti and H. denise) are found on gorgonian sea fans in the coral patch areas — extremely small, perfectly camouflaged, and invisible without expert guide assistance. Ornate and robust ghost pipefish are regular sightings, drifting near feather stars and crinoids. Mantis shrimp occupy burrows throughout the rubble zones. Various frogfish species — including painted, hairy, and clown frogfish — are distributed across the site. Nudibranchs are abundant and varied. The sandy open areas produce sand-dwelling species including snake eels, stargazers, and the occasional blue-ringed octopus. Flamboyant cuttlefish patrol the mid-slope, and thorny seahorses cling to rubble and low growth.

## Dive Profile

Makawide rewards a structured dive approach that visits each substrate type in sequence. Start at the coral patch zones (typically 15–22 meters) for pygmy seahorse and ghost pipefish work, where guides know the specific fan corals that host resident pygmy seahorses. Then transition to the rubble zones for frogfish and mantis shrimp, before finishing the dive on the open sand slope in the shallower sections. The habitat transitions are the most productive zones — concentrate time at the edges between sand and rubble, and between rubble and coral growth.

## Entry and Exit

Access by banca boat. Entry via back roll or giant stride. The guide will navigate the habitat transitions throughout the dive. Surface with SMB deployed.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Pygmy seahorses are among the most difficult subjects in all of muck diving to find without a guide — they are 1–2 cm long and perfectly match the fan coral polyps they inhabit; watch your guide's pointer direction carefully before you begin searching
- Multiple consecutive dives at Makawide reveal different species on different passes — an afternoon dive at the same site often produces different sightings from a morning dive at the same location
- The site's diversity makes it particularly well-suited for divers who want to tick multiple Lembeh bucket-list species in a single dive
- Intermediate difficulty reflects the need for precise buoyancy near the delicate coral patch areas where fan corals host pygmy seahorses
- A focus light is valuable for illuminating pygmy seahorse colouration and nudibranch detail in the coral zones

## Safety Considerations

The coral patch areas require heightened buoyancy awareness — contact with gorgonian fans harms both the coral and potentially dislodges resident pygmy seahorses. Approach fan corals from a distance and hover neutrally before examining them. Standard blue-ringed octopus caution applies. Do not handle any critters regardless of size. Carry an SMB.

## Photography

Makawide may be the single best all-around macro photography site in the Lembeh Strait for photographers seeking variety in a single dive. Pygmy seahorses demand supermacro technique — a dioptre attachment and steady hand are essential for the 1–2 cm subjects. Ghost pipefish allow more conventional 100mm macro shooting at slightly greater distance. The habitat variety gives photographers a chance to work multiple focal lengths and approaches in one dive. Wide-aperture, supermacro shooting at the coral patch zones with a focus light produces the best pygmy seahorse images.
