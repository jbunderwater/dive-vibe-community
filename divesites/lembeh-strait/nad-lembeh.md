## NAD Lembeh

NAD Lembeh is a muck dive site in the southern Lembeh Strait, North Sulawesi, serving as both an access point for the NAD-Lembeh Resort and a productive dive site in its own right. The house reef connects to the famous Hairball site, allowing divers to explore the black volcanic sand and rubble slopes that define Lembeh's character.

## Overview

NAD Lembeh sits on the Bitung mainland side of the southern strait. The site features a gently sloping black sand bottom from around 5 meters down to 20 meters, dotted with sponge patches, rubble clusters, and scattered debris — the ideal habitat for Lembeh's celebrated critters. NAD-Lembeh Resort operates with a 2:1 guest-to-guide ratio, and expert guides comb the sandy slope searching for rare macro subjects throughout the day.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Lembeh Strait, North Sulawesi, Indonesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Muck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 5–15 meters
- **Water Temperature**: 26–29°C year-round
- **Current**: Typically mild; stronger near the main channel
- **Best Time**: Year-round; August–October for best visibility

## Marine Life

The sandy slope hosts a variety of Lembeh's signature macro critters. Multiple species of frogfish are regularly sighted, including hairy and painted frogfish. Coconut octopus hide inside shell clusters and discarded bottles. Nudibranchs — including Nembrotha, Chromodoris, and Glossodoris species — are scattered across the rubble. Pipefish, seahorses, and ghost pipefish work the sponge and algae patches. Night dives on the house reef produce stargazers, bobtail squid, and Spanish dancer nudibranchs.

## Dive Profile

The dive begins at the entry point in shallow water (3–5 m) over black sand, then follows the slope downward. The most critter-rich zone is typically between 8 and 18 meters where sponge patches and rubble clusters concentrate marine life. Divers are encouraged to move slowly and scan the sand surface — most animals rely on camouflage and will not flee unless disturbed.

## Entry and Exit

Access is by dive boat from NAD-Lembeh Resort or local operators. Entry is by giant stride or back roll. The house reef is also accessible for unlimited shore/entry dives for resort guests. SMB deployment is recommended for safety stop and boat pickup in the channel.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Move slowly and let your guide lead — the dark volcanic sand hides camouflaged subjects that are invisible without a trained eye
- Check inside open clam shells, coconut husks, and debris for coconut octopus
- Bring a torch even on day dives to reveal fluorescent nudibranchs tucked into crevices
- Macro lenses (60mm or 100mm) are ideal; super-macro is useful for tiny shrimp and juvenile frogfish
- The site is accessible at any time of day as a house reef — early morning light creates excellent shooting conditions

## Safety Considerations

Currents are generally mild at this site but can increase near the main channel. Visibility can drop during run-off events after heavy rain. Be careful not to touch or kick the volcanic sand — stirring sediment ruins visibility for everyone. Stonefish and scorpionfish rest on the bottom and are effectively invisible; never place hands on the sandy substrate.

## Photography

NAD Lembeh is an outstanding macro photography site. The black volcanic sand provides a clean, high-contrast background for colorful subjects. Frogfish and nudibranchs are the main subjects, but patient photographers also find flamboyant cuttlefish, dragonets, and decorator crabs. A 1:1 macro or super-macro extension tube allows frame-filling shots of tiny juveniles.
