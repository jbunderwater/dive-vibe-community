## Angel's Window

Angel's Window is the most popular island-side dive site in the northern Lembeh Strait, North Sulawesi, Indonesia. The site is a twin-peaked pinnacle that rises to within a metre of the surface, with a large cave creating a dramatic swim-through on the deep end at around 24–25 metres. Morning sunlight illuminating the cave opening from above inspired the name — the light enters like a window to the angels. This is a genuinely mixed site: a coral slope on one side, a wall with a shelf on the other, and the deep cave pinnacle structure that sets it apart from every other site in the strait.

## Overview

The site's varied topography places it in a different category from Lembeh's pure muck sites. One side of the pinnacle is a coral slope levelling out to a sand flat at 15 metres; the other side drops as a wall to a shelf at 8 metres, then further to 20–32 metres. The swim-through cave at around 24–25 metres is covered in crinoids, feather stars, and soft corals. The surrounding sandy areas and rubble patches provide the muck diving component. Currents can be more variable here than at the protected strait-side sites and timing the dive correctly is advisable. Visibility runs 8–15 meters. Water temperature averages 26–29°C year-round.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Lembeh Strait, North Sulawesi, Indonesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Pinnacle/seamount
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 32 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 8–15 meters (25–50 feet)
- **Current**: Light to moderate
- **Best Time**: Year-round; October to May for calmest conditions

## Marine Life

Angel's Window hosts four species of pygmy seahorse, making it one of the most productive pygmy seahorse sites in the Lembeh Strait. Bargibanti, Denise, and Pontohi species are all documented here, clinging to the gorgonian sea fans (Muricella species) on the pinnacle structure. The deeper cave and wall zones attract species typical of hard structural habitat: leaf scorpionfish, schooling anthias and damselfish, and a notable resident stonefish camouflaged in algae-covered rock. The sandy areas around the pinnacle base produce the muck diving complement: frogfish, ghost pipefish, octopus including Octopus cyanea, cuttlefish, and nudibranchs.

## Dive Profile

This is a dive that benefits from a structured approach due to its depth and topographic variety. Descend directly to the cave swim-through and deeper fan coral zones around 24–32 metres, and spend the first portion of the dive working the structural elements where pygmy seahorses and structural species are found. Then ascend to the mid-slope (15–22 meters) for classic muck diving on the sandy areas, and finish in the shallower coral zones. Monitor air and no-decompression limits carefully — the 32-meter maximum means NDL limits will restrict bottom time at the deepest cave zone, so prioritise that first.

## Entry and Exit

Access by banca boat. Back roll or giant stride entry. The guide descends to the pinnacle structure first; follow closely as the swim-through formation is the navigational anchor for the dive. Currents should be assessed on arrival — the pinnacle's exposed position means conditions vary more than at sheltered strait-side sites. Surface with SMB deployed — particularly important at this site given the deeper profile and longer ascent time required.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Brief your guide explicitly on pygmy seahorse species as a target before descending; without a guide to find them on the specific gorgonian fans, they are effectively impossible to locate
- The swim-through cave formation is the photographic landmark — spend time framing compositions through the cave opening before ascending to the muck slope
- At 32 meters maximum depth, no-decompression limits on a standard recreational dive computer will restrict bottom time at the deepest zone to around 20–25 minutes on a first dive; plan accordingly
- Angel's Window rewards divers who start deep and finish shallow — do not spend too long on the sand slope first or you will run out of NDL before reaching the cave
- The hybrid pinnacle-muck character makes this an excellent contrast dive after days of pure sand-slope muck diving; the cave swim-through is unlike anything else in the strait

## Safety Considerations

The 32-meter depth makes this the most demanding site in this collection and requires solid intermediate dive skills. Monitor no-decompression limits carefully, particularly if this is a second or third dive of the day. Ascend slowly from depth and complete a 3–5 minute safety stop at 5 meters without exception. The cave swim-through is large and accessible with proper training but requires controlled buoyancy in the confined section. Leaf scorpionfish and the resident stonefish in the structural zones are venomous — maintain distance and do not reach into crevices. Assess current before the dive. Carry an SMB for signalling on a longer ascent.

## Photography

Angel's Window offers the widest photographic range of any site in the strait. The cave swim-through allows close-focus wide-angle compositions framing divers silhouetted against the opening above, or the wall faces encrusted with crinoids and soft corals. The gorgonian zones call for supermacro technique for pygmy seahorses. The surrounding sand and rubble provides macro subjects. Photographers with a single lens choice should use a 100mm macro — the pygmy seahorse sightings at the pinnacle justify the supermacro setup, and it covers the muck subjects effectively. A second dive with a wide-angle lens for the cave itself is worth planning.
