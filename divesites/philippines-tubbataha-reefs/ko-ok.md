## Ko-ok

Named after the brown-footed booby that lives on nearby Bird Island, Ko-ok is famous for bumphead parrotfish, soft coral terraces, and evening dives revealing nocturnal reef behavior.

## Overview

Ko-ok takes its name from the Ko-ok booby that lives on Bird Island near Tubbataha. This is typically designated as the last dive of the day, allowing divers to observe the dramatic transition as the sun goes down and the nocturnal reef awakens. The site features impressive terraces filled with white soft corals scattered among huge barrel sponges. During new moon nights, the usually solitary enigmatic sailfin snapper gather here in remarkable aggregations, representing a unique natural phenomenon.

## Site Information

- **Location**: South Atoll, Philippines Tubbataha Reefs
- **Entry Type**: liveaboard
- **Site Type**: Coral reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 25 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 30-45 meters
- **Current**: Variable, can be strong
- **Best Time**: March 16 - June 15 (new moon nights particularly good)

## Marine Life

Ko-ok is known for the largest shoals of bumphead parrotfish found at Tubbataha. The terraces feature spectacular soft coral coverage with barrel sponges providing shelter for various crustaceans and small fish. During new moon phases, sailfin snappers gather in dense aggregations, a rare and special phenomenon. The reef top supports a variety of fish species including emperors, snappers, groupers, and various schooling fish that become active as evening approaches.

## Dive Profile

Begin your dive on the reef top at 5-8 meters, exploring the soft coral terraces and barrel sponge gardens. Gradually descend to 15-20 meters to explore deeper terrace sections. The site is particularly noted as an evening dive, allowing you to observe the reef behavior change as sunset approaches. If the new moon sailfin snapper aggregation is active (seasonal), the densest schools typically appear during golden hour. Plan for a gentle exploration of the terraces before conducting a safety stop at 5 meters during twilight.

## Entry and Exit

Access is by liveaboard tender. Entry is via giant stride or back roll. Descend to the reef top using natural guide features. Explore the terraces at moderate depths (10-20 meters). Plan to ascend gradually as daylight fades, completing your safety stop during the twilight period. Signal the boat with an SMB for pickup - visibility may be reduced at dusk.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Schedule this as an afternoon/evening dive to experience the reef's daily transition
- Time your visit for new moon if possible to potentially see sailfin snapper aggregations
- Watch the bumphead parrotfish behavior - they actively feed during daylight
- Bring a good torch for emerging nocturnal creatures
- This is an excellent site for experiencing reef behaviors beyond daytime visiting species
- The soft coral photography is best with side lighting during the day

## Safety Considerations

This is a relaxed reef dive with a gentle slope. Be aware that lighting conditions change dramatically during evening dives - carry a good torch and surface before complete darkness. Variable currents are possible - be prepared to move shallower if conditions change. Maintain good buoyancy to avoid damaging the delicate soft coral formations.

## Photography

The white soft corals against darker water and backgrounds create striking compositions during daylight hours. Position yourself with the sun behind you to backlight the coral fronds. Evening dives offer unique lighting as the sun approaches the horizon. If sailfin snappers are present during new moon, wide-angle compositions of the dense aggregations are possible.
