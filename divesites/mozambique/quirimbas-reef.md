## Overview

The Quirimbas Archipelago in northern Mozambique is a chain of 32 Indian Ocean islands along Cabo Delgado Province, with some of the healthiest coral reef systems in the western Indian Ocean. Approximately 50 genera of coral and 373 species of reef fish have been recorded in the archipelago's waters. The reefs are largely unexplored and largely undived compared to southern Mozambique's destinations.

Named dive sites within the archipelago confirmed by sources include: Zala Bank (coral-covered sandbank), Rush Hour (schooling game fish, ~14 m), Ibo Island wall (18 m, overhangs), Cliffs of Insanity (vertical wall, 25–40 m, Medjumbe Island), Sambi Sambi (steep drop-off to 40+ m), Neptune's Arm (canyons and pinnacles near Vamizi Island), and Rocha Rocks (30 m, intermediate). None of these is called "Quirimbas Reef."

Confirmed marine life for the archipelago overall includes over 350 species of reef fish, reef sharks, turtles (hawksbill and green), tuna, barracuda, Napoleon wrasse, Spanish mackerel, dolphins, humpback whales (June–November), and whale sharks. Dugongs are present in the area. Advanced sites at Medjumbe feature exceptional wall diving with coral-encrusted caves at exceptional depths.

**Security note:** As of early 2026, at least one major dive operator (Original Diving) has suspended dive holidays to the Quirimbas Archipelago due to the security situation in Cabo Delgado Province (ongoing since 2017). Travellers should check current Foreign Office and operator advisories before planning a trip to this region.
