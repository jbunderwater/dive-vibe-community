# Mosquito Pier

An old military pier off Vieques Island that has become one of Puerto Rico's most productive artificial reef sites — the pilings, rubble, and structure create exceptional conditions for macro life, invertebrates, and the nocturnal diving that Mosquito Pier is famous for.

## Overview

Mosquito Pier (Muelle de Mosquito) is a disused military pier located on the north shore of Vieques Island, the Spanish Virgin Island off Puerto Rico's east coast. The pier's concrete and steel pilings extend into 12 metres of water and have accumulated decades of marine growth — encrusting sponges, hydroids, sea squirts, and tunicates coat every surface in dense concentration. The pier structure has created an artificial reef habitat that draws extraordinary diversity and density of life. Mosquito Pier is particularly celebrated as a night dive destination — the structure is one of the best night diving sites in Puerto Rico, with octopus, moray eels, lobster, and resting fish all more accessible after dark. Visibility averages 15–25 metres. Water temperature is 26–28°C.

## Marine Life

The pier pilings support exceptional diversity due to decades of encrustation. Hawksbill and green turtles are regularly encountered along the pier. Spotted and green morays occupy crevices between the pilings. Caribbean reef octopus are active hunters at the base of the structure. Caribbean spiny lobster shelter under ledges. Tarpon school through the structure. Southern stingrays and flatfish rest on the sandy bottom beneath the pier. Parrotfish, French angelfish, barjacks, and blue-headed wrasses are common reef fish. Goliath groupers and manatees have been documented at the site. Frogfish and seahorse sightings are reported anecdotally by local divers but are not guaranteed.

## Dive Profile

Enter via the ladder at the end of the pier, descend to the sandy base at approximately 12 metres, and navigate along the pier pilings, examining each closely for macro subjects. The pier stretches for nearly a mile and the full length can be covered across multiple dives. At night, begin in the sandy areas beneath the pier where octopus and hunting fish are most active, then work your way along the pilings. Safety stop at the piling tops at 3–4 metres before exiting up the ladder.

## Entry and Exit

Shore dive accessible from the pier itself. Operators or divers drive out to near the end of Mosquito Pier via pick-up truck, then enter the water via a ladder and platform on the left side of the pier — no rocky scramble required. Exit is by the same ladder. The pier stretches nearly a mile into the sea, so the drive out handles the distance. Local operators (Black Beard Sports, Isla Nena Scuba) run regular trips to the pier and organise transport.

## Tips and Recommendations

Night dives at Mosquito Pier are the highlight — plan a day dive first to orient yourself with the structure before the night dive. Carry a primary torch and a backup on night dives. Move extremely slowly along the pilings — macro life is everywhere but requires patient observation. A macro lens setup is ideal for this site.

## Safety Considerations

Boat proximity to the pier structure requires caution on ascent — surface away from the pier and signal the boat clearly. At night, additional caution is required. The structure has some sharp metal edges — do not grab the pilings. DSMB required for open-water ascents.

## Photography

Mosquito Pier is one of Puerto Rico's best macro photography sites. The richly encrusted pilings provide extraordinary close-focus subjects — sea horse portraits, frogfish camouflage, and shrimp cleaning station behaviour are all available. Night dive photography with a single strobe illuminating the piling surfaces creates dramatic macro images. The silversides schooling behaviour in daytime provides wide-angle baitball compositions.
