## Dry Tortugas

Pristine reefs and protected marine park with incredible biodiversity.

## Description

Dry Tortugas National Park is an exceptional remote diving destination located 70 miles west of Key West, Florida. This pristine marine preserve offers 9 documented dive sites ranging from 4.5 to 30 meters depth, with world-class visibility (18-37 meters/60-120 feet typical). The park protects some of the healthiest coral reefs and most abundant marine life in Florida waters, including over 400 species of reef fish, sea turtles, nurse sharks, and vibrant coral ecosystems.

### Diving Opportunities

- **Beginner-friendly reefs**: Little Africa Reef, Bird Key Reef, Fort Jefferson Wall — shallow, clear, fish-rich
- **Intermediate reefs**: White Shoal, Brilliant Shoal, Long Key Reef — moderate depth with distinctive coral formations
- **Advanced pinnacles**: Texas Rock — deep-water seamounts with dramatic topography and black coral
- **Historic wreck diving**: Windjammer Wreck (Avanti, 1907) — shallow, intact, accessible wreck
- **Shore diving**: Fort Jefferson moat wall — unique historic structure accessible from the beach
- **Liveaboard access**: Most sites require boat charter; ferry and private boat options available from Key West

### Accessibility

- **Getting There**: Key West airport (EYW, ~1 hour from Miami). Seaplane services direct to the park. Ferry services and private boat charters depart Key West daily.
- **Dive Operators**: FINZ Dive Center (Key West) and other local operators provide charter boats, gear rental, and guided dives.
- **Accommodation**: Most divers base in Key West (extensive options). Limited camping available at Fort Jefferson.
- **Transportation**: Dry Tortugas Ferry (Yankee Freedom) and private charter boats. Seaplanes available for day trips.
- **Facilities**: Primitive — Fort Jefferson has basic facilities. Bring your own gear or rent in Key West. No dive shops in the park.

### Marine Life & Environment

- **Water Conditions**: Subtropical Caribbean waters, 26-29°C (79-84°F) summer, 22°C (72°F) winter. Exceptional visibility 18-37 meters (60-120 feet). Currents variable, can be strong.
- **Marine Biodiversity**: Over 400 species of reef fish. Sea turtles common. Nurse sharks and reef sharks abundant. Exceptional coral health. Reef squid, barracuda, grouper, snapper, parrotfish, angelfish, triggerfish. Occasional pelagic visitors (large tuna, jacks).
- **Coral**: Stony corals (star, plate, brain, branching), gorgonian corals (sea fans), black coral on deep pinnacles. Some of the healthiest coral in Florida Keys.
- **Conservation**: Strict National Park protection. All coral, marine life, and historic artifacts protected. Follow all regulations. Mooring buoys required to protect reef.

## Additional Information

- **Best Time to Visit**: May to September (warm water 79-84°F, calm seas, optimal visibility). Winter diving possible but water cooler, more variable conditions.
- **Difficulty Range**: Beginner to Advanced sites available. Most intermediate divers can safely experience the park.
- **Diving Season**: Year-round but weather-dependent. Hurricane season July-November can limit access.
- **Cost**: Expensive destination requiring ferry ($193 round trip) or charter boat ($500-1500+ for boat). Factor in Key West accommodation and gear.
- **Currency**: US Dollar
- **Language**: English
- **Safety**: Remote location — verify nearest recompression chamber location (Key West). Dive within certification limits. Strong currents possible. Always use buddy system. Never exceed recreational depth limits at pinnacles.
