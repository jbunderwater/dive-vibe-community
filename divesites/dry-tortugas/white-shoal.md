## White Shoal

White Shoal is a very shallow coral reef near the eastern boundary of Dry Tortugas National Park, marked by a navigational light tower, and serves as one of the park's official Coral Reef Evaluation and Monitoring Project (CREMP) stations.

## Overview

A navigational light tower marks White Shoal, where the reef consists of scattered, very shallow coral heads — ranging from the surface down to about 20 feet. Before modern navigational aids, this shoal was a ship-killer, and divers can still find the scattered remains of historic shipwrecks among the coral. With the exception of elkhorn and staghorn coral, almost every species of stony coral documented in the Florida Keys can be found here. White Shoal is an established coral growth monitoring station, reflecting its ecological significance. The site is ideal for snorkelers and divers to explore simultaneously given its depth range.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Eastern area of Dry Tortugas National Park (near park boundary)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 6 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 18–37 meters (60–120 feet), best during flood tide
- **Current**: Variable; increases near park boundary
- **Best Time**: May to September
- **Water Temperature**: 26–29°C (79–84°F) summer

## Marine Life

White Shoal supports the assemblage of Atlantic tropical reef fish typical of Dry Tortugas reefs under the park's no-take protection. The site is a CREMP coral monitoring station, and its coral diversity — almost every stony coral species found in the Florida Keys except elkhorn and staghorn — provides rich habitat. No confirmed site-specific species list distinct from the broader Dry Tortugas reef community was found in reviewed sources.

## Dive Profile

White Shoal features scattered coral heads rising from sandy bottom, with some coral heads reaching to the surface and the maximum depth around 20 feet. This profile makes it equally accessible to snorkelers and divers, and both can explore the same structures simultaneously. Historic wreck remains are scattered among the coral. Best visibility is during flood tide. The site's position near the park boundary means current can increase beyond the boundary buoys — stay inside the park markers.

## Entry and Exit

Access is by private or charter boat only. The eastern park boundary location makes White Shoal one of the more distant sites from Garden Key. Anchor on sandy bottom clear of coral. Dive charter vessels from Key West offer multi-site day or overnight trips. The Yankee Freedom III ferry cannot carry compressed tanks; air-equipped charter or private vessels are required.

## Tips and Recommendations

- One of the best sites in the park for mixed snorkel-and-dive groups — shallow enough for snorkelers, diverse enough for divers
- Dive during flood tide for peak visibility
- Investigate crevices between coral heads for lobster and reef fish
- Do not touch or disturb coral — White Shoal is an active long-term monitoring station
- Carry a surface marker buoy given proximity to the park boundary

## Safety Considerations

Current can increase near the park boundary and in open water beyond the buoys. Carry a surface marker buoy. The site's remote position within an already remote national park demands self-sufficiency. No emergency support exists in the park; the nearest hyperbaric chamber is in Key West. Dive with a charter or surface-equipped vessel, carry VHF radio, and display a dive flag.
