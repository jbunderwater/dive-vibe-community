## Fort Jefferson Wall

The moat wall of Fort Jefferson — the largest masonry structure in the Western Hemisphere — has become a thriving marine habitat accessible by shore entry, where reef squid, nurse sharks, and hogfish inhabit the structure in very shallow water.

## Overview

Fort Jefferson's massive hexagonal moat wall was designed as a military defense, but nature has had other plans. Today the wall's outer face hosts a community of reef fish, squid, and invertebrates in water just 6–8 feet deep, making it one of the most accessible and beginner-friendly dives in the Dry Tortugas. The NPS notes that reef squid, nurse sharks, hogfish, cement barrels, and anchor chains are among the marine features along the wall. Swimming inside the moat is prohibited; the dive is along the wall's exterior.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Fort Jefferson moat wall outer face, Garden Key, Dry Tortugas National Park
- **Entry Type**: Shore entry
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 2.5 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 18–37 meters (60–120 feet)
- **Current**: Generally minimal along the sheltered moat wall
- **Best Time**: May to September
- **Water Temperature**: 26–29°C (79–84°F) summer

## Marine Life

The NPS specifically identifies the following species at the Fort Jefferson moat wall: reef squid, nurse sharks, hogfish, and various reef fish. Night diving along the moat wall reveals octopus, basket starfish, spiny lobster, decorator crabs, arrowhead crabs, coral shrimp, and squid — the NPS notes that "fish are much more docile at night and can be approached much closer." American crocodile sightings have been documented within the park generally, though the NPS attributes these to the park at large rather than specifically to the moat wall.

## Dive Profile

Enter from the beach at Garden Key and wade to the moat wall's exterior. The wall drops into 6–8 feet of water; reef fish and squid inhabit the structure from the surface down to the sandy bottom. Explore along the wall face, observing coral growth, squid behavior, and nurse sharks resting on the bottom. The very shallow depth allows extended bottom time. No safety stop required. Return via the same beach entry.

## Entry and Exit

Enter directly from the beach at Garden Key adjacent to Fort Jefferson. No special equipment beyond mask and fins is required for snorkeling; tanks and BCD for scuba. Check sea state and wind before entry — surf and surge can develop on exposed sides of the key. Swimming inside the moat is prohibited; explore the exterior wall only.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The site is accessible without a boat — an advantage for ferry day-trippers from Key West
- Night diving here is particularly rewarding; the NPS recommends this as one of the park's best night dive experiences
- Reef squid are highly intelligent and curious — move slowly and they may approach
- Snorkelers can enjoy the same features as scuba divers given the very shallow depth
- A small torch is useful for peering into recesses in the wall structure
- Comply with NPS regulations regarding proximity to the fort structure

## Safety Considerations

The very shallow depth eliminates decompression concerns. Entry conditions are the primary hazard — check for wave surge and surf before entering. Never swim inside the moat (protected area). The park's remoteness from Key West (70 miles) means no onsite emergency dive support; the nearest hyperbaric chamber is in Key West. Always dive with a buddy and carry a marine VHF radio.
