## Windjammer Wreck

Windjammer Wreck is a historic wreck dive in Dry Tortugas, North America.

## Overview

The Windjammer Wreck is the remains of the iron-hulled, three-masted sailing ship Avanti, built in 1875 and lost on January 22, 1907, after battling a Gulf hurricane — all 19 crew survived. The 261-foot hull rests in just 6–7 metres of water roughly 1,100 yards southwest of Loggerhead Key, its iron skeleton standing upright and well-preserved due to iron's corrosion resistance, with the wreck now colonised by stony and gorgonian corals and populated by goliath grouper, barracuda, snapper, and spiny lobster.

## Wreck History

The vessel was built in 1875 by John Reid & Co. in Port Glasgow, Scotland, originally named *Killean* and registered by Mackinnon Frew & Co. In 1891 she was sold to the French company Antoine-Dominique Bordes & Fils and renamed *Antonin*. She passed to Norwegian owners Acties Avanti in 1901, taking the name *Avanti* by which the wreck is now identified. On January 22, 1907, while carrying a cargo of lumber from Pensacola, Florida, to Montevideo, Uruguay, she struck Loggerhead Reef; NPS maritime archaeologists believe she was lost in a storm, though no historical documents record the details of the sinking. The crew of 19 survived. The hull lay undiscovered until 1970 and was positively identified in 1990. The ship measures 261.4 feet long, 39.3 feet in beam, and 17.5 feet in draft, with a gross tonnage of 1,862 tons. It was listed on the National Register of Historic Places because of its archaeological significance.

## Marine Life

Goliath grouper are the signature species of the Windjammer, with large individuals resident among the iron ribs and keel. Yellowtail and black snapper school around the gorgonian-encrusted framework, and spiny lobster are plentiful in the structure's recesses. Nurse sharks rest on the sandy bottom alongside the wreck, and angelfish, sergeant majors, blue tang, and wrasses are common. Brain coral and stony corals have colonised portions of the hull. The NPS notes that a goliath grouper is often encountered in the hull itself in shallow, clear water.

## Dive Profile

The wreck lies approximately 1,100 yards southwest of Loggerhead Key in 18–21 feet (6–7 m) of water. The hull has collapsed into two primary debris fields: a ~110-foot bow section oriented east–west and an equal-length stern section oriented north–south, which retains masts and rigging. Iron's corrosion resistance has preserved the structure better than a wooden vessel would survive. The NPS produces a laminated underwater trail guide available at the visitor centre. Visibility is typically excellent over the white sand bottom.

## Entry and Exit

Access is by boat only — the park is 70 miles west of Key West. Day visitors typically arrive aboard the Yankee Freedom III high-speed ferry from Key West (Garden Key), but the Windjammer is off Loggerhead Key and requires a separate boat ride within the park. Private boats and dive charter vessels out of Key West and the Florida Keys can anchor nearby; overnight anchoring is permitted in sand and rubble within one nautical mile of the Garden Key harbour light. Note that the Yankee Freedom ferry cannot carry compressed dive tanks; divers must arrive via private or charter vessel equipped with tanks. A mooring buoy marks the site — contact the NPS on VHF channel 16 for current mooring status before arrival.

## Tips and Recommendations

A laminated trail guide from the NPS visitor centre helps orient divers to the wreck's two debris fields. Spending time at the bow and stern separately on a single tank is feasible given the 20-foot depth. Bring a dive light to peer into recesses where lobster and grouper shelter. Do not touch, move, or remove any artefacts — removal is a federal offence with serious penalties.

## Safety Considerations

Maximum depth is only about 7 metres, making depth and narcosis non-issues, but the park's remoteness demands self-sufficiency: no dive support infrastructure exists on the island, the nearest chamber is in Key West, and weather can deteriorate rapidly in the Gulf. Watch for fire coral growing on the wreck structure. Observe lionfish, which have colonised Gulf reefs and wrecks; do not handle them. Respect the archaeological protection zone — the wreck is a National Register site.

## Photography

The shallow depth and excellent visibility make the Windjammer ideal for wide-angle reef/wreck photography. Resident goliath grouper offer exceptional close-approach opportunities; stay calm and let them approach rather than chasing. The iron ribs silhouetted against the surface light provide strong compositional lines. Natural light is sufficient for topside shots, but a strobe or video light brings out the orange of the gorgonians and the colour of the encrusting corals.
