## Windjammer Wreck

The Windjammer Wreck is the remains of the iron-hulled sailing ship *Avanti*, lost on Loggerhead Reef in 1907, resting in just 20 feet of water and encrusted with coral — one of the most accessible wreck dives in the United States.

## Overview

The Windjammer Wreck preserves the remains of the *Avanti*, a three-masted, iron-hulled sailing ship built in 1875 that struck Loggerhead Reef on January 22, 1907, while carrying a lumber cargo from Pensacola to Montevideo, Uruguay. All 19 crew survived. The vessel sank in approximately 20 feet (6–7 meters) of water about 1,100 yards southwest of Loggerhead Key, where its iron skeleton has remained largely intact due to iron's natural resistance to corrosion. The hull is now colonized by stony and gorgonian corals and populated by goliath grouper, snapper, and spiny lobster. The NPS produces a laminated underwater trail guide available at the Garden Key visitor center.

## Wreck History

The vessel has passed through three names. Built in 1875 by John Reid & Co. in Port Glasgow, Scotland, she was first registered as *Killean*. She was subsequently sold to the French company Antoine-Dominique Bordes & Fils and renamed *Antonin*; this sale is documented in one NPS source as occurring in 1891, though another NPS article cites the name *Avanti* as adopted in 1901 when she was sold to Norwegian owners (Acties Avanti). The NPS states her dimensions as 261.4 feet long, 39.3 feet beam, and 17.5 feet draft, with a gross tonnage of 1,862 tons.

**Sinking date conflict:** NPS official sources give the date as January 22, 1907. At least one secondary source (South Dade News Leader) states January 9, 1907. The NPS documentation is the authoritative record.

**Hull material conflict:** The NPS consistently describes the vessel as iron-hulled. At least one non-NPS source describes it as "steel-hulled." NPS is the authoritative source; the vessel is iron-hulled.

The cause of sinking is not recorded in historical documents. NPS maritime archaeologists speculate the vessel was lost in a storm, but no eyewitness accounts or contemporary records survive. The wreck was discovered in 1971 by an archaeological survey crew and not positively identified until 1990. It is listed on the National Register of Historic Places.

## Marine Life

The NPS notes that the Windjammer is "an aquarium of reef-dwelling, free-swimming, and benthic life." Goliath grouper are the signature species; the NPS specifically mentions that a goliath grouper is often encountered within the hull. Yellowtail and black snapper school around the gorgonian-encrusted framework. Spiny lobster shelter in the structure's recesses. The NPS photograph captions identify hogfish at the wreck. Nurse sharks have been reported resting on the sandy bottom near the wreck in general Dry Tortugas dive accounts. Brain coral and stony corals have colonized portions of the hull.

## Dive Profile

The wreck lies approximately 1,100 yards southwest of Loggerhead Key in 18–21 feet (6–7 meters) of water. The hull has collapsed into two primary debris fields: a bow section approximately 110 feet long oriented east–west, and a roughly equal-length midships and stern section oriented north–south that retains masts and rigging. The wreck sits on a white sand bottom, providing excellent contrast and visibility. Visibility is typically excellent — the NPS describes it as suitable for snorkelers and divers together. A mooring buoy marks the site.

## Entry and Exit

Access is by boat only — the park is 70 miles west of Key West. The Windjammer is off Loggerhead Key, not Garden Key; visitors who arrive on the Yankee Freedom III ferry at Garden Key would need a separate vessel to reach the wreck. Private boats and dive charter vessels can anchor or use the mooring buoy on sandy bottom away from the wreck. The Yankee Freedom ferry cannot carry compressed dive tanks; divers must arrive via private or charter vessel equipped with tanks. A NPS mooring buoy marks the site — contact the NPS on VHF channel 16 for current mooring status.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Collect the NPS laminated underwater trail guide from the Garden Key visitor center before diving
- Spend time at both the bow and stern sections, which are oriented in different directions
- Bring a dive light to peer into recesses where lobster and grouper shelter
- Do not touch, move, or remove any artifacts — the wreck is a National Register site and removal is a federal offense with serious penalties
- Lionfish have colonized Gulf reefs and wrecks; observe but do not handle them

## Safety Considerations

Maximum depth is approximately 7 meters, making depth-related narcosis and decompression non-issues. The park's remoteness demands self-sufficiency: no dive support infrastructure exists on the island, the nearest chamber is in Key West, and weather can deteriorate rapidly in the Gulf. Watch for fire coral growing on the wreck structure. Observe but do not handle lionfish. Respect the archaeological protection zone.

## Photography

The shallow depth and white sand bottom make the Windjammer ideal for wide-angle wreck photography. Resident goliath grouper offer exceptional close-approach opportunities — stay calm and let them approach rather than chasing. The iron ribs silhouetted against the surface light provide strong compositional lines. Natural light is sufficient in shallow sections, but a strobe or video light brings out the orange of the gorgonians and the color of the encrusting corals.
