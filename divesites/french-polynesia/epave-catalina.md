## Epave Catalina

Epave Catalina is a wreck dive site in the Tahiti lagoon, French Polynesia, featuring the remains of a Consolidated PBY Catalina flying boat — a WWII-era maritime patrol and search-and-rescue amphibious aircraft that sank and now rests on the sandy lagoon floor.

## Overview

The Catalina PBY was one of the most widely used Allied aircraft of the Second World War, serving in the Pacific theatre on long-range patrol and rescue missions. The wreck at Tahiti lies on a sandy bottom at approximately 20 metres depth in clear lagoon water. The aircraft's distinctive twin-engine parasol-wing design is still recognisable, with the fuselage, wing sections, and engine nacelles intact enough to provide a compelling structural framework for marine life colonisation. Visibility in the lagoon here averages 25–30 metres, and water temperature is a warm 27–28°C year-round.

## What to Expect

The Catalina wreck is thoroughly encrusted in hard and soft corals, sponges, and tunicates accumulated over decades of submersion. The fuselage interior and engine compartments harbour glassfish schools, lionfish, and moray eels. The sandy flat around the wreck is habitat for stingrays and flatfish. Blacktip reef sharks circle the site on most dives. The aircraft's cockpit section is of particular interest to history-minded divers, while photographers seek wide-angle shots of the coral-covered wing span. At 20 metres with calm lagoon conditions, the site is accessible to entry-level divers, though buoyancy control is important to avoid disturbing the wreck.

## Conditions

- **Best season**: Year-round; April–November for best visibility
- **Visibility**: 25–30 m typical
- **Current**: Very mild in lagoon setting
- **Water temperature**: 27–28°C
- **Entry**: Boat dive

## Tips

A torch is recommended for peering into the cockpit and fuselage sections. Dive operators in the Papeete area include this wreck as part of combination dive trips. The shallow depth allows a long bottom time and makes it suitable for Open Water divers on their first wreck experience.
