# Gab Gab Reef

A popular reef site at Gab Gab Beach inside Naval Station Guam on the western coast of Apra Harbor. The reef slopes gently from 15 feet to 75 feet and is consistently calm with good visibility. Notable for a high density of sea turtles, resident anemones with clownfish, parrotfish, and the occasional view of the Atlantis Submarine passing by. Access requires a valid photo ID for entry to the naval base.

## Overview

Gab Gab Reef is located off Gab Gab Beach within Naval Station Guam on the western side of Apra Harbor. The protected harbor position means the water is consistently calm with no waves or current, and visibility typically ranges 75–100 feet — considerably better than much of the inner harbor. The reef is a gently sloping structure starting at approximately 15 feet and descending to around 75 feet.

The site has earned a strong reputation for the density and habituation of its sea turtle population. Parrotfish, which are uncommon at many other Guam sites, are found here in abundance. Two large resident anemones hosting clownfish and cleaner shrimp colonies are established features of the reef. Large jacks and trumpetfish are also commonly observed. The Atlantis Submarine (tourist submarine) operates in the area, and divers may encounter it underwater.

Access to this shore dive requires a valid photo ID to enter Naval Station Guam. Non-military divers must follow the base entry process. Entry and exit is via stairs with a railing at the beach, though the rocks at the stair base are slippery.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Gab Gab Beach, Naval Station Guam, Apra Harbor

## Marine Life

Sea turtles are seen on most dives and in high density relative to other Guam sites. Two large resident anemones with clownfish colonies and cleaner shrimp are established reef features. Parrotfish are abundant here, notably more so than at other island sites. Trumpetfish are regularly present. Large jacks patrol the reef. A nurse shark has been reported at this site. The Atlantis Submarine may pass during the dive, offering an unusual encounter.

## Dive Profile

Enter via the stairs and descend to the reef. The structure slopes gently from 15 feet at the inner reef to approximately 75 feet at the deeper outer sections. The most productive diving is in the 15–50 foot range where coral cover and fish life are densest. The two large resident anemones are reliable landmarks and can serve as navigation anchors. Circle the reef formation before ascending for the safety stop. The shallow depth allows generous bottom time.

## Entry and Exit

Shore entry via the stairs at Gab Gab Beach. The railing assists entry and exit, but rocks at the base of the stairs are slippery — use care. Concrete float-securing buckets are positioned at approximately 15 and 25 feet for orientation. Exit at the same stairs. Proof of certification (dive card) is required on the base.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Bring a dive certification card — the base may require it
- Check Naval Station Guam base access requirements before your visit; rules for civilian access can change
- The resident anemones make good landmarks for navigation and for locating the clownfish
- The parrotfish here are unusually abundant — a good photographic opportunity
- Watch for the submarine passing; it's an unusual and memorable underwater sight

## Safety Considerations

One of Guam's most benign dive sites. The harbor location, calm conditions, and shallow reef make this accessible to beginners. Slippery rocks at the stair entry are the main hazard — move carefully. Maintain awareness of submarine activity when near the surface.
