# Gab Gab Reef

A popular shore reef dive inside Naval Base Guam off Gab Gab Beach, accessible via stairs leading directly into calm harbor water. A sloping reef with abundant anemones, turtles, moray eels, and giant trevally. Beginner-friendly with no current and 8–21 meters visibility.

## Overview

Gab Gab Reef (also called Gab Gab I) is located off Gab Gab Beach within Naval Base Guam on the western side of Apra Harbor. Access requires a military base pass or accompaniment by a military sponsor; some dive operators can arrange access. Entry is by stairs with a railing leading directly into the water, making this one of the most diver-friendly entry points on Guam.

The reef slopes gently from the entry steps out to a drop where the bottom falls from around 6 meters to over 30 meters. Sources cite the primary dive zone from shore to the reef slope as reaching a maximum of approximately 18 meters (60 feet), with a deeper plateau area (Gab Gab II) beyond. The harbor location provides calm conditions — no surge, no significant current — making this an excellent site for beginner divers, check-out dives, and extended photography.

A separate deeper site, Gab Gab II (also called the Atlantis Submarine reef), is accessible by a 4–5 minute open-water swim and sits at approximately 14 meters (45 feet). Gab Gab II features feeding stations established for the Atlantis tourist submarine operation, which concentrate large numbers of fish. The Atlantis submarine itself descends to approximately 44 meters at this site.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Gab Gab Beach, Naval Base Guam, Apra Harbor
- **Entry Type**: Shore entry
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 18 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 8–21 meters (harbor location; generally good, improving with morning dives)
- **Water Temperature**: approximately 29°C
- **Current**: Minimal inside the harbor
- **Best Time**: Year-round

## Marine Life

Confirmed sightings at this site per PADI, Guam Dive Guide, and MDA Guam: sea turtles, moray eels, giant trevally (large jacks), and abundant anemones. Reef fish are present in high numbers; the Gab Gab II feeding stations maintained by Atlantis submarine operations concentrate nurse sharks, eels, octopus, nudibranchs, and large anemone colonies. Whithetip reef sharks have been reported at the site by divers, though these sightings are not consistent across all sources.

Note: The current markdown previously mentioned a "tourist submarine operating in the area" as an unusual underwater encounter — this refers to the Atlantis tourist submarine that operates at Gab Gab II, not the main Gab Gab I reef. Divers at Gab Gab II may observe the submarine during its daytime operations.

## Dive Profile

Enter via the stairs and swim out from the beach, heading north toward the drop-off zone. Concrete buoy tie-off points at approximately 4.5 and 7.5 meters serve as navigation landmarks. The reef slopes from the entry area to the drop, where the bottom falls away. For Gab Gab II, navigate by compass on an open-water crossing of 4–5 minutes to reach the plateau with the feeding stations. The shallow depth at both sites allows generous bottom time.

## Entry and Exit

Shore entry via the stairs at Gab Gab Beach. The base of the stairs has slippery rocks — enter carefully. Military base access is required; verify entry arrangements with your dive operator before planning this dive.

## Tips and Recommendations

- This is an excellent site for check-out dives and beginners due to calm conditions and easy entry
- Head north from the stairs toward the drop for the best reef structure
- Gab Gab II requires a compass crossing — bring a compass and buddy familiar with the navigation
- Military access must be arranged in advance — confirm with your operator

## Safety Considerations

A gentle harbor site with minimal hazards. The slippery rocks at the base of the entry stairs are the primary hazard on entry. The harbor location means the Atlantis submarine operates nearby at Gab Gab II — maintain awareness. The open-water crossing to Gab Gab II requires compass navigation; do not attempt without a guide or thorough briefing.
