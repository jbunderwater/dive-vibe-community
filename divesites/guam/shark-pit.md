# Shark Pit

A former military dumping ground along Orote Peninsula that has become one of Guam's most unusual dives. A large pinnacle rises from approximately 40 meters to within 7 meters of the surface. Sea fans at 12 meters are among the shallowest on Guam. WWII-era military equipment — jeeps, anchors, landing craft, and other artifacts — is scattered across the site. Green turtles are regular visitors. Pyramid butterflyfish school around the pinnacle in large numbers.

## Overview

Shark Pit is located along the Orote Peninsula in southern Guam, in waters that served as a military disposal area after World War II. The accumulated debris and natural reef topography combine to create a dive unlike anything else on Guam.

The defining feature is a large rock pinnacle rising from approximately 40 meters (130 feet) to within about 7 meters (25 feet) of the surface. The pinnacle's upper structure at 12 meters (40 feet) hosts gorgonian sea fans — notably, these are among the shallowest sea fans found anywhere on Guam. The walls support soft corals as well.

The surrounding seafloor is scattered with post-WWII military disposal material: jeeps, anchors, bottles, landing craft, wheels, and assorted metal equipment from the war's end. Deeper at around 40 meters are reported to be larger artifacts including tracked vehicles, though these are at the edge of recreational depth limits. The site is also known as "Buffalo Graveyard" due to the presence of Buffalo amphibious vehicles at depth.

Note on naming: the site's name derives from its history as a shark-frequented area, but multiple current sources indicate that shark sightings are now historically frequent but presently rare. Green sea turtles are now the most reliable large-animal encounter.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Orote Peninsula, southern Guam (boat access)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 40 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 21–30 meters (70–100 feet)
- **Water Temperature**: 28–29 degrees C
- **Current**: Variable along the peninsula
- **Best Time**: Year-round

## Marine Life

Green sea turtles are regularly sighted and are the most reliable large-animal encounter. Large schools of pyramid butterflyfish are a defining feature of this site. Arc-eyed hawkfish and velvet hawkfish are frequently observed posing on the coral surfaces. Gorgonian sea fans at 12 meters are a particular draw — unusually shallow for Guam. Moray eels shelter in the WWII debris. Sharks are historically associated with the site but are currently rare.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the pinnacle. The upper reef at 7 meters provides an extended safety stop area. The sea fans at 12 meters are an early highlight. Explore the pinnacle walls from 12 down to 25 meters for the best soft coral and hawkfish. The WWII artifacts on the surrounding sandy bottom are worth circling — jeeps, anchors, landing craft, and other metal debris create an eerie historical landscape. Deeper sections of the artifact field approach 40 meters; plan depth limits carefully. "Boulder Alley" north of the main pinnacle is worth exploring for additional marine life. Currents along the peninsula can be variable — follow the dive operator's assessment for current direction.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive only. Giant stride entry. Boat pickup after the dive. The peninsula's orientation means currents can be variable — discuss conditions with the operator before the dive.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The sea fans at 12 meters are photogenic and unusual — a wide-angle lens captures the full fan width against the blue
- Explore the artifact debris carefully — new items turn up as sediment shifts
- Do not touch or remove any WWII artifacts — they are historically significant
- Live ordnance (ammunition) has been reported on the site — do not touch any cylindrical metal objects
- Boulder Alley north of the main pinnacle adds variety to the second half of the dive

## Safety Considerations

Currents along the Orote Peninsula can be variable and should be assessed with your operator before diving. The deeper sections of the artifact field approach recreational depth limits — monitor depth carefully on the bottom exploration. The pinnacle's relief from 7 to 40 meters means it is easy to drift deeper than planned. Live ordnance has been reported at the site — treat unidentified metal objects with caution and do not disturb them. WWII artifacts are fragile; observe without contact.
