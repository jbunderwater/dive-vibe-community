# Tokai Maru

A WWII Japanese passenger-cargo transport, 139 meters long and 8,365 GRT, torpedoed and sunk in Apra Harbor on 27 August 1943 by USS Snapper. The wreck rests immediately adjacent to — and touching — the WWI German raider SMS Cormoran, creating one of the world's most historically remarkable dive sites: two wrecks from different World Wars that can be touched simultaneously.

## Overview

Tokai Maru was built by Mitsubishi Zosen's Nagasaki shipyard — laid down 26 November 1929, launched 15 May 1930, and completed 14 July 1930. The vessel measured 139 meters in length with a beam of 18 meters and a gross registered tonnage of 8,365 tons. Powered by two Sulzer diesel engines producing 8,262 horsepower, she could make 18.4 knots. She originally operated a Kobe-to-New York passenger-cargo route via the Panama Canal.

On 12 October 1941 the Imperial Japanese Navy requisitioned Tokai Maru as a military transport. She was armed with two 12 cm guns and four anti-aircraft guns in mid-1942. While moored in Apra Harbor during the Japanese occupation of Guam, she was attacked three times by American submarines: USS Flying Fish struck her with one torpedo on 26 January 1943 (damaging but not sinking her); USS Permit attacked on 5 May 1943; and finally USS Snapper (SS-185) delivered the fatal blow on 27 August 1943, striking her port side engine room with two torpedoes. She sank by the stern and came to rest directly on top of — and touching — the previously scuttled SMS Cormoran.

The two hulls make contact at approximately 29 meters (95 feet) depth, allowing divers to simultaneously place their hands on wrecks from two different World Wars. This is widely described as a unique occurrence in the world.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Apra Harbor, Guam
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 40 meters
- **Upper Sections**: forward bridge starting from approximately 12 meters (40 feet)
- **Typical Visibility**: 8–12 meters (Apra Harbor)

- **Best Time**: Year-round

## Marine Life

No site-specific marine life survey data is available for the Tokai Maru wreck. The massive hull structure functions as artificial reef and provides shelter for harbor fish communities. General Apra Harbor reef species are present. No specific species have been confirmed at this wreck site in available sources.

## Dive Profile

Descend the shared mooring line. The forward bridge is the shallowest section, beginning at approximately 12 meters (40 feet), making it accessible even at low gas levels. The hull extends along its 139-meter length with six empty cargo holds, intact engine room with two Sulzer engines on original catwalks, visible depth charges on the stern, and gun platforms. Evidence of torpedo and strafing damage is visible on the bow and near cargo hold #4. The deepest sections reach approximately 36–40 meters — plan your deepest section first while gas supply is full. The point where Tokai Maru touches SMS Cormoran is located at around 29 meters along the aft bridge contours. No single dive can cover the full length of this 139-meter vessel — plan at least two dives.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive only, from Apra Harbor operators. The Tokai Maru shares a mooring buoy with the adjacent SMS Cormoran. Giant stride entry. Harbor conditions are calm at the surface. Always deploy an SMB on ascent — boat traffic is active in the harbor.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The touching-two-World-Wars moment is the signature experience — the contact point is at about 29 meters, so include it on your deepest phase
- At 139 meters, plan a minimum of two dives: bow-to-midship on the first, midship-to-stern on the second
- Carry a powerful torch — harbor visibility averages 8–12 meters and can be lower after heavy vessel traffic; morning dives tend to have better visibility
- Nitrox is recommended given the depth and the amount to explore
- Research both ships' histories before diving — the historical layering makes this a remarkable site

## Safety Considerations

The deepest sections reach approximately 36–40 meters, making this a demanding deep wreck dive. Gas management is critical — plan conservatively and do not exceed your planned bottom time. Narcosis is possible at maximum depth. The wreck structure is deteriorating; do not touch hull plating. Interior penetration requires wreck diving certification. Harbor boat traffic is active — always deploy an SMB on ascent.
