# Tokai Maru

A WWII Japanese transport, built in 1930 by Mitsubishi Heavy Industries at Nagasaki, measuring 134 meters (440 feet) in length and 8,365 gross registered tons, sunk in Apra Harbor on 27 August 1943 by the USS Snapper. Adjacent to the WWI German raider SMS Cormoran — one of the only dive sites in the world where divers can touch wrecks from two World Wars simultaneously.

## Overview

The Tokai Maru was laid down on 26 November 1929 at the Mitsubishi Heavy Industries shipyard in Nagasaki, launched on 15 May 1930, and completed by August 1930. She measured 134 meters (440 feet) long and 18 meters (60 feet) wide, with a gross registered tonnage of 8,365 tons. Before the war she operated on Osaka Shosen Kaisha's Kobe-to-New York service via the Panama Canal as a high-speed cargo-passenger vessel. In 1941 she was requisitioned as an auxiliary transport for the Imperial Japanese Navy.

The vessel suffered two submarine attacks in Apra Harbor:

- **24 January 1943**: The USS Flying Fish (SS-229) fired two torpedoes; one struck the Tokai Maru but did not sink her.
- **27 August 1943**: The USS Snapper (SS-185) under Lieutenant Commander Merrill K. Clementson fired four torpedoes; sources vary on how many struck — combined fleet records indicate two hits in the engine room portside, while other accounts state three hits. The Tokai Maru sank and settled with an 85-degree list to her port side.

The wreck rests at approximately 37 meters (120 feet) at her deepest point, with the shallowest accessible point — the upper forward bridge area — at around 12 meters (40 feet). The Tokai Maru settled immediately adjacent to the WWI wreck SMS Cormoran, which had been scuttled in the same harbor in 1917. At the point where the two hulls meet, divers can reach out and touch both vessels simultaneously.

Note on Cormoran length: The original markdown stated 87 meters for the Cormoran's length. Guampedia records the vessel at 334.9 feet (approximately 102 meters), while X-Ray Mag cites 88 meters. The Tokai Maru at 134 meters is confirmed by multiple independent sources.

At least four unexploded depth charges remain visible in the Tokai Maru's stern area — do not approach or disturb them.

## Marine Life

The massive hull structure has become an artificial reef with coral and sponge growth on the exterior. No site-specific marine species list has been confirmed by sources consulted for this site specifically. Guam's inner harbor fauna (reef fish, occasional sharks) can be expected around the wreck. No species should be attributed to this specific wreck without site-specific source confirmation.

## Dive Profile

Descend the mooring line — attached to the Tokai Maru. The 134-meter length means a single dive cannot cover the full vessel; plan dives to focus on either the bow or stern section, plus the contact point with the Cormoran. Torpedo and strafing damage is visible on the bow and cargo hold number four. The engine room still has intact engines and catwalks. Truck frames and other cargo remain in the holds. The deepest accessible sections reach 37 meters — plan your deepest exploration first while gas supply is full. Wreck penetration of interior spaces requires wreck diver certification. The unexploded depth charges in the stern area must not be touched or disturbed.

The point where the Tokai Maru and SMS Cormoran touch is a mid-section area reachable at around 24–30 meters. This is the signature moment of the dive.

## Entry and Exit

Access by dive boat from Apra Harbor operators (Cabras Marina area). The shared mooring buoy provides a convenient descent line. Entry by giant stride. Harbor conditions are calm year-round.

## Tips and Recommendations

- At 134 meters, this wreck requires at least two dives to explore properly — plan bow and stern on separate dives
- Nitrox (32%) is recommended by MDA Guam to extend bottom time at average depths of 24–33 meters
- Visibility is harbor-dependent (8–18 meters); carry a powerful dive light for interior passages
- The two-wars contact point is the must-experience moment — allow time and gas for it before exploring further
- Do not approach the unexploded depth charges in the stern

## Safety Considerations

At 37 meters maximum depth with harbor visibility averaging 8–18 meters, this is a challenging deep wreck dive. Gas management is critical — plan conservatively and do not exceed planned bottom time. The wreck is deteriorating; avoid contact with hull plating that may be unstable. Interior penetration is for wreck-certified divers only. The harbor location means boat traffic overhead — deploy a surface marker buoy during ascent.
