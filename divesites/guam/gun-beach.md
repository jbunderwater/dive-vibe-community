# Gun Beach

The only scuba dive site in Tumon Bay, Guam's main resort district, and regularly cited as the best shore dive on the island. Named for a WWII Japanese gun emplacement on the beach. Shore entry through the reef flat via a cable/pipe channel leads to a sloping reef of candlestick corals that descends from 15 feet to 100 feet before transitioning to sandy plain. Green sea turtles are seen on virtually every dive. Located within the Tumon Marine Preserve.

## Overview

Gun Beach is situated at the northern end of Tumon Bay within the Tumon Bay Marine Preserve, adjacent to Guam's main hotel district. The site takes its name from a WWII Japanese gun emplacement that still stands on the beach above the entry point. It is the only designated scuba dive site within Tumon Bay and is consistently rated among Guam's best shore dives for its combination of convenient access, protected status, and consistently productive marine life.

Entry follows a cable/pipe channel from the beach through the shallow reef flat — approximately 30 feet of walking from the car park to knee-deep water. The channel provides a safe passage through the otherwise sharp, shallow coral. Once past the reef line, a sloping reef extends outward covered in distinctive candlestick corals, beginning at 15 feet and descending gradually to around 100 feet before giving way to a sandy plain. The marine preserve status has allowed turtle and fish populations to become dense and habituated to divers.

Visibility is typically around 60 feet (18 meters). Manta rays have been reported as occasional visitors, though this is not a reliable encounter.

## Site Information

- **Location**: North Tumon Bay, within Tumon Marine Preserve, Guam
- **Entry Type**: Shore entry
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: ~18 meters (60 feet)
- **Water Temperature**: 28–29°C
- **Current**: Generally mild inside the bay
- **Best Time**: Year-round; also an excellent night dive

## Marine Life

Green sea turtles are encountered on virtually every dive and are the signature attraction — multiple turtles resting among the hard corals are commonly reported. Schools of goatfish, humpnose surgeonfish, and convict tangs are regularly observed. Rays rest on sandy areas between reef sections. Octopus are found in rocky crevices and are more easily spotted on night dives. The candlestick coral formations provide habitat for colourful reef fish. Manta rays have been recorded here on occasion but are not a reliable encounter.

## Dive Profile

Follow the cable/pipe channel from shore through the reef flat — stay on the channel to avoid the sharp shallow coral. Once outside the reef line, head left (north, toward the hotel area) for the best candlestick coral formations. The reef slopes from 5 meters down to approximately 30 meters before becoming sandy bottom. Turtles are encountered throughout the depth range. Explore the reef edge and sandy areas for rays and octopus before returning along the reef to the entry point. The cable channel is essential for safe re-entry through the reef flat.

## Entry and Exit

Gear up at your car (parking available nearby), walk approximately 30 feet to the water, and follow the cable/pipe channel through the reef flat. This marks the safe entry and exit route. Do not cross the shallow reef flat away from the channel — the coral is sharp and delicate. Exit the same way, following the cables back to the beach.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Always follow the pipes/cables through the reef flat — do not attempt to cross the reef outside the channel
- Head left (north) toward the hotel area for the best candlestick coral and turtle encounters
- Excellent night dive: octopus and other nocturnal species are more active after dark, and the beach access makes night entry straightforward
- The WWII gun emplacement on the beach is worth examining before or after your dive
- Turtles here are well-accustomed to divers but should not be touched or chased

## Safety Considerations

Follow the cable channel strictly for safe entry and exit through the reef flat — the shallow reef outside the channel has sharp coral. Currents are generally mild. Boat traffic within the bay is possible — maintain surface awareness and consider a surface marker buoy when ascending. The candlestick coral is fragile; maintain neutral buoyancy throughout.
