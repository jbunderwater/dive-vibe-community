# Gun Beach

The only scuba dive site in Tumon Bay, Guam's main resort area, and widely described as the best shore dive on Guam. Named for a WWII gun emplacement still visible on the beach. Shore entry follows a pipe along the seafloor through the reef flat. The reef slopes from 5 meters to approximately 30 meters (100 feet) through candlestick coral formations, transitioning to a sandy plain. Green sea turtles are seen regularly; manta rays are occasional visitors. Located within the Tumon Bay Marine Preserve.

## Overview

Gun Beach is the only scuba dive site in Tumon Bay, Guam's main hotel and tourism district. A World War II gun emplacement remains on the beach (the site's namesake). The site is frequently called the best shore dive on Guam, combining easy access, marine preserve protection, and diverse marine life.

Entry from the beach follows a pipe channel perpendicular to the beach through the reef flat — this is the designated safe passage through the shallow reef. Once past the reef line, a gently sloping reef extends outward, covered in candlestick corals that grade from 5 meters down to approximately 30 meters (100 feet), where the reef gives way to a sandy plain. Heading left from the entry point (toward the hotel area) provides the best coral formations. The Tumon Bay Marine Preserve designation has supported healthy marine populations.

The site is best in summer when seas are calm. A strong outflowing current can develop during falling tides — conditions should be checked before entry.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Tumon Bay, Guam (north Tumon Bay, past the last hotel)
- **Entry Type**: Shore entry
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–18 meters (50–60 feet)
- **Water Temperature**: 28–29 degrees C
- **Current**: Generally mild inside the bay; outflowing currents possible during falling tides
- **Best Time**: Summer (calmer seas); avoid during strong outflowing tide

## Marine Life

Green sea turtles are regularly encountered throughout the reef and are a major draw. Manta rays are occasional visitors, with multiple individuals sometimes seen feeding near the surface. Octopus hide in crevices and are found by observant divers. Candlestick corals, brain corals, and antler corals are the dominant reef structure. Sea fans, hard corals, and sea anemones cover the reef. Schools of barracuda are reported in the area. Fish populations are diverse within the protected preserve. No regional species list has been substituted for this site — all species noted above are reported at Gun Beach specifically.

## Dive Profile

Follow the pipe channel perpendicular to the beach through the reef flat in ankle-to-knee-depth water. Once past the reef line, descend and head left (toward the hotel area) for the most developed candlestick coral sections. The reef slopes steadily from 5 meters to approximately 30 meters. Turtles are encountered at all depth ranges. Search rocky crevices at the reef-sand transition for octopus. Return along the reef to the pipe entry point.

## Entry and Exit

Gear up at the car park and walk to the water following the pipe. Entry is through ankle-to-knee-deep water along the pipe channel — this is essential; do not attempt to cross the shallow reef away from the pipe, as reef damage and injury risk are high. Exit the same way.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Always follow the pipe/channel for safe entry and exit through the reef flat
- Head left from the entry toward the hotel area for the best candlestick coral
- The turtles here are accustomed to divers and allow relatively close approach
- Excellent night dive site — octopus and other nocturnal species are more active after dark
- Visit the WWII gun emplacement on the beach before or after the dive

## Safety Considerations

Follow the pipe channel for entry and exit — the surrounding reef flat is shallow with sharp coral. A strong outflowing current can develop during falling tides; check conditions before diving. Boat traffic is possible in Tumon Bay — maintain awareness of surface activity.
