# Fish Eye Marine Park

A snorkel and introductory dive site within the Piti Bomb Holes Marine Preserve — Guam's most ecologically diverse marine protected area. The park's commercial centerpiece is the only underwater observatory in Micronesia, accessible by walking a sea bridge to a viewing chamber at approximately 6–9 meters depth. Divers and snorkelers explore the natural percolation pits ("bomb holes") surrounded by dense hard and soft coral.

## Overview

Fish Eye Marine Park is located within the Piti Bomb Holes Marine Preserve in Piti Bay on Guam's western coast. Despite the name, the "bomb holes" are natural percolation pits where freshwater filters through the limestone into the shallow lagoon — not craters from bombing. The preserve was established in 1999 and is the most ecologically diverse of Guam's five marine protected areas. Following the preserve's establishment, fish populations increased by more than 100%.

The commercial Fish Eye Marine Park was constructed in 1996 within the largest pit. Its centerpiece is the only underwater observatory in Micronesia — a viewing chamber at approximately 6 meters depth, accessed by walking across a sea bridge and descending inside the structure. The chamber provides 360-degree views through 18 square and 8 round windows without requiring visitors to enter the water.

Divers and snorkelers entering the water around the observatory explore the natural reef at depths of 3–9 meters. The protected preserve status has produced high fish density and coral diversity. The shallow sandy bottom and calm bay conditions make this an ideal site for beginner dives and training.

The Piti Bomb Holes preserve is home to three endemic species — two mollusks and one sea urchin — found nowhere else on earth, per Wikipedia.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Piti Bay Marine Preserve, 818 N Marine Corps Drive, Piti, Guam
- **Entry Type**: Shore entry
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 9 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 10–20 meters inside the bay
- **Water Temperature**: 28–29°C
- **Current**: Calm inside the bay
- **Best Time**: Year-round; open 9:00 am–5:00 pm

## Marine Life

The preserve supports over 200 fish species and dense coral populations with hard and soft coral growth. Confirmed species observed at this site and visible from the observatory include: bluefin trevally, unicorn fish, barracuda, needlefish schools, stingrays (Aetobatus narinari), blacktip reef sharks (typically appearing in evening hours), anemonefish (Amphiprion melanopus and Amphiprion perideraion), pink anemonefish, and damselfish (Chromis viridis), per the Guam Visitors Bureau and Piti Bomb Holes Wikipedia article.

Three endemic invertebrate species live within the preserve (two mollusk species, one sea urchin species), though these are not typical diver encounters.

## Dive Profile

Enter the water from the sea bridge area near the observatory and explore the surrounding reef at depths of 3–9 meters. The natural percolation pits are the defining feature — explore the rim and interior walls of the pits, which have the densest coral growth and most concentrated fish populations. The calm bay conditions and shallow depth allow extended bottom time. Fish are habituated to people due to the high visitor volume from the observatory.

## Entry and Exit

Access via the Fish Eye Marine Park facility. Walk across the sea bridge and enter the water near the observatory structure. Exit at the same location.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The protected status means fish are unusually approachable — ideal for underwater photography beginners
- The underwater observatory offers a dry view of the same reef — combine the dive with an observatory visit for a different perspective
- An estimated 50–200 dives occur daily in the preserve; expect other divers in the water
- The endemic invertebrate species are small and require a keen eye — consult with the park staff if interested in finding them

## Safety Considerations

One of the gentlest dive sites on Guam. Calm bay conditions, shallow depth, and well-maintained facilities make this among the safest dive locations in Micronesia. Maintain awareness of snorkelers and observatory visitors sharing the water.
