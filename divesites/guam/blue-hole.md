# Blue Hole

Guam's signature natural feature dive — a vertical limestone shaft off Orote Point that opens at 18 meters (60 feet) and exits onto a sheer ocean wall at 40 meters (130 feet). Divers descend through the dark shaft and emerge into open blue water, then drift the knife-edge outer wall back to the reef flat. Strong currents are typical, especially later in the day.

## Overview

Blue Hole is located off the western coast of Guam at the southern tip of Orote Peninsula, at the entrance to Apra Harbor (coordinates: 13°26′24″N 144°37′08″E). It is a natural underwater limestone sinkhole described as the most requested dive site on Guam.

The top of the sinkhole begins at approximately 60 feet (18 meters) on the reef flat. Divers enter the shaft and descend through 70 feet of overhead environment — the interior appears as a heart shape when looking upward toward the opening — before reaching the exit window at approximately 130 feet (40 meters). The shaft opens directly onto a sheer knife-edge wall that drops to depths exceeding 300 feet (91 meters). After exiting the Blue Hole, divers typically drift east to west along the outer wall before ascending to the 60-foot reef flat for a safety stop.

Access is boat-only, with dive boats typically departing from Cabras Marina in Apra Harbor (approximately 20 minutes away). The site uses a mooring buoy for descent. In east-to-west currents, boats cannot maintain position and divers must deploy a surface marker buoy for pickup in open ocean. Strong currents are normal at this site, particularly later in the day — it should be assessed carefully before entry.

The endemic Guam Pencil dottyback (*Lubbockichthys myersi*), discovered here in 2004, was first documented at this site.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Off Orote Peninsula, southern entrance to Apra Harbor, Guam
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Cave/cavern
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Top of Shaft**: ~18 meters (60 feet)
- **Exit Window**: ~40 meters (130 feet)
- **Wall Maximum Depth**: Exceeds 91 meters (300 feet) — well beyond recreational limits
- **Typical Visibility**: 30+ meters (100 feet) on outer wall
- **Water Temperature**: 28–29°C
- **Current**: Strong currents typical, especially afternoons
- **Best Time**: Morning dives to avoid stronger afternoon currents

## Marine Life

Blacktip reef sharks and grey reef sharks are regularly observed along the outer wall. Green sea turtles are encountered on the reef flat. Lionfish and moray eels are present at the shaft entrance. Large pelagic fish cruise the blue water beyond the wall. Schooling fish inhabit the 60-foot reef flat. The endemic *Lubbockichthys myersi* (Guam Pencil dottyback) was first documented at this site in 2004.

## Dive Profile

Descend the mooring line to the reef flat at 18 meters. Enter the shaft and descend through the overhead section, watching depth carefully — the shaft drops quickly. Exit the shaft onto the outer wall at approximately 40 meters; this is the deepest planned point of the dive. Begin drifting west along the wall at 30–40 meters, observing the wall face and watching for sharks. As gas decreases, ascend progressively to the reef flat at 18 meters for a safety stop. Deploy an SMB if the boat cannot maintain position for pickup.

## Entry and Exit

Boat-only access from Cabras Marina (Apra Harbor), approximately 20 minutes away. Entry via mooring buoy hookup. In current conditions, boat pickup may require deploying an SMB for open-ocean retrieval. Do not attempt this dive in strong current without an experienced guide.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Dive in the morning — currents strengthen in the afternoon and can make pickup hazardous
- The transition from the dark shaft to the open blue wall at 40 meters is one of the most dramatic moments in Guam diving
- Use the mooring line during strong currents for controlled descent
- This dive demands strict depth discipline — the wall below 40 meters is visually compelling but beyond recreational limits
- Deploy an SMB before ascending in case of drift from the boat

## Safety Considerations

Strong currents are typical, particularly in the afternoon. The shaft creates an overhead environment — ensure you have sufficient gas for a controlled exit before descending. The wall drops well beyond recreational and even technical limits; maintain strict depth awareness. Nitrogen narcosis is a real concern at 40 meters. Plan gas management conservatively, accounting for the exit point at 40 meters and the full ascent. Never dive this site solo.
