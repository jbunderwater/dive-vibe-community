# Blue Hole

A natural vertical shaft in the reef off Orote Point, accessible only by boat. The opening begins at 18 meters (60 feet) and drops through a dramatic heart-shaped sinkhole to beyond 91 meters (300 feet). Exit onto a sheer outer wall at approximately 40 meters, where strong currents typically make this a drift dive. Green sea turtles, blacktip and whitetip reef sharks, and visibility exceeding 30 meters on the open wall.

## Overview

Blue Hole is a natural limestone sinkhole in the reef flat off Orote Point on Guam's southern coast. The site is accessible only by boat — sheer cliffs and the ocean swell make shore access impractical. A mooring buoy marks the descent point, from which divers are picked up after the dive in open water.

The sinkhole opening begins at approximately 18 meters (60 feet). The shaft drops vertically through the reef to depths beyond 91 meters (300 feet), well beyond recreational limits. At approximately 40 meters (125–130 feet) a large opening in the shaft wall exits onto the open outer reef wall. This exit point is the signature transition of the dive: from the enclosed blue shaft to the dramatic sheer wall that plunges out of sight below. Divers exit at 40 meters and drift along the outer wall at shallower depths, ascending to the reef flat at 18 meters before surfacing.

The outer wall is swept by east-to-west currents, and the dive is typically done as a drift. Visibility on the outside wall frequently exceeds 30 meters (100 feet). The site is known for regular green sea turtle encounters and for the presence of blacktip and whitetip reef sharks along the outer wall. In 2004 researchers discovered a new species here — the Guam Pencil dottyback (*Lubbockichthys myersi*) — endemic to these waters.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Off Orote Point, southwestern Guam (boat access from Cabras Marina, approximately 20 minutes)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Cave/cavern
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 55 meters
- **Exit onto Wall**: approximately 40 meters (125–130 feet)
- **Reef Top**: 18 meters (60 feet)
- **Typical Visibility**: 30+ meters (outer wall); reduced inside the shaft
- **Water Temperature**: 28–29 degrees C
- **Current**: Variable to strong; typically drifts east to west on the outer wall
- **Best Time**: Morning dives tend to have calmer currents

## Marine Life

Green sea turtles are regularly encountered around the site and the Orote Peninsula. Blacktip and whitetip reef sharks are commonly seen along the outer wall. Schooling fish and coral species inhabit the 18-meter reef flat at the top. The Guam Pencil dottyback (*Lubbockichthys myersi*), discovered here in 2004 and endemic to Guam, is a notable species. Soft corals, sea whips, and hard corals grow inside the shaft. Large pelagic fish cruise the blue water beyond the wall.

## Dive Profile

Boat pickup from mooring buoy. Descend the mooring line into the shaft opening at 18 meters. The shaft drops through a heart-shaped opening — maintain awareness of descent rate; the depth gradient is dramatic and narcosis risk increases quickly. Exit onto the outer wall at approximately 40 meters (125–130 feet). This is the deepest point of the dive. Drift along the outer wall, ascending progressively to 30 meters, then to the reef flat at 18 meters. A mandatory safety stop at 5 meters precedes surfacing in open water. Deploy an SMB for boat pickup — the east-to-west current will have moved you from the mooring point.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive only. Descend the mooring line. Surface in open water after drifting along the wall — boat pickup is required. The current runs east to west; plan accordingly with the boat crew. Earlier in the day currents are typically lighter.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Discuss the dive plan in detail with the boat crew before descending — the open-water surface pickup requires coordination
- The transition from the shaft interior to the open wall at 40 meters is one of the most dramatic moments in Guam diving — look up into the shaft as you exit for perspective
- Monitor depth obsessively in the shaft — the vertical nature makes it easy to exceed planned depth
- Currents are typically stronger in the afternoon; morning dives are recommended
- The site is often combined with a second shallower dive at Hap's Reef

## Safety Considerations

This is an advanced deep dive with multiple hazards: a vertical shaft creating an overhead-environment phase, dramatic depth with narcosis risk at 40 meters, strong variable currents on the outer wall, and an open-water surface pickup requiring SMB use. The shaft descends well beyond recreational limits — strict depth discipline is non-negotiable. Plan gas management conservatively: the exit at 40 meters means a long ascent ahead when you exit the hole. Do not attempt without experience in drift diving and deep diving in current.
