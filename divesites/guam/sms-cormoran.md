# SMS Cormoran

A WWI German armed merchant raider, originally the Russian steamer SS Rjasan (also spelled Ryazan), built in 1909 with a gross registered tonnage of 3,443 tons. Scuttled by her crew in Apra Harbor on 7 April 1917 when the United States entered the war. Lying adjacent to the WWII Japanese transport Tokai Maru — one of the only places in the world where divers can touch wrecks from two World Wars on a single dive.

## Overview

SMS Cormoran II began life as the SS Rjasan, a Russian passenger-cargo vessel built at the Schichau Yard in Elbing, Germany, in 1909, with a gross registered tonnage of 3,443 tons. On 4 August 1914, the German light cruiser SMS Emden intercepted and captured her near Vladivostok at the outbreak of World War I. The Germans converted the vessel into an armed merchant raider and redesignated her SMS Cormoran II.

In December 1914 the Cormoran sought refuge in Apra Harbor, Guam — then a US territory — and was interned there for more than two years. On the morning of 7 April 1917, word reached Guam that the United States had declared war on Germany. Rather than surrender the vessel, Captain Adalbert Zuckschwerdt ordered his crew to scuttle her. Nine crew members died during the scuttling. Notably, the shot fired at the Cormoran by a US sailor during the event is documented as one of the first shots fired by American forces in World War I.

The wreck now rests in Apra Harbor at approximately 34 meters (110 feet), lying on her port side. A critical note on sources: Wikipedia and MDA Guam both describe the wreck as lying on her port side, while other sources (including a dive report on Lakwatsero and one reference in Guam Dive Guide) describe her as lying on her starboard side. Divers and operators should verify current orientation locally before planning the dive.

Decades after the scuttling, the WWII Japanese transport Tokai Maru settled immediately adjacent to the Cormoran after being sunk in 1943. The two wrecks share a mooring buoy, and at the point where their hulls meet divers can reach out and touch both vessels simultaneously — spanning two World Wars with one arm's length. This is one of the only places in the world where this is possible.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Apra Harbor, Guam
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 34 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 8–18 meters (harbor-dependent; can drop further after rain or heavy boat traffic)
- **Water Temperature**: 28–29°C
- **Current**: Generally mild inside the harbor
- **Best Time**: Year-round; morning dives typically offer better visibility before harbor traffic stirs sediment

## Marine Life

The combined Cormoran–Tokai Maru site has become an artificial reef in Apra Harbor. Coral and sponge growth encrust both hulls. No site-specific marine species list has been confirmed by sources consulted; the harbor environment supports fish sheltering in the wreck structures but site-specific species reports were not found. Regional fauna applying to Guam's inner harbor (reef fish, occasional reef sharks) can be expected, but no species should be attributed to this specific site without site-specific source confirmation.

## Dive Profile

Descend the shared mooring line — attached to the Tokai Maru — to the wreck complex. Following the Tokai Maru's midsection hull leads divers to the Cormoran at approximately 24 meters on the harbor floor. The Cormoran's engine room can be accessed through skylights. Two open cargo loading doors behind the bridge allow interior access; wreck penetration requires wreck diver certification. The point where the Cormoran and Tokai Maru touch is the signature experience — this is typically near the Cormoran's propeller area at depth. Gas management must be planned conservatively at this depth; the recommended maximum depth of 34 meters leaves limited bottom time on air. Nitrox (32%) is recommended by local operators to extend bottom time at average depths of 24–33 meters.

## Entry and Exit

Access by dive boat from Apra Harbor operators (Cabras Marina area). The wrecks share a mooring buoy. Entry by giant stride. The sheltered harbor provides calm surface conditions year-round.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The two-wars-one-dive experience is globally rare — coordinate with your buddy for the photograph at the contact point
- Visibility in Apra Harbor averages 8–18 meters and is affected by rain, tides, and boat traffic — morning dives are preferable
- The Cormoran and Tokai Maru together are too large for a single dive; plan two separate dives for thorough exploration of each vessel
- Research the scuttling history and its significance as one of the first events of American involvement in WWI

## Safety Considerations

At approximately 34 meters, this is an advanced deep wreck. Gas management must be planned carefully. The wreck structure is over 100 years old and deteriorating — maintain good buoyancy and avoid contact with the hull. Interior penetration requires wreck diving certification and a reel. The harbor location means boat traffic overhead — always deploy a surface marker buoy during ascent.
