# Yamagiri Maru

Famous for the massive 46cm armor-piercing battleship shells in Hold 5 -- ordnance intended as replacement ammunition for the main guns of the Yamato-class super-battleships, found nowhere else in the diving world.

## Overview

The Yamagiri Maru was a 133-meter, 6,438-ton passenger/cargo vessel built by Mitsubishi Heavy Industries and launched in 1939 for Yamashita Kisen Line. Requisitioned by the Imperial Japanese Navy in September 1941, she was converted to a military transport for special cargoes. On February 17, 1944, during Operation Hailstone, U.S. Navy dive bombers from USS Yorktown and USS Bunker Hill attacked her at anchor in Chuuk Lagoon. A direct bomb hit forward put a massive hole in the port side amidships, sinking her quickly. She now lies on her port side in approximately 33-34 meters of water, with the starboard hull rising to approximately 15 meters below the surface.

The Yamagiri Maru is famous for one extraordinary feature: the massive 16–18 inch (approximately 46cm) armor-piercing battleship shells in Hold 5. Each shell weighs approximately 3,219 pounds (1,460 kg) -- ordnance intended as replacement ammunition for the main guns of Japan's Yamato-class super-battleships. The Yamato and Musashi carried the largest naval guns ever built (46cm/45 Type 94), and the shells in Hold 5 match these specifications in weight and diameter. Seeing them tumbled in the hold is a profoundly sobering reminder of the industrial scale of the Pacific War. Beyond this unique cargo, the wreck features a large refrigerated hold and an extensive engine room. Her position on the port side with the starboard hull at 15 meters provides a range of depths suitable for extended exploration.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Chuuk Lagoon, Chuuk State, Micronesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 34 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15-30 meters (50-100 feet)
- **Current**: Generally mild inside the lagoon
- **Best Time**: Year-round; December to April offers calmest conditions

## Marine Life

The Yamagiri Maru's exposed starboard hull at 15 meters supports healthy coral growth in the well-lit shallows, with hard and soft corals, sea fans, and encrusting sponges. The spaces along the port side on the lagoon floor provide habitat for lionfish and scorpionfish. Schools of fusiliers and jacks patrol the mid-water around the wreck. Glassy sweepers congregate in the interior spaces, particularly in the refrigerated hold and engine room. The hull serves as an artificial reef attracting a range of reef fish.

## Dive Profile

The wreck lies on her port side with the starboard hull at approximately 15 meters -- this is the shallowest point and a good starting area for orientation. Hold 5 with the large battleship shells is the primary destination and should be visited while gas supply is full. The massive shells are unmistakable and deeply impressive. The large refrigerated hold offers a different interior environment worth exploring. The engine room is extensive and accessible, providing insight into the vessel's mechanical systems. The port side rests on the lagoon floor at 34 meters. The relatively moderate maximum depth allows generous bottom time compared to Chuuk's deeper wrecks, making it possible to explore multiple features on a single dive.

## Entry and Exit

Access is by dive boat from Weno-based operators. Entry is by giant stride. Descend to the starboard hull at approximately 15 meters. Navigate along and into the wreck to reach the holds and engine room at greater depth. Surface at the mooring or deploy an SMB. The sheltered lagoon provides calm surface conditions.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Hold 5 and the large battleship shells are the must-see feature -- do not leave the wreck without visiting them
- The shells are unique in the diving world; there is nowhere else you can see ordnance of this caliber underwater
- The starboard hull at 15 meters provides an excellent extended safety stop area with wreck structure to explore
- The extensive engine room is worth dedicated time for those interested in marine engineering
- At 34 meters maximum depth, bottom time is more generous than on many Chuuk wrecks -- take advantage of it
- Carry a torch for hold and engine room exploration; the interior of the wreck lying on its side receives limited light

## Safety Considerations

The Yamagiri Maru is a war grave -- removing any artifacts is strictly illegal under Chuuk State law. The large battleship shells in Hold 5 are live ordnance of extraordinary destructive potential -- do not touch, stand on, or disturb them under any circumstances. The wreck lies on her port side, which disorients interior navigation; decks are now vertical surfaces and bulkheads become floors. Exercise particular caution in the engine room, which is extensive and presents multiple overhead environment challenges. Wreck penetration training, redundant lights, and a clear exit plan are essential for any interior exploration. The moderate maximum depth of 34 meters is within advanced recreational limits but gas management should still be carefully planned.

## Photography

The large battleship shells in Hold 5 are the most historically significant photographic subjects on any wreck in Chuuk Lagoon. Their sheer size is difficult to convey in a single image -- including a diver for scale is essential. Wide-angle lenses are necessary for the hold and engine room interiors. The starboard hull at 15 meters receives good ambient light and supports natural-light wide-angle compositions of the coral-encrusted hull surface. The large refrigerated hold and extensive engine room provide additional interior subjects. Strobes are essential for hold and engine room photography to restore color and illuminate the darker spaces.
