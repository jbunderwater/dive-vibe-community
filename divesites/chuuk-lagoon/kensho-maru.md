# Kensho Maru

A 117-meter cargo vessel that sees fewer visitors than Chuuk's marquee wrecks -- rewarding those who make the dive with an intimate exploration of mines, munitions, and well-preserved ship structure draped in coral.

## Overview

The Kensho Maru was a 117-meter, 4,862-ton cargo vessel built in the 1930s, requisitioned by the Imperial Japanese Navy as a munitions and supply transport. She was at anchor in Chuuk Lagoon when Operation Hailstone commenced on February 17, 1944. Struck by bombs from U.S. carrier aircraft, she sank upright onto the lagoon floor at 40 meters.

The Kensho Maru is one of the less-visited wrecks in the lagoon, overshadowed by the famous names nearby. This relative obscurity is an advantage -- divers who explore her are often alone on the wreck, a rare experience in Chuuk. The holds contain mines and munitions stacked as cargo, creating scenes similar to but distinct from the Sankisan Maru. The superstructure is well-preserved and accessible. Coral growth on the upper works is excellent, benefiting from less diver contact. The deeper maximum depth of 40 meters at the sand line places this wreck in the Advanced category and limits bottom time on air.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Chuuk Lagoon, Chuuk State, Micronesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 40 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15-30 meters (50-100 feet)
- **Current**: Generally mild; occasional light current
- **Best Time**: Year-round; December to April for calmest conditions

## Marine Life

The Kensho Maru benefits from lighter diver traffic, and the coral growth reflects it. Hard corals and soft corals cover the superstructure, masts, and deck fixtures in healthy abundance. Sea fans and gorgonians grow along railings and cable runs. The upper masts carry dense coral colonies that rival the Shinkoku Maru's growth in quality if not in scale.

Resident fish populations are robust. Schools of fusiliers and surgeonfish circle the wreck. Batfish aggregate near the superstructure. Inside the holds and corridors, glassy sweepers and cardinalfish form dense clouds. Lionfish and scorpionfish are plentiful. The wreck's position in slightly deeper water brings occasional encounters with larger pelagics -- grey reef sharks and whitetip reef sharks patrol the perimeter. Turtles are seen resting on the upper deck.

## Dive Profile

Descend the mooring line to the deck at approximately 18-22 meters. The bridge superstructure is accessible and well-preserved, with ship's fittings and equipment still recognizable. Explore the bridge level before moving to the holds.

The forward and aft holds contain the munitions cargo -- mines and shells are visible in the lower hold levels at 30-38 meters. The holds are smaller and more intimate than those on larger wrecks, giving an up-close perspective on the ordnance. Bottom time at this depth is limited, so prioritize what you want to see.

Work back to shallower depths along the deck. The masts and upper superstructure at 10-15 meters offer excellent coral viewing and extend your dive during decompression or safety stop time. The wreck's compact 117-meter length means a single dive can cover a reasonable portion of the ship.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive from Weno-based operators, approximately 20-30 minutes to the site. A mooring buoy is in place. Enter by giant stride and descend the mooring line. The lagoon provides calm surface conditions. Ascend via the mooring line and complete a safety stop at 5 meters. Given the 40-meter depth, plan conservatively for potential decompression obligations.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Dive this wreck for the experience of exploring without crowds -- request it specifically from your operator if it is not on the standard itinerary
- Nitrox is strongly recommended to extend bottom time at the 18-22 meter deck level; bottom time at 40 meters on air is very short
- The compact size makes this a satisfying single-dive wreck -- you can see the key features in one well-planned dive
- Pair with a shallower wreck like the Sankisan Maru for a comfortable two-dive day
- Carry a strong primary torch for the holds -- the deeper depth means less ambient light than shallower wrecks

## Safety Considerations

The Kensho Maru is a war grave and all artifacts are protected by law. The holds contain mines and live munitions -- maintain distance and never touch ordnance. The 40-meter maximum depth is the primary safety concern: bottom time on air is approximately 8-10 minutes before mandatory decompression, and nitrogen narcosis is a factor for many divers at this depth. Plan gas supply carefully and carry adequate reserves. Interior penetration in the superstructure and holds requires wreck certification, redundant lights, and awareness of silt conditions. The wreck structure at depth may be more fragile than shallower wrecks due to less frequent inspection -- avoid pulling on or weighting structural elements.

## Photography

The Kensho Maru rewards photographers willing to work at depth. The munitions in the holds -- mines and shells -- make powerful subjects with careful strobe placement. The relatively compact hold spaces mean wide-angle lenses can capture both the ordnance and the surrounding ship structure in a single frame. The coral-covered masts and superstructure at shallower depths provide colorful wide-angle compositions with natural light. The absence of crowds means clean shots without other divers in frame -- a significant advantage over marquee wrecks. The deeper depth challenges exposure and color; bring strong video lights or wide-beam strobes to compensate for the reduced ambient light at 30-40 meters.
