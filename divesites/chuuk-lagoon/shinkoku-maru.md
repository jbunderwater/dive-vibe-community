## Shinkoku Maru

The second largest wreck in Chuuk Lagoon and considered by many aficionados to be the most beautiful — the 152-metre oil tanker Shinkoku Maru sits upright on the sandy bottom, her enormous hull draped in colourful coral gardens from bridge to keel. One of eight tankers that refuelled the Japanese fleet that attacked Pearl Harbor, she was herself sunk two years later during Operation Hailstone.

## Overview

Built in 1939 by Kawasaki Dockyard in Kobe, the 152-metre (500-foot), 10,030-ton Shinkoku Maru was a fleet oil tanker requisitioned by the Imperial Japanese Navy in October 1941 — just four weeks before the attack on Pearl Harbor. She was one of eight oilers that refuelled the striking fleet, including the carriers Akagi and Kaga, during their approach to Hawaii. She continued fuelling Japanese warships throughout the Pacific campaigns until Operation Hailstone caught her north of Fefan Island on February 17–18, 1944. Dive-bombers from USS Yorktown struck her amidships; torpedo bombers from USS Enterprise delivered the fatal near-miss blow that opened the hull near the engine room. She sank upright in 39 metres, killing 86 men including 16 crew. The mast top originally showed above the water surface and was subsequently cut off for navigation safety.

## What Divers See

The Shinkoku Maru sits fully upright, which makes navigation intuitive along the entire length of the ship. The bridge is accessible at 11 metres — a shallow starting point that allows divers to survey the enormous scale of the wreck from above before descending. The bridge, engine room, and medical bay are all penetrable.

Inside the superstructure, the sick bay and operating room contain brown and blue glass medicine bottles scattered on the floor. White-tiled Japanese bathtubs and toilets emerge from beneath marine growth — an unexpectedly domestic detail on a warship. The engine room, entered through a torpedo blast hole in the aft hull at around 38 metres, leads to a cramped, silty lower engine room where human remains lie undisturbed. The upper engine room is more accessible and is bathed in an eerie emerald green light.

The exterior is blanketed in an exceptional concentration of soft and hard corals, earning the wreck a reputation as one of the most colourful in the lagoon. Large schools of fish fill the water column around the superstructure. The size of the wreck means it rewards at least two full dives.

## Dive Profile

Bridge at 11 metres; main deck around 15–18 metres; engine room and aft torpedo blast entry at approximately 36–38 metres; propeller and keel at 39 metres. A first dive on the bow and bridge, a second on the amidships and superstructure, a third on the aft engine room section will cover the wreck comprehensively.

## Marine Life

The coral coverage on this wreck is exceptional — soft corals, sea fans, and encrusting hard corals cover virtually every surface of the superstructure. The sheer biomass of reef fish around the bridge and mast sections is among the highest in the lagoon. Grey reef sharks patrol the exterior perimeter, and turtles are regularly seen resting on the deck.

## Safety Considerations

The torpedo blast entry to the lower engine room is a deep penetration at 36–38 metres — this section is appropriate only for experienced wreck divers with penetration training and appropriate gas planning. Human remains are present in the lower engine room and must not be disturbed. All Chuuk Lagoon wrecks are designated war graves; no artifacts may be removed.

## Photography

The colourful soft coral coverage makes the Shinkoku Maru one of the best wide-angle wreck photography subjects in the Pacific. The bridge silhouetted against open water is a classic shot. The emerald-lit upper engine room interior is a challenging but rewarding low-light wide-angle subject.
