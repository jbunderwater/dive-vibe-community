## Sankisan Maru

One of the most dramatic stories in Chuuk Lagoon: the Sankisan Maru's aft section was catastrophically destroyed by a torpedo detonating her ammunition cargo in 1944, leaving the bow section intact while the stern and rudder were blown across the seabed. The intact forward hull, with its masts almost reaching the surface and adorned in some of the finest soft corals in the lagoon, is among the most popular recreational dives in the Ghost Fleet.

## Overview

A 112-metre (370-foot), 4,776-ton Japanese cargo ship, the Sankisan Maru was loaded with military personnel and supplies when Operation Hailstone began on February 17, 1944. She was anchored near Uman Island when US Navy aircraft struck the aft hold, which contained ammunition. On February 18, a torpedo hit triggered a massive secondary explosion that destroyed the bridge and the entire stern section. The crew had already been evacuated by the Japanese captain to the shores of Uman. The bow section came to rest upright, remarkably intact; the stern was blown off and the propeller and rudder now sit upright at 48 metres on the edge of a large explosion crater, 60 metres away from the aft section.

The bow sits just below the surface — at times visible from the surface — making this one of the shallowest and most accessible major wrecks in the lagoon.

## What Divers See

The main dive focuses on the intact bow and midship section. The masts extend close to the surface and are draped in some of the best soft coral growth in Chuuk Lagoon — brilliant orange and red sea fans across the rigging. The forward holds contain thousands of rounds of ammunition, truck chassis, machine guns, aircraft engines, and small medicine bottles.

Six trucks are lashed to the deck, covered in decades of coral growth. The forward deck is encrusted with coral formations. Divers can enter the holds through multiple openings and follow guide ropes through the ammunition-filled interior.

The explosion crater itself, and the isolated propeller and rudder standing upright at 48 metres, can be visited by advanced or technical divers as a separate dive to the main bow section. Most visitors concentrate on the upper sections of the intact forward hull, which offer superb recreational diving from 3 to 30 metres.

## Dive Profile

The top of the wreck is accessible at 3 metres, which makes nitrox largely unnecessary for the upper sections. Working deeper, the main deck and holds sit between 10 and 25 metres with the keel of the intact section at approximately 30 metres. The stern crater at 48 metres requires separate planning and is rarely included in standard recreational itineraries.

## Marine Life

The masts and rigging are exceptional for marine life due to the dense soft coral growth attracting a high density of reef fish. The holds attract glassy sweepers and smaller reef species. Grey reef sharks are regularly seen on the exterior.

## Safety Considerations

The forward holds contain live ammunition — disturbing the sediment around ordnance is a serious hazard. Buoyancy control inside the holds is critical. Do not approach the stern crater without technical diving training and appropriate gases. All Chuuk Lagoon wrecks are war graves; no artifacts may be removed.

## Photography

The mast rigging, draped in soft corals with blue water behind, is one of the classic wide-angle images of Chuuk diving. The truck silhouettes on the deck make strong wide-angle subjects. The ordnance-packed holds offer dramatic interior shots.
