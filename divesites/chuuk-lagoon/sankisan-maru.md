# Sankisan Maru

A 112-meter cargo vessel famous for her perfectly preserved rows of aerial bombs stacked in the holds -- one of the most striking and sobering sights in all of wreck diving.

## Overview

The Sankisan Maru was a 112-meter, 4,776-ton cargo vessel built in 1942, pressed into service as a munitions transport for the Imperial Japanese Navy. She was anchored in Chuuk Lagoon when Operation Hailstone struck in February 1944. On February 18, 1944 -- the second day of attacks -- a torpedo hit in the aft holds triggered an explosion that destroyed the bridge to stern section. She sank upright onto the lagoon floor.

What makes the Sankisan Maru extraordinary is her cargo. The forward holds contain rows of aerial bombs -- still neatly arranged as they were stacked for transport over 80 years ago. The orderly lines of bombs, each about a meter long, create a haunting visual that is simultaneously beautiful and deeply unsettling. This is the most famous single feature of any Chuuk wreck after the Fujikawa Maru's Zero fighters. A truck chassis sits in one of the holds alongside the munitions. The wreck's relatively shallow depth means good ambient light penetrates the holds, making the bomb rows clearly visible without artificial light.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Chuuk Lagoon, Chuuk State, Micronesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15-30 meters (50-100 feet)
- **Current**: Generally mild inside the lagoon
- **Best Time**: Year-round; December to April for calmest conditions

## Marine Life

The Sankisan Maru supports healthy coral growth on her upper superstructure and masts. Hard corals and sea fans cover the railings, king posts, and exposed deck structures. Soft corals add color to the shallower sections above 15 meters. The coral coverage is less dense than on wrecks like the Shinkoku Maru, but the wreck still functions as productive artificial reef habitat.

Schools of batfish frequent the superstructure area. Fusiliers and anthias cloud the upper works. Inside the holds, glassy sweepers form dense schools among the bomb rows. Lionfish are common in the shadows. Nudibranchs and flatworms inhabit the encrusted hull surfaces. The surrounding sand occasionally yields stingrays. Reef sharks pass through the area but are less frequently seen than at deeper wrecks.

## Dive Profile

Descend the mooring line to the deck at approximately 12-15 meters. The forward holds are the main event -- proceed to Hold No. 1 and No. 2 where the aerial bombs are visible from above, neatly arranged in rows reaching down to 28-30 meters. The hold openings are large enough that natural light illuminates the bomb rows from above, particularly on sunny days, creating dramatic shafts of light across the ordnance.

The truck chassis is located in one of the aft holds and is worth finding. The bridge superstructure amidships offers accessible exploration with good artifacts. Deck machinery, winches, and bollards are well-preserved.

The relatively shallow depth profile -- deck at 12-15 meters, maximum around 30 meters -- makes this wreck suitable for longer dives than the deeper Chuuk wrecks. Use the shallow superstructure and mast areas for an extended safety stop.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive from Weno-based operators, approximately 30-40 minutes to the site. A permanent mooring buoy marks the wreck. Enter by giant stride and descend the mooring line. Surface conditions are typically calm in the sheltered lagoon. Ascend via the mooring line and complete your safety stop at 5 meters.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Visit the forward holds first while air supply is full -- the bomb rows are the reason everyone dives this wreck
- The bombs are best viewed and photographed from above, looking down into the holds; descending to hold bottom level gives a different perspective but costs bottom time
- The shallow deck depth makes this an excellent Nitrox dive with significantly extended bottom time
- The Sankisan pairs well with the nearby Rio de Janeiro Maru for a two-wreck day
- Bring a torch to pick out detail in the lower hold sections, but the bombs are visible in natural light on clear days

## Safety Considerations

The Sankisan Maru is a war grave -- artifact removal is illegal. The holds contain live ordnance. The aerial bombs have been underwater for over 80 years and while they appear stable, they are genuine military munitions and must never be touched, sat on, or disturbed in any way. Do not enter the holds to handle the bombs for photographs. Silt on hold floors can reduce visibility rapidly. The wreck structure is aging and some sections may be weakened -- avoid contact with overhead structure inside the wreck. The 30-meter maximum depth is manageable for certified Open Water divers with wreck experience, but interior penetration beyond the open holds requires wreck certification.

## Photography

The aerial bomb rows in the forward holds are the defining photographic subject. From the hold rim at 15 meters, wide-angle shots looking down capture the rows receding into the depths -- particularly effective when natural light shafts illuminate the scene. A fisheye lens emphasizes the rows' dramatic perspective. For detail shots, a torch or strobe brings out the patina and markings on individual bombs. The truck chassis in the aft hold is an unusual still-life subject. On the exterior, the wreck's relatively shallow profile allows natural-light wide-angle shooting of the superstructure and coral growth. The manageable depth and good ambient light make this one of the easier Chuuk wrecks to photograph well.
