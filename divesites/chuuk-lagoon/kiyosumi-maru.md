# Kiyosumi Maru

A 137-meter armed merchant cruiser that was sunk by bomb hits at Truk Lagoon while undergoing repairs -- with eclectic cargo including bicycles, propeller blades, and gun turrets visible in the holds.

## Overview

The Kiyosumi Maru was a 137-meter, 8,614-ton combined cargo-passenger vessel built in 1934 by IHI Corporation at Kawasaki shipyard in Kobe for Kokusai Kisen K.K. (International Shipping Company, operating under the K-Line brand). The Imperial Japanese Navy requisitioned her in November 1941, converting her into an armed merchant cruiser fitted with 152.4mm (6-inch) guns cannibalized from old cruisers, twin torpedo tubes, and anti-aircraft weapons. She served in operations including the Battle of Midway in June 1942.

In early 1944, the Kiyosumi Maru was struck by three torpedoes and was being towed to Truk for repairs when Operation Hailstone commenced on February 17, 1944. U.S. carrier aircraft from USS Enterprise and USS Yorktown attacked her with bombs while she was still undergoing repairs; the hits caused fatal flooding and she sank to the lagoon floor at approximately 30 meters, lying on her port side.

The Kiyosumi Maru's original 6-inch guns have been removed from the wreck, but gun turrets and military hardware remain visible in the holds. The holds contain an eclectic mix of cargo including bicycles, propeller blades, gun turrets, shells, and general military supplies -- a fascinating time capsule of the logistics needed to sustain a massive naval base.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Chuuk Lagoon, Chuuk State, Micronesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15-30 meters (50-100 feet)
- **Current**: Generally mild inside the lagoon
- **Best Time**: Year-round; December to April for calmest conditions

## Marine Life

The Kiyosumi Maru supports a well-developed reef community across her upper works. Hard corals and sea fans cover the railings and superstructure, softening the military hardware with decades of organic growth. Soft corals in reds and purples add color to the masts and king posts at shallower depths. Sponges and tunicates encrust the hull plates.

Schools of batfish are a reliable presence around the bridge area. Jacks and trevally patrol the open water nearby. Inside the holds, glassy sweepers congregate in dense formations. Lionfish and scorpionfish hide among the cargo and structural beams. Whitetip reef sharks are seen resting beneath the hull overhang. Nudibranchs and flatworms can be found on the encrusted surfaces for macro enthusiasts.

## Dive Profile

Descend the mooring line to the deck at approximately 12-16 meters. The bridge superstructure amidships is accessible and contains ship's fittings and navigation equipment. Move forward to explore the holds, where propeller blades and gun turrets are among the most visually striking cargo items. Bicycles and various spare parts are scattered among the general supplies. The hull shows damage from the bombs and prior torpedo hits that sank the vessel -- swim along the exterior at depth to appreciate the scale of the damage.

The 30-meter maximum depth and 12-16 meter deck give a comfortable profile. The wreck's 135-meter length allows coverage of the major features in a single dive, though a second dive permits more thorough exploration of the holds and stern section.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive from Weno-based operators, approximately 20-30 minutes to the site. A permanent mooring buoy is in place. Enter by giant stride and descend the mooring line to the deck. The lagoon location provides calm surface conditions. Ascend via the mooring line for your safety stop at 5 meters.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The propeller blades and gun turrets in the holds are the standout cargo items -- ask your guide for their locations to save bottom time searching
- The bicycles in the holds are a poignant reminder of the individual soldiers this supply ship was sustaining
- Swim the exterior hull to see the battle damage that sank the vessel
- Nitrox extends bottom time comfortably at the 12-16 meter deck level
- This wreck is less crowded than the Fujikawa Maru or Shinkoku Maru, making it a good choice when multiple boats are on the popular sites

## Safety Considerations

The Kiyosumi Maru is a war grave and artifact removal is illegal under Chuuk State law. Military hardware in the holds should be observed but not climbed on or manipulated. The holds contain miscellaneous military cargo that must not be disturbed. Silt in the holds can reduce visibility rapidly -- maintain good buoyancy and trim. Damage areas on the hull may have weakened structure; approach for viewing but avoid entering blast holes. Interior penetration beyond the open holds and bridge requires wreck certification and redundant lights. The 30-meter maximum depth is manageable for experienced recreational divers but demands standard gas management discipline.

## Photography

The hull damage and battle scars provide dramatic wide-angle compositions. Inside the holds, the propeller blades, gun turrets, and bicycles make unusual still-life subjects -- use strobes to bring out detail in the darker lower holds. The bridge area offers good artifact close-ups. The coral growth on the masts at shallow depth allows natural-light wide-angle work. The wreck's manageable depth and good ambient light at deck level make exposure management straightforward.
