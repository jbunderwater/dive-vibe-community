## Fujikawa Maru

Consistently ranked among the top wreck dives in the world, the Fujikawa Maru is the crown jewel of Chuuk Lagoon's Ghost Fleet — a perfectly preserved WWII aircraft transport whose forward holds contain the remains of Mitsubishi Zero fighter planes.

## Overview

Built in 1938 for the Toyo Kaiun Kisen Kaisha shipping company, the 133-metre (437-foot), 6,938-ton Fujikawa Maru was requisitioned by the Imperial Japanese Navy and converted into an auxiliary aircraft transport, ferrying disassembled warplanes and parts to island bases across the Pacific. She survived two torpedo attacks before Operation Hailstone on February 17–18, 1944, when aircraft from USS Bunker Hill and USS Yorktown delivered the fatal blows. She now sits upright on the sandy lagoon floor, a time capsule from the final hours of Japan's Pacific stronghold.

The Times named it one of the top 10 wreck dives in the world, and it is regarded by multiple authoritative surveyors as the single best dive site in all of Chuuk Lagoon.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Chuuk Lagoon, Federated States of Micronesia

- **Maximum Depth**: 33 metres (keel) — deck at 18m, top of superstructure at 9m

- **Best Time**: Year-round; December to April for calmest conditions

## What Divers See

The dive begins with descent to the bow gun — a 6-inch naval gun manufactured by Britain's Elswick Ordnance Company in 1899, its builder's plaque still readable underwater. The ship sits fully upright, making orientation straightforward.

The forward holds contain the wreck's most celebrated feature: at least nine disassembled Mitsubishi fighter aircraft. Eight are A6M Zeros and the ninth is an A5M Claude, the only known surviving example of the type underwater. Belly tanks, propeller blades, wheels, and tires are scattered through the holds alongside shell casings, machine guns, gas masks, and beer bottles.

The engine room, though its roof has partially collapsed in recent years, still allows access to a well-preserved machine shop containing a lathe, bench drill, grinding wheels, and air compressor. Deeper holds contain fuel drums and military supplies. The stern deck and superstructure are encrusted with soft and hard corals, and the masts are draped in sea fans and anemones.

## Dive Profile

Descend the mooring line to the superstructure at 9 metres and work progressively deeper. The deck sits at 18 metres, the holds and engine room at 22–28 metres, and the keel at 33 metres. Most penetration of the cargo holds is at 18–25 metres, giving good bottom time on recreational air. Plan at least two dives — one for the bow section and aircraft holds, another for the bridge, engine room, and stern.

## Marine Life

Schools of glassy sweepers pack the interior passageways. Grey reef sharks patrol the exterior, and turtles are regularly seen resting on the superstructure. Schools of barracuda and jacks circle the masts. Lionfish and scorpionfish shelter in coral-encrusted crevices, and angelfish and butterflyfish move through the superstructure in large numbers.

## Safety Considerations

Wreck penetration requires a dive torch and a guide or wreck specialty certification. Silt disturbance inside the holds can reduce visibility rapidly — maintain neutral buoyancy throughout. All wrecks in Chuuk Lagoon are designated war graves; the removal of any artifact is strictly prohibited. Always dive with a local guide familiar with the wreck's current condition.

## Photography

The forward aircraft holds offer unrivalled wide-angle opportunities — the Zero fuselages create dramatic frames with natural light filtering from above. The 6-inch bow gun is one of the most photographed subjects in all of Chuuk. A powerful torch is essential for revealing the colors inside the holds and engine room.
