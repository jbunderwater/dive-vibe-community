# Fujikawa Maru

The crown jewel of Chuuk Lagoon -- a 133-meter refrigerated cargo vessel sitting upright on sand, her holds packed with wartime relics including the world's only surviving A5M Claude fighter, her superstructure draped in 80 years of coral growth.

## Overview

The Fujikawa Maru was a 133.3-meter, 6,938-ton refrigerated cargo vessel built in 1938 by Mitsubishi Zosensho in Nagasaki for Toyo Kaiun K.K. Requisitioned by the Imperial Japanese Navy, she served as an auxiliary armed aircraft ferry and supply transport during World War II. She was hit by a torpedo from a USS Bunker Hill Avenger on February 17, 1944, and continued to take further bomb and torpedo hits through the night. She finally sank upright onto a sandy bottom on February 18, 1944 at approximately 7:15am -- the second day of Operation Hailstone -- where she remains today as the single most popular wreck dive in Chuuk Lagoon.

The wreck is remarkably intact. Her 15cm (6-inch) bow gun still points skyward, the bridge structure is accessible, and the masts and king posts rise to within 6 meters of the surface, covered in spectacular hard and soft coral growth that ranks among the finest wreck encrustation anywhere in the world. The forward holds are the main attraction, containing at least nine disassembled Mitsubishi fighter aircraft -- eight A6M Zeros and one A5M Claude, the sole known survivor of the A5M type anywhere in the world. Wings, fuselages, and engine cowlings are stacked in the lower hold levels, along with crates of ammunition and munitions. The midships and aft holds contain additional war cargo including machine parts and supplies.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Chuuk Lagoon, Chuuk State, Micronesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 33 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15-30 meters (50-100 feet)
- **Current**: Generally mild inside the lagoon
- **Best Time**: Year-round; December to April offers calmest conditions

## Marine Life

The Fujikawa Maru is a thriving artificial reef after eight decades of coral colonization. The masts and superstructure support massive formations of hard corals, sea fans, and vibrant soft corals in orange, red, and purple. Anemones with resident clownfish cluster along railings and davits. The exterior hull is encrusted with sponges and tunicates.

Schools of fusiliers, batfish, and jacks patrol the mid-water around the wreck. Inside the holds, glassy sweepers form shimmering clouds in torch beams. Lionfish and scorpionfish lurk in shadowed recesses. Grey reef sharks and whitetip reef sharks are regularly seen in the surrounding waters. Hawksbill turtles occasionally rest on the superstructure. The wreck hosts a resident population of giant grouper that have grown accustomed to divers over decades of visits.

## Dive Profile

Most dives begin at the permanent mooring line attached near the bow. Descend along the line to the foredeck at approximately 15 meters. The bow gun is immediately forward -- an iconic photo stop. Move aft to Hold No. 1 and No. 2, where the disassembled Zero fighters and the rare A5M Claude are stacked in the lower levels at 25-33 meters. The holds are large and well-lit from above, making them accessible to divers without full wreck penetration certification, though a guide is strongly recommended.

Continue aft along the deck past the bridge superstructure, which can be entered at multiple levels. The engine room is accessible but dark and silty -- for experienced wreck divers only. The aft holds contain additional cargo. On the return, ascend the masts for the coral garden at 6-12 meters, which serves as an excellent extended safety stop. Bottom time at 33 meters is limited, so most divers spend the deeper portion in the forward holds and the remaining time on the shallower superstructure.

## Entry and Exit

Access is by dive boat from Blue Lagoon Resort or other Weno-based operators, with a 20-30 minute ride depending on departure point. A permanent mooring buoy marks the site. Entry is by giant stride. Descend along the mooring line directly to the wreck. Surface at the mooring line or deploy an SMB if current has carried you off the wreck. The sheltered lagoon location means surface conditions are typically calm.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Dedicate at least two dives to this wreck -- one focused on the forward holds and Zero parts, another on the superstructure, engine room, and coral-covered masts
- Carry a primary torch and backup light for hold exploration; the Zero fighter parts are in the lower holds where ambient light is limited
- Visit the mast coral garden during your safety stop -- it is one of the most photographed features in all of Chuuk
- Plan your deepest penetration first while air supply is full, then work shallower as the dive progresses
- Nitrox (EANx32) significantly extends bottom time at the 15-18 meter deck level and is available from most operators

## Safety Considerations

The Fujikawa Maru is a war grave -- removing any artifacts is strictly illegal under Chuuk State law and carries severe penalties. The holds contain live ordnance including ammunition and shells; do not touch or disturb any cargo. Silt accumulation inside the holds and engine room can reduce visibility to zero with careless fin kicks -- maintain proper trim and buoyancy at all times. Overhead environments within the superstructure and engine room require wreck penetration training, a continuous guideline or clear exit plan, and redundant lights. The maximum depth of 33 meters limits bottom time on air; plan gas management carefully and always maintain adequate reserves for ascent and a 3-5 minute safety stop.

## Photography

The Fujikawa Maru offers world-class wreck photography at every level. Wide-angle shots of the 15cm bow gun silhouetted against blue water are iconic Chuuk images. The disassembled Zero fighters and A5M Claude in the forward holds reward careful lighting with strobes or video lights -- the wing sections and engine cowlings are instantly recognizable. The coral-encrusted masts and king posts in shallow water provide spectacular wide-angle compositions with natural light. Macro subjects include nudibranchs on hull surfaces, coral polyps on railings, and the faces of resident lionfish. The relatively good visibility and calm conditions inside the lagoon make this one of the more forgiving wreck photography sites in Chuuk.
