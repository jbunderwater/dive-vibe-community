# Rio de Janeiro Maru

A 140-meter passenger-cargo vessel turned submarine tender, sitting upright with a gentle port list -- famous for her ornate engine order telegraph and the iconic soft coral cluster on the forward kingpost.

## Overview

The Rio de Janeiro Maru was a 140-meter, 9,627-ton passenger-cargo vessel built in 1929 by Mitsubishi Shipyard in Nagasaki for Osaka Shosen Kaisha (OSK Line), originally serving the Japan-South America passenger route -- hence her Brazilian name. Requisitioned by the Imperial Japanese Navy, she was converted into a submarine tender and supply transport. On February 17, 1944, during Operation Hailstone, the Rio de Janeiro Maru was struck by bombs and torpedoes from U.S. carrier aircraft. She sank upright onto the lagoon floor at 33 meters with a slight list to port.

The wreck is well-preserved and offers a compelling mix of historical artifacts and marine growth. Her most celebrated feature is the engine order telegraph on the bridge, which is among the most ornate and best-preserved in Chuuk. The forward holds reportedly contained supplies including beer and sake bottles, remnants of which are still visible scattered among the cargo. The forward kingpost is crowned with a spectacular cluster of soft corals that has become one of the most photographed single features in the lagoon.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Chuuk Lagoon, Chuuk State, Micronesia
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 33 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15-30 meters (50-100 feet)
- **Current**: Generally mild inside the lagoon
- **Best Time**: Year-round; December to April for calmest conditions

## Marine Life

The Rio de Janeiro Maru supports healthy coral growth across her upper works. The forward kingpost carries the wreck's signature feature -- a massive, brilliantly colored cluster of soft corals in deep reds and oranges that rises like a living bouquet. Hard corals, sponges, and sea fans encrust the railings, davits, and superstructure. Anemones with clownfish populate sheltered areas along the deck.

Schools of batfish are commonly seen around the superstructure. Fusiliers and jacks patrol the open water nearby. Inside the holds and interior spaces, glassy sweepers form dense clouds. Lionfish and scorpionfish are found throughout. Whitetip reef sharks occasionally shelter beneath the hull. The wreck's proximity to deeper water brings occasional visits from grey reef sharks.

## Dive Profile

Descend the mooring line to the deck amidships at approximately 18 meters. The bridge is a natural first stop -- locate the engine order telegraph, which is beautifully preserved with legible Japanese characters. It stands upright and makes an outstanding photography subject.

Move forward along the deck to the forward holds. Hold No. 1 and No. 2 contain the remaining cargo, including scattered bottles and crates. The holds descend to 30-33 meters at the bottom. The forward kingpost with its iconic soft coral cluster is at approximately 12-15 meters -- an essential stop both for photography and as a shallower way to extend your dive.

The stern section contains additional holds and machinery. The aft superstructure is accessible for exploration. A circuit of the exterior hull at deck level reveals torpedo damage on the port side. Finish with time on the shallow superstructure and kingpost area before ascending for your safety stop.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive from Weno-based operators, approximately 30 minutes to the site. A permanent mooring buoy is attached to the wreck. Enter by giant stride and descend the mooring line. Surface conditions in the lagoon are typically calm. Ascend the mooring line or deploy an SMB during your safety stop for pickup.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Make the engine order telegraph and the soft coral kingpost your two priority subjects -- both are unique to this wreck
- The forward holds with scattered bottles are worth exploring with a good torch, though do not disturb or remove anything
- Nitrox extends bottom time at the 18-20 meter deck level and is recommended
- This wreck pairs well with the nearby Sankisan Maru for a two-wreck dive day
- Early morning dives often produce the best light on the forward kingpost corals

## Safety Considerations

The Rio de Janeiro Maru is a war grave -- all artifacts, including the bottles and cargo in the holds, are legally protected. Do not touch, move, or remove anything. The holds contain military cargo and should be explored with care; silt on the hold floors can reduce visibility quickly with careless finning. Interior penetration beyond the open holds and bridge requires wreck certification and redundant lights. The 33-meter maximum depth at the sand line limits bottom time on air. The slight port list is not dangerous but can be disorienting inside corridors. Maintain awareness of your depth and air supply throughout the dive.

## Photography

The two signature shots on this wreck are the engine order telegraph and the soft coral kingpost. The telegraph is best photographed with a single strobe to bring out its detail and patina, positioned at eye level from the front. The forward kingpost soft coral cluster is best shot in wide-angle with natural light from above and strobes to capture the vivid reds against the blue water column. The scattered bottles in the holds create evocative still-life compositions with careful torch positioning. Wide-angle shots along the deck looking forward or aft emphasize the wreck's 140-meter length. The wreck's manageable depth and generally good visibility make it one of the more forgiving photography sites in Chuuk.
