## Aquarium

Located in the Miloli'i Bay area of South Kona, the Aquarium takes its name from expectations of fish abundance — though sources clarify that the site's real appeal is its dramatic collapsed lava tube grottoes and skylights, and the exceptionally relaxed Hawaiian green sea turtle population that frequents the area.

## Overview

The Aquarium is a boat dive site in the South Kona area featuring fields of coral-covered basalt rock interspersed with grottoes and caverns formed by collapsed ancient lava tubes. Many of these caverns have openings in their ceilings that allow sunlight to spill through in beams — the defining visual feature of the site. The name "Aquarium" carries a degree of irony that operators acknowledge: the Underwater Photography Guide to Kona notes that "the fish life was just average" and that the actual draw is the underwater topography rather than fish density.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Miloli'i Bay area, South Kona Coast
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Cave/cavern
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 12 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15–25 metres
- **Water Temperature**: 26–28°C
- **Best Time**: April to November

## Underwater Character

The site consists of coral-encrusted basalt with numerous grottoes and caverns created by collapsed lava tube roofs. The openings in the cavern ceilings produce natural skylights — sunlight enters from above in beams, illuminating the interior in the manner typical of Kona's shallow lava sites. The maze of grottoes creates multiple swim-through opportunities and varied terrain within a shallow depth range.

## Marine Life

Hawaiian green sea turtles (Chelonia mydas): confirmed as abundant and "well accustomed to divers" along the Kona Coast at this type of lava grotto site. Close encounters are reported as common.

Fish life: described by the Underwater Photography Guide as "average" for the area — the variety and density of fish at this site is not exceptional compared to other Kona reefs. Small reef fish, crustaceans, and octopus inhabit the grottoes and overhangs. No site-specific species records beyond turtles are available from current independent sources.

The site name creates an expectation of spectacular fish abundance that sources do not support; this has been corrected in the description above.

## Diving the Site

Dives navigate between the various grottoes and cavern systems. Buoyancy control is important to avoid damaging the coral-encrusted lava walls. The natural skylights create excellent photographic opportunities — the site is more visually interesting topographically than biologically, making it particularly rewarding for underwater landscape photography.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Bring a dive light to illuminate grotto interiors and reveal the true colours of corals and invertebrates
- Wide-angle lens setups work well for capturing the skylights and cavern architecture
- Morning light provides the best natural illumination through the skylights
- Observe turtles from at least 1.5 metres; do not touch or pursue

## Safety Considerations

- Maintain proper buoyancy in confined grottoes; the walls are sharp lava
- Use a dive light to avoid disorientation in darker cavern sections
- Never penetrate beyond the light-penetrating zone
- Stay with your divemaster and buddy
- Carry a backup light
