# Arorangi School Passage

A west-coast passage on Rarotonga with frequent strong currents, ridge-and-gully terrain running to 35 meters, and sightings of large spotted eagle ray schools, barracuda, and octopus. The smallest and shallowest of Rarotonga's five main passages; conditions must be assessed before diving.

## Overview

Arorangi School Passage is located on the west side of Rarotonga, directly offshore from Arorangi School. The passage is the smallest and shallowest of Rarotonga's five reef passages, and it frequently carries strong currents that flush the inner lagoon. These currents make it a legitimate drift dive when running — divers can begin a drift at the passage and follow the current along the outside of the reef. The passage itself has been dredged in recent years to allow smaller vessels through, changing the upper passage topography.

Outside the passage, the terrain consists of volcanic rock ridges and gullies running WNW from the sandy shallows at 10 to 18 meters out to the reef edge, where the ridges descend to 30 to 35 meters. Coral cover is approximately 15%, concentrated on the ridges and gully walls. Good-sized Porites bommies are scattered around the passage area.

## Site Information

- **Location**: West side, off Arorangi School, Rarotonga, Cook Islands
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Drift dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 35 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 40+ meters
- **Water Temperature**: 24-27°C
- **Current**: Variable; frequently strong through the passage itself
- **Best Time**: Year-round, conditions permitting

## Marine Life

Spotted eagle rays are a highlight: schools of up to approximately 20 individuals have been observed cruising along the ridge lines and around the passage area. Barracuda (typically small schools of 3 to 5) and bluefin trevally patrol the deeper ridge sections. Octopus shelter in the gullies and can be spotted in the overhangs. Guineafowl moray eels shelter in volcanic rock crevices. Giant clams sit in the gullies.

## Dive Profile

Drop to the sandy area at 10 to 18 meters and follow the ridges as they run WNW toward the reef edge. The passage current can be used as a drift; assess the tidal state before committing. The eagle rays tend to cruise parallel to the reef in mid-water alongside the ridges. The gullies between the ridges are where the octopus and morays are most often found. The ridges drop to 30 to 35 meters at the outer edge — watch your depth here.

## Entry and Exit

Access by dive boat from Avatui Harbour (approximately 12 to 18 minutes) or from the south coast via Rutaki Passage (approximately 12 minutes). Entry by giant stride. When currents are running, plan the drift direction and agree a pickup point with the boat.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Check the tidal state before diving — the passage can carry strong currents that require drift technique
- Eagle rays tend to cruise parallel to the reef; watch the blue water off the ridge edges
- The gullies are the best spots for octopus, particularly in shaded overhangs
- When the current is minimal, the site can be explored as a standard ridge-and-gully reef dive

## Safety Considerations

The passage can carry strong currents without warning. Always dive with a local operator who can assess tidal conditions. When the current is running, stay close to the reef to avoid being swept into open water. Monitor depth carefully along the ridges as they slope into deeper water.
