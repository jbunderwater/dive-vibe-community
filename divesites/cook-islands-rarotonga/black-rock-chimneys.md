# Black Rock Chimneys

An easy, shallow reef dive off Rarotonga's prominent Black Rock landmark on the west side, eight minutes from the harbour. Ridges and gullies from 7 meters to 25 meters, with Christmas tree worms, giant clams, guineafowl pufferfish, and shallow caverns worth exploring at the end of the dive.

## Overview

Black Rock Chimneys lies just offshore from the prominent Black Rock (Tuoro) landmark on Rarotonga's west coast — a dark volcanic outcrop that is a sacred site in Cook Islands culture. The site is approximately eight minutes from Avatiu Harbour, north of Edgewater Resort and south of the airport runway's western end.

The anchor drops at approximately 7 meters on the reef surface. An adjacent gully descends to 12 meters, and the ridges extend out and down to a maximum of 25 meters. The underlying geology is volcanic rock with approximately 15% live coral cover; sponges and gorgonians are absent, which is characteristic of Rarotonga's west coast. Shallow caverns at the outer end of the dive are worth exploring when air allows, and are known to shelter crayfish and lionfish.

The exceptional visibility (consistently exceeding 40 meters) and shallow depths make this a reliable site for newer divers and photographers. GPS coordinates from survey data: 21° 12.315' S, 159° 49.530' W.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Off Black Rock (Tuoro), west side, Rarotonga, Cook Islands
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Beginner
- **Maximum Depth**: 25 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 40+ meters
- **Water Temperature**: 24-27°C
- **Current**: Generally mild
- **Best Time**: Year-round

## Marine Life

Christmas tree worms (Spirobranchus) in blues, yellows, and pinks dot the reef surface. Giant clams sit in the gullies. Guineafowl pufferfish are regular residents. Two barracuda and bluefin trevally pass through the ridge system. Surgeonfish, threadfin and longnose butterflyfish, and Moorish idols are present throughout. Crayfish and lionfish shelter in the shallow caverns at the outer end of the site. Black sea cucumbers are abundant on the sandy patches.

## Dive Profile

From the anchor at 7 meters, navigate down into the main gully at 12 meters and follow the ridges outward to 25 meters. The Christmas tree worms and giant clams are distributed throughout the depth range. Natural navigation follows the gully systems — swim out along one gully and return along a parallel one. If air permits, explore the shallow caverns at the outer end of the site before ascending; these are where crayfish and lionfish tend to shelter. The shallow depths allow extended bottom times.

## Entry and Exit

Access by dive boat from Avatiu Harbour, approximately eight minutes. The anchor is set 30 to 50 meters off the fringing reef. Entry by giant stride into generally calm water; the west side is typically sheltered.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The shallow caverns at the outer end of the dive are worth the extra minutes — check them for crayfish and lionfish
- A macro lens rewards time spent on the Christmas tree worms, which are vivid against the dark volcanic rock
- This is one of the most appropriate sites on Rarotonga for newer divers
- Giant clams will close if approached too quickly; hover at a distance to observe their open mantles

## Safety Considerations

A gentle site with minimal hazards. Currents are rarely significant on the west side. Maximum depth of 25 meters is well within recreational limits. Maintain good buoyancy near the clams and reef surface.
