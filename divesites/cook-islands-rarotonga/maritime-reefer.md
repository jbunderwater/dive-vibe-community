# Maritime Reefer

A fishing vessel deliberately sunk in 1981 on Rarotonga's west side, now broken into two main sections at 26 meters after storm damage over the decades. The propeller and rudder remain visible at the stern. Resident giant moray and lionfish inhabit the wreck.

## Overview

The Maritime Reefer is a fishing vessel that was deliberately sunk off Rarotonga's west coast, southwest of Black Rock, in 1981 — making it the first purpose-sunk dive wreck on the island. Multiple cyclones since then have broken the wreck into two main sections and scattered debris across the seabed at approximately 26 meters.

The stern section is the most recognisable, with the propeller and rudder still visible — the propeller is notably bent, suggesting the engine was running at the time of sinking or the damage occurred during a subsequent storm. The bow lies on its starboard side. The vessel's length has been estimated but no verified specifications have been located in maritime records; the wreck does not appear in Lloyd's Register for the relevant period, and dive operators give conflicting accounts of the vessel's origin. Do not treat vessel dimensions published online as confirmed facts.

Despite its fragmented state, the wreck structure concentrates fish life in a part of the reef where ambient marine life is otherwise sparse. A giant moray is a long-term resident, and lionfish shelter in the broken hull sections.

## Site Information

- **Location**: SW of Black Rock, west side, Rarotonga, Cook Islands
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 26 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 40+ meters
- **Water Temperature**: 24-27°C
- **Current**: Generally mild
- **Best Time**: Year-round

## Marine Life

A giant moray is a confirmed resident of the wreck structure. Lionfish shelter among the broken hull sections. Christmas tree worms (blue, yellow, and pink) have colonised exposed surfaces. Surgeonfish, trevally, and triggerfish are seen in the vicinity. The exceptional visibility occasionally allows spotted eagle rays to be observed passing through the area.

## Dive Profile

Descend from the anchor at approximately 12 meters down to the wreck at 26 meters — the clear water means the wreck becomes visible well before you reach it. Begin at whichever section is closer, noting the bent propeller and rudder at the stern. Move between the two sections, which lie at different orientations from each other. Do not enter the interior — the broken structure is unstable, and safe exterior viewing is straightforward given the visibility. The 26-meter depth requires careful gas management for a useful bottom time.

## Entry and Exit

Access by dive boat from Avatiu Harbour, approximately 11 minutes to the site. Entry by giant stride. The west-side location typically provides calm surface conditions, though the site should be avoided in strong westerly swells.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The bent propeller and rudder at the stern are the most photogenic features
- Torch recommended for checking inside the broken sections from outside
- Do not penetrate the interior — the structure has been significantly degraded by decades of storm activity
- The wreck's fragmentation actually increases marine life habitat; look among smaller debris for critters

## Safety Considerations

At 26 meters, gas management is critical and bottom time is limited on air. Do not enter the hull — the broken structure is unstable and penetration is not warranted given the exterior views available. Sharp metal edges exist throughout the wreck. Depth is suitable for Advanced Open Water divers and above.
