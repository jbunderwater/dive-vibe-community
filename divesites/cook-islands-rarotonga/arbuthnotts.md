# Arbuthnotts

A volcanic reef site on Rarotonga's northeast corner, seven minutes from Avatiu Harbour. Sandy shallows at 8 meters give way to ridges, canyons, and a reef dropoff at 25 to 30 meters, with whitetip reef sharks and guineafowl moray eels among the main resident species.

## Overview

Arbuthnotts sits on the northeast corner of Rarotonga, approximately seven minutes by boat from Avatiu Harbour. The site is named after the owners of two large houses visible on the adjacent shoreline. The dive begins over a sandy patch at approximately 8 meters, with coral bommies leading out to the reef edge at 25 to 30 meters. Beyond the edge, the reef slopes away into canyons and gullies through the volcanic rock to 35 meters and deeper.

Live coral cover is low — approximately 5% — which is characteristic of Rarotonga's northeast coast. The volcanic rock substrate dominates, and sponges and gorgonians are absent. Despite the sparse coral, the exceptional visibility (consistently exceeding 40 meters) and resident marine life make the site worthwhile. Water temperature is approximately 24 to 25°C in June.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Northeast corner, Rarotonga, Cook Islands
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 35 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 40+ meters
- **Water Temperature**: 24-27°C
- **Current**: Generally mild
- **Best Time**: Year-round

## Marine Life

Whitetip reef sharks are regularly seen along the reef edge and in the canyons. Guineafowl moray eels (heavy-bodied morays with a distinctive spotted pattern) shelter in the volcanic rock crevices. Triggerfish, surgeonfish, Moorish idols, threadfin and longnose butterflyfish, and bannerfish are present throughout the depth range. Giant clams sit in the gullies. One firefish (lionfish) was recorded in the crevices. Fish populations are modest overall.

## Dive Profile

Drop to the sandy area at approximately 8 meters and navigate through the coral bommies toward the reef edge at 25 to 30 meters. Explore the canyons and gullies cutting through the volcanic rock, checking overhangs and crevices for sharks resting underneath and morays. The reef slopes to 35 meters and beyond — monitor depth carefully. Return via the bommies and sandy patches to the anchor for a safety stop. The excellent visibility makes navigation straightforward.

## Entry and Exit

Access by dive boat from Avatiu Harbour, approximately seven minutes to the site. Entry by giant stride into open water.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Check crevices and overhangs along the reef edge carefully — whitetip reef sharks rest here
- The volcanic reef structure is characteristic of Rarotonga's northeast coast, quite different from typical Pacific coral reefs
- The outstanding visibility (40+ metres) makes this a good wide-angle photography site despite the sparse coral

## Safety Considerations

The reef edge drops into deeper water — monitor depth carefully near the canyons. Currents are generally mild but can increase near the reef edge. The 35-meter maximum depth limits bottom time on air.
