## Kongsfjorden — Arctic Kelp Forest & Research Fjord

World's premier Arctic kelp forest dive site.

## Overview

Kongsfjorden is a major Arctic diving destination in northwest Spitsbergen, known for lush brown kelp forests from the surface to 15-25 meters depth. The fjord is the focal point for international Arctic marine research, including long-term kelp forest studies examining climate change effects. Divers explore extensive kelp ecosystems with exceptional biodiversity, supporting everything from zooplankton to seals and beluga whales. Water temperature ranges 2-7°C, visibility 10-30 meters. The fjord is accessible by liveaboard and day boat expeditions from Longyearbyen.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Kongsfjorden, northwest Spitsbergen
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Coral reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 25 meters
- **Water Temperature**: 2-7°C (36-45°F)
- **Typical Visibility**: 10-30 meters
- **Current**: Variable fjord currents and glacial outflow
- **Best Time**: June-September (optimal August)
- **Access**: Liveaboard and day boat expeditions from Longyearbyen

## Marine Life

The fjord's defining feature is lush brown kelp (Saccharina, Laminaria species) forests extending 5-25 meters depth. Divers encounter golden-green jellyfish the size of marbles, colorful amphipods, graceful sea angels, sea slugs, anemones, soft corals, starfish, crabs, and Arctic fish including polar cod. Seals are frequently observed; beluga whales and walruses visit. On a single kelp plant, up to 100,000 invertebrates may live. The kelp forest is expanding due to reduced sea ice.

## Dive Profile

Begin in shallow water (5 m) exploring the kelp forest canopy. Descend gradually along kelp-covered slopes to 15-25 meters. The kelp forest provides natural visual reference and protection from strong currents in the fjord. Most marine life is encountered 5-20 meters within the kelp. Stay within the forest structure to avoid drifting in open water beyond the kelp edge. Conservative bottom time (40-50 minutes) due to extreme cold. Ascend gradually, following kelp stalks upward. Conduct safety stop within the kelp if possible for visual reference.

## Entry and Exit

Access is exclusively by boat from local operators or liveaboards. Entry is typically via giant stride or back roll at designated dive site anchors. Follow dive master briefing for current patterns and descent points. Deploy a surface marker buoy (SMB) during your safety stop for boat pickup. Coordinate with dive boat crew for exit procedures.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Fully waterproof your camera—the kelp forest offers exceptional macro and wide-angle opportunities
- Look closely at kelp holdfasts and branches for invertebrate species
- Book with experienced Arctic dive operators familiar with fjord currents
- Check glacier discharge conditions—turbid meltwater affects light and visibility
- Respect the fragile kelp ecosystem; maintain excellent buoyancy control
- This is research-grade diving—communicate with dive ops about ongoing scientific work

## Safety Considerations

Extreme cold (2-7°C) requires dry suit and 30+ dives experience. Hypothermia risk, ice, glacial discharge, and Arctic marine mammals (walrus, beluga) require careful risk assessment. Dive only with experienced Arctic operators. Always dive with a buddy, carry an SMB, and maintain margin for emergencies. Current can vary rapidly based on glacier discharge—brief thoroughly before descent.

## Photography

Kelp forest diving offers world-class macro and wide-angle opportunities. Backlit kelp fronds are stunning; use wide-angle lenses to capture the scale of the forest. Macro subjects include nudibranchs, amphipods, small fish, and invertebrate-covered holdfasts. Focus lighting and composition to navigate the dense kelp environment.
