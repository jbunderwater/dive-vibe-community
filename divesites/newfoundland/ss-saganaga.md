## SS Saganaga

SS Saganaga is a historic wreck dive in Newfoundland, North America.

## Overview

The SS Saganaga was the first vessel sunk in the Bell Island U-boat attacks of September 5, 1942, when U-513 torpedoed two ore carriers in quick succession in Conception Bay — the only German submarine attack on North American soil during World War II. She lies at around 30 meters in Conception Bay, her iron-ore cargo still in the holds and her superstructure providing a haunt for cod, sculpins, and Greenland sharks that frequent these cold waters. The site is part of a designated National Historic Site alongside the other Bell Island wrecks, lending the dive a solemn historical significance beyond its cold-water wreck diving appeal.

## Wreck History

The SS Saganaga was a British steam ore carrier built by Harland and Wolff (launched March 6, 1935, completed April 15, 1935), owned by the South Georgia Company Ltd. (Christian Salvesen & Company) of Leith, Scotland. On the morning of September 5, 1942, she lay at anchor off Bell Island, Conception Bay, loaded with iron ore bound for North Sydney, Nova Scotia. At approximately 11:45–11:46 AM Newfoundland time, U-513 — commanded by Kapitänleutnant Rolf Rüggeberg — fired two torpedoes that struck the Saganaga amidships. (The 11:07 AM time cited in some sources reflects Atlantic Standard Time or a misread of the U-boat logbook; Veterans Affairs Canada and U-513 patrol records both indicate approximately 11:45–11:46 Newfoundland time.) She sank within approximately 30 seconds. Sources consistently report 29 killed, comprising 25 British Merchant Navy sailors, 1 Canadian, and 3 Royal Navy DEMS gunners; 14 crew members survived. The attacks on Saganaga and Lord Strathcona that day are considered the only German submarine attacks on North American soil during World War II. The Bell Island U-boat attacks were designated an Event of Provincial Significance by the Heritage Foundation of Newfoundland and Labrador in 2011.

## Marine Life

Lumpfish, cunner, Atlantic cod, and shorthorn sculpin are commonly observed throughout the superstructure, with ocean pout occupying shadowed recesses in the hull. White, orange, and gold plumose anemones cover the wreck's surfaces, and nudibranchs (*Coryphella verrucosa*) and basket stars have been recorded on the encrusted steel. Pink lion's mane jellyfish (*Cyanea capillata*) drift among the ship's rigging during summer months.

## Dive Profile

The Saganaga is the second shallowest of the four Bell Island wrecks, with the superstructure at approximately 18 m (60 ft) and the main deck around 24 m (80 ft), making it accessible to recreational divers and allowing extended bottom times. The wreck features large open cargo holds with accessible swim-throughs, a massive spare anchor blown free of the bow and resting on the deck amidships, torpedo holes with peeled-back steel, and a stern-mounted gun covered in marine growth. Bulkheads between holds have window-like openings that allow divers to move through the wreck's interior. The site is treated as a war grave; all artefacts remain undisturbed.

## Entry and Exit

All Bell Island wreck diving is conducted by boat. Ocean Quest Adventures (based at Conception Bay South, Newfoundland) operates guided trips to the Saganaga throughout spring, summer, and autumn. The dive boat travels to Bell Island from Conception Bay South, and divers descend a mooring line to the wreck. Ocean Quest has been operating these wreck trips since 1999 and enforces a strict take-nothing policy.

## Tips and Recommendations

A drysuit with serious thermal undergarments is mandatory — water temperature in Conception Bay hovers around 2–4°C (35–39°F) year-round at depth. Visibility can reach 30 m (100 ft) in good conditions but varies seasonally. The wreck is best suited to divers comfortable with cold-water environments and drysuit diving. Dive lights are useful inside the holds despite the shallower depth. This is a war grave: no artefact removal is permitted under Canadian law.

## Safety Considerations

Cold-water exposure is the primary hazard. Drysuit failure or insufficient thermal protection can cause rapid incapacitation; always dive with a buddy and maintain situational awareness regarding thermal comfort. The collapsing superstructure presents some overhead-environment risks inside the holds. Divers should be briefed by the operator on current wreck conditions, as deterioration is ongoing.
