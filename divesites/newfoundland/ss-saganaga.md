## SS Saganaga

SS Saganaga is a WWII iron ore carrier sunk by German U-boat U-513 off Bell Island, Newfoundland — a well-preserved wreck at recreational depths, covered in brilliant anemones and dense with historic artifacts.

## Overview

On September 5, 1942, U-513 fired two torpedoes at the SS Saganaga as she lay at anchor in Wabana Roads awaiting orders to load iron ore. She sank in under three minutes, taking 30 of her 44 crew to the bottom of Conception Bay. Built in Glasgow in 1935 and owned by Christian Salvesen & Company of Edinburgh, the British steamship now rests largely intact with her superstructure beginning at 18 meters (60 feet) and her main deck at approximately 24 meters (80 feet). Portholes, radio sets, and numerous other artifacts remain intact, preserved by Newfoundland's cold, clear water.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Bell Island, Conception Bay, Newfoundland
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate

- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 12–30 meters (40–100 feet)
- **Water Temperature**: 2–6°C (36–43°F) — drysuit mandatory

## Marine Life

Saganaga's surfaces are covered in a riot of plumose anemones in white and pink, with coralline algae adding vivid color to the encrusted hull. The cold, nutrient-rich waters support wolffish, Greenland cod, lumpfish, nudibranchs, sea stars, and pink lion's mane jellyfish. Swim-throughs in the superstructure provide habitat for filter feeders and small fish species. The wreck is considered one of the most marine-life-rich of the Bell Island four.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the superstructure at 18 meters and explore the impressive swim-throughs and intact internal features. Artifacts including 5-inch ammunition shells, 2-ton anchors, and scattered deck fittings document the ship's violent end. The main deck at 24 meters offers open exploration with good ambient light during summer months. Maximum depth of ~30 meters stays within recreational no-decompression limits for most dive computers. Plan a 5-meter safety stop before surfacing.

## Entry and Exit

Dive boats depart from Bell Island (accessible by ferry from Portugal Cove, Newfoundland). The wreck is moored. Entry is giant stride or back roll from the dive boat. Deploy a surface marker buoy (SMB) on ascent.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Drysuit certification is mandatory — water stays near 2–6°C year-round
- Cold-water regulators (rated to -10°C) are essential
- The 5-inch shells scattered across the deck are safe — all four wrecks were cleared of live ordnance by the Royal Canadian Navy in 2021
- Torch essential for swim-through exploration and illuminating interior colors
- One of the best Bell Island wrecks for intact artifacts and marine life density
- Pair with PLM-27 as a two-dive day at shallower depths

## Safety Considerations

Despite recreational depths, water temperature near freezing creates significant cold stress and limits bottom time. Drysuit and quality undersuit are mandatory. The wreck's interior spaces can reduce visibility through silt disturbance — maintain buoyancy carefully and avoid fin-kick inside the hull. Always dive with a buddy and deploy an SMB on ascent.

## Photography

Saganaga is exceptional for both wide-angle and macro photography. The dense anemone coverage creates stunning backdrop compositions. Interior swim-throughs with light-beam effects reward wide-angle strobe work. Macro photographers will find rich subjects in the encrusting life on hull surfaces and on scattered artifacts. Ambient light is often sufficient at the superstructure level during summer months.
