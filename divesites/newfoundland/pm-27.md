## PM-27

PM-27 is a historic wreck dive in Newfoundland, North America.

## Overview

PM-27 was a Canadian ore carrier sunk during the German U-boat attacks on Bell Island's loading piers on November 2, 1942, the same raid that claimed the SS Rose Castle. The wreck rests at around 30 meters in Conception Bay alongside the other Bell Island WWII victims, forming part of the most historically significant collection of war wrecks in Canadian waters. The cold dark Atlantic conditions — temperatures hover near 2°C in winter and rarely exceed 14°C even in summer — preserve the hull remarkably well, and Greenland sharks are occasional visitors to the deeper sections.

## Wreck History

The PLM-27 (the acronym stands for Paris-Lyon-Marseilles) was a 5,633-gross-ton steam ore carrier built in July 1922 by Sir Raylton Dixon & Co Ltd in Middlesbrough, England. Originally named P.L.M. 22, she was owned at the time of her loss by Gibbs & Co of Cardiff and operated by the British Ministry of War Transport. She sailed under a Free French flag during World War II with a multinational crew of 50, including French, Vietnamese, Egyptian, and other sailors from French colonial territories. On November 2, 1942, at approximately 07:03–07:06 hours, U-518 (Kapitänleutnant Friedrich-Wilhelm Wissmann) fired three torpedoes at vessels anchored off Bell Island. One torpedo struck the PLM-27 amidships, splitting her nearly in two; she sank rapidly, killing 12 of her crew. (Sources conflict on the exact sequence: uboat.net records the salvo as simultaneous with the Rose Castle attack; other accounts describe the PLM-27 being struck approximately one minute after the Rose Castle.) The attack on PLM-27 and Rose Castle formed the second U-boat attack on Bell Island, unique as enemy submarine action on North American soil during WWII.

## Marine Life

Flounders rest on the wreck's flat surfaces, and crabs inhabit the rubble around the hull. Plumose anemones colonise the superstructure, and the PLM-27's shallower depth means the wreck receives more ambient light than the deeper Rose Castle, supporting a somewhat richer visible invertebrate community. The intact propeller — the only one preserved among the four Bell Island wrecks — is heavily encrusted with marine growth.

## Dive Profile

The PLM-27 is the shallowest of the four Bell Island wrecks, with the main deck at approximately 18 m (60 ft) and the deepest sections around 30 m (100 ft), making it accessible to recreational divers at all levels and allowing the longest bottom times of the four wrecks. The wreck split nearly in two when it sank; the torpedo hole amidships, where steel plates are peeled back, is a primary feature. The massive intact propeller with one blade missing a chunk from hitting bedrock is a highlight. The forecastle, poop deck, a complete bathtub, and a lifeboat frame reduced to its outline in marine growth are all identifiable. A derrick arrangement in each cargo hold is visible. Water temperature is approximately 7°C (45°F) throughout the descent. Visibility can be variable with a white haze reported at times; the site receives the most light of the four Bell Island wrecks, making it excellent for photography.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive only. Ocean Quest Adventures (Conception Bay South, NL) operates guided trips from spring through autumn. The PLM-27 is recommended for all certification levels given its recreational depth and long bottom times. The anchor and chain from the PLM-27 are displayed at the Bell Island war memorial ashore.

## Tips and Recommendations

The PLM-27's light penetration and shallowest profile make it the most photogenic and accessible of the four Bell Island wrecks. Drysuit and thermal undergarments are mandatory despite the relatively shallow depth — Conception Bay temperatures remain cold year-round. Because this is the easiest of the four wrecks, it is often used as an introduction to the Bell Island WWII dive sites before progressing to the deeper Lord Strathcona and Rose Castle.

## Safety Considerations

Cold water (approximately 7°C / 45°F) requires full drysuit protection for comfortable diving. As with all Bell Island wrecks, this is a designated war grave; no artefact removal is permitted. The split hull creates some overhead areas; divers entering the interior should carry appropriate lighting.
