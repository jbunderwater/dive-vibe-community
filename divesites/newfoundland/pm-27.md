## PM-27

PM-27 (also known as PLM-27) is the shallowest and most diver-accessible of the four WWII wrecks off Bell Island, Newfoundland — the ideal first wreck for divers new to this extraordinary site.

## Overview

The PLM-27 was a French cargo steamer operating under the Free French Forces of General de Gaulle when a German U-boat torpedo struck her dead amidships on November 2, 1942. The ship split almost perfectly in two, and 12 men were killed. The name PLM stood for Paris-Lyon-Marseilles, the French railway company that owned the vessel. Today, the two halves lie at 18 meters (60 feet) to the main deck, making her the most accessible of the Bell Island wrecks and a reasonable dive for Advanced Open Water certified divers with drysuit training. PLM-27 is the only Bell Island wreck with a fully intact propeller — a highlight for photographers.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Bell Island, Conception Bay, Newfoundland
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Deck Depth**: 18 meters (60 feet)
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 12–30 meters (40–100 feet)
- **Water Temperature**: 2–6°C (36–43°F) — drysuit mandatory
- **Current**: Minimal; Conception Bay is well protected
- **Best Time**: June to October

## Marine Life

Dense colonies of plumose anemones cover the wreck surfaces in pink and white, alongside encrusting algae in vivid barbie-pink hues that are unique to Newfoundland's cold, nutrient-rich waters. The split hull creates dramatic swim-throughs and overhangs that shelter nudibranchs, sea stars, wolffish, lumpfish, and Greenland cod. The propeller and stern section are particular highlights for marine life congregations.

## Dive Profile

The relatively shallow deck depth at 18 meters allows for extended bottom times compared to the deeper Bell Island wrecks, making this the best site for exploration and photography. The torpedo split has created an exposed cross-section of the ship's interior. Railings, rigging, compartments, and the intact propeller reward thorough exploration. Maximum depth of ~30 meters is within recreational limits. Plan a safety stop at 5 meters before surfacing.

## Entry and Exit

Dive boats depart from Bell Island (accessible by ferry from Portugal Cove, Newfoundland). The wreck is moored and diver-ready. Entry is giant stride or back roll from the dive boat. Deploy a surface marker buoy (SMB) during your ascent.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Drysuit certification is mandatory — water stays near 2–6°C year-round
- Cold-water regulators (rated to -10°C) are essential
- The split in the hull from the torpedo strike offers dramatic photography and exploration
- The intact propeller at the stern is a must-see highlight
- Torch essential for interior exploration and illuminating the brilliant encrusting life
- The shallowest Bell Island wreck — best choice for maximizing bottom time

## Safety Considerations

Despite the relatively shallow depth, water temperature near freezing creates significant cold stress. Drysuit and quality undersuit are non-negotiable. Iceberg passage has damaged parts of the superstructure — be aware of unstable sections when diving. Always dive with a buddy and deploy an SMB on ascent.

## Photography

The split hull creates dramatic wide-angle compositions with natural light penetrating through the opening. The intact propeller provides a classic wreck photography subject. Plumose anemone colonies offer vibrant macro opportunities. A wide-angle lens with strobe gives the best results at this depth.
