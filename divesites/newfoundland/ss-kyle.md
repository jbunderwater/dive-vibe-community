## SS Kyle

SS Kyle is a historic 220-foot steamship that ran aground in Harbour Grace, Newfoundland in 1967 and now rests in the harbour's mud flats — a partially submerged heritage wreck accessible from shore.

## Overview

The SS Kyle, built in 1913 and nicknamed the "Spirit of Harbour Grace," spent over 50 years serving coastal communities of Newfoundland and Labrador before running ashore during a storm on February 4, 1967. Driven to the end of the Harbour Grace inlet, she was wedged into the mud flats at Riverhead where she remains today. The 220-foot (67-meter) steamship is partially submerged, with much of her rusted hull still visible above the waterline at low tide — making her both a popular shore-side heritage attraction and a shallow dive site accessible directly from the beach. The wreck sits in very shallow water, with the submerged sections reachable in 3–6 meters depending on tidal stage.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Harbour Grace, Conception Bay, Newfoundland
- **Entry Type**: Shore entry
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 6 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 3–8 meters (harbour conditions, can be murky)
- **Water Temperature**: 2–10°C — drysuit strongly recommended
- **Current**: Tidal, generally mild
- **Best Time**: June to September

## Marine Life

In the cold harbour waters, the hull supports encrusting invertebrates including mussels, barnacles, sea anemones, and coralline algae. Small fish species shelter in structural overhangs. Harbour seals occasionally visit the area. The shallow, sheltered environment supports a variety of intertidal and subtidal Newfoundland marine life adapted to cold, nutrient-rich conditions.

## Dive Profile

The wreck is grounded on mud flats in very shallow water. Submerged sections extend from approximately 1–6 meters depth depending on tidal stage. Dive at higher tide for maximum water depth. The hull is partially collapsed and structurally deteriorating — penetration is not recommended. Explore the exterior hull, hull plating, and associated debris field in the surrounding sediment. The proximity to the surface allows snorkelling as an alternative to scuba in calm conditions.

## Entry and Exit

Enter directly from the beach at Riverhead, Harbour Grace. A boardwalk along the shore provides viewing access to the wreck above the waterline. Shore entry is straightforward in calm conditions — the harbor is sheltered. Be aware of boat traffic in the harbour. Exit at the same entry point.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The wreck is famous as a heritage landmark — much of it is visible above water from the beach boardwalk
- Visit at higher tide for the best submerged diving conditions
- The wreck is structurally deteriorating — do not attempt to board the above-water sections
- Cold water requires at minimum a thick wetsuit; a drysuit is strongly recommended
- Combine with a visit to Harbour Grace's aviation heritage (Amelia Earhart departed from here in 1932)
- Low visibility harbour conditions — a torch is useful even at shallow depth

## Safety Considerations

Despite the very shallow depth, the cold water (2–10°C) requires appropriate thermal protection. Harbour conditions can reduce visibility significantly. The wreck is structurally unstable and collapsing in sections — do not enter or board above-water portions of the hull. Be alert for harbour boat traffic. Always dive with a buddy.

## Photography

The wreck is iconic as a semi-submerged shoreline subject — excellent for wide-angle photos that include both above-water and below-water elements. The rusted hull plating and encrusting life create textured macro subjects. The atmospheric combination of fog, rust, and cold water makes for evocative documentary photography.
