## SS Rose Castle

SS Rose Castle is a historic wreck dive in Newfoundland, North America.

## Overview

The SS Rose Castle was a Canadian ore carrier torpedoed by German submarine U-518 on November 2, 1942, in Conception Bay off Bell Island — one of the most audacious U-boat attacks in North American waters. She rests on her port side at around 48 meters in the cold, dark waters of Conception Bay, her hull largely intact and recognisable despite eight decades of submersion. This is a technical dive requiring advanced certification and drysuit experience; the depth, near-zero winter temperatures, and low visibility demand meticulous planning, but the historical weight of diving a genuine WWII torpedo victim makes it one of Atlantic Canada's most compelling wreck dives.

## Wreck History

The SS Rose Castle was a 7,803-gross-ton Canadian-flagged steam ore carrier built in 1915 by Short Bros Ltd, Pallion, Sunderland, owned by Rose Castle SS Co Ltd and managed by Donaldson Brothers Ltd of Sydney, Nova Scotia. She carried a crew of 43. She had survived one earlier attack — a dud torpedo from U-69 on October 20, 1942 — but on November 2, 1942, at approximately 07:03–07:06 hours local time (commonly cited as around 3:34 AM Newfoundland time in some sources), U-518 (Kapitänleutnant Friedrich-Wilhelm Wissmann) struck her with two or three torpedoes while she lay at anchor in Wabana Anchorage, Conception Bay, and she sank. Sources conflict on the exact casualty figure: the Community Stories commemorative project, which records 24 individually named crew members killed, is the most granular source; uboat.net records 23 dead; multiple secondary diving and historical sources state 28 killed. The named-individual count of 24 (with 19 survivors, totalling the vessel's complement of 43) represents the best-documented figure, but researchers should be aware of this unresolved conflict. The attack came shortly after U-518 had also torpedoed the PLM-27. A section of the U-518 torpedo that sank the Rose Castle was discovered by local divers in July 2000 and the O-ring was presented to the mayor of Wabana.

## Marine Life

Divers have observed multicoloured soft corals on fishing nets near the engine room entrance, and wolf eels inhabiting the torpedo compartment. Crabs patrol the torpedo area. At this depth (29–49 m) the cold and low light favour encrusting invertebrates over mobile fish; the dense anemone and soft-coral coverage creates a vivid colour display when illuminated by dive lights.

## Dive Profile

The Rose Castle is the deepest of the four Bell Island wrecks and lies on her port side. The main deck sits at approximately 29 m (95 ft) and the keel reaches beyond 49 m (160 ft) — placing the deepest sections in technical diving territory. Three intact deck levels offer penetration opportunities for suitably qualified divers; the engine room, accessible via a tight crack, contains a telegraph that is a highlight of the dive. The Marconi radio room retains remains of its equipment. The bow forecastle is well-preserved. The hull has deteriorated more slowly than the shallower wrecks due to the depth and cold, but bulkheads have begun collapsing, creating new swim-through passages. Bottom temperature drops to approximately 0°C (32°F) at the deepest sections; temperatures around 7°C (45°F) exist above the thermocline in summer.

## Entry and Exit

Boat dive only. Ocean Quest Adventures (Conception Bay South, NL) operates guided trips throughout spring, summer, and autumn. The deepest sections of the Rose Castle exceed standard recreational certification limits and require advanced or technical diving qualifications.

## Tips and Recommendations

The Rose Castle is best suited to divers with advanced certification and drysuit experience. The depth places the keel well into decompression territory on air; technical divers typically use nitrox or trimix for deeper exploration. The intact penetration opportunities — particularly the engine room telegraph — reward properly equipped wreck divers. Dive lights are essential throughout. Conditions are coldest at the Bell Island wrecks from December to April; summer months offer the warmest water (still under 10°C) and best visibility. This is a designated war grave.

## Safety Considerations

The maximum depth of 48–49 m is beyond recreational diving limits; access to the deepest sections requires appropriate technical training, gas planning, and decompression equipment. Drysuit failure at these depths and temperatures is a serious emergency. The intact superstructure's overhead environments require proper wreck diving training and redundant lights. No artefact removal is permitted.
