## Jackson reef

Jackson Reef is one of the most celebrated dive sites in the Strait of Tiran, northern Red Sea, Egypt. Situated at the southern entrance to the Gulf of Aqaba alongside Thomas Reef, Woodhouse Reef, and Gordon Reef, it forms part of the legendary Tiran Island reef system that attracts liveaboard divers from Sharm el-Sheikh year-round.

## Overview

Jackson Reef rises steeply from the seabed to within a few meters of the surface, its sheer outer walls plunging beyond recreational depth. The reef is best known for the rusted skeleton of the Lara, a cargo vessel that ran aground on the reef's northern tip in 1981 and remains visible above the waterline — a striking orientation marker for divers. Strong tidal currents funnel through the strait between the reefs, making Jackson a prime pelagic encounter site. Visibility regularly exceeds 30 meters in clear conditions, and the thermocline between warm surface water and cooler deep water adds visual drama to dives along the wall.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Strait of Tiran, northern Red Sea, Egypt
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 25-40 meters (80-130 feet)
- **Current**: Strong tidal currents, direction depends on tide
- **Best Time**: September to May (summer winds can disrupt access)

## Marine Life

The current-swept walls draw a reliable cast of pelagic species. Schooling hammerhead sharks are a signature sighting, particularly in the early morning hours when they gather in the blue water off the wall. Grey reef sharks cruise the upper wall edges, while oceanic whitetip sharks patrol the open water column. humphead wrasse in impressive sizes are resident along the deeper sections. Large schools of barracuda and trevally hold position in the current, and spotted eagle rays pass through regularly. The wall itself is colonized by dense soft corals, sea fans, and hard coral formations harboring lionfish, moray eels, and chromis clouds at shallower depths.

## Dive Profile

Divers typically enter upcurrent and drift along the outer eastern wall, descending to 20-30 meters to explore the coral-encrusted buttresses and sandy ledges. The wall face is most productive between 10-25 meters where soft coral growth is densest. Pelagic encounters occur in the blue water off the wall — keep an eye into the open ocean, particularly around reef corners. Ascend with 50 bar remaining to allow a comfortable safety stop in the blue water before surfacing. Drift diving technique is essential here: move with the current, do not fight it, and signal the chase boat early for pickup.

## Entry and Exit

All access is by boat — day boats and liveaboards depart from Sharm el-Sheikh (approximately 45 minutes to 1 hour). Entry via giant stride or back roll. The chase boat follows divers throughout the drift. Surface with an SMB deployed well before your safety stop so the crew can track your position in the current.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Dive on an incoming tide for the best hammerhead sightings in the open water
- Bring a reef hook if conditions allow, to hold position on the wall without touching coral
- Early morning first dives yield the most consistent hammerhead activity
- Watch your depth on the wall — the drop-off is visually compelling and leads to easy depth creep
- A 5mm wetsuit is sufficient in summer; 7mm recommended October through March when thermoclines can be sharp
- The Lara wreck on the northern tip makes an excellent orientation point and photo backdrop

## Safety Considerations

Currents at Jackson Reef can be strong and unpredictable due to tidal flows through the strait. Always dive with a charged SMB and signal horn. Keep your group together — separation from the boat in open water is a serious hazard. This site is rated Advanced; divers without drift diving experience should dive with an experienced guide on their first visit. Fire coral is present on the wall — exposure suits and careful buoyancy are essential.

## Photography

The combination of wide-angle wall scenery and pelagic encounters makes Jackson Reef an outstanding photography site. Shoot the open-water schooling hammerheads with a wide angle and good ambient light in the morning hours. The Lara wreck above the waterline makes a dramatic surface break shot. On the wall, soft corals in orange and red tones photograph well against the deep blue background with strobe fill.
