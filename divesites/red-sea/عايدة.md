## عايدة

عايدة (Aida) is a wreck dive site located near Big Brother Island (جزيرة الأخ الأكبر) in the Brothers Islands group, approximately 60 kilometers off the Egyptian Red Sea coast. The Aida is one of two wrecks at the Brothers — the other being the Numidia — and together they form one of the most celebrated wreck diving destinations in the Red Sea.

## Overview

The Aida II (to distinguish it from an earlier vessel) was a 70-meter Egyptian Navy supply vessel that sank in 1957 after running aground on the southern tip of Big Brother Island. The wreck sits on a steep reef slope on the south side of the island, with the bow section at approximately 30 meters and the stern sliding deeper. The ship lies at an angle on the steep wall, giving the dive a dramatic feel as divers move through a space that is simultaneously a shipwreck and a sheer coral wall. Decades of encrustation have transformed the hull into a vibrant artificial reef, with soft corals, sea fans, and hard corals covering most surfaces.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Big Brother Island (Al-Akh al-Akbar), Brothers Islands, Red Sea, Egypt (liveaboard only)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 25-40 meters (80-130 feet)
- **Current**: Moderate to strong
- **Best Time**: May to October (summer months); year-round with liveaboard

## Marine Life

The Aida wreck is one of the Red Sea's most productive marine life environments. Oceanic whitetip sharks are frequent visitors to the Brothers Islands and are often encountered in open water adjacent to the wreck. Grey reef sharks patrol the wall below and beside the ship. Large schools of barracuda and glassfish mass around the superstructure. The wreck itself hosts resident humphead wrasse, enormous grouper, and large moray eels. The soft coral growth on the hull is exceptional — gorgonian fans, bright red soft corals, and crinoids cover the hull extensively. Sea turtles are occasionally seen resting on the deck sections.

## Dive Profile

The dive begins with a descent to the highest accessible wreck section (around 20-25 meters for the bow rail) and a progression along the hull toward the stern, which descends deeper along the wall. The bow section is the shallowest and most accessible portion; the stern and keel lie significantly deeper and should only be visited by divers comfortable with deeper diving. Plan the profile to spend the maximum allowable time on the wreck before beginning a gradual ascent along the wall, finishing with the safety stop at 5 meters.

## Entry and Exit

Liveaboard access only. The Brothers Islands are accessible by multi-day liveaboards from Hurghada, Marsa Alam, or Port Ghalib. Zodiac or inflatable tender drop to the water. The Brothers are in open sea; currents can be significant and conditions change with weather. The dive guide's briefing on current direction is essential for planning the dive and pickup point.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The Aida is typically combined with the Numidia wreck on the same liveaboard stop — plan two to three dives at the Brothers to do both wrecks thoroughly
- The wreck's angle on the slope creates disorienting perspectives — maintain spatial awareness relative to the hull
- Bring a torch for the interior sections and to illuminate the spectacular soft coral colors
- Oceanic whitetip encounters in open water around the island are more likely early morning — plan a dawn dive
- The steep wall below the wreck extends to extreme depth; monitor depth instruments actively

## Safety Considerations

The Brothers Islands are in open sea with exposure to swell and wind. Boat and tender operations can be affected by weather; be prepared for conditions to change. Current at the Brothers can be strong and variable. Oceanic whitetip sharks are present and bold — maintain group cohesion and do not stray far from the reef or wreck. Advanced diving skills are required; this is not a site for inexperienced divers. Always carry and deploy an SMB. Remote location means medical evacuation is a multi-hour operation.

## Photography

The Aida wreck offers some of the most compelling wreck photography in the Red Sea. The soft coral-encrusted hull, shot with a wide-angle lens from a distance that reveals the ship's full scale, is the signature image. Oceanic whitetip sharks against the deep blue beyond the wreck rail are outstanding subjects. Interior sections with available light and glassfish swarms produce atmospheric compositions. The combination of wreck structure, clear blue water, and abundant marine life makes this a premier all-round underwater photography site.
