# Sirnaka Island

A remote southern Red Sea island site near the St. John's reef complex — home to the sunken fishing trawler El Kamash on the southwestern side, combined with fringing reef suitable for night diving.

## Overview

Sirnaka Island (also spelled Sernaka) lies at approximately 23.83°N, 35.81°E in the southern Egyptian Red Sea, within the St. John's reef complex area. The island is notable for the wreck of the El Kamash, a sunken fishing trawler on the southwestern side of the island. The wreck sits at depths of 30–50 metres (one source reports 25–52 metres). The fringing reef and sandy bottom surrounding the island extends from approximately 5–20 metres and is suitable for night dives. Sea eagles are reported to nest on the island. The area sees very low diver traffic given its remote location, accessible only by extended liveaboard from Hamata or Marsa Alam.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Southern Red Sea, Egypt (St. John's reef area, approximately 23.83°N, 35.81°E)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 25–35 meters
- **Current**: Occasional strong currents, particularly on the wreck side
- **Best Time**: October to May

## Entry and Exit

Liveaboard access only from Hamata or Marsa Alam on extended itineraries. Giant stride entry from the zodiac tender. Full self-sufficiency is required given the remote location. SMB deployment is mandatory for all ascents.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The El Kamash wreck on the SW side reaches significant depth — plan gas accordingly and be aware of NDL limits
- The reef and sandy bottom at 5–20 metres is accessible to intermediate divers and makes an excellent night dive
- The St. John's area is best explored on a multi-day liveaboard allowing 3–4 dives per day
- Sea eagles have been observed nesting on the island — look above the waterline between dives

## Safety Considerations

Remote location is the primary safety consideration — emergency evacuation requires multi-hour response times. Occasional strong currents on the wreck side require experienced dive management. The wreck depth (30–50 metres) requires advanced gas planning. Always carry a fully rigged SMB.
