# Thomas Reef

The smallest but most colourful reef in the Straits of Tiran, featuring three dramatic swim-through arches and walls encrusted with gorgonian fans, black coral, and anemone fields.

## Overview

Thomas Reef is the second reef encountered when travelling north through the Straits of Tiran from Sharm el-Sheikh, positioned between Gordon and Woodhouse reefs. Named after one of the British cartographers who first charted the strait, it is the smallest of the four Tiran reefs but widely considered the most visually striking — known locally as the most colourful reef in the strait. The eastern wall drops from the reef top to a sandy plateau at 25–28 metres, with particularly dense soft coral and gorgonian coverage. The signature feature is a 80-metre-long canyon on the eastern face at around 30–35 metres, crossed by three impressive stone arches (the "Three Thomas Arches") formed by collapsed reef blocks. This canyon drops to around 90 metres and is properly the domain of technical divers, but the arches themselves can be reached by recreational advanced divers. The current funnelled through the Tiran Strait keeps the walls exceptionally nourished.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Straits of Tiran, northern Egyptian Red Sea (between Sinai and Tiran Island)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–35 meters
- **Current**: Moderate to strong; can be unpredictable
- **Best Time**: October to May

## Marine Life

Thomas Reef hosts some of the most diverse reef fish life in the Tiran complex. The gorgonian fan gardens on the eastern wall shelter longnose hawkfish and nudibranchs. Anemone fields dot the reef face, hosting false clownfish. Whitetip reef sharks are regular sightings on the sandy plateau below the wall and inside the canyon. Hawksbill turtles feed on soft corals in the mid-wall zone at 10–15 metres. Large parrotfish, groupers, and lyretail cod are common throughout. Schools of fusiliers and anthias fill the upper reef. The strong currents attract hunting trevally and barracuda at the northern and southern corners. Giant moray eels occupy ledges in the wall crevices. Pelagic visitors in the blue water beyond the wall include occasional hammerheads.

## Dive Profile

Entry is typically on the southern corner of the eastern face, descending to 20–25 metres before working along the wall in the direction of the current. The east wall offers the best coral coverage at 15–25 metres. The three arches lie in the canyon at around 30–35 metres on the eastern face — advanced divers can descend to view them, though the canyon itself continues to 90+ metres and is not for recreational diving. Conditions permitting, circumnavigation of the entire reef is possible for fit, experienced divers with good air management. Finish the dive on the western side, which has more crevices and caverns, before ascending in the current shadow zone.

## Entry and Exit

Day boat access from Sharm el-Sheikh; the journey takes approximately 1.5 hours each way. The Tiran reefs are included on most Sharm el-Sheikh operators' excursion menus. No fixed mooring — entry is drift-style with the boat following divers. Deploy an SMB before final ascent and follow your divemaster's briefed current plan. The lack of moorings means drift discipline is essential.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The Three Thomas Arches at 30–35 m are the unique feature to target — but do not penetrate the canyon below them
- Check the current direction carefully before entering; the southern corner entry works when current runs north-to-northeast
- The western wall has many caverns and crevices worth exploring if conditions and air permit on a long dive
- Look carefully in gorgonian fans for longnose hawkfish — they hold station in the fans and are easy to miss
- A 3mm wetsuit is sufficient in summer; bring a 5mm in winter as temperatures can drop to 21°C

## Safety Considerations

Thomas Reef has no fixed moorings, making drift dive discipline essential. Currents can be strong at the northern and southern tips where water is funnelled through the strait. Be alert for current direction changes — the four reefs create complex eddy patterns. Divers should not attempt the canyon below the arches without technical training and appropriate equipment. Always carry an SMB and dive with a guide familiar with that morning's conditions. Advanced Open Water certification and solid drift diving experience are required.

## Photography

Thomas Reef rewards patient close-focus wide-angle photography on the gorgonian-covered eastern wall. The soft coral formations and anemone fields provide rich reef portraits. The Three Arches, while challenging to photograph due to depth and current, offer dramatic structural compositions when light angles cooperate. Nudibranchs and hawkfish in the gorgonians reward macro technique. Schooling fusiliers and anthias against the deep blue background are accessible throughout the dive.
