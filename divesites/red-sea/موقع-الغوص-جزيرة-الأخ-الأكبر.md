## موقع الغوص - جزيرة الأخ الأكبر

موقع الغوص - جزيرة الأخ الأكبر (Dive Site — Big Brother Island) refers to the wall diving around Big Brother Island (الأخ الأكبر, Al-Akh al-Akbar), the larger of the two Brothers Islands in the Egyptian Red Sea. Big Brother hosts two of the most famous wrecks in the Red Sea — the Numidia and the Aida — as well as dramatic vertical walls on its perimeter that constitute some of the best wall diving in Egypt.

## Overview

Big Brother Island is an isolated reef rising from very deep water approximately 60 kilometers off the Egyptian coast. The island is long and narrow, with vertical walls dropping to extreme depths on its eastern and western faces. The combination of wreck diving and wall diving at the same location, in open-sea conditions with frequent pelagic encounters, makes Big Brother one of the premier liveaboard destinations in the Red Sea. The island is accessible only by multi-day liveaboard and requires a permit from the Egyptian authorities. Currents here can be significant and the site is categorically for experienced divers.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Big Brother Island (Al-Akh al-Akbar), Brothers Islands, Red Sea, Egypt (liveaboard only)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 40 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 25-40 meters (80-130 feet)
- **Current**: Moderate to strong (very strong in certain conditions)
- **Best Time**: May to October

## Marine Life

Big Brother's open-sea location makes it a magnet for pelagic species. Oceanic whitetip sharks are the signature encounter — these open-ocean sharks are bold and curious and appear on almost every dive during the summer season. Grey reef sharks and hammerhead sharks are also regularly sighted. Manta rays transit the island periodically. The walls themselves are covered in large gorgonian sea fans and black coral trees at depth. Dense schools of anthias, glassfish, and barracuda populate the upper wall sections. humphead wrasse, sea turtles, and large moray eels are all resident. Thresher sharks have been reported on early morning dives.

## Dive Profile

Wall dives at Big Brother typically begin at the north or south tip of the island where currents sweep around the corners and bring the best fish concentration. Descend quickly to 30-40 meters to check for deep pelagic activity, then ascend the wall gradually to explore the rich soft coral growth in the 15-25 meter range. The upper wall from 5-15 meters features excellent hard coral coverage. Safety stop at 5 meters on the wall face; deploy an SMB before ascending to the surface.

## Entry and Exit

Liveaboard access only. Inflatable or Zodiac tender drop from the liveaboard. The island is exposed to open-sea conditions and tender operations can be challenging in wind and swell. All divers must be self-sufficient and capable of independent SMB deployment and mid-water safety stops. The liveaboard maintains watch for diver surface markers.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Plan dives for early morning to maximize pelagic encounters, particularly sharks
- The current direction around the island changes; ask the dive guide for the briefing before each dive
- Carry a reef hook on a reel for stabilizing in strong current without damaging the coral
- Both the Numidia and Aida wrecks should be prioritized on any liveaboard stop at Big Brother
- Nitrox is recommended for maximizing bottom time on the most productive depth range of 20-35 meters

## Safety Considerations

Big Brother Island is an advanced site requiring strong open-water diving skills and comfort with current. The exposed ocean location means conditions can change rapidly. Oceanic whitetip sharks may approach divers closely — maintain group cohesion and follow guide instructions. Never surface alone. Deploy an SMB before every ascent and ensure it is highly visible. The remote location means medical response time is measured in hours; all emergency protocols must be followed precisely.

## Photography

Big Brother Island offers world-class wide-angle opportunities. Oceanic whitetip sharks in clear blue open water are the definitive image. The soft-coral walls provide stunning textures and color. Hammerhead silhouettes against the blue water column are achievable in season. The combination of pelagic action and rich reef photography in a single dive makes Big Brother exceptionally productive for serious underwater photographers. Shoot with fast shutter speeds and a dome port; maintain neutral buoyancy to frame moving subjects.
