## אתר צלילה -סטי"ל (24 מטר)

אתר צלילה -סטי"ל (24 מטר) — known in English as the "Steel Wreck at 24 Meters" — is a wreck dive site in the Gulf of Aqaba near Eilat, Israel. The site sits at the crossroads where Israeli, Jordanian, and Egyptian waters meet, in one of the most visited wreck diving areas of the northern Red Sea.

## Overview

The Gulf of Aqaba is one of the world's most productive recreational diving areas for wreck enthusiasts, with several vessels sitting in accessible depths on the sandy floor between the reefs. This steel wreck at 24 meters is one of the smaller wrecks in the Eilat area dive circuit and has been on the seabed long enough to be well-colonized with marine growth. The site is catalogued by Israeli dive operators under its Hebrew designation and is accessible by boat from Eilat. The calm, clear waters of the Gulf of Aqaba offer excellent visibility and generally mild conditions year-round.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Gulf of Aqaba, Eilat area, Northern Red Sea
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20-30 meters (65-100 feet)
- **Current**: Light
- **Best Time**: Year-round

## Marine Life

The wreck structure has developed into a compact artificial reef populated by a typical Gulf of Aqaba community. Dense schools of glassfish and glassy sweepers occupy the interior spaces and shadowed hull sections. Lionfish are resident throughout. Grouper and large snapper patrol the exterior. Moray eels have established themselves in the lower hull. The sandy bottom surrounding the wreck harbors flatfish, garden eels, and occasionally stingrays. The clear Gulf of Aqaba water means the wreck is surrounded by open blue in good visibility, and pelagic fish are sometimes visible in mid-water.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the wreck and begin a systematic exterior circuit. The wreck sits at approximately 20-24 meters at its deepest points. Explore the hull exterior noting the encrustation and resident fish before examining any accessible interior sections. Spend the deepest portion of the dive at the bottom of the hull before progressively shallowing toward the superstructure. Ascend with adequate reserve for a safety stop at 5 meters.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access from Eilat dive operators. The Gulf of Aqaba is protected from swell and conditions are typically calm. Standard back roll or giant stride entry. Deploy an SMB at the safety stop for boat crew to locate pickup position.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Bring a dive torch — the interior sections of steel wrecks in this area are often the most interesting
- Night dives on Eilat-area wrecks are a popular option and can produce excellent marine life sightings
- The year-round diving season in the Gulf of Aqaba means this site can be dived in any month
- Check permit and access requirements with local Eilat operators before diving
- Combine with other Eilat area wrecks for a dedicated wreck diving day

## Safety Considerations

Do not enter the wreck interior without appropriate training and equipment, including a torch and redundant light source. Metal edges on older wrecks can be sharp. Lionfish in overhangs are a consistent stinging hazard — watch hand and foot placement carefully. The Gulf of Aqaba is generally calm with minimal current, making this a manageable site, but always carry and deploy an SMB.

## Photography

Steel wrecks in the Gulf of Aqaba's clear water photograph well. The compact dimensions of this wreck allow full hull shots with a wide-angle lens. Interior shots with available light streaming through openings create atmospheric images. Lionfish, moray eels, and glassfish swarms are reliable portrait subjects. The clear blue water provides an ideal background for mid-distance wreck shots.
