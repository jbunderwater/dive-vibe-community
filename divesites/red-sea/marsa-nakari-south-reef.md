## Marsa Nakari South Reef

Marsa Nakari South Reef forms the southern arm of the Marsa Nakari bay entrance on Egypt's southern Red Sea coast, near the Wadi el Gemal National Park. Paired with the North Reef directly across the bay mouth, the South Reef shares the same context of exceptional marine conservation and minimal diver impact that characterises this stretch of coastline, while offering slightly different topography and microhabitats along the southern reef wall.

## Overview

The South Reef sits at 24.926°N, 34.964°E — just 150 meters south of the North Reef across the bay mouth. While both reefs share the same outstanding water quality, protected status, and relatively undisturbed fish populations, the South Reef is noted for its distinct coral architecture and the opportunity to combine both reefs in a single extended dive or as a paired dive on the same day's itinerary. The outer south-facing reef slope experiences slightly different current exposure than the north reef, which can influence both coral growth patterns and the mix of pelagic visitors along the wall.

## Overview

The south reef arm at Marsa Nakari features a sloping outer wall with coral formations extending from the surface down to the sandy bottom at approximately 20 meters. The reef's sheltered inner bay face provides a contrasting environment of calmer, shallower coral gardens suitable for the end-of-dive shallow section. The Wadi el Gemal National Park context means the site receives genuine conservation protection — no commercial fishing, no coastal development, and limited dive traffic.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Marsa Nakari, southern Red Sea coast, Egypt (near Wadi el Gemal National Park)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive (liveaboard access)
- **Site Type**: Coral reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20-30 meters (65-100 feet)
- **Current**: Moderate; variable at the outer reef tip
- **Best Time**: October to May

## Marine Life

Sea turtles are the headline wildlife encounter throughout the Marsa Nakari system, and the South Reef is no exception — both green and hawksbill turtles regularly patrol the reef, resting on sandy sections and grazing. The south-facing outer slope supports good populations of larger fish: napoleon wrasse, groupers, and grey reef sharks are all reported consistently by operators visiting this site. The inner bay side of the south reef has softer, gentler coral growth suited to slower exploration, with anthias, chromis, butterflyfish, and the full complement of Red Sea reef fish in abundance.

## Dive Profile

The dive is most effective as a circuit: descend at the outer reef tip to 15-20 meters, follow the outer wall south to cover the outer slope coral formations, then turn and work back along the inner bay face at 8-15 meters before ascending to the shallower reef top for the final phase. Alternatively, when combined with the North Reef, divers can do a trans-bay traverse covering both reefs in a single long dive on favourable current. Monitor air carefully when exploring both reefs.

## Entry and Exit

Liveaboard and day-boat access from Marsa Alam or Hamata area. The sheltered bay approach protects entry conditions from northerly winds. Confirm current conditions with the guide before diving the outer reef tip section. Deploy SMB before ascending.

## Tips and Recommendations

- If visiting Marsa Nakari on a liveaboard, plan to dive both the North and South reefs on the same day for a complete picture of the bay system
- The south reef tip can have the most interesting current-driven fish concentrations — worth exploring despite the slightly more demanding conditions there
- Carry a torch for exploring the deeper sections of the outer wall
- Sea turtle encounters are enhanced by slow, patient approach — spend time in the seagrass patches adjacent to the reef

## Safety Considerations

Remote location and minimal nearby services require complete dive self-sufficiency. The outer reef tip can produce stronger and unpredictable current — abort to the inner bay side if conditions exceed your comfort. Fire coral is present on all hard coral formations. Always carry a fully charged SMB.

## Photography

The South Reef offers similar photography opportunities to the North Reef with the added dimension of the outer slope's different light angle in the afternoon. Wide-angle reef portraits on the outer wall, sea turtle subjects in the seagrass margin, and napoleon wrasse close-ups are all well-suited to this site. Morning dives favour the North Reef for best light; afternoon dives favour the South Reef's westward-facing outer wall.
