## Sandy Island

Sandy Island is a reef site near Safaga in the northern Red Sea, Egypt. At coordinates placing it within the Safaga region (approximately 26.8°N), this site is part of the dense constellation of reefs, shoals, and small islands that define Safaga's diving landscape. Safaga has long been appreciated as a destination with excellent current-driven diving, and Sandy Island offers a moderately challenging reef dive in this productive zone.

## Overview

Sandy Island consists of a coral-encrusted reef rising from a sandy seafloor, reaching to within a few meters of the surface. The reef is characterized by coral formations with sandy channels and rubble zones at the base, transitioning to denser coral growth on the upper sections. The Safaga area regularly receives upwelled nutrient-rich water driven by the prevailing wind patterns, supporting unusually high reef fish density and regular pelagic visitors. Visibility is consistently strong in this region.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Safaga, Red Sea, Egypt
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Coral reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20-35 meters (65-115 feet)
- **Current**: Moderate to strong
- **Best Time**: October to May

## Marine Life

The Safaga reef system supports a diverse and abundant reef fish community. Schools of fusiliers and anthias are a constant presence over the coral tops, and napoleon wrasse patrol the deeper reef edges. Parrotfish, surgeonfish, and triggerfish are common. Moray eels are residents in the coral crevices, and lionfish and scorpionfish conceal themselves in ledges. The sandy substrate around the base of the reef harbors garden eels, stingrays, and flatfish. Spinner dolphins are frequently seen in the surrounding water, and hammerhead sharks are reported in the area during cooler months.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the reef crest at around 5–8 meters and follow the reef contour down to the sandy base at 18–20 meters. The dive can be structured as a circuit around the island, exploring both current-exposed and sheltered sides for contrasting species and coral character. Spend time in the mid-depth range of 10–15 meters where coral cover is densest. Ascend along the reef with a safety stop near the top of the structure at 3–5 meters.

## Entry and Exit

Access is by dive boat from Safaga harbor operators or from a liveaboard moored on the site. Giant stride or back roll entry. Descend promptly if current is running. At the end of the dive, ascend the reef to the safety stop depth and deploy an SMB before surfacing for boat pickup.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Check current direction before the dive and plan your route accordingly
- Winter months (October to January) offer the best chance of hammerhead sightings
- Both sides of the island have different character — explore the current-exposed and sheltered faces
- Safaga day-trip boats typically run multiple dives in a single day around nearby sites
- A safety sausage is essential given the boat traffic in the Safaga channel

## Safety Considerations

Currents can be strong at Safaga, particularly between islands and reef structures. Be aware of channel flow that can push divers off the reef. Carry and deploy an SMB for all open-water ascents. Fire coral is present on reef faces — keep a slight distance from the reef surface. Lionfish and scorpionfish blend into the reef; avoid placing hands on coral ledges.

## Photography

The combination of current-exposed walls, sandy channels, and excellent visibility makes Sandy Island a productive wide-angle site. Schooling fusiliers and anthias over coral heads are classic Red Sea images. The sandy base offers stingray and garden eel portraits. Morning light produces the best ambient conditions for wide-angle work.
