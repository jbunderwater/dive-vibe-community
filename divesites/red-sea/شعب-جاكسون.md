## شعب جاكسون

شعب جاكسون (Jackson Reef) is one of the four legendary reefs in the Tiran Strait between the Sinai Peninsula and Tiran Island, in the northern Red Sea, Egypt. Jackson Reef is the northernmost of the Tiran reefs and is considered one of the finest advanced wall dives in the region, with strong currents, dramatic wall structure, and outstanding marine life.

## Overview

Jackson Reef sits at the northern end of the Tiran Strait reef system, alongside Thomas, Woodhouse, and Gordon Reefs. The reef rises sharply from the deep water of the strait and features vertical walls on multiple sides that plunge well beyond recreational depth limits. The top of the reef is visible at the surface and bears the rusting hull of the MV Lara, a cargo vessel that grounded on the reef in 1981 and has been a landmark ever since. The surrounding walls are among the most richly decorated in the Sinai, with dense soft coral and gorgonian coverage. Current is the defining characteristic of Jackson Reef — the Tiran Strait funnels significant water movement, producing conditions that make this site exclusively suitable for experienced drift divers.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Tiran Strait (northernmost reef), near Sharm el-Sheikh, Sinai Peninsula, Red Sea, Egypt
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20-35 meters (65-115 feet)
- **Current**: Moderate to very strong
- **Best Time**: September to May

## Marine Life

Jackson Reef is one of the Sinai's premier shark diving sites. Grey reef sharks are a near-constant presence, often in groups of three to eight individuals, cruising the wall in the current. Hammerhead sharks appear seasonally, particularly in winter months. humphead wrasse of impressive size are resident and approachable. Schools of big-eye trevally and barracuda form large formations in the water column. Eagle rays and stingrays glide along the wall base. The walls themselves host enormous gorgonian sea fans at depth, with brightly colored soft corals and crinoids at recreational depths. Anthias cloud the upper reef in spectacular numbers.

## Dive Profile

Jackson Reef is almost always dived as a drift dive. The entry point is determined by current direction on the day. Drop into the water upflow of the wall and drift along the west or east face depending on conditions. Maintain 15-25 meters to balance the best marine life viewing with reasonable no-decompression time. The wreck of the Lara is visible at and above the surface on the reef top and can be used as a landmark for navigation. Ascend well before the down-current end of the reef, deploy an SMB in mid-water, and complete the safety stop before surfacing for boat pickup.

## Entry and Exit

Day boat from Sharm el-Sheikh operators. The dive guide will assess current and brief the entry point accordingly. Back roll from the boat deck. Mid-water SMB deployment is essential for boat pickup in drift conditions. Boat crews experienced with Tiran reef dives monitor the current and position for pickup, but clear SMB visibility is critical.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The wreck of the Lara on the reef top is a landmark and reference point — note its position during entry
- Strong current here can be exceptional for marine life — go with it rather than fighting it
- Carry a reef hook on a short leash for stabilizing in strong current without touching the reef
- Winter months (November to March) maximize hammerhead shark encounter probability
- Full experience with drift diving is a prerequisite; do not attempt Jackson for the first time in strong conditions

## Safety Considerations

Jackson Reef is an advanced site and should not be attempted by inexperienced drift divers. Currents can be powerful and may shift direction. An uncontrolled descent along the wall is a real hazard — maintain buoyancy actively and monitor depth constantly. SMB deployment in moving water requires practice; all divers should be comfortable with this skill before the dive. Never surface without checking for boat traffic — the Tiran Strait is an active shipping lane. Carry signaling devices beyond the SMB: whistle, mirror, and a dive alert horn.

## Photography

Jackson Reef's combination of sharks, schools of pelagic fish, and richly decorated walls makes it one of the most rewarding photography dives in the Sinai. Grey reef shark groups photographed against the wall backdrop are the defining image. Drift photography requires buoyancy control and a comfortable grip — avoid using the reef or wall as an anchor. The gorgonian sea fans at depth are stunning wide-angle subjects in clear blue water. The Lara wreck on the reef surface is visible above the water and can be photographed from the surface during boat transit for contextual shots.
