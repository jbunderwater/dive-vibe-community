## جيرة ديدلاس

جيرة ديدلاس (Geziret Daedalus, or Daedalus Reef) is one of the most remote and celebrated wall dive sites in the Egyptian Red Sea. Located approximately 80 kilometers off the Egyptian coast in the open Red Sea, the reef is an isolated atoll rising from deep water and is accessible only by liveaboard. It is one of the top sites in the Red Sea for pelagic shark encounters.

## Overview

Daedalus Reef (officially Elphinstone East, though Daedalus designates a separate lighthouse reef to the north) is a landmark liveaboard destination in the Egyptian Red Sea. The reef is a narrow atoll rising steeply from extreme depths, surrounded on all sides by dramatic walls dropping into the abyss. The remoteness of the site — far from any coastline — means that pelagic fish life is abundant and encounters with open-ocean species are a near-certainty. A lighthouse on the reef surface is manned by Egyptian border authorities, and dive boats moor in the lee of the reef. Current at Daedalus can be strong and conditions can change rapidly, making it a site for experienced divers.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Open Red Sea, approximately 80 km off the Egyptian coast (liveaboard only)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 40 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 25-40 meters (80-130 feet)
- **Current**: Moderate to very strong
- **Best Time**: June to October (for hammerhead and thresher sharks); year-round for oceanic whitetips

## Marine Life

Daedalus is world-renowned for oceanic whitetip sharks, which are reliably encountered on virtually every dive throughout the year. These open-ocean sharks are inquisitive and may approach divers closely, making this one of the premier oceanic whitetip experiences on the planet. Scalloped hammerhead sharks school at Daedalus, particularly in summer months. Thresher sharks are occasionally sighted in the early morning. Grey reef sharks are present year-round. Manta rays and eagle rays transit the reef. Schools of big-eye jack and barracuda patrol the wall. The wall itself is covered in large sea fans, soft corals, and black coral trees.

## Dive Profile

Daedalus is typically dived by descending quickly along the wall to 35-40 meters in the early part of the dive, watching for sharks in the blue water off the wall. Ascend progressively along the wall face, pausing to explore the sea fans and overhangs. The upper section from 10-20 meters has the densest coral growth and the highest concentration of reef fish. Safety stop at 5 meters — hold the wall if current is present. Drift dives are standard here; coordinate carefully with the boat on pickup location.

## Entry and Exit

Liveaboard access only. Zodiac tender drop to the mooring or directly into the water. Given the strong currents and exposed position, divers must enter and exit efficiently. Deploy an SMB before ascending and ensure the boat crew is watching. In strong current conditions, divers may surface a considerable distance from the boat; carry a signaling whistle and mirror.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Oceanic whitetip encounters are almost guaranteed — respect the "no hand" rule around these sharks and stay in a group
- Early morning dives during summer months maximize the chance of hammerhead and thresher shark encounters
- A reef hook is useful at depth when current is strong and the goal is to stay in place watching sharks in the blue
- Stay close to the wall in strong current — the exposed atoll position means conditions can change rapidly
- Brief any less-experienced divers thoroughly on shark behavior protocols before the dive

## Safety Considerations

Strong and unpredictable current is the primary hazard at Daedalus. Divers must be comfortable with drift diving and mid-water SMB deployment. Oceanic whitetip sharks are bold and may make close passes — do not dive alone or stray from the group. The remote location means medical facilities are many hours away; all emergency equipment must be aboard the liveaboard. If conditions deteriorate rapidly, abort the dive immediately and return to the tender. Always carry SMB, whistle, and signaling mirror.

## Photography

Daedalus is among the world's top shark photography sites. Oceanic whitetip sharks pass very close to divers and offer exceptional wide-angle portrait opportunities. Schools of hammerheads photographed against the blue open water are iconic images. The wall itself provides texture and reef fish subjects for those shooting between shark encounters. Sunball shots looking up through the clear water toward the surface are particularly striking here. High ISO settings and fast shutter speeds are needed for moving sharks in open water.
