# Elphinstone Reef

The Red Sea's premier site for oceanic whitetip shark encounters, with sheer vertical walls draped in gorgonians plunging into open blue water 12 km offshore from Marsa Alam.

## Overview

Elphinstone Reef is a cigar-shaped offshore reef running roughly north-south, around 400 metres in length, located approximately 12 kilometres east of Marsa Alam in the southern Egyptian Red Sea. It is consistently ranked among the world's top ten dive sites. The reef's defining feature is its dramatic vertical walls — sheer drops on all sides that descend into deep blue water, covered in massive gorgonian sea fans, soft corals, and sea whips. The northern and southern tips broaden into wide plateaus at 20–40 metres, draped in dense soft coral growth. Elphinstone is famous above all for its resident population of oceanic whitetip sharks, which are reliably encountered at the southern plateau between October and December, often circling divers with their characteristic unhurried inquisitiveness.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Offshore Marsa Alam, southern Egyptian Red Sea
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 40 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–40 meters
- **Current**: Moderate to strong; can be very strong on the plateaus
- **Best Time**: October to May (oceanic whitetips peak October–December)

## Marine Life

Elphinstone's primary draw is the oceanic whitetip shark (*Carcharhinus longimanus*) — one of the most reliable encounters with this species anywhere in the world. They concentrate around the southern plateau, typically at 20–30 metres, and are genuinely curious, often making multiple passes close to divers. Scalloped hammerhead sharks school on the northern plateau, particularly in winter. Manta rays pass through between May and August. Dolphins are common along the western wall. The wall itself hosts massive schools of black snappers, anthias clouds, angelfish, butterflyfish, and surgeonfish. Giant moray eels inhabit the crevices. The gorgonian fans provide shelter for longnose hawkfish and glassfish. Hawksbill turtles are regular sightings at cleaning stations along the wall.

## Dive Profile

Most dives begin at the southern plateau, descending to 20–30 metres on the plateau itself before working along the eastern or western wall back toward the boat. The wall is the main feature — keep the reef face on one side and the open blue on the other, scanning for pelagics. The plateau at the southern end sees the highest concentration of oceanic whitetips. The north plateau is shallower (15–25 m) and rewards patient hovering in blue water during morning dives for hammerhead sightings. Both ends require drift awareness as the current can accelerate unpredictably. Plan your safety stop for the 5-metre level over the reef top; this is also where oceanic whitetips sometimes appear, having followed divers up from depth.

## Entry and Exit

Access by day boat from Marsa Alam or as part of liveaboard itineraries combining Elphinstone, Brothers, and Daedalus. Boats typically moor on the western side of the reef. Entry by backroll or giant stride. Current direction determines whether the eastern or western wall is dived first. Always deploy an SMB before ascending — drift distances from the reef can be significant. Dawn dives maximise shark encounter probability and minimise crowding.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Arrive early — Elphinstone is heavily visited and morning conditions are calmer and less crowded
- Do not chase or attempt to touch the oceanic whitetips; their approach behaviour rewards stillness
- The southern plateau is the prime location for whitetip encounters; plan at least 15 minutes hovering there
- Bring a reef hook for the plateau if your guides permit — holding position without effort lets you focus on the pelagics
- Nitrox is advisable for maximising bottom time on the plateau dives
- Three to four dives are typical on a day trip, allowing exploration of both north and south ends

## Safety Considerations

This is an open-ocean reef with no shelter — current can be strong and unpredictable, particularly at the plateaus. The exposed location means surface conditions can deteriorate quickly. Advanced divers only; a minimum of Advanced Open Water certification is required and most operators require 30+ logged dives. Oceanic whitetip sharks are powerful animals; give them space and maintain a calm demeanour — erratic movement can increase interest. Always carry a safety sausage (SMB) and whistle. The deep blue beyond the wall is deceptively inviting — depth discipline is essential.

## Photography

Elphinstone may be the finest destination in the world for oceanic whitetip shark photography. Their bold approach and the intense blue open-water backdrop produce exceptional images. Use a wide-angle lens (10–17mm) and position yourself at the shark's level rather than looking down. The gorgonian-covered wall is outstanding for close-focus wide-angle reef portraits. For hammerheads at the north plateau, arrive at first light and position yourself at the current-facing edge.
