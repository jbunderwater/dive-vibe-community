## Salem Express

The Salem Express is one of the most emotionally significant and historically important wreck dives in the Red Sea. The ro-ro passenger ferry struck Hyndman Reef near Safaga on the night of December 14–15, 1991, sinking in approximately 20 minutes while en route from Jeddah, Saudi Arabia, to Safaga, Egypt. The official Egyptian death toll figure stands at 464–470, though some accounts suggest higher numbers and reports of the total aboard conflict with this figure. Approximately 117 bodies were recovered; roughly 180 survivors were rescued. The majority of those aboard were Muslim pilgrims returning from Mecca; the specific nationalities of passengers are not consistently described across sources. The ferry was reportedly overcrowded beyond its 1,256-passenger capacity. The wreck lies at a maximum depth of approximately 27 metres, with upper deck structures accessible from around 15 metres. The ship measured 115 metres in length and 4,771 gross tons.

## Overview

The Salem Express rests on her starboard side on a sandy slope adjacent to Hyndman Reef. She is relatively intact, which makes for a haunting and atmospheric dive. Coral growth has been colonizing the hull steadily since 1991, and the wreck now hosts a significant reef ecosystem. Many divers treat this site with particular solemnity given the loss of life associated with the sinking. Penetration of the interior is possible but should be undertaken only by wreck-qualified divers — the interior is silty, and the wreck still contains the personal effects of those who perished. Many operators and dive guides ask that visitors refrain from penetrating the passenger areas out of respect for the dead.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Hyndman Reef, Safaga, Red Sea, Egypt
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 27 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 15-30 meters (50-100 feet)
- **Current**: Moderate
- **Best Time**: October to May

## Marine Life

The wreck superstructure supports a rich reef community. Dense schools of glassfish hover around railings and over open deck areas, pursued by hunting trevally. Giant moray eels occupy larger crevices in the hull, while lionfish patrol ledges and companionways. Groupers have taken up residence in sheltered sections. The hull is encrusted with soft corals, tunicates, and sponge growth that increases with each passing year. Turtles and occasionally dolphins are sighted in the water column above the wreck.

## Dive Profile

Descend the mooring line to the upper hull at approximately 15 meters. From here, work along the exterior — the bow, bridge section, stern ramp, and lifeboat davits are all distinctive features. The deepest accessible point is around 27 meters at the sandy bottom beside the starboard side. Penetration divers with wreck certification can enter the vehicle deck through the stern ramp. Bottom time is limited at depth; plan a conservative dive with plenty of time for a safety stop and the ascent.

## Entry and Exit

Access is by liveaboard or day boat from Safaga harbor. A mooring buoy is typically attached to the wreck. Entry via giant stride. Descend directly to the wreck on the mooring line. Ascend on the line or do a blue-water ascent deploying an SMB at 5 meters.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Treat this as a memorial dive — avoid disturbing the interior passenger areas
- Wreck penetration requires proper training and a powerful dive torch
- Silt inside the wreck disturbs easily; maintain excellent buoyancy control
- Book with an operator who provides a proper briefing on the site's history and safety protocols
- An SMB and dive computer are essential for this site

## Safety Considerations

The interior of the wreck contains significant silt and disorientation risks — only trained wreck penetration divers should enter. Strong currents can develop near the reef edge. The stern ramp has sharp metal edges. Always ascend on the mooring line or deploy an SMB if ascending in open water. Carry redundant lighting if penetrating. This is an emotionally demanding dive for many divers; be prepared.

## Photography

The scale of the wreck makes wide-angle and fisheye lenses ideal. The encrusted railings and davits photographed against blue water are iconic images. The schools of glassfish hovering over open deck areas are a compelling mid-water subject. Inside the vehicle deck, a wide-angle torch-lit shot can capture the otherworldly atmosphere. Macro subjects are found on every colonized surface of the hull.
