# Daedalus Reef

A remote liveaboard-only atoll 80 km offshore in the southern Red Sea, delivering schooling scalloped hammerheads, thresher sharks, and pristine walls carpeted in soft coral.

## Overview

Daedalus Reef (also known as Abu Kizan) lies approximately 80 kilometres east of Marsa Alam, well isolated in the central Egyptian Red Sea — roughly halfway between Egypt and Saudi Arabia. Its complete remoteness means it can only be reached by liveaboard, which, together with its protected marine park status, has kept its reefs in outstanding condition. The reef is less than one kilometre long and around 100 metres wide, with near-vertical drop-offs on all sides descending to 40 metres and beyond. A Victorian-era lighthouse stands on the northern tip; the wreck of the Zealot, an old steamer, lies in 80–100 metres at the base of the northern slope. "Anemone City" on the eastern wall is a long stretch of reef covered entirely in anemones and hosting a dense community of anemonefish. Currents are strong and run predominantly north to south along the walls, which keeps the coral in excellent health and draws significant pelagic life.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Central Egyptian Red Sea, 80 km east of Marsa Alam (liveaboard only)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive (liveaboard)
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 40 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–30 meters
- **Current**: Strong to very strong
- **Best Time**: March to December (hammerheads May–September; whale sharks May–June; threshers winter)

## Marine Life

Scalloped hammerhead sharks are Daedalus's signature species, schooling on the northern side of the reef primarily from May to September — one of the most reliable hammerhead encounters in the entire Red Sea. Oceanic whitetip sharks are present from spring through autumn. Whale sharks pass through during May and June. Thresher sharks are more frequently seen in winter, particularly at dawn on the southern end. Silky sharks and grey reef sharks are year-round residents. In addition to the pelagics, the reef holds dogtooth tuna, giant trevally, schooling barracuda, and reef species including large groupers, moray eels, hawksbill turtles, and the enormous anemonefish colonies of Anemone City. The walls are smothered in soft corals, large gorgonian fans, and black coral trees.

## Dive Profile

Daedalus divides naturally into north, east, south, and west faces. The northern point is the prime location for hammerhead encounters — position yourself facing the open blue at 25–35 metres and wait. The eastern wall features Anemone City and is excellent for slower, observational dives. The southern point has thresher shark potential in winter. Dives are typically drift dives following the prevailing north-to-south current. Most marine life is found between 15–35 metres. The Zealot wreck at 80–100 metres is for technical divers only. Limit bottom time at the northern tip to allow decompression-free profiles, and plan safety stops carefully given the open-water conditions.

## Entry and Exit

Liveaboard access only from Hurghada, Port Ghalib, or Marsa Alam, typically on itineraries also covering Brothers and Elphinstone. The reef has mooring buoys for liveaboards. Entry by backroll or giant stride; descent should be rapid on the northern end to avoid being swept off the reef tip. All ascents in open-ocean conditions require SMB deployment. Night conditions can be demanding — check sea state before any night dives.

## Tips and Recommendations

- For hammerheads, dive the northern tip at first light — before other liveaboards are in the water
- Keep your body still and avoid sudden movements when hammerheads are present; erratic movement disperses schools
- Anemone City on the eastern wall rewards a slow, close-focus dive at mid-depths (15–20 m)
- A minimum of 30 logged dives is recommended; many operators require 50+ for this reef
- Nitrox greatly extends useful bottom time at the northern plateau
- Bring a dive computer with a depth alarm — the open blue is disorienting and descent can go unnoticed

## Safety Considerations

Daedalus is an advanced-level open-ocean dive site with no shore proximity or shelter. Currents can exceed 2 knots and can change without warning. Surface conditions can be rough with ocean swells. Divers must be completely comfortable with open-ocean drift diving, deploying SMBs mid-water, and ascending without a reef reference. All pelagic sharks here are wild and should not be approached aggressively. Minimum requirement is Advanced Open Water certification with 30 logged dives; most responsible operators enforce stricter limits. Always carry a safety sausage, compass, and torch.

## Photography

The northern plateau hammerhead schooling is among the most sought-after photographs in Red Sea diving — wide-angle with a strong blue background and multiple silhouettes. Anemone City rewards macro and close-focus wide-angle equally: the clownfish-anemone combinations are vivid and accessible. For thresher sharks (winter, dawn, south end), long lenses are rarely useful underwater; position yourself correctly at depth and shoot wide. The soft-coral-draped walls are ideal for reef environmental portraits.
