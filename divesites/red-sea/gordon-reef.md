## Gordon Reef

Gordon Reef is the southernmost of the four major reefs in the Straits of Tiran, one of the Red Sea's most celebrated dive destinations. It sits in the narrow strait between the Sinai Peninsula and Tiran Island, where strong tidal currents fuel an extraordinary abundance of marine life.

## Overview

Gordon Reef (also spelled Gordan) is named for Colonel Gordon of the British Army, who reportedly used this strategic channel during the 19th century. The reef forms a broad platform that barely clears the surface, and its most dramatic feature is the rusting hulk of the Loullia, a Lebanese freighter that ran aground on the reef's shallow eastern edge in 1981. The eastern and southern faces drop away in dramatic near-vertical walls draped with large sea fans and soft corals, making this primarily a wall dive with an optional wreck element. The sheer volume of water funnelled through the Tiran Straits means the current here is nearly always running, which in turn means the nutrient supply — and consequently the fish life — is exceptional.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Straits of Tiran, northern Egyptian Red Sea, near Sharm el-Sheikh
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive with shallow wreck (MV Loullia)
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters (main dive profile; wall extends deeper)
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–35 meters
- **Current**: Strong to very strong; essential planning required
- **Best Time**: October to May

## Marine Life

The strong tidal currents through the Straits of Tiran attract one of the Red Sea's most diverse pelagic communities. Schooling hammerhead sharks are a regular winter sighting (December–March). Grey reef sharks and whitetip reef sharks patrol the wall. humphead wrasse of impressive size cruise the reef edge. Schools of barracuda, trevally, and jacks hang in the current, and manta rays pass through seasonally. The walls are festooned with large gorgonian fans and carpeted with soft corals fed by the current. Hawksbill turtles are year-round residents, and moray eels occupy every available crevice.

## Dive Profile

Gordon Reef is best dived as a drift dive, entering up-current and floating with the flow along the eastern or southern wall. Descend quickly to get below the current layer in the first 5 meters and pick up the drift at 10–15 meters along the wall. Advanced divers can use depressions and swim-throughs in the wall to adjust pace. The wreck of the Loullia on the reef top adds an interesting element at the end of the drift. Finish the dive in the protected northern area and ascend there for the pickup.

## Entry and Exit

All-day boat access from Sharm el-Sheikh and Naama Bay operators; the Tiran reefs are on virtually every operator's roster. Drift entry: backroll or giant stride and follow the briefed current direction. Always surface away from the reef crest on the current shadow side, deploying an SMB well before the final ascent.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Never attempt Gordon Reef without a pre-dive current briefing from a guide who dived it that morning
- Advanced buoyancy control is critical — mid-water hovering in current is required
- Carry an SMB; in emergency drift situations, it is your primary safety device
- The wreck of the Loullia provides a natural end to the dive and a reference point for ascent
- Hammerhead sightings are most common December–March in the blue water off the eastern wall
- Visit on a neap tide if possible for the most manageable current

## Safety Considerations

Gordon Reef carries genuine advanced-level risk due to current intensity. Inexperienced divers should not attempt this site. Current speeds can exceed 2 knots and the narrow strait creates unpredictable eddies. A diver who surfaces in the current can be swept significant distances from the boat. Always dive with a reliable SMB and a dive knife (useful if you become entangled in mooring lines). The boat captain must maintain watch at all times during the dive.

## Photography

The strong current makes photography challenging, but the subjects are extraordinary. Wide-angle shots of schooling hammerheads or barracuda against the intense blue of the strait are iconic Red Sea images. The wall itself is highly photogenic — the gorgonian fans and soft coral gardens are best captured with close-focus wide-angle technique.
