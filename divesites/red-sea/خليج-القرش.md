## خليج القرش

خليج القرش (Khalij al-Qirsh, meaning "Shark Bay" in Arabic) is a reef dive site near Sharm el-Sheikh on the Sinai Peninsula, Egypt. True to its name, the bay is known for reliable shark sightings — particularly whitetip reef sharks resting on the sandy bottom — as well as excellent coral reef diving in a relatively sheltered location.

## Overview

Shark Bay is located along the coast north of Sharm el-Sheikh town and forms part of the broader Na'ama Bay diving area. The site is characterized by a sloping reef with a sandy channel running along the bottom, and the sandy areas are where whitetip reef sharks are most commonly found resting during the day. The reef itself drops from a shallow hard coral plateau to approximately 25-30 meters where the slope meets the open sea floor. The site sees regular boat traffic but remains a quality dive due to the consistent shark and turtle sightings that draw repeat visitors.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Shark Bay, north of Sharm el-Sheikh, Sinai Peninsula, Red Sea, Egypt
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Coral reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20-30 meters (65-100 feet)
- **Current**: Light to moderate
- **Best Time**: September to May

## Marine Life

Whitetip reef sharks are the signature marine life of Shark Bay, typically found resting motionless on the sandy bottom or in the channels between coral formations. Green and hawksbill sea turtles are almost guaranteed sightings, often in the shallows. Napoleon wrasse are regularly encountered and can be very approachable here. The reef supports the full Sinai reef community including large schools of anthias, parrotfish, and surgeonfish. Triggerfish are common in the upper reef zone. Moray eels and lionfish are abundant in the crevices. Eagle rays are occasionally seen gliding over the sandy floor.

## Dive Profile

Descend to the reef slope and work toward the sandy channels where sharks are most likely resting. The sand floor at 20-25 meters is the prime shark-watching zone; approach slowly and maintain neutral buoyancy to avoid startling the animals. After observing sharks and any turtles in the deeper section, ascend progressively up the reef slope. The upper reef at 5-10 meters has excellent coral coverage and high fish density. Safety stop on the reef top before ascending.

## Entry and Exit

Boat access from Sharm el-Sheikh and Na'ama Bay operators. This site is also accessible as a shore dive from the Shark Bay beach for those comfortable with shore access. Boat entry by back roll or giant stride. Coordinate with the boat for the pickup point after the safety stop.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Move slowly and calmly near resting whitetip reef sharks — sudden movements will cause them to leave
- Sea turtles are almost certain sightings; give them plenty of space and don't pursue or approach from above
- The sandy channels are worth spending time in, even if sharks have moved on, as rays and flatfish are common
- Shore diving is possible here for independent divers with appropriate experience
- Afternoon dives can be productive as water activity decreases and reef behavior normalizes

## Safety Considerations

Whitetip reef sharks at Shark Bay are not aggressive under normal circumstances — maintain calm behavior and never corner or chase them. Fire coral is present throughout the upper reef; maintain buoyancy discipline. Triggerfish nesting areas in the upper reef can produce territorial behavior — give nesting individuals wide berth. Current is usually mild but can increase; carry an SMB and be prepared to abort if conditions change.

## Photography

Shark Bay is one of the more reliably productive photography sites in the Sharm el-Sheikh area due to the consistent shark and turtle sightings. Whitetip reef sharks resting on sand provide controlled, accessible portrait opportunities. Sea turtles are relaxed subjects in good natural light. Wide-angle reef-and-shark compositions are the standard subject. The sandy channels allow low-angle shots looking up toward the surface with sharks or turtles silhouetted against the light.
