# Shaab Marsa Alam

A 790-metre offshore L-shaped reef just 4 km from Marsa Alam marina — featuring coral gardens on both sides, a small sunken cruise ship (the Legend), caverns, and spinner dolphin transits.

## Overview

Shaab Marsa Alam is a large offshore reef located approximately 4 kilometres from the Marsa Alam marina in the southern Egyptian Red Sea — one of the most accessible offshore reefs in the region, reachable in under 15 minutes by boat. The reef is shaped like an inverted L, around 350–400 metres in length and 50–80 metres wide at the northern end narrowing to 10–20 metres in the south. The northwestern side hosts a large coral garden, and a smaller one covers the eastern face. Small caverns are found in the middle section of the reef. The most historically notable feature is the wreck of the *Legend*, a sunken cruise ship resting on the southern side, providing an additional wreck element. The reef's position just outside the main Marsa Alam shipping channel means spinner dolphins frequently transit through the area on their morning return from feeding grounds.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Offshore Marsa Alam, southern Egyptian Red Sea (4 km from marina)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–25 meters
- **Current**: Mild to moderate; stronger on the eastern face
- **Best Time**: Year-round; October to May optimal

## Marine Life

Spinner dolphins transit the reef area in the morning — encounters with pods passing through in mid-water are common on early dives. Hawksbill turtles rest on the coral gardens and the shallow reef platform. Groupers hold territory at the northern dive area. Schools of snappers, bannerfish, and jacks are consistent residents. Whitetip reef sharks patrol the deeper sections of the eastern wall. Garden eels extend from the sandy areas. Moray eels and octopuses occupy reef crevices. Scorpionfish and crocodilefish are camouflaged on the sandy substrate. The wreck of the Legend on the southern face provides a compact wreck experience alongside the reef.

## Dive Profile

North and south ends are the primary dive areas. The northern end supports the main coral garden at 10–20 metres — work along the northwestern face for the densest coral and fish life. The cavern section in the middle of the reef is worth exploring: small, well-lit caverns accessible without technical training. The southern end at the Legend wreck provides an optional wreck circuit. On the eastern face, moderate current allows a pleasant drift along the reef slope. Safety stop at 5 metres over the shallow reef platform.

## Entry and Exit

Short boat transfer from Marsa Alam marina — one of the most convenient offshore reef day trips from Marsa Alam. Giant stride entry from dive dhow or RIB. Multiple operators offer daily trips, often combining Shaab Marsa Alam with nearby sites such as the Abu Dabbab dugong site or Shaab Sataya.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Dive early for the best chance of spinner dolphin encounters — pods transit the area in the morning after nighttime feeding trips offshore
- The Legend wreck on the southern face is a compact and accessible wreck element worth adding to the circuit plan
- The caverns in the middle section can be entered without overhead training — they are small, well-lit, and open
- Combine with Abu Dabbab beach (dugongs) for a diverse two-site day from Marsa Alam
- The eastern drift face rewards a passive approach — let the current carry you along the reef rather than fighting it

## Safety Considerations

Conditions are generally suited to intermediate divers, though the eastern face current can be moderate to strong. Drift divers must deploy an SMB before ascending from the outer reef edge. The Legend wreck — full penetration into enclosed sections — requires wreck diving training. Caverns in the middle section are safe for recreational exploration within the light zone. Standard reef hazards apply: fire coral on upper surfaces, sea urchins in crevices. Always carry an SMB for open-water ascents.

## Photography

The coral garden on the northwestern face is a standard wide-angle reef composition subject with good light in morning conditions. Spinner dolphins on transit — if you are in the water early — are outstanding action wide-angle subjects. Hawksbill turtle portraits on the reef platform are consistently accessible. The cavern section rewards a wide-angle torch shot from inside looking toward the open-water entrance. The Legend wreck provides hull and structure compositions for wreck photographers.
