# Sha'ab Ohroob / Wreck of Sabri Baba 1

A remote southern Red Sea reef with an Egyptian cargo vessel lying on the reef slope — a dual wreck-and-reef experience in near-pristine water where coral colonisation has advanced across the hull and the surrounding reef sees minimal diver pressure.

## Overview

Sha'ab Ohroob (the reef) and the Wreck of Sabri Baba 1 (the vessel) occupy the same position at approximately 22.63°N, 36.56°E in the southern Egyptian Red Sea — deep in liveaboard-only territory between the main St. John's Reef complex and the Sudanese border. The Sabri Baba 1 was an Egyptian cargo or supply vessel that came to rest on the reef slope, where it has undergone extensive coral colonisation. Like many wrecks in this part of the Red Sea, it sees very few divers — the combination of wreck and pristine reef makes it a standout site on any itinerary that reaches this far south. The name "Sha'ab Ohroob" translates roughly as "reef of retreating" or "escape reef."

## Site Information

- **Location**: Southern Egyptian Red Sea (~22.63°N, 36.56°E); liveaboard territory
- **Entry Type**: Liveaboard dive only
- **Site Type**: Wreck/Reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20–35 meters
- **Current**: Variable; moderate is typical
- **Best Time**: October to May

## Marine Life

The wreck structure provides substrate for extensive coral colonisation — soft corals and hard coral growth cover most accessible external surfaces. Schools of snapper and glassfish occupy the interior spaces accessible through open deck sections. Moray eels inhabit the corroded cavities. The surrounding reef supports the full southern Red Sea assemblage: grey reef sharks on the outer slope, anthias clouds over the reef crest, and hawksbill turtles on the reef platform. Groupers are resident in the sheltered sections of the wreck. The remote position means fish density is noticeably higher than at equivalent sites in the more visited northern Red Sea.

## Dive Profile

Begin on the wreck structure: descend to the deepest accessible section at 15–18 metres and work systematically up the hull, examining coral growth and resident fish populations. Penetration is not recommended without overhead training; exterior hull exploration is fully satisfying. Transition to the adjacent reef slope at 10–15 metres after 20–25 minutes on the wreck. Complete the dive on the reef crest at 5–10 metres. Safety stop at 5 metres over the reef.

## Entry and Exit

Liveaboard access only — part of extended southern Red Sea itineraries. Egyptian permits required for this latitude zone. Giant stride entry from the tender, positioned over the wreck. SMB deployment mandatory before ascending — the remote position requires boat tracking of all diver positions.

## Tips and Recommendations

- The transition from wreck to surrounding reef makes this a particularly satisfying single dive — budget time for both components
- Carry a torch for the wreck exterior cavities — moray eels and resident groupers occupy the corroded sections
- The undisturbed nature of the wreck and reef rewards slow, methodical exploration rather than rushing between highlights
- Check the sandy slope adjacent to the wreck for eagle rays and blue-spotted stingrays

## Safety Considerations

Wreck penetration is not appropriate without overhead-environment training — the vessel is corroded and internal navigation is complex. External hull exploration provides full access to the marine life. Remote location: dive conservatively and within limits. SMB on all ascents. Current assessment before entry.

## Photography

The coral-colonised exterior of the Sabri Baba 1 in clear southern Red Sea water provides outstanding wide-angle wreck portrait conditions — the combination of colonised hull surfaces and natural light creates images unavailable at mainstream wreck sites. Glassfish schools in the interior spaces photographed through open deck sections reward wide-angle with torch backlighting. The adjacent reef slope in pristine condition provides secondary wide-angle subjects.
