# Elba Reef, North Plateau

The northern plateau of Egypt's most remote reef complex — a pristine pelagic waypoint near the Sudanese border where grey reef sharks and hammerheads patrol gorgonian-lined walls of exceptional clarity.

## Overview

The North Plateau of Elba Reef is the northern section of the Elba Region reef complex at the Egyptian-Sudanese border at approximately 22°N. The plateau structure descends from the reef surface at 15–20 metres before transitioning to the steep outer wall that drops to 45+ metres. The northern exposure of this plateau catches different current patterns from the south and west sections of the reef, often producing the best conditions for schooling hammerheads and the most intense gorgonian fan growth on the wall edge. Like all sections of Elba Reef, this site is exclusively accessible by extended liveaboard with Egyptian Navy permit and represents some of the most pristine diving in the entire Red Sea.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Northern section of Elba Reef, Egyptian-Sudanese border area (liveaboard only)
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive (liveaboard)
- **Site Type**: Coral reef
- **Difficulty Level**: Intermediate
- **Maximum Depth**: 20 meters (plateau; wall continues deeper)
- **Typical Visibility**: 30–40 meters
- **Current**: Moderate to strong at the northern tip
- **Best Time**: March to November

## Marine Life

Grey reef sharks are regular residents of the northern plateau edge, patrolling between the reef wall and the open water column. Hammerhead sharks are more frequently encountered at the northern end of the reef on morning dives, when they approach from deeper water to shallower zones. Schooling bigeye trevally and dogtooth tuna are characteristic of the open water around the northern tip. The plateau surface supports dense hard coral coverage — brain corals, massive Porites, and tabulate Acropora — undisturbed by the human pressure that characterises more accessible Red Sea sites. Expansive gorgonian fans at 20–25 metres provide structure for smaller reef species. Bumphead parrotfish are observed throughout the Elba system.

## Dive Profile

Descent to the northern plateau at 15–20 metres, positioning facing the open water to the north for the best pelagic sightings at dawn. Hammerheads on the northern plateau are most reliably encountered during the first 30 minutes after sunrise. After the open-water viewing period, move along the plateau edge and drop to 20 metres to examine the gorgonian garden. Complete the dive at the reef top at 5–10 metres. The northern tip of the reef can carry accelerated current — plan the approach conservatively.

## Entry and Exit

Extended liveaboard from Port Berenice or Port Ghalib with Egyptian Navy clearance. Entry by zodiac tender. Giant stride entry. Current assessment is mandatory at the northern tip before every dive. SMB deployment required for all ascents.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Dawn dives at the northern plateau maximise hammerhead shark probability
- Approach the plateau edge slowly and hover at the edge rather than descending over it — this gives the widest field of view into the blue water below
- The gorgonian fans at 20–25 m deserve careful, close-focus exploration
- Bring redundant navigational tools — the featureless open water beyond the northern tip is disorienting
- This site is rarely dived: behave accordingly — slow movement, quiet approach, and patient observation

## Safety Considerations

Remote location requires full self-sufficiency. The northern plateau tip can concentrate strong current flowing over the reef edge. Divers should remain within the plateau area and not be drawn over the edge into deeper water. Advanced buoyancy control is essential. Always carry a fully rigged SMB and deploy before every ascent. Dive conservatively given the remote medical evacuation requirements.

## Photography

The northern plateau provides the widest open-water vista in the Elba system for hammerhead shark photography. Dawn positioning at the plateau edge with a wide-angle lens (10–17mm) facing north offers the best chance of silhouettes against first light. The gorgonian fans at 20–25 m reward close-focus wide-angle with strobe fill. The pristine coral coverage on the plateau top produces exceptional reef panoramas in the exceptional Elba visibility.
