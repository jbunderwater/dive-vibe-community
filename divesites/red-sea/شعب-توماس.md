## شعب توماس

شعب توماس (Thomas Reef) is one of the four reefs that form the Brothers Reef cluster — collectively known as "The Brothers" (Al-Akhawain) — in the northern Sinai area near Sharm el-Sheikh. Thomas Reef is a separate pinnacle/wall site in the Tiran Strait, part of the famous Tiran Island reef system, and is considered an advanced wall dive due to the strong currents in the strait.

## Overview

Thomas Reef is one of four reefs in the Tiran Strait — along with Jackson, Woodhouse, and Gordon Reefs — that sit in the shipping channel between Tiran Island and the Sinai coast. The strong tidal currents that pass through the strait have produced exceptional wall diving on the vertical faces of these reef structures. Thomas Reef is a pinnacle rising from deep water with walls dropping dramatically on multiple sides. The current here regularly brings nutrient-rich water that supports enormous fish populations and exceptional soft coral growth. The reef is exclusively accessible by day boat from Sharm el-Sheikh.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Tiran Strait, near Sharm el-Sheikh, Sinai Peninsula, Red Sea, Egypt
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wall dive
- **Difficulty Level**: Advanced
- **Maximum Depth**: 30 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 20-35 meters (65-115 feet)
- **Current**: Moderate to very strong
- **Best Time**: September to May

## Marine Life

The Tiran reefs host some of the most spectacular pelagic fish action in the northern Red Sea. Grey reef sharks are almost always present at Thomas Reef, often in groups of several individuals patrolling the wall. Schools of large barracuda and giant trevally wheel in the current off the reef face. Hammerhead sharks are occasionally encountered. Napoleon wrasse are resident and regularly seen. Thousands of anthias and glassfish cloud the upper sections of the wall. The soft coral growth on the wall surfaces supports nudibranchs, crinoids, and a range of smaller reef creatures for macro divers.

## Dive Profile

This is typically a drift dive in the direction of the prevailing current. Divers enter upgcurrent of the reef and drift along the wall face, maintaining 15-25 meters as the optimal depth for marine life sightings. The wall drops deeper than recreational limits, so depth management requires active attention. Begin the ascent before reaching the down-current end of the reef and execute a mid-water safety stop with SMB deployment. The current will determine pacing — be prepared for a fast drift.

## Entry and Exit

Day boat from Sharm el-Sheikh operators. Entry depends on current assessment; the dive guide will brief the entry point based on current direction. Back roll entry from the boat. Mid-water SMB deployment for the safety stop and boat pickup. Experienced boat crews manage Tiran drift dives routinely but communication is essential.

## Tips and Recommendations

- Never attempt the Tiran Strait reefs without proper drift diving experience and comfort with current
- A reef hook or SMB with reel is recommended for managing yourself in strong current sections
- Brief with the dive guide on the current forecast for the day — Thomas Reef conditions vary significantly
- Carry a signaling mirror and whistle in addition to the SMB in case you are swept away from the boat
- Watch for shark activity in the mid-water off the wall — grey reef sharks are often in evidence

## Safety Considerations

Strong current in the Tiran Strait is the defining safety factor. Divers who are not experienced with current diving should not attempt this site. Unintentional deep descents are a risk on vertical walls when distracted by marine life or disoriented by current. Always carry an SMB and ensure it can be deployed mid-water in current. Never dive alone. If separated from your buddy or guide, ascend to 5 meters, deploy the SMB, and complete the safety stop before surfacing.

## Photography

Thomas Reef offers exceptional action photography opportunities. Grey reef sharks patrolling the wall face with the deep blue behind them are the signature image. Schools of barracuda and trevally in the current produce dramatic wide-angle scenes. The soft coral walls provide vibrant backgrounds for reef portraits. Shooting from a reef hook in the current allows stable framing of fish passing through the water column. Fast shutter speeds and a wide field of view are optimal settings.
