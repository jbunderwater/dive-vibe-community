## אשקלון - העוגנים (Ashkelon - The Anchors)

אשקלון - העוגנים — "Ashkelon - The Anchors" — is a dive site near Ashkelon associated with ancient anchors on the seabed. The Ashkelon coast has a documented history of underwater archaeological finds, including anchors and shipwreck material from the Hellenistic and Roman periods discovered during surveys by the Israel Antiquities Authority.

## Overview

Archaeological surveys conducted off the coast of Ashkelon have revealed substantial underwater heritage material. A Hellenistic/Early Roman shipwreck assemblage discovered approximately 100 metres offshore at around 3–4 metres depth yielded four large iron anchors along with copper-alloy nails, lead sheathing, fishing-net sinkers, bronze balance-scales, cooking ware, and an oil lamp — artefacts associated with a vessel believed to have sunk between the 1st and 2nd century BCE.

The site name "The Anchors" likely refers to this assemblage of ancient anchors visible on the shallow seabed near Ashkelon. The site sits at coordinates consistent with the shallow nearshore zone. Precise current access status for recreational diving is not confirmed — underwater archaeological sites in Israel are managed by the Israel Antiquities Authority and may have restrictions.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Off Ashkelon coast, southern Mediterranean coast of Israel
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Wreck dive
- **Maximum Depth**: 10 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 5–10 metres (seasonal)
- **Current**: Mild
- **Best Time**: October to May

## Archaeological Significance

The Ashkelon coast hosts evidence of one of the busiest ancient Mediterranean trading routes. In addition to the Hellenistic wreck assemblage, surveys have identified marble columns, capitals, and architectural elements in the shallow nearshore zone — cargo destined for Roman-period construction at Ashkelon. Iron Age shipwrecks have also been found in deeper water offshore.

## Safety and Access

Underwater archaeological sites are protected under Israeli law. Do not disturb or remove any artefacts. Contact the Israel Antiquities Authority Marine Unit for dive permits if required. Always dive with a buddy and carry an SMB.

## Tips and Recommendations

- This site is of primary interest to archaeologically-minded divers
- Underwater photography is the ideal way to document finds without disturbance
- Shallow depth makes it accessible to Open Water-level divers
