## הנקרות (Rosh Hanikra Grottoes)

Israel's most famous underwater dive site — a network of ten interconnected sea grottoes carved into dramatic white chalk cliffs at the Lebanese border, where divers surface inside pools visible to tourists above.

## Overview

The Rosh Hanikra grottoes (הנקרות, "the grottoes" in Hebrew) are cavernous hollows carved by centuries of Mediterranean wave action into the soft white chalk cliffs at Israel's northernmost coastal point, on the border with Lebanon. The site features ten spectacular interconnected underwater grottoes linked by a large underwater tunnel approximately 180 metres long. The caves have spacious interiors with high ceilings and wide passages — well-lit from multiple entrances, making them notably accessible for cave-trained recreational divers.

The site's peak experience is the large interior pool inside the Rosh Hanikra visitor park, where divers can surface and be seen by tourists arriving by cable car from above. The chalk cliffs rise 60 metres above the sea. Depth is very shallow — approximately 7 metres maximum — making this an accessible dive despite the cave environment.

Diving here requires IDF Navy clearance (due to the Lebanese border), must be conducted with a licensed local guide familiar with the site, and access is typically arranged through local dive clubs.

## Site Information

- **Location**: Rosh Hanikra, Israel-Lebanon border, Northern Mediterranean
- **Entry Type**: Boat dive
- **Site Type**: Cave/cavern
- **Maximum Depth**: 7 meters
- **Typical Visibility**: 5–15 metres (excellent when calm; wave action can reduce it)
- **Current**: Wave surge can be significant — do not dive in rough conditions
- **Best Time**: May to October (calmer Mediterranean conditions)
- **Special Requirements**: IDF Navy clearance required; must dive with licensed local guide

## Marine Life

The grottoes host colourful fish, small groupers, spiny sea urchins, multi-coloured anemones, and occasional loggerhead sea turtles. Crabs inhabit crevices throughout the cave network. Light levels vary dramatically between cave sections, creating striking colour gradients.

## Dive Profile

Entry is through the seaward cave openings, which are large and well-lit. Divers navigate through the interconnected network of tunnels and chambers, surfacing in interior pools. The shallow depth (maximum 7 metres) allows extended bottom time. Constant wave action keeps the caves clear of silt, maintaining excellent visibility when sea conditions are calm.

## Tips and Recommendations

- This site is only dived through local operators — contact dive clubs in Nahariya (Putsker) or Shavei Zion (Indigo) to arrange access and IDF clearance
- Surface conditions can change rapidly — do not dive if swell exceeds 0.5 m
- The cave walls and formations are fragile chalk — maintain careful buoyancy
- Surfacing inside the grotto pool is a highlight — coordinate with your guide
- Water colour changes dramatically through the day (green morning, blue afternoon)

## Safety Considerations

Diving inside the grottoes requires awareness of wave surge, which can push divers against cave walls. Exits must be confirmed before entry. The site is very close to the Lebanese border — always follow local operator guidance on permitted areas. Emergency extraction would be complex in rough conditions.

## Photography

The grottoes offer extraordinary underwater photography. Light shafts penetrating the cave openings, the chalk formations, and the contrast between cave interiors and open sea create dramatic compositions. A wide-angle lens is recommended.
